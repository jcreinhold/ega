## 1.1. Review of the exterior algebra complex

**1.1.1.**

<!-- label: III.1.1.1 -->

Let $A$ be a ring, $\mathbf{f} = (f_{i})_{1 \leq i \leq r}$ a system of $r$ elements of $A$. The *exterior algebra
complex* $K_{\bullet}(\mathbf{f})$ corresponding to $\mathbf{f}$ is a chain complex `(G, I, 2.2)` defined as follows:
the graded $A$-module $K_{\bullet}(\mathbf{f})$ is equal to the *exterior algebra* $\Lambda(A^{r})$, graded in the usual
way, and the boundary operator is the *interior multiplication* $i_{\mathbf{f}}$ by $\mathbf{f}$, viewed as an element
of the dual $(A^{r})^{\vee}$. Recall that $i_{\mathbf{f}}$ is an *antiderivation* of degree $-1$ on $\Lambda(A^{r})$,
and that if $(\mathbf{e}_{i})_{1 \leq i \leq r}$ is the canonical basis of $A^{r}$ then $i_{\mathbf{f}}(\mathbf{e}_{i})
= f_{i}$; the verification of $i_{\mathbf{f}} \circ i_{\mathbf{f}} = 0$ is immediate.

An equivalent definition is the following. For each $i$, consider the chain complex $K_{\bullet}(f_{i})$ defined by
$K_{0}(f_{i}) = K_{1}(f_{i}) = A$, $K_{n}(f_{i}) = 0$ for $n \neq 0, 1$, and with boundary operator $d_{1} : A \to A$
equal to *multiplication by $f_{i}$*. Then $K_{\bullet}(\mathbf{f})$ is the *tensor product*

```text
  K_•(𝐟) = K_•(f_1) ⊗ K_•(f_2) ⊗ ⋯ ⊗ K_•(f_r)                                (G, I, 2.7)
```

equipped with its total grading; the isomorphism between this complex and the one defined above is immediate.

> _Translator's note._ EGA's $complexe de l'alg\grave{e}bre ext\acute{e}rieure$ is what is now universally called the
> *Koszul complex*. We retain EGA's name on first introduction and use it interchangeably below.

**1.1.2.**

<!-- label: III.1.1.2 -->

For every $A$-module $M$, define the *chain complex*

```text
  K_•(𝐟, M) = K_•(𝐟) ⊗_A M                                                   (1.1.2.1)
```

and the *cochain complex* `(G, I, 2.2)`

```text
  K^•(𝐟, M) = Hom_A(K_•(𝐟), M).                                              (1.1.2.2)
```

If $g$ is a $k$-cochain of the latter complex, and if we set

```text
  g(i_1, …, i_k) = g(𝐞_{i_1} ∧ ⋯ ∧ 𝐞_{i_k}),
```

then $g$ identifies with an *alternating* map from $[1, r]^{k}$ to $M$, and the above definitions give

```text
  d^k g(i_1, i_2, …, i_{k+1})
        = Σ_{h=1}^{k+1} (−1)^{h−1} f_{i_h} g(i_1, …, î_h, …, i_{k+1}).        (1.1.2.3)
```

<!-- original page 83 -->

**1.1.3.**

<!-- label: III.1.1.3 -->

From the preceding complexes one deduces, as usual, the *homology* and *cohomology* $A$-modules `(G, I, 2.2)`

```text
  H_•(𝐟, M) = H_•(K_•(𝐟, M)),                                                (1.1.3.1)
  H^•(𝐟, M) = H^•(K^•(𝐟, M)).                                                (1.1.3.2)
```

One defines an $A$-isomorphism $K_{\bullet}(\mathbf{f}, M) \xrightarrow{\sim} K^{\bullet}(\mathbf{f}, M)$ by associating
to each chain $z = \Sigma (\mathbf{e}_{i_{1}} \wedge \cdots \wedge \mathbf{e}_{i_{k}}) \otimes z_{i_{1}, \cdots, i_{k}}$
the cochain $g_{z}$ with

```text
  g_z(j_1, …, j_{r−k}) = ε · z_{i_1, …, i_k},
```

where $(j_{h})_{1 \leq h \leq r-k}$ is the strictly increasing sequence complementary to the strictly increasing
sequence $(i_{h})_{1 \leq h \leq k}$ in `[1, r]` and $\epsilon = (-1)^{\nu}$, with $\nu$ the number of inversions of the
permutation $i_{1}, \cdots, i_{k}, j_{1}, \cdots, j_{r-k}$ of `[1, r]`. One checks that $g_{dz} = d(g_{z})$, yielding an
isomorphism

```text
  H^i(𝐟, M) ≅ H_{r−i}(𝐟, M)                  for 0 ≤ i ≤ r.                 (1.1.3.3)
```

In this chapter we shall mostly consider the cohomology modules $H^{\bullet}(\mathbf{f}, M)$.

For a given $\mathbf{f}$, it is immediate `(G, I, 2.1)` that $M \mapsto H^{\bullet}(\mathbf{f}, M)$ is a *cohomological
functor* `(T, II, 2.1)` from the category of $A$-modules to the category of graded $A$-modules, vanishing in degrees
`< 0` and $> r$. In addition

```text
  H^0(𝐟, M) = Hom_A(A/(𝐟), M),                                               (1.1.3.4)
```

writing $(\mathbf{f})$ for the ideal of $A$ generated by $f_{1}, \cdots, f_{r}$; this follows immediately from
`(1.1.2.3)`, and $H^{0}(\mathbf{f}, M)$ is clearly identified with the submodule of $M$ annihilated by $(\mathbf{f})$.
Similarly, `(1.1.2.3)` gives

```text
  H^r(𝐟, M) = M / (Σ_{i=1}^r f_i M) = (A/(𝐟)) ⊗_A M.                         (1.1.3.5)
```

We shall use the following well-known result, whose proof we recall for completeness.

**Proposition (1.1.4).**

<!-- label: III.1.1.4 -->

*Let $A$ be a ring, $\mathbf{f} = (f_{i})_{1 \leq i \leq r}$ a finite family of elements of $A$, and $M$ an $A$-module.
If, for $1 \leq i \leq r$, the multiplication $z \mapsto f_{i} z$ on $M_{i-1} = M / (f_{1} M + \cdots + f_{i-1} M)$ is
injective, then $H^{p}(\mathbf{f}, M) = 0$ for $p \neq r$.*

**Proof.** It suffices to prove that $H_{i}(\mathbf{f}, M) = 0$ for every $i > 0$ by virtue of `(1.1.3.3)`. We argue by
induction on $r$, the case $r = 0$ being trivial. Set $\mathbf{f}' = (f_{i})_{1 \leq i \leq r-1}$; this family satisfies
the hypotheses of the statement, so on setting $L_{\bullet} = K_{\bullet}(\mathbf{f}', M)$ we have $H_{i}(L_{\bullet}) =
0$ for $i > 0$ by induction and $H_{0}(L_{\bullet}) = M_{r-1}$ by `(1.1.3.3)` and `(1.1.3.5)`. To abbreviate, set
$K_{\bullet} = K_{\bullet}(f_{r}) = K_{0} \oplus K_{1}$, with $K_{0} = K_{1} = A$ and $d_{1} : K_{1} \to K_{0}$ equal to
multiplication by $f_{r}$; by definition (1.1.1) we have $K_{\bullet}(\mathbf{f}, M) = K_{\bullet} \otimes_{A}
L_{\bullet}$. We invoke the following lemma.

**Lemma (1.1.4.1).**

<!-- label: III.1.1.4.1 -->

*Let $K_{\bullet}$ be a chain complex of free $A$-modules, zero except in dimensions `0` and `1`. For every chain
complex $L_{\bullet}$ of $A$-modules, there is an exact sequence*

```text
  0 → H_0(K_• ⊗ H_p(L_•)) → H_p(K_• ⊗ L_•) → H_1(K_• ⊗ H_{p−1}(L_•)) → 0
```

*for every index $p$.*

<!-- original page 84 -->

This is a particular case of an exact sequence of low-degree terms of the Künneth spectral sequence `(M, XVII, 5.2 (a))`
and `(G, I, 5.5.2)`; one can also prove it directly, as follows. Regard `K_0` and `K_1` as chain complexes (zero in
dimensions $\neq 0$ and $\neq 1$ respectively); then there is an exact sequence of complexes

```text
  0 → K_0 ⊗ L_• → K_• ⊗ L_• → K_1 ⊗ L_• → 0,
```

to which the exact sequence in homology applies:

```text
  ⋯ → H_{p+1}(K_1 ⊗ L_•) → H_p(K_0 ⊗ L_•) → H_p(K_• ⊗ L_•) → H_p(K_1 ⊗ L_•)
                       → H_{p−1}(K_0 ⊗ L_•) → ⋯.
```

But $H_{p}(K_{0} \otimes L_{\bullet}) = K_{0} \otimes H_{p}(L_{\bullet})$ and $H_{p}(K_{1} \otimes L_{\bullet}) = K_{1}
\otimes H_{p-1}(L_{\bullet})$ for every $p$; one verifies immediately, in addition, that the boundary $\partial : K_{1}
\otimes H_{p}(L_{\bullet}) \to K_{0} \otimes H_{p}(L_{\bullet})$ coincides with $d_{1} \otimes 1$. The lemma follows
from the exact sequence above and from the definitions of $H_{0}(K_{\bullet} \otimes H_{p}(L_{\bullet}))$ and
$H_{1}(K_{\bullet} \otimes H_{p-1}(L_{\bullet}))$.

With this lemma in hand, the rest of the proof of (1.1.4) is immediate: the induction hypothesis combined with (1.1.4.1)
gives $H_{p}(K_{\bullet} \otimes L_{\bullet}) = 0$ for $p \geq 2$; moreover, if we show that $H_{1}(K_{\bullet},
H_{0}(L_{\bullet})) = 0$, then the same lemma also gives $H_{1}(K_{\bullet} \otimes L_{\bullet}) = 0$. But by definition
$H_{1}(K_{\bullet}, H_{0}(L_{\bullet}))$ is the kernel of the multiplication $z \mapsto f_{r} z$ on $M_{r-1}$, and that
kernel is zero by hypothesis. The proof is complete.

**1.1.5.**

<!-- label: III.1.1.5 -->

Let $\mathbf{g} = (g_{i})_{1 \leq i \leq r}$ be a second sequence of $r$ elements of $A$, and set $\mathbf{fg} = (f_{i}
g_{i})_{1 \leq i \leq r}$. One can define a canonical homomorphism of complexes

$$ \phi_{\mathbf{g}} : K_{\bullet}(\mathbf{fg}) \to K_{\bullet}(\mathbf{f}) (1.1.5.1) $$

as the canonical extension to the exterior algebra $\Lambda(A^{r})$ of the $A$-linear map $(x_{1}, \cdots, x_{r})
\mapsto (g_{1} x_{1}, \cdots, g_{r} x_{r})$ from $A^{r}$ to itself. To see that this is a homomorphism of complexes,
note in general that if $u : E \to F$ is an $A$-linear map, $\mathbf{x} \in F^{\vee}$, and $\mathbf{y} =
{}^{t}u(\mathbf{x}) \in E^{\vee}$, then

```text
  (Λ u) ∘ i_𝐲 = i_𝐱 ∘ (Λ u);                                                 (1.1.5.2)
```

indeed, both sides are antiderivations of $\Lambda F$, and it suffices to check that they coincide on $F$, which follows
immediately from the definitions.

When one identifies $K_{\bullet}(\mathbf{f})$ with the tensor product of the $K_{\bullet}(f_{i})$ (1.1.1),
$\phi_{\mathbf{g}}$ is the tensor product of the $\phi_{g_{i}}$, where $\phi_{g_{i}}$ reduces to the identity in degree
`0` and to multiplication by $g_{i}$ in degree `1`.

**1.1.6.**

<!-- label: III.1.1.6 -->

In particular, for every pair of integers $m$, $n$ with $0 \leq n \leq m$, we have homomorphisms of complexes

$$ \phi_{\mathbf{f}^{m-n}} : K_{\bullet}(\mathbf{f}^{m}) \to K_{\bullet}(\mathbf{f}^{n}) (1.1.6.1) $$

and hence homomorphisms

```text
  φ_{𝐟^{m−n}} : K^•(𝐟^n, M) → K^•(𝐟^m, M),                                   (1.1.6.2)
  φ_{𝐟^{m−n}} : H^•(𝐟^n, M) → H^•(𝐟^m, M).                                   (1.1.6.3)
```

<!-- original page 85 -->

The latter satisfy the obvious transitivity relation $\phi_{\mathbf{f}^{m-p}} = \phi_{\mathbf{f}^{m-n}} \circ
\phi_{\mathbf{f}^{n-p}}$ for $p \leq n \leq m$; they thus define two inductive systems of $A$-modules. We set

```text
  C^•((𝐟), M) = lim_→ K^•(𝐟^n, M),                                           (1.1.6.4)
                  n
  H^•((𝐟), M) = H^•(C^•((𝐟), M)) = lim_→ H^•(𝐟^n, M),                        (1.1.6.5)
                                     n
```

the last equality coming from the fact that the inductive limit commutes with the functor $H^{\bullet}$ `(G, I, 2.1)`.
We shall see later (1.4.3) that $H^{\bullet}((\mathbf{f}), M)$ depends only on the *ideal* $(\mathbf{f})$ of $A$ (in
fact only on the $(\mathbf{f})$-preadic topology on $A$), which justifies the notations.

It is clear that $M \mapsto C^{\bullet}((\mathbf{f}), M)$ is an exact $A$-linear functor and $M \mapsto
H^{\bullet}((\mathbf{f}), M)$ a cohomological functor.

**1.1.7.**

<!-- label: III.1.1.7 -->

Let $\mathbf{f} = (f_{i}) \in A^{r}$ and $\mathbf{g} = (g_{i}) \in A^{r}$; write $e_{\mathbf{g}}$ for the left
multiplication by the vector $\mathbf{g} \in A^{r}$ on the exterior algebra $\Lambda(A^{r})$. We have the *homotopy
formula*

```text
  i_𝐟 e_𝐠 + e_𝐠 i_𝐟 = ⟨𝐠, 𝐟⟩ · 1                                            (1.1.7.1)
```

on the $A$-module $\Lambda(A^{r})$ (where `1` denotes the identity automorphism of $\Lambda(A^{r})$); this relation
says, equivalently, that *in the complex $K_{\bullet}(\mathbf{f})$*

```text
  d e_𝐠 + e_𝐠 d = ⟨𝐠, 𝐟⟩ · 1.                                                (1.1.7.2)
```

If the ideal $(\mathbf{f})$ is equal to $A$, then there exists $\mathbf{g} \in A^{r}$ such that $\langle \mathbf{g},
\mathbf{f}\rangle = \Sigma^{r}_{i=1} g_{i} f_{i} = 1$. Therefore `(G, I, 2.4)`:

**Proposition (1.1.8).**

<!-- label: III.1.1.8 -->

*Suppose the ideal $(\mathbf{f})$ generated by the $f_{i}$ is equal to $A$. Then the complex $K_{\bullet}(\mathbf{f})$
is homotopically trivial, and so are the complexes $K_{\bullet}(\mathbf{f}, M)$ and $K^{\bullet}(\mathbf{f}, M)$ for
every $A$-module $M$.*

**Corollary (1.1.9).**

<!-- label: III.1.1.9 -->

*If $(\mathbf{f}) = A$, then $H^{\bullet}(\mathbf{f}, M) = 0$ and $H^{\bullet}((\mathbf{f}), M) = 0$ for every
$A$-module $M$.*

**Proof.** We then have $(\mathbf{f}^{n}) = A$ for every $n$.

**Remark (1.1.10).**

<!-- label: III.1.1.10 -->

With the notation above, let $X = \operatorname{Spec}(A)$, and let $Y$ be the closed subscheme of $X$ defined by the
ideal $(\mathbf{f})$. We shall prove in §9 that $H^{\bullet}((\mathbf{f}), M)$ is isomorphic to the cohomology
$H^{\bullet}_{Y}(X, \tilde{M})$ of $X$ with support in the filter of closed subsets of $Y$ `(T, 3.2)`. We shall also
show that Proposition (1.2.3), applied to $X$ and to $\mathcal{F} = \tilde{M}$, is a particular case of an exact
cohomology sequence

```text
  ⋯ → H_Y^p(X, ℱ) → H^p(X, ℱ) → H^p(X − Y, ℱ) → H_Y^{p+1}(X, ℱ) → ⋯.
```

## 1.2. Čech cohomology of an open cover

**1.2.1. Notations.**

<!-- label: III.1.2.1 -->

Throughout this article we shall write:

- $X$ for a prescheme;
- $\mathcal{F}$ for a quasi-coherent $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-module;

<!-- original page 86 -->

- $A = \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_{X})$, $M = \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F})$;
- $\mathbf{f} = (f_{i})_{1 \leq i \leq r}$ for a finite system of elements of $A$;
- $U_{i} = X_{f_{i}}$ for the open set $(0_{I}, 5.5.2)$ of $x \in X$ with $f_{i}(x) \neq 0$;
- $U = \bigcup U_{i}$;
- $\mathfrak{U}$ for the open cover $(U_{i})_{1 \leq i \leq r}$ of $U$.

**1.2.2.**

<!-- label: III.1.2.2 -->

Suppose $X$ is either a prescheme whose underlying space is Noetherian, or a scheme whose underlying space is
quasi-compact. Then `(I, 9.3.3)` gives $\Gamma(U_{i}, \mathcal{F}) = M_{f_{i}}$. Set

```text
  U_{i_0, …, i_p} = ⋂_{k=0}^p U_{i_k} = X_{f_{i_0} ⋯ f_{i_p}}                (0_I, 5.5.3);
```

we then likewise have

```text
  Γ(U_{i_0, …, i_p}, ℱ) = M_{f_{i_0} ⋯ f_{i_p}}.                             (1.2.2.1)
```

But $(0_{I}, 1.6.1)$ identifies $M_{f_{i_{0}} \cdots f_{i_{p}}}$ with the inductive limit
`lim_→_n M_{i_0, …, i_p}^{(n)}`, where $M^{(n)}_{i_{0}, \cdots, i_{p}} = M$ and the transition map $\phi_{n,m} :
M^{(m)}_{i_{0}, \cdots, i_{p}} \to M^{(n)}_{i_{0}, \cdots, i_{p}}$ is multiplication by $(f_{i_{0}} \cdots
f_{i_{p}})^{n-m}$ for $m \leq n$. Denote by $C^{p}_{n}(M)$ the set of alternating maps from $[1, r]^{p+1}$ to $M$ (for
every $n$); these $A$-modules likewise form an inductive system under the $\phi_{n,m}$. If $C^{p}(\mathfrak{U},
\mathcal{F})$ is the group of alternating Čech $p$-cochains of $\mathcal{F}$ relative to the cover $\mathfrak{U}$
`(G, II, 5.1)`, the foregoing gives

```text
  C^p(𝔘, ℱ) = lim_→ C^p_n(M).                                                 (1.2.2.2)
                n
```

With the notation of (1.1.2), $C^{p}_{n}(M)$ is identified with $K^{p+1}(\mathbf{f}^{n}, M)$, and the map $\phi_{n,m}$
with the map $\phi_{\mathbf{f}^{n-m}}$ defined in (1.1.6). Thus, for every $p \geq 0$, there is a canonical
functorial-in-$\mathcal{F}$ isomorphism

```text
  C^p(𝔘, ℱ) ≅ C^p((𝐟), M).                                                   (1.2.2.3)
```

Moreover, the formula `(1.1.2.3)` and the definition of the cohomology of a cover `(G, II, 5.1)` show that the
isomorphisms `(1.2.2.3)` are compatible with the coboundary operators.

**Proposition (1.2.3).**

<!-- label: III.1.2.3 -->

*If $X$ is a prescheme whose underlying space is Noetherian, or a scheme whose underlying space is quasi-compact, then
there is a canonical functorial-in-$\mathcal{F}$ isomorphism*

```text
  H^p(𝔘, ℱ) ≅ H^{p+1}((𝐟), M)              for every p ≥ 1.                  (1.2.3.1)
```

*In addition, there is a functorial-in-$\mathcal{F}$ exact sequence*

```text
  0 → H^0((𝐟), M) → M → H^0(𝔘, ℱ) → H^1((𝐟), M) → 0.                         (1.2.3.2)
```

<!-- original page 87 -->

**Proof.** The relations `(1.2.3.1)` are an immediate consequence of (1.2.2). On the other hand, $C^{0}(\mathfrak{U},
\mathcal{F}) = C^{1}((\mathbf{f}), M)$, so $H^{0}(\mathfrak{U}, \mathcal{F})$ identifies with the subgroup of
`1`-cocycles of $C^{\bullet}((\mathbf{f}), M)$; since $M = C^{0}((\mathbf{f}), M)$, the exact sequence `(1.2.3.2)` is
the one that comes from the definition of the cohomology groups $H^{0}((\mathbf{f}), M)$ and $H^{1}((\mathbf{f}), M)$.

**Corollary (1.2.4).**

<!-- label: III.1.2.4 -->

*Suppose the $X_{f_{i}}$ are quasi-compact and that there exist $g_{i} \in \Gamma(U, \mathcal{F})$ such that $\Sigma_{i}
g_{i} (f_{i} \mid U) = 1_{U}$. Then, for every quasi-coherent $(\mathcal{O}_{X} \mid U)$-module $\mathcal{F}$, we have
$H^{p}(\mathfrak{U}, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for $p > 0$; if in addition $U = X$, the canonical homomorphism `(1.2.3.2)` $M
\to H^{0}(\mathfrak{U}, \mathcal{F})$ is bijective.*

**Proof.** Since by hypothesis the $U_{i} = X_{f_{i}}$ are quasi-compact, so is $U$, so we may reduce to the case $U =
X$; the hypothesis then implies $H^{p}((\mathbf{f}), M) = 0$ for every $p \geq 0$ (1.1.9). The corollary follows from
`(1.2.3.1)` and `(1.2.3.2)`.

Note that since $H^{0}(\mathfrak{U}, \mathcal{F}) = H^{0}(U, \mathcal{F})$ `(G, II, 5.2.2)`, the result `(I, 1.3.7)` is
recovered here as a special case.

**Remark (1.2.5).**

<!-- label: III.1.2.5 -->

Suppose $X$ is an affine scheme; then the $U_{i} = X_{f_{i}} = D(f_{i})$ are affine open sets, as are the $U_{i_{0},
\cdots, i_{p}}$ (though $U$ is not necessarily affine). In this case the functors $\Gamma(X, \cdot)$ and
$\Gamma(U_{i_{0}, \cdots, i_{p}}, \cdot)$ are exact in $\mathcal{F}$ `(I, 1.3.11)`. Given an exact sequence $0 \to
\mathcal{F}' \to \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}'' \to 0$ of quasi-coherent $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-modules, the sequence of
complexes

```text
  0 → C^•(𝔘, ℱ') → C^•(𝔘, ℱ) → C^•(𝔘, ℱ'') → 0
```

is exact, and yields a long exact sequence in cohomology

```text
  ⋯ → H^p(𝔘, ℱ') → H^p(𝔘, ℱ) → H^p(𝔘, ℱ'') → H^{p+1}(𝔘, ℱ') → ⋯.
```

On the other hand, setting $M' = \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}')$, $M'' = \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}'')$, the sequence $0 \to M' \to
M \to M'' \to 0$ is exact; as $C^{\bullet}((\mathbf{f}), M)$ is an exact functor of $M$, we also obtain the exact
sequence in cohomology

```text
  ⋯ → H^p((𝐟), M') → H^p((𝐟), M) → H^p((𝐟), M'') → H^{p+1}((𝐟), M') → ⋯.
```

Since the diagram

```text
  0 → C^•(𝔘, ℱ')     → C^•(𝔘, ℱ)     → C^•(𝔘, ℱ'')     → 0
        ↓                 ↓                 ↓
  0 → C^•((𝐟), M')   → C^•((𝐟), M)   → C^•((𝐟), M'')   →
```

<!-- original page 88 -->

is commutative, the diagrams

```text
              H^p(𝔘, ℱ'')   →   H^{p+1}(𝔘, ℱ')
                  ↓                  ↓                                       (1.2.5.1)
              H^{p+1}((𝐟), M'') → H^{p+1}((𝐟), M')
```

are commutative for every $p$ `(G, I, 2.1.1)`.

## 1.3. Cohomology of an affine scheme

**Theorem (1.3.1).**

<!-- label: III.1.3.1 -->

*Let $X$ be an affine scheme. For every quasi-coherent $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-module $\mathcal{F}$, we have $H^{p}(X,
\mathcal{F}) = 0$ for every $p > 0$.*

**Proof.** Let $\mathfrak{U}$ be a finite cover of $X$ by affine open sets $X_{f_{i}} = D(f_{i})$ ($1 \leq i \leq r$);
the ideal of $A = \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_{X})$ generated by the $f_{i}$ is then $A$. By `(1.2.4)` we have
$\check{H}^{p}(\mathfrak{U}, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for $p > 0$. Since there are arbitrarily fine finite affine open covers
of $X$ `(I, 1.1.10)`, the definition of Čech cohomology `(G, II, 5.8)` shows that $\check{H}^{p}(X, \mathcal{F}) = 0$
for $p > 0$. The same argument applies to every $X_{f}$ for $f \in A$ `(I, 1.3.6)`, so
$\check{H}^{p}(X_{f}, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for $p > 0$. As $X_{f} \cap X_{g} = X_{fg}$, we conclude that
$H^{p}(X, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for every $p > 0$ by virtue of `(G, II, 5.9.2)`.

**Corollary (1.3.2).**

<!-- label: III.1.3.2 -->

*Let $Y$ be a prescheme and $f : X \to Y$ an affine morphism `(II, 1.6.1)`. For every quasi-coherent
$\mathcal{O}_{X}$-module $\mathcal{F}$, we have $R^{q} f_{*}(\mathcal{F}) = 0$ for $q > 0$.*

**Proof.** By definition, $R^{q} f_{*}(\mathcal{F})$ is the $\mathcal{O}_{Y}$-module associated to the presheaf $U
\mapsto H^{q}(f^{-1}(U), \mathcal{F})$, where $U$ ranges over the open sets of $Y$. The affine open sets form a base of
$Y$, and for such a $U$ the preimage $f^{-1}(U)$ is affine `(II, 1.3.2)`, hence $H^{q}(f^{-1}(U), \mathcal{F}) = 0$ by
`(1.3.1)`, proving the corollary.

**Corollary (1.3.3).**

<!-- label: III.1.3.3 -->

*Let $Y$ be a prescheme and $f : X \to Y$ an affine morphism. For every quasi-coherent $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-module
$\mathcal{F}$, the canonical homomorphism*

```text
  H^p(Y, f_*(ℱ)) → H^p(X, ℱ)                                   (0, 12.1.3.1)
```

*is bijective for every $p$.*

**Proof.** By `(0, 12.1.7)` it suffices to show that the edge homomorphisms $''E^{p, 0}_{2} = H^{p}(Y,
f_{*}(\mathcal{F})) \to H^{p}(X, \mathcal{F})$ of the second spectral sequence of the composite functor $\Gamma f_{*}$
are bijective. The `E_2` term of this sequence is $''E^{p, q}_{2} = H^{p}(Y, R^{q} f_{*}(\mathcal{F}))$
`(G, II, 4.17.1)`, and (1.3.2) gives $''E^{p, q}_{2} = 0$ for $q > 0$; the spectral sequence therefore degenerates. The
assertion follows from `(0, 11.1.6)`.

**Corollary (1.3.4).**

<!-- label: III.1.3.4 -->

*Let $f : X \to Y$ be an affine morphism and $g : Y \to Z$ a morphism.*

<!-- original page 89 -->

*For every quasi-coherent $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-module $\mathcal{F}$, the canonical homomorphism $R^{p}
g_{*}(f_{*}(\mathcal{F})) \to R^{p}(g \circ f)_{*}(\mathcal{F})$ `(0, 12.2.5.1)` is bijective for every $p$.*

**Proof.** Note that, by (1.3.3), for every affine open $W$ of $Z$, the canonical homomorphism $H^{p}(g^{-1}(W),
f_{*}(\mathcal{F})) \to H^{p}(f^{-1}(g^{-1}(W)), \mathcal{F})$ is bijective; this shows that the presheaf homomorphism
defining the canonical homomorphism $R^{p} g_{*}(f_{*}(\mathcal{F})) \to R^{p}(g \circ f)_{*}(\mathcal{F})$ is bijective
`(0, 12.2.5)`.

## 1.4. Application to the cohomology of arbitrary preschemes

**Proposition (1.4.1).**

<!-- label: III.1.4.1 -->

*Let $X$ be a scheme and $\mathfrak{U} = (U_{i})$ a cover of $X$ by affine open sets. For every quasi-coherent
$\mathcal{O}_{X}$-module $\mathcal{F}$, the cohomology modules $H^{\bullet}(X, \mathcal{F})$ and
$H^{\bullet}(\mathfrak{U}, \mathcal{F})$ (over $\Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_{X})$) are canonically isomorphic.*

**Proof.** Since $X$ is a scheme, every finite intersection $V$ of open sets of the cover $\mathfrak{U}$ is affine
`(I, 5.5.6)`, so $H^{q}(V, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for $q \geq 1$ by `(1.3.1)`. The proposition follows from Leray's theorem
`(G, II, 5.4.1)`.

**Remark (1.4.2).**

<!-- label: III.1.4.2 -->

The conclusion of (1.4.1) remains valid whenever the finite intersections of the $U_{i}$ are affine, even if $X$ is not
assumed to be a scheme.

**Corollary (1.4.3).**

<!-- label: III.1.4.3 -->

*Let $X$ be a scheme whose underlying space is quasi-compact, set $A = \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_{X})$, and let $\mathbf{f}

*and a functorial-in-$\mathcal{F}$ exact sequence*

```text

```

**Proof.** This follows from (1.4.1) and (1.2.3).

**Proposition (1.4.5).**

<!-- label: III.1.4.5 -->

*Let $X$ be a quasi-compact scheme, $\mathcal{L}$ an invertible $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-module, and consider the graded ring
$A_{\bullet} = \Gamma_{*}(\mathcal{L})$ `(II, 5.4.6)`. Then $H^{\bullet}(X, \mathcal{F}) = \oplus_{n} H^{\bullet}(X,
\mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{L}^{\otimes n})$ is a graded $A_{\bullet}$-module, and for every $f \in A_{d}$ there is a
canonical isomorphism*

```text
  H^•(X_f, ℱ) ≅ (H^•(X, ℱ))_{(f)}                                            (1.4.5.1)
```

*of $(A_{\bullet})_{(f)}$-modules.*

**Proof.** Since $X$ is a quasi-compact scheme, the cohomology of all the $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-modules $\mathcal{F} \otimes
\mathcal{L}^{\otimes n}$ can be computed with one and the same finite cover $\mathfrak{U} = (U_{i})$ by affine open sets
such that the restriction $\mathcal{L} \mid U_{i}$ is isomorphic to $\mathcal{O}_{X} \mid U_{i}$ for each $i$ (1.4.1).

$H^{\bullet}(X_{f}, \mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{L}^{\otimes n})$ can also be computed with the cover $\mathfrak{U} \mid
X_{f} = (U_{i} \cap X_{f})$ (1.4.1). It is immediate that for every $f \in A_{d}$ multiplication by $f$ defines a
homomorphism $C^{\bullet}(\mathfrak{U}, \mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{L}^{\otimes n}) \to C^{\bullet}(\mathfrak{U},
\mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{L}^{\otimes(n+d)})$, hence a homomorphism $H^{\bullet}(\mathfrak{U}, \mathcal{F} \otimes
\mathcal{L}^{\otimes n}) \to H^{\bullet}(\mathfrak{U}, \mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{L}^{\otimes(n+d)})$, which
establishes the first assertion. On the other hand, for $f \in A_{d}$, `(I, 9.3.2)` gives an isomorphism of complexes of
$(A_{\bullet})_{(f)}$-modules

```text

```

taking `(I, 1.3.9, (ii))` into account. Passing to the cohomology of both complexes, one obtains the isomorphism
`(1.4.5.1)`, using that the functor $M \mapsto M_{(f)}$ is exact on the category of graded $A_{\bullet}$-modules.

**Corollary (1.4.7).**

<!-- label: III.1.4.7 -->

*Let $X$ be a quasi-compact scheme and $f \in \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_{X})$.*

*(i) Suppose the open set $X_{f}$ is affine. Then, for every quasi-coherent $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-module $\mathcal{F}$,

*(ii) Conversely, suppose $X_{f}$ is quasi-compact and that for every quasi-coherent sheaf of ideals $\mathcal{J}$ of
$\mathcal{O}_{X}$ and every $\xi \in H^{1}(X, \mathcal{J})$ there exists $n > 0$ such that $f^{n} \xi = 0$. Then $X_{f}$

**Proof.** (i) If $X_{f}$ is affine, then $H^{i}(X_{f}, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for every $i > 0$ (1.3.1), so the assertion
follows from (1.4.6).

(ii) By Serre's criterion `(II, 5.2.1)`, it suffices to show that for every quasi-coherent sheaf of ideals
$\mathcal{J}'$ of $\mathcal{O}_{X_{f}}$ we have $H^{1}(X_{f}, \mathcal{J}') = 0$. Since $X_{f}$ is a quasi-compact open

\mathcal{J}))_{f}$, and the hypothesis forces the right side to be zero, whence the conclusion.

**Lemma (1.4.9).**

<!-- label: III.1.4.9 -->

*Let $X$ be a quasi-compact scheme, $\mathfrak{U} = (U_{i})_{1 \leq i \leq n}$ a finite cover of $X$ by affine open
sets, and $\mathcal{F}$ a quasi-coherent $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-module. The complex of sheaves
$\mathcal{C}^{\bullet}(\mathfrak{U}, \mathcal{F})$ defined by the cover $\mathfrak{U}$ `(G, II, 5.2)` is then a
quasi-coherent $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-module.*

**Proof.** By the definitions `(G, II, 5.2)`, $\mathcal{C}^{\bullet}(\mathfrak{U}, \mathcal{F})$ is a direct sum of
sheaves obtained as direct images of the $\mathcal{F} \mid U_{i_{0}, \cdots, i_{p}}$ under the canonical injections
$U_{i_{0}, \cdots, i_{p}} \to X$. The hypothesis that $X$ is a scheme implies that these injections are affine morphisms

**Proposition (1.4.10).**

<!-- label: III.1.4.10 -->

*Let $u : X \to Y$ be a separated and quasi-compact morphism. For every quasi-coherent $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-module
$\mathcal{F}$, the $R^{q} u_{*}(\mathcal{F})$ are quasi-coherent $\mathcal{O}_{Y}$-modules.*

**Proof.** The question being local on $Y$, we may suppose $Y$ affine. Then $X$ is a finite union of affine open sets
$U_{i}$ ($1 \leq i \leq n$); let $\mathfrak{U} = (U_{i})$. Since $Y$ is a scheme, `(I, 5.5.10)` shows that for every
affine open $V \subseteq Y$ the canonical injection $u^{-1}(V) \to X$ is an affine morphism; therefore (by (1.4.1) and
`(G, II, 5.2)`) there is a canonical isomorphism

```text
  H^•(u^{-1}(V), ℱ) ≅ H^•(Γ(V, 𝒢)),                                          (1.4.10.1)
```

where $\mathcal{G} = u_{*}(\mathcal{C}^{\bullet}(\mathfrak{U}, \mathcal{F}))$. By (1.4.9) and `(I, 9.2.2)`,
$\mathcal{G}$ is a quasi-coherent $\mathcal{O}_{Y}$-module; moreover, since $\mathcal{C}^{\bullet}(\mathfrak{U},
\mathcal{F})$ is a complex of sheaves, so is $\mathcal{G}$. The definition of the cohomology
$\mathcal{H}^{\bullet}(\mathcal{G})$ `(G, II, 4.1)` then shows that the latter consists of quasi-coherent
$\mathcal{O}_{Y}$-modules `(I, 4.1.1)`. Since, for $V$ affine in $Y$, the functor $\Gamma(V, \cdot)$ is exact in
$\mathcal{G}$ on the category of quasi-coherent $\mathcal{O}_{Y}$-modules, `(G, II, 4.1)` gives

```text
  H^•(Γ(V, 𝒢)) = Γ(V, 𝓗^•(𝒢)).                                               (1.4.10.2)
```

Finally, the definition of the canonical homomorphism $H^{\bullet}(\mathfrak{U}, \mathcal{F}) \to H^{\bullet}(X,
\mathcal{F})$ given in `(G, II, 5.2)` shows that, if $V' \subseteq V$ is a second affine open set in $Y$, the diagram

```text
  H^•(u^{-1}(V), ℱ)   ≅   H^•(Γ(V, 𝒢))
        ↓                       ↓
  H^•(u^{-1}(V'), ℱ)  ≅   H^•(Γ(V', 𝒢))
```

is commutative. We conclude from the foregoing that the isomorphisms `(1.4.10.1)` define an isomorphism of
$\mathcal{O}_{Y}$-modules

$$ R^{\bullet }u_{*}(\mathcal{F}) \cong \mathcal{H}^{\bullet}(\mathcal{G}), (1.4.10.3) $$

<!-- original page 92 -->

and hence $R^{q} u_{*}(\mathcal{F})$ is quasi-coherent. The relations `(1.4.10.3)`, `(1.4.10.2)`, `(1.4.10.1)` also
yield:

*is an isomorphism for every $q \geq 0$.*

**Corollary (1.4.12).**

<!-- label: III.1.4.12 -->

*Under the hypotheses of (1.4.10), suppose in addition that $Y$ is quasi-compact. Then there exists an integer $r > 0$
such that for every quasi-coherent $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-module $\mathcal{F}$ and every integer $q > r$ we have $R^{q}
u_{*}(\mathcal{F}) = 0$. If $Y$ is affine, one may take for $r$ any integer such that $X$ admits an affine open cover of

**Proof.** Since $Y$ can be covered by finitely many affine open sets, it suffices, by (1.4.11), to prove the second

**Proposition (1.4.14).**

<!-- label: III.1.4.14 -->

*Let $f : X \to Y$ be a separated quasi-compact morphism, and $g : Y \to Z$ an affine morphism. For every quasi-coherent
$\mathcal{O}_{X}$-module $\mathcal{F}$, the canonical homomorphism*

```text
  R^p(g ∘ f)_*(ℱ) → g_*(R^p f_*(ℱ))                                  (0, 12.2.5.2)
```

*is bijective for every $p$.*

**Proposition (1.4.15).**

<!-- label: III.1.4.15 -->

*Let $u : X \to Y$ be a separated morphism of finite type, $v : Y' \to Y$ a flat morphism of preschemes $(0_{I},
6.7.1)$. Set $u' = u_{(Y')}$, so that one has the commutative diagram*

```text
       X  ←  X' = X ×_Y Y'
       ↓        ↓                                                            (1.4.15.1)
       Y  ←  Y'
```

*Then, for every quasi-coherent $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-module $\mathcal{F}$ and every $q \geq 0$, $R^{q}
u'_{*}(\mathcal{F}')$, where $\mathcal{F}' = v'^{*}(\mathcal{F}) = \mathcal{F} \otimes_{\mathcal{O}_{X}}
\mathcal{O}_{X'}$, is canonically isomorphic to $R^{q} u_{*}(\mathcal{F}) \otimes_{\mathcal{O}_{Y}} \mathcal{O}_{Y'} =
v^{*}(R^{q} u_{*}(\mathcal{F}))$.*

<!-- original page 93 -->

**Proof.** The canonical homomorphism $\rho : \mathcal{F} \to v'_{*}(v'^{*}(\mathcal{F}))$ $(0_{I}, 4.4.3.2)$ defines by
functoriality a homomorphism

```text
  R^q u_*(ℱ) → R^q u_*(v'_*(ℱ')).                                            (1.4.15.2)
```

On the other hand, setting $w = u \circ v' = v \circ u'$, one has the canonical homomorphisms `(0, 12.2.5.1)` and
`(0, 12.2.5.2)`

```text
  R^q u_*(v'_*(ℱ')) → R^q w_*(ℱ') → v_*(R^q u'_*(ℱ')).                       (1.4.15.3)
```

Composing `(1.4.15.3)` and `(1.4.15.2)` gives a homomorphism

```text
  ψ : R^q u_*(ℱ) → v_*(R^q u'_*(ℱ'))
```

and hence the canonical homomorphism (whose definition uses no hypothesis on $v$)

```text
  Φ : v^*(R^q u_*(ℱ)) → R^q u'_*(ℱ'),                                        (1.4.15.4)
```

which one must show is an isomorphism when $v$ is flat. The question is local on $Y$ and $Y'$, so we may suppose $Y =
\operatorname{Spec}(A)$ and $Y' = \operatorname{Spec}(B)$. We use the following lemma.

**Lemma (1.4.15.5).**

<!-- label: III.1.4.15.5 -->

*Let $\phi : A \to B$ be a ring homomorphism, $Y = \operatorname{Spec}(A)$, $X = \operatorname{Spec}(B)$, $f : X \to Y$
the morphism corresponding to $\phi$, and $M$ a $B$-module. The $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-module $\tilde{M}$ is $f$-flat
$(0_{I}, 6.7.1)$ if and only if $M$ is a flat $A$-module. In particular, the morphism $f$ is flat if and only if $B$ is
a flat $A$-module.*

**Proof.** This follows from the definition $(0_{I}, 6.7.1)$ and from $(0_{I}, 6.3.3)$, taking `(I, 1.3.4)` into
account.

That being so, by `(1.4.11.1)` and the definitions of the homomorphisms `(1.4.15.3)` (cf. `0, 12.2.5`), $\Phi$
corresponds to the composite morphism

```text
  H^q(X, ℱ) →^ρ H^q(X, v'_*(ℱ')) →^σ H^q(X', v'^*(v'_*(ℱ'))) →^θ H^q(X', ℱ'),
```

where $\rho$ and $\sigma$ are the homomorphisms corresponding in cohomology to the canonical morphisms $\rho$ and
$\sigma : v'^{*}(v'_{*}(\mathcal{F}')) \to \mathcal{F}'$, and $\theta$ is the $\phi$-morphism `(0, 12.1.3.1)` relative
to the $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-module $v'_{*}(v'^{*}(\mathcal{F}))$. By the functoriality of $\theta$, one has the commutative
diagram

```text
   H^q(X, ℱ)              →^{ρ_φ}              H^q(X, v'_*(v'^*(ℱ)))
        ↓                                          ↓
   H^q(X', v'^*(ℱ))                  H^q(X', v'^*(v'_*(v'^*(ℱ))))
```

<!-- original page 94 -->

and since by definition $(0_{I}, 4.4.3)$ $v'^{*}(\rho)$ is the inverse of $\sigma$, the composite morphism considered
above is none other than $\theta_{\phi}$; thus $\Phi$ is the $B$-homomorphism associated to $H^{q}(X, \mathcal{F})
\otimes_{A} B \to H^{q}(X', \mathcal{F}')$. Since $u$ is of finite type, $X$ is a finite union of affine open sets
$U_{i}$ ($1 \leq i \leq r$); let $\mathfrak{U} = (U_{i})$. Moreover, $v$ is an affine morphism, hence so is $v'$
`(II, 1.6.2, (iii))`, and the $U'_{i} = v'^{-1}(U_{i})$ form a finite affine open cover $\mathfrak{U}'$ of $X'$. We know
`(0, 12.1.4.2)` that the diagram

```text
   H^•(𝔘, ℱ)    →    H^•(𝔘', ℱ')
        ↓                  ↓
   H^•(X, ℱ)    →    H^•(X', ℱ')
```

is commutative, and the vertical arrows are isomorphisms, since $X$ and $X'$ are schemes (1.4.1). It thus suffices to
show that the canonical $\phi$-morphism $\theta : H^{\bullet}(\mathfrak{U}, \mathcal{F}) \to H^{\bullet}(\mathfrak{U}',
\mathcal{F}')$ is such that the associated $B$-homomorphism $H^{\bullet}(\mathfrak{U}, \mathcal{F}) \otimes_{A} B \to
H^{\bullet}(\mathfrak{U}', \mathcal{F}')$ is an isomorphism. For every sequence $s = (i_{h})_{0 \leq h \leq p}$ of $p +
1$ indices in `[1, r]`, set $U_{s} = \bigcap_{h} U_{i_{h}}$, $U'_{s} = \bigcap_{h} U'_{i_{h}} = v'^{-1}(U_{s})$, $M_{s}
= \Gamma(U_{s}, \mathcal{F})$, $M'_{s} = \Gamma(U'_{s}, \mathcal{F}')$. The canonical map $M_{s} \otimes_{A} B \to
M'_{s}$ is an isomorphism `(I, 1.6.5)`, so the canonical map $C^{\bullet}(\mathfrak{U}, \mathcal{F}) \otimes_{A} B \to
C^{\bullet}(\mathfrak{U}', \mathcal{F}')$ is an isomorphism, under which $d \otimes 1$ is identified with the coboundary
operator $C^{\bullet}(\mathfrak{U}', \mathcal{F}') \to C^{\bullet+1}(\mathfrak{U}', \mathcal{F}')$. Since $B$ is a flat
$A$-module, the definition of the cohomology modules immediately gives that the canonical map $H^{\bullet}(\mathfrak{U},
\mathcal{F}) \otimes_{A} B \to H^{\bullet}(\mathfrak{U}', \mathcal{F}')$ is an isomorphism $(0_{I}, 6.1.1)$. This result
will be generalized later (§6).

**Corollary (1.4.16).**

<!-- label: III.1.4.16 -->

*Let $A$ be a ring, $X$ an $A$-scheme of finite type, $B$ an $A$-algebra faithfully flat over $A$. Then $X$ is affine if
and only if $X \otimes_{A} B$ is affine.*

**Proof.** The condition is evidently necessary `(I, 3.2.2)`; we show it is sufficient. Since $X$ is separated over $A$
and the morphism $\operatorname{Spec}(B) \to \operatorname{Spec}(A)$ is flat, (1.4.15) gives

```text
  H^i(X ⊗_A B, ℱ ⊗_A B) = H^i(X, ℱ) ⊗_A B                                    (1.4.16.1)
```

for every $i \geq 0$ and every quasi-coherent $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-module $\mathcal{F}$. If $X \otimes_{A} B$ is affine,
the left side of `(1.4.16.1)` is zero for $i = 1$, hence so is $H^{1}(X, \mathcal{F})$, since $B$ is faithfully flat
over $A$. As $X$ is a quasi-compact scheme, we conclude by Serre's criterion `(II, 5.2.1)`.

**Proposition (1.4.17).**

<!-- label: III.1.4.17 -->

*Let $X$ be a prescheme and $0 \to \mathcal{F}' \to \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}'' \to 0$ an exact sequence of
$\mathcal{O}_{X}$-modules. If $\mathcal{F}'$ and $\mathcal{F}''$ are quasi-coherent, so is $\mathcal{F}$.*

<!-- original page 95 -->

**Proof.** The question is local on $X$, so we may suppose $X = \operatorname{Spec}(A)$ affine; it then suffices to show
that $\mathcal{F}$ satisfies the conditions $(d_{1})$ and $(d_{2})$ of `(I, 1.4.1)` (with $V = X$). The verification of
$(d_{1})$ is immediate: if $t \in \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F})$ has zero restriction to $D(f)$, then so does its image $v(t)
\in \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}'')$; thus there exists $m > 0$ such that $f^{m} v(t) = v(f^{m} t) = 0$ `(I, 1.4.1)`. Since
$\mathcal{F}'$ is exact at left, $f^{m} t = u(s)$ for some $s \in \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}')$; since $u$ is injective, the
restriction of $s$ to $D(f)$ is zero, hence by `(I, 1.4.1)` there is $n > 0$ with $f^{n} s = 0$, and finally $f^{m+n} t
= u(f^{n} s) = 0$.

Now verify $(d_{2})$. Let $t' \in \Gamma(D(f), \mathcal{F})$. Since $\mathcal{F}''$ is quasi-coherent, there exists an
integer $m$ such that $f^{m} v(t') = v(f^{m} t')$ extends to a section $t'' \in \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}'')$ `(I, 1.4.1)`.
By (1.3.1) (or `(I, 5.1.9.2)`) applied to the quasi-coherent $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-module $\mathcal{F}''$, the sequence
$\Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}) \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}'') \to 0$ is exact, so there exists $\tau \in \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F})$
with $v(\tau) = t''$. We see that $v(f^{m} t' - \tau \mid D(f)) = 0$; writing `t'''` for the restriction of $\tau$ to
$D(f)$, we have $f^{m} t' - t''' = u(s')$ for some $s' \in \Gamma(D(f), \mathcal{F}')$. Since $\mathcal{F}'$ is
quasi-coherent, there exists $n > 0$ such that $f^{n} s'$ extends to a section $s \in \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}')$; then
$f^{m+n} t' - f^{n} t''' = u(f^{n} s')$, so $f^{m+n} t'$ is the restriction to $D(f)$ of the section $f^{n} \tau +
u(f^{n} s) \in \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F})$, completing the proof.
