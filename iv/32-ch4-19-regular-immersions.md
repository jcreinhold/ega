## §19. Regular and transversally regular immersions

The present section is devoted, on the one hand, to a study of regular immersions `(16.9.2)` `Y → X` of preschemes,
notably in the case where `X` and `Y` are flat over the same prescheme `S`; on the other hand, it gives complements on
`M`-regular sequences and normal flatness, generalizing in particular flatness results established by H. Hironaka in his
theory of resolution of singularities `[35]`.

### 19.1. Properties of regular immersions

**Proposition (19.1.1).**

<!-- label: IV.19.1.1 -->

*Let `X` be a locally Noetherian prescheme, `j : Y → X` an immersion, `y` a regular point of `Y`. For `j` to be a
regular immersion at the point `y`, it is necessary and sufficient that `X` be regular at the point `j(y)`.*

Taking `(16.9.10)` into account, one is reduced to proving the

**Corollary (19.1.2).**

<!-- label: IV.19.1.2 -->

*Let `A` be a Noetherian local ring, `𝔍` an ideal of `A`; suppose that the quotient ring `B = A/𝔍` is regular. For `A`
to be regular, it is necessary and sufficient that the ideal `𝔍` be regular.*

Indeed, if `A` is regular, one knows `(0, 17.1.9)` that `𝔍` is generated by a part of a regular system of parameters of
`A`, hence `𝔍` is regular. Conversely, if `𝔍` is regular, and if `(f_i)_{1 ⩽ i ⩽ n}` is an `A`-regular sequence
generating `𝔍`, the `(f_i)` form a part of a system of parameters of `A` `(0, 16.4.1)`, so one deduces from `(0, 17.1.7)`
that `A` is regular.

**Definition (19.1.3).**

<!-- label: IV.19.1.3 -->

*Let `X` be a prescheme, `Y` a sub-prescheme of `X`, `U` an open set of `X` containing `Y` and such that `Y` is defined
by an Ideal `𝓘` of `𝒪_U`; suppose that `𝓘/𝓘²` is a locally free `(𝒪_U/𝓘)`-Module (which is the case if `Y` is
quasi-regularly immersed in `X`). For every `y ∈ Y`, we call **transversal codimension of `Y` in `X` at the point `y`**,
and denote `codim_y^t(Y, X)`, the rank of the free `(𝒪_{Y, y})`-module `(𝓘/𝓘²)_y`. If `f : Z → X` is an immersion of
image `Y`, we likewise call **transversal codimension of `f` at the point `z ∈ Z`** the transversal codimension in `X`
at the point `f(z)` of the sub-prescheme `Y`.*

**Proposition (19.1.4).**

<!-- label: IV.19.1.4 -->

*Let `X` be a locally Noetherian prescheme, `Y` a sub-prescheme of `X` regularly immersed; then for every `y ∈ Y`, one
has*

```text
  (19.1.4.1)    codim_y^t(Y, X) = codim_y(Y, X).
```

By virtue of `(5.1.3.2)`, `codim_y(Y, X)` is equal to the smallest of the dimensions of the local rings
`A = 𝒪_{X, z}`, where `z` is the generic point of an irreducible component of `Y` containing

<!-- original page 186 -->

`y`; as such a point `z` is contained in every neighbourhood of `y` in `X`, `𝓘_z/𝓘_z²` is a free `A`-module of rank
`n = codim_y^t(Y, X)`; it suffices to see that `dim(A) = n`. Now, since `z` is a maximal point of the sub-prescheme `Y` defined by `𝓘`, one has
`dim(𝒪_{Y, z}) = 0`, so `𝓘_z` is an ideal of definition of the Noetherian local ring `A`; furthermore, the hypothesis
that `𝓘` is quasi-regular entails that `𝓘_z^k/𝓘_z^{k+1}` is a free `(A/𝓘_z)`-module of rank `binom(n+k-1, n-1)`; if `r`
is the length of the Artinian ring `A/𝓘_z`, the length of `𝓘_z^k/𝓘_z^{k+1}` is therefore `r · binom(n+k-1, n-1)`; the
Hilbert-Samuel polynomial of `A` for the `𝓘_z`-preadic filtration is therefore of degree `n`, which proves the
proposition `(0, 16.2.3)`.

By virtue of `(19.1.4)`, we shall henceforth say "codimension" instead of "transversal codimension" when dealing with
a sub-prescheme `Y` regularly immersed in `X` (*even when `X` is not locally Noetherian*).

**Proposition (19.1.5).**

<!-- label: IV.19.1.5 -->

*(i) For an immersion `j : Y → X` to be open, it is necessary and sufficient that it be regular and of codimension `0`
at every point.*

*(ii) Let `f : Y → X` be a regular (resp. quasi-regular) immersion, `g : X' → X` a flat morphism,
`Y' = Y ×_X X'`; then the morphism `f' = f_{(X')} : Y' → X'` is a regular (resp. quasi-regular) immersion, and for every
`y' ∈ Y'`, if `y` is the projection of `y'` in `Y`, the codimension of `f'` at the point `y'` is equal to the codimension
of `f` at the point `y`. Conversely, if `f` is an immersion and `g : X' → X` a faithfully flat and quasi-compact
morphism, then, if `f'` is a quasi-regular immersion, so is `f`.*

*(iii) If `f : Y → X` and `g : Z → Y` are regular immersions, then `f ∘ g : Z → X` is a regular immersion, and for every
`z ∈ Z`, the codimension of `f ∘ g` at the point `z` is the sum of the codimension of `g` at the point `z` and of the
codimension of `f` at the point `g(z)`. Moreover, the sequence of canonical homomorphisms `(16.2.7.1)`*

```text
  (19.1.5.1)    0 → g*(𝒩_{Y/X}) → 𝒩_{Z/X} → 𝒩_{Z/Y} → 0
```

*is exact, and for every `z ∈ Z`, there exists a neighbourhood of `z` in `Z` in which the restrictions of the
homomorphisms of this sequence form a split sequence.*

*(iv) Let `X` be a locally Noetherian prescheme, `f : Y → X` and `g : Z → Y` two immersions, `z` a point of `Z`. The
following conditions are equivalent:*

*a) `g` is a regular immersion at the point `z`, and `f` is a regular immersion at the point `g(z)`.*

*b) `g` and `f ∘ g` are regular immersions at the point `z`.*

*c) `f ∘ g` is a regular immersion at the point `z`, `f` is a regular immersion at the point `g(z)`, and the sequence of
canonical homomorphisms `(19.1.5.1)`, restricted to a sufficiently small open neighbourhood of `z` in `Z`, is exact and
split.*

*(v) Let `X` be a locally Noetherian prescheme, `f : Y → X`, `g : Z → Y` two immersions, `z` a point of `Z`; suppose
that `y = g(z)` is a regular point of `Y` and `x = f(g(z))` a regular point of `X` (which entails, by `(19.1.1)`, that
`f` is a regular immersion at the point `g(z)`); then,*

<!-- original page 187 -->

*for `f ∘ g` to be a regular immersion at the point `z`, it is necessary and sufficient that `g` be a regular immersion
at the point `z`.*

Assertion (i) is another way of expressing `(16.1.10)`. To prove the first assertion of (ii), one may restrict to the
case where `Y` is a closed sub-prescheme of `X` defined by a regular (resp. quasi-regular) Ideal `𝓘`; then `Y'` is the
closed sub-prescheme of `X'` defined by the Ideal `g*(𝓘) · 𝒪_{X'}` `(I, 4.4.5)`, which is here identified with `g*(𝓘)`
since `g` is flat; by replacing `X` by a sufficiently small affine open set, one may suppose that `𝓘` admits a regular
(resp. quasi-regular) sequence of generators `f_i` (sections of `𝒪_X` over `X`). If the sequence `(f_i)` is regular, so
is the sequence `(f_i ⊗ 1)` (which generates `g*(𝓘)`) by virtue of `(0, 15.2.5)`; the analogous result for
quasi-regular sequences follows at once from the flatness of `g` and the criterion `(16.9.4)`. Likewise, if `g` is
faithfully flat and quasi-compact, and if `g*(𝓘)` is quasi-regular, `𝓘` is of finite type by virtue of `(2.5.2)`; by
flatness, one has `g*(𝓘)/g*(𝓘)² = g*(𝓘/𝓘²)`, hence, since `g*(𝓘/𝓘²)` is locally free by hypothesis `(16.9.4)`, so is
`𝓘/𝓘²` `(2.5.2)`. Finally, condition (iii) of `(16.9.4)` relative to `g*(𝓘)` entails the same condition for `𝓘` by
virtue of the faithful flatness of `g` `(2.2.7)`. One therefore concludes by `(16.9.4)` that `𝓘` is quasi-regular.

To prove (iii), one may likewise suppose that `Y` and `Z` are closed sub-preschemes of `X`, defined respectively by
Ideals `𝓘`, `𝒥` of `𝒪_X` with `𝓘 ⊂ 𝒥`; furthermore, one may suppose that there exist sections `f_i` `(1 ⩽ i ⩽ m)` of
`𝓘` over `X` generating `𝓘` and such that, for `1 ⩽ i ⩽ m`, the endomorphism of `𝒪_X/(∑_{j < i} f_j 𝒪_X)` defined by
`f_i` is injective, and sections `f_i` `(m+1 ⩽ i ⩽ n)` of `𝒥/𝓘` over `X` generating `𝒥/𝓘` and such that, for
`m+1 ⩽ i ⩽ n`, the endomorphism of `(𝒪_X/𝓘) / (∑_{m < j < i} f_j (𝒪_X/𝓘))` defined by `f_i` is injective. For every
`z ∈ Z`, let `U` be an open neighbourhood of `z` in `X` such that there exists a section `f_i` of `𝒥` over `U` whose
class in `Γ(U, 𝒥/𝓘)` is `f_i|U` for `m+1 ⩽ i ⩽ n`; then `(𝒪_X/𝒥) | U = (𝒪_U/(𝒥 | U))` is isomorphic to
`𝒪_U/((𝓘|U) + ∑_{i=m+1}^{n} f_i 𝒪_U) = 𝒪_U/(∑_{i=1}^{n} f_i 𝒪_U)`, and one therefore sees that the sequence
`(f_i)_{1 ⩽ i ⩽ n}` is regular in `𝒪_U`; as it generates `𝒥|U`, this proves the first assertion of (iii). To prove the
last assertion of (iii), one may restrict (with the same notations) to the case where the images `f̄_i` of the `f_i`
in `𝒥/𝒥²` (for `1 ⩽ i ⩽ n`) form a basis of this `(𝒪_X/𝒥)`-Module `(16.9.5)`; for the same reason, one may suppose that
the `f̄_i` for `1 ⩽ i ⩽ m` are the canonical images of elements of a basis of the `(𝒪_X/𝒥)`-Module `g*(𝓘/𝓘²)`, and that
the canonical images `f̃_i` of the `f̄_i` in `(𝒥/𝓘)/(𝒥/𝓘)²` for `m+1 ⩽ i ⩽ n` form a basis of this `(𝒪_X/𝒥)`-Module.
This completes the proof of (iii).

Let us pass to the proof of (iv). The fact that a) implies c) follows from (iii). Let us prove that c) implies a). Set
again `A = 𝒪_{X, g(z)}`, so that `(𝒪_{Y, g(z)}) = A/𝔍`,

<!-- original page 188 -->

`(𝒪_{Z, z}) = A/𝔎` with `𝔍 ⊂ 𝔎`. By hypothesis, `𝔍` and `𝔎` are regular ideals of `A` and one has a split exact sequence

```text
  0 → 𝔍/𝔍² → 𝔎/𝔎² → (𝔎/𝔍)/(𝔎/𝔍)² → 0
```

(cf. `(16.2.7)`). This entails that there exist elements `f_j` `(1 ⩽ j ⩽ r + s)` of `𝔎` whose images `f̄_j` in `𝔎/𝔎²`
form a basis of this `(A/𝔎)`-module, and such that moreover the `f_j` of index `j ⩽ r` belong to `𝔍` and are such that
their images in `𝔍/𝔍²` form a basis of this `(A/𝔍)`-module. The `f_j` for `1 ⩽ j ⩽ r + s` therefore generate `𝔎`, and
the `f_j` for `1 ⩽ j ⩽ r` generate `𝔍` since `A` is Noetherian (Bourbaki, *Alg. comm.*, chap. II, §3, n° 2, prop. 5);
as `𝔎` is a regular ideal, it follows from `(16.9.5)` that the sequence `(f_j)_{1 ⩽ j ⩽ r + s}` is regular. By
definition, the images of `f_{r+1}, …, f_{r+s}` in `𝔎/𝔍` therefore form a regular sequence in this `(A/𝔍)`-module, which
completes the proof that c) entails a) in (iv).

Finally, taking `(16.9.10)` into account, it is immediate that (v), as well as the equivalence of a) and b) in (iv),
result from the

**Corollary (19.1.6).**

<!-- label: IV.19.1.6 -->

*Let `A` be a Noetherian local ring, `𝔍`, `𝔎` two ideals of `A` contained in its maximal ideal `𝔪` and such that
`𝔍 ⊂ 𝔎`, `A' = A/𝔍`, `𝔎' = 𝔎/𝔍`. Then:*

*(i) If `𝔍` and `𝔎'` are regular ideals, so is `𝔎`.*

*(ii) If `𝔎` and `𝔎'` are regular ideals, so is `𝔍`.*

*(iii) Suppose that the rings `A` and `A' = A/𝔍` are regular. For the ideal `𝔎` to be regular, it is necessary and
sufficient that `𝔎'` be regular.*

Assertion (i) is a particular case of `(19.1.5, (iii))`. To prove (ii), consider the canonical surjective homomorphism
`u : 𝔎/𝔎² → 𝔎'/𝔎'² = 𝔎/(𝔎² + 𝔍)`. As by hypothesis `𝔎/𝔎²` and `𝔎'/𝔎'²` are free `(A/𝔎)`-modules, whose respective ranks
we denote `p + q` and `q`, the kernel of `u` is a projective `(A/𝔎)`-module (Bourbaki, *Alg.*, chap. II, 3rd ed., §2,
n° 2, prop. 4), hence free of rank `p` since `A/𝔎` is a Noetherian local ring `(0_III, 10.1.3)`; in other words, there
exists a basis of `𝔎/𝔎²` whose `p` first elements form a basis of `(𝔎² + 𝔍)/𝔎²`. This means again (by virtue of
Nakayama's lemma) that there exists a system of generators `(f_i)_{1 ⩽ i ⩽ p + q}` of `𝔎`, forming a regular sequence
in `A`, such that the sequence `(f_i)_{1 ⩽ i ⩽ p}` is formed of elements of `𝔍`; we must show that this latter sequence
generates `𝔍`. To do so, denote by `𝔍' ⊂ 𝔍` the ideal it generates; by considering the ring `A/𝔍'`, one sees that one
is reduced to proving the following lemma:

**Lemma (19.1.6.1).**

<!-- label: IV.19.1.6.1 -->

*Let `B` be a Noetherian local ring of maximal ideal `𝔫`, `(g_j)_{1 ⩽ j ⩽ q}` a regular sequence in `B` of elements of
`𝔫`, `𝔟` the ideal it generates, `𝔠 ⊂ 𝔟` an ideal such that the canonical images of the `g_j` in `B/𝔠` again form a
regular sequence in this ring. Then one has `𝔠 = 0`.*

The lemma being trivial for `q = 0`, let us reason by induction on `q`. The induction hypothesis, applied to the ring
`B/g_1 B` and to the canonical images of the `g_j` `(2 ⩽ j ⩽ q)`

<!-- original page 189 -->

and of `𝔠` in this quotient ring, shows that `𝔠 ⊂ g_1 B`. Let `𝔟'` be the ideal of `B` formed by `x ∈ B` such that
`g_1 x ∈ 𝔠`; one then has `𝔠 = g_1 𝔟'`; but since by hypothesis `g_1` is regular in `B/𝔠`, one necessarily has
`𝔟' = 𝔠`, hence `𝔠 = g_1 𝔠`. As `𝔠` is of finite type and `g_1` is contained in the radical of `B`, Nakayama's lemma
shows that `𝔠 = 0`.

Let us finally prove assertion (iii) of `(19.1.6)`. By virtue of (i) and of `(19.1.2)`, it suffices to see that if `𝔎'`
is regular, so is `𝔎`. Note that `𝔍` is regular `(19.1.2)`, and let us reason by induction on the rank `q` of the free
`(A/𝔍)`-module `𝔍/𝔍²`. If `q = 0`, one has `𝔍 = 0` by Nakayama's lemma and the assertion is trivial. As `A` and `A/𝔍`
are regular, one knows `(0, 17.1.9)` that `𝔍` is generated by a part with `q` elements of a regular system of parameters
of `A`; so, if `f` is one of the elements of this system of generators of `𝔍`, `A_1 = A/fA` is regular and `f ∉ 𝔪²`
`(0, 17.1.8)`. Let `𝔍_1`, `𝔎_1` be the canonical images of `𝔍`, `𝔎` in `A_1`; one has `𝔍_1 ⊂ 𝔎_1`, `A_1/𝔍_1 = A/𝔍`, so
`A_1/𝔍_1` is regular and consequently `𝔍_1` is a regular ideal `(19.1.2)`. Let us show that `𝔎` is a regular ideal in
`A_1`; as `𝔎` is a regular ideal in `A`, it suffices to show that `f` is part of a regular system of generators of `𝔎`,
and for this `(16.9.5)` it suffices to show that the image of `f` in `𝔎/(𝔎² + 𝔪𝔎) = 𝔎/𝔪𝔎` is part of a basis of this
`(A/𝔪)`-vector space, in other words that `f ∉ 𝔪𝔎`, which indeed follows from `f ∉ 𝔪²`. Then, as `𝔍_1/𝔍_1²` is a free
`(A_1/𝔍_1)`-module of rank `q − 1`, the induction hypothesis shows that `𝔎_1' = 𝔎_1/𝔍_1` is a regular ideal of
`A_1/𝔍_1`, and as `𝔎'` is identified with `𝔎_1'` in the canonical isomorphism between `A/𝔍` and `A_1/𝔍_1`, `𝔎'` is
regular. Q.E.D.

**Remarks (19.1.7).**

<!-- label: IV.19.1.7 -->

*(i) We do not know whether the composite of two quasi-regular immersions is quasi-regular.*

*(ii) In a ringed space in local rings `X`, for every Ideal `𝓘` of `𝒪_X`, `𝒪_X/𝓘` is of finite type and so has closed
support in `X` `(0_I, 5.2.2)`. One may consequently define, as in `(I, 4.1.3 and 4.2.1)`, the notions of (locally
closed) ringed sub-space of `X` defined by an Ideal `𝓘` of a sheaf `𝒪_U`, where `U` is an open set of `X`, and the
notion of immersion. The definitions of quasi-regular and regular immersions, of transversal codimension then apply
without modification, and the results of `(19.1.5, (i) to (iv))` are still valid, supposing in the second assertion of
(i) and in (iv) that the sheaf `𝒪_X` is coherent and the local rings `𝒪_{X, x}` Noetherian; as for (ii), one must
define `Y'` as the ringed sub-space defined by the `𝒪_{X'}`-Ideal `g*(𝓘)`; the proofs are unchanged.*

**(19.1.8)**

<!-- label: IV.19.1.8 -->

We have already pointed out (notably in `(16.9.6, (ii))`) that in non-Noetherian rings, the notion of "regular sequence"
of elements of the ring does not possess the properties that would make it usable. Recent research seems to show that
the notion that should in this case replace that of regular sequence is that of a sequence `f = (f_1, …, f_n)` having
the property that the exterior-algebra complex `K_•(f, M)` `(III, 1.1.3)` has its homology zero in degrees `> 0` (cf.
`(III, 1.1.4)`); see in particular A. Grothendieck, *Séminaire de géométrie algébrique*, 1966, to appear shortly.

### 19.2. Transversally regular immersions

**Definition (19.2.1).**

<!-- label: IV.19.2.1 -->

*Let `u : X → S` be a morphism of preschemes, `ℱ` a quasi-coherent `𝒪_X`-Module. We say that a sequence
`(f_i)_{1 ⩽ i ⩽ n}` of sections of `𝒪_X` over `X` is **transversally `ℱ`-regular relatively to `S`** (or *relatively to
`u`*) if the sequence `(f_i)` is `ℱ`-regular and such that the `𝒪_X`-Modules `ℱ/(∑_{j ⩽ i} f_j ℱ)` are `S`-flat for
`0 ⩽ i ⩽ n`. We say that a quasi-coherent Ideal `𝓘` of `𝒪_X` is **transversally regular relatively to `S`** (or
*relatively to `u`*) at a point `x ∈ Supp(𝒪_X/𝓘)` if there exists an open neighbourhood `U` of `x` and a finite sequence
`(f_i)` of sections of `𝓘` over `U` which is transversally `𝒪_U`-regular relatively to `S` and which generates `𝓘|U`.*

*In an `A`-algebra `B`, we say that an ideal `𝔍` is **transversally regular relatively to `A`** if `𝓘 = 𝔍̃` is
transversally regular relatively to `S = Spec(A)` at every point of `V(𝔍)` in `Spec(B)`.*

**Definition (19.2.2).**

<!-- label: IV.19.2.2 -->

*Let `S` be a prescheme, `X`, `Y` two `S`-preschemes, `u : Y → X` an `S`-morphism which is an immersion. We say that `u`
is a **transversally regular immersion relatively to `S`** at a point `y ∈ Y` if there exists a neighbourhood `V` of
`u(y)` in `X` such that the sub-prescheme induced on `u(Y) ∩ V` by the sub-prescheme of `X` associated to `u` is defined
by an Ideal of `𝒪_V` transversally regular relatively to `S` at the point `u(y)`.*

It is clear that the set of points of `Y` where an immersion is transversally regular relatively to `S` is open in `Y`;
if it equals `Y`, one says simply that `u` is a *transversally regular immersion relatively to `S`*.

If `S → T` is a flat morphism, then if `𝓘` is an Ideal of `𝒪_X` transversally regular relatively to `S` at `x ∈ X`, it
is so also relatively to `T` `(0_I, 6.2.1)`. An `S`-immersion `Y → X` transversally regular at a point `y ∈ Y`
relatively to `S` is therefore also a `T`-immersion transversally regular relatively to `T` at this point.

**Proposition (19.2.3).**

<!-- label: IV.19.2.3 -->

*Let `S` be a prescheme, `X`, `Y` two `S`-preschemes, `u : Y → X` an `S`-morphism which is a transversally regular
immersion relatively to `S` at a point `y ∈ Y`. Then, for every base change `S' → S`, if one sets `X' = X ×_S S'`,
`Y' = Y ×_S S'`, the immersion `u' = u_{(S')} : Y' → X'` is transversally regular relatively to `S'` at every point
`y' ∈ Y'` above `y`.*

This follows at once from the definition and from `(0, 15.1.15)`.

**Proposition (19.2.4).**

<!-- label: IV.19.2.4 -->

*Let `S` be a prescheme, `X`, `Y` two `S`-preschemes, `u : Y → X` an `S`-morphism which is an immersion. Suppose either
that `S` and `X` are locally Noetherian, or that the structure morphisms `g : X → S`, `h : Y → S` are locally of finite
presentation. Let `y` be a point of `Y`, `s` its image in `S`. The following conditions are equivalent:*

*a) `u` is transversally regular relatively to `S` at the point `y`.*

*b) The morphisms `g` and `h` are flat in a neighbourhood of the points `u(y)` and `y` respectively, and if one sets
`X_s = g⁻¹(s)`, `Y_s = h⁻¹(s)`, the immersion `u_s : Y_s → X_s` is regular at the point `y`.*

*b') The morphisms `g` and `h` are flat in a neighbourhood of the points `u(y)` and `y` respectively, and, for every
base change `S' → S`, if one sets `X' = X ×_S S'`, `Y' = Y ×_S S'`, the immersion `u' = u_{(S')} : Y' → X'` is regular at
every point of `Y'` above `y`.*

*c) The morphism `h` is flat in a neighbourhood of the point `y`, and the immersion `u` is regular in a neighbourhood of
the point `y`.*

<!-- original page 191 -->

*c') The morphism `h` is flat in a neighbourhood of the point `y`, and the immersion `u` is quasi-regular in a
neighbourhood of the point `y`.*

It was already proved in `(19.2.3)` that a) entails b') without finiteness hypothesis, and it is trivial that b')
entails b). Let us show that b) entails c). For this (the question being local on `X` and on `S`) one may suppose that
`Y` is a closed sub-prescheme of `X` defined by a quasi-coherent Ideal `𝓘` of `𝒪_X`, and `u` the canonical injection.
The hypothesis that `𝒪_X/𝓘` is `S`-flat entails that the sequence

```text
  0 → 𝓘 ⊗_{𝒪_S} k(s) → 𝒪_X ⊗_{𝒪_S} k(s) → (𝒪_X/𝓘) ⊗_{𝒪_S} k(s) → 0
```

is exact `(0_I, 6.1.2)`, so `Y_s` is the closed sub-prescheme of `X_s` defined by the Ideal `𝓘_s` of
`𝒪_{X_s} = 𝒪_X ⊗_{𝒪_S} k(s)` which is identified with `𝓘 ⊗_{𝒪_S} k(s) = 𝓘/𝔪_s 𝓘`. Consider a finite sequence `(f_i)` of
sections of `𝓘` over a neighbourhood of `y` in `X` whose images in
`𝓘_y / (𝔪_s 𝒪_{X, y} · 𝓘_y + 𝓘_y²)` form a basis of this
`(𝒪_{X, y}/(𝔪_s 𝒪_{X, y} + 𝓘_y))`-module (which is free by virtue of the hypothesis b)). Since `X_s` is locally
Noetherian, it follows from `(16.9.11)`, `(16.9.5)` and the hypothesis b) that the images `(f̄_i)_y = (f_i)_y ⊗ 1`,
sections of `𝓘_s = 𝓘 ⊗_{𝒪_S} k(s)` over a neighbourhood of `y` in `X_s`, form a regular sequence generating the
restriction of `𝓘_s` to this neighbourhood. As `𝔪_s 𝒪_{X, y}` is contained in the radical of `𝒪_{X, y}`, and as in both
cases considered `𝓘_y` is an ideal of finite type of `𝒪_{X, y}`, it follows from Nakayama's lemma that the `(f_i)_y`
also generate `𝓘_y`; as `𝓘` is an Ideal of finite type of `𝒪_X` in both cases considered, there is a neighbourhood `U`
of `y` in `X` such that the `f_i | U` generate `𝓘 | U` `(0_I, 5.2.2)`. The fact that b) entails c) is then a consequence
of `(0, 15.1.16)` when `S` and `X` are locally Noetherian, and of `(11.3.8)` when `g` and `h` are locally of finite
presentation; in this latter case, `(11.3.8)` also proves the equivalence of c) and c') (which is trivial when `X` and
`S` are locally Noetherian `(0, 15.1.11)`). Finally, if c) is verified, to prove a) one may again restrict to the case
where `Y` is a closed sub-prescheme of `X` defined by `𝓘`, and by hypothesis there is a regular sequence `(f_i)` of
sections of `𝓘` over a neighbourhood `U` of `y` in `X` such that the `f_i` generate `𝓘 | U` and `h` is flat in `U`; to
see that c) entails a), it again suffices to apply `(0, 15.1.6)` when `S` and `X` are locally Noetherian, and `(11.3.8)`
when `g` and `h` are locally of finite presentation.

**Corollary (19.2.6).**

<!-- label: IV.19.2.6 -->

*Suppose either that `S` and `X` are locally Noetherian, or that `g` and `h` are locally of finite presentation. Suppose

**Proposition (19.2.7).**

<!-- label: IV.19.2.7 -->

*(i) Every `S`-open immersion is transversally regular relatively to `S`.*

*(ii) Let `f : Y → X` be an `S`-immersion, `g : S' → S` a morphism, and set `X' = X_{(S')}`, `Y' = Y_{(S')}`,
`f' = f_{(S')} : Y' → X'`. If `f` is a transversally regular immersion relatively to `S`, `f'` is a transversally
regular immersion relatively to `S'`. The converse is true if `g` is faithfully flat, and moreover if `X` and `Y` are
locally of finite presentation over `S`.*

*(iii) If `f : Y → X` and `g : Z → Y` are transversally regular immersions relatively to `S`, so is `f ∘ g : Z → X`.*

*(iv) Let `X` be an `S`-prescheme, `f : Y → X`, `g : Z → Y` two `S`-immersions. Suppose either that `S` and `X` are
locally Noetherian, or that `X`, `Y` and `Z` are locally of finite presentation over `S`. Then, if `g` and `f ∘ g` are
transversally regular at the point `z ∈ Z` relatively to `S`, `f` is transversally regular at the point `g(z)`
relatively to `S`.*

*(v) Under the general conditions of (iv), suppose that `X` and `Y` are `S`-flat, `Y_s` regular at the point `g(z)` and
`X_s` regular at the point `g(z)`; then, if `f ∘ g` is transversally regular at the point `z`, so is `g`.*

Assertion (i) is immediate, and the first assertion of (ii) follows at once from `(19.2.3)`. To prove the second
assertion of (ii), let us apply `(19.2.4)`: `X'` and `Y'` being locally of finite presentation over `S'`, it follows
first from this criterion that `X'` and `Y'` are flat over `S'`, so `X` and `Y` are flat over `S` `(2.5.1)`; on the
other hand, for every `s ∈ S`, if `s' ∈ S'` is a point above `s`, `Y'_{s'} → X'_{s'}` is by hypothesis a regular
immersion, and one deduces from `(19.1.5, (ii))` that the same holds for `Y_s → X_s`, since these preschemes are locally
Noetherian and it then amounts to the same to say that the immersion `Y_s → X_s` is regular or quasi-regular. To prove
(iii), one reduces to the case where `Z` is a closed sub-prescheme of `Y` and `Y` a closed sub-prescheme of `X`, and one
forms as in `(19.1.5, (iii))` a system of generators of the Ideal of `𝒪_X` defining `Z`. To prove (iv), one notes that
the hypothesis combined with `(19.2.4)` shows first that `X`, `Y` and `Z` are `S`-flat in a neighbourhood of `z`, and it
suffices moreover, if `s` is the image of `z` in `S`, to prove that the immersion `f_s : Y_s → X_s` is regular at the
point `z`, knowing that the same holds for `g_s : Z_s → Y_s` and for `f_s ∘ g_s`; but this follows from
`(19.1.5, (iv))`. Finally, for (v), one reasons in the same way as for (iv); the hypothesis on `f ∘ g` already implies
by `(19.2.4, b))` that `Z` is `S`-flat in a neighbourhood of `z`, and the same holds for `X` and `Y` by hypothesis; one
is then reduced to proving that `g_s` is regular at the point `z`, knowing that the same holds for `f_s ∘ g_s`, which
follows from `(19.1.5, (v))`, given the hypotheses made on `X_s` and `Y_s`.

**Proposition (19.2.8).**

<!-- label: IV.19.2.8 -->

*Let `X` be an `S`-prescheme, `Y` a closed sub-prescheme of `X` defined by a quasi-coherent Ideal `𝓘` of `𝒪_X`,
`u : Y → X` the canonical injection. If `u` is transversally regular relatively to `S`, the `𝒪_X`-Modules `𝓘^n` and
`𝓘^n/𝓘^m` `(0 ⩽ n < m)` are `S`-flat at the points of `Y`. For every base change `S' → S`, if `X' = X_{(S')}`,
`Y' = Y_{(S')}` and `u' = u_{(S')}`, one has `𝓘^n_{(u')} = 𝓘^n_{(u)} ⊗_{𝒪_S} 𝒪_{S'}` up to isomorphism, for every
`n > 0`.*

Indeed, `𝒪_X` and `𝒪_X/𝓘` are by hypothesis `S`-flat at the points of `Y`, and `𝓘^n/𝓘^{n+1}` is a locally free
`(𝒪_X/𝓘)`-module `(16.9.3)`, so it is `S`-flat at the points of `Y`. The first assertion then follows from
`(0_I, 6.1.2)` and the second from `(11.2.9.2)`.

The following proposition generalizes and refines `(17.16.1)`:

**Proposition (19.2.9).**

<!-- label: IV.19.2.9 -->

*Let `f : X → S` be a morphism, `x` a point of `X`, `s = f(x)`. Suppose that `f` is of finite presentation at the
point `x`, and is a Cohen-Macaulay morphism at the point `x` `(6.8.1)`. Then there exists a sub-prescheme `Y` of `X`
containing `x` and having the following properties:*

*(i) The canonical injection `j : Y → X` is transversally regular relatively to `S`.*

*(ii) The morphism `g = f ∘ j : Y → S` is Cohen-Macaulay (which amounts to saying, taking (i) into account, that all the
fibres `g⁻¹(f(x))` of `g` are Cohen-Macaulay preschemes).*

*(iii) The point `x` is a maximal point of `Y_s = g⁻¹(s)`.*

Set `X_s = f⁻¹(s)`; by hypothesis, the ring `𝒪_{X_s, x}` is Cohen-Macaulay; let `(t_i)_{1 ⩽ i ⩽ n}` be a system of
parameters of this ring, which is therefore a regular sequence `(0, 16.5.7)`. There is an open neighbourhood `U` of `x`
in `X` and sections `h_i` `(1 ⩽ i ⩽ n)` of `𝒪_X` over `U` such that the `t_i` are the canonical images of the `(h_i)_x`
in the quotient ring `𝒪_{X_s, x}` of `𝒪_{X, x}`. Let `𝓘` be the Ideal of `𝒪_U` generated by the `h_i` and take for `Y`
the closed sub-prescheme of `U` defined by `𝓘`. One may suppose that `f|U` is locally of finite presentation, and it is
therefore the same for `g` by definition of `Y`; it follows then from the equivalence of c') and a) in `(11.3.8)` that
the sequence `((h_i)_x)` is regular in `𝒪_{X, x}` and that `𝒪_{Y, x}` is flat over `𝒪_{S, s}`; by virtue of `(11.3.1)`,
`f` and `g` are therefore flat in a neighbourhood of `x` and it follows from `(19.2.4)` that `j` is a transversally
regular immersion relatively to `S` (replacing if necessary `Y` by `Y ∩ V` for an open neighbourhood `V` of `x` in `X`).
One has thus proved (i) and one sees that one may suppose that `g : Y → S` is a flat morphism. As `𝒪_{Y_s, x}`, quotient
of `𝒪_{X_s, x}` by the ideal generated by the `t_i`, is Artinian by construction `(0, 16.3.6)`, `x` is a maximal point
of `Y_s`. Finally, the ring `𝒪_{Y_s, x}` being Artinian is a Cohen-Macaulay ring `(0, 16.5.1)`, so, by replacing if
necessary `Y` by its intersection with an open neighbourhood of `x` in `X`, one may suppose that `g` is a Cohen-Macaulay
morphism, by virtue of `(12.1.7, (iii))`.

In particular, one recovers the existence of "flat quasi-sections" `Y` of a morphism `f : X → S` at a point `x ∈ X_s`
which is closed in `X_s` and such that `f` is flat at the point `x` and `𝒪_{X_s, x}` is a Cohen-Macaulay ring
`(17.16.1)`, and one sees moreover that the immersion `Y → X` is transversally regular relatively to `S`. This last
complement is also valid for the étale quasi-section defined in `(17.16.3, (i))`.

### 19.3. Relative complete intersections (flat case)

**Definition (19.3.1).**

<!-- label: IV.19.3.1 -->

*Let `A` be a Noetherian local ring. We say that `A` is a **complete intersection ring** (or also **absolute complete
intersection ring**, when there is danger of confusion) if the completion `Â` is isomorphic to the quotient of a
complete regular Noetherian local ring `B` by a regular ideal (i.e. `(16.9.7)` an ideal generated by a regular sequence
of elements of `B`).*

Every regular local ring is a complete intersection ring.

One says that a locally Noetherian prescheme `X` is a **(absolute) complete intersection** at the point `x ∈ X` if the
ring `𝒪_{X, x}` is a complete intersection ring.

**Proposition (19.3.2).**

<!-- label: IV.19.3.2 -->

*Let `B` be a regular Noetherian local ring, `𝔍` an ideal of `B`. For `A = B/𝔍` to be a complete intersection ring, it
is necessary and sufficient that the ideal `𝔍` be regular (i.e. `(16.9.7)` generated by a regular sequence of elements
of `B`).*

One may write `Â = B̂/𝔍 B̂` and since `B̂` is a faithfully flat `B`-module and `B̂` Noetherian, `𝔍 B̂` is regular if and
only if `𝔍` is `(19.1.5, (ii))`. This shows (by virtue of `(0, 17.1.5)`) on the one hand that the condition of the
statement is sufficient, and on the other hand that to prove it is necessary, one may restrict to the case where `A` and
`B` are complete. By hypothesis, there then exists a complete regular Noetherian local ring `B'` such that `A` is
isomorphic to a quotient `B'/𝔍'`, where `𝔍'` is a regular ideal of `B'`. Consider then the fibre product
`B ×_A B' = B''` for the surjective homomorphisms `B → A`, `B' → A` `(0, 18.1.2)`; we shall first prove the following
lemma:

**Lemma (19.3.2.1).**

<!-- label: IV.19.3.2.1 -->

*Let `A`, `E`, `F` be three rings, `f : E → A`, `g : F → A` two homomorphisms, and set `G = E ×_A F` `(0, 18.1.2)`.*

*(i) If `f` is surjective and if `E` and `F` are Noetherian rings, so is `G`.*

*(ii) If `A`, `E` and `F` are local rings and `f` and `g` are local homomorphisms, `G` is a local ring and the canonical
homomorphisms `G → E`, `G → F` are local.*

*(iii) If the conditions of (i) and (ii) are verified, if `g` is surjective, and if `E` and `F` are complete Noetherian
local rings, so is `G`.*

(i) Taking into account `(0, 18.1.3 and 18.1.5)`, `G` is an `A`-ring augmented over `F`, whose augmentation ideal `𝔍'`
is canonically identified with the kernel `𝔍` of `f`, the `G`-module structure on `𝔍'` being identified with that of
`E`-module on `𝔍` via the canonical homomorphism `G → E`. If `𝔞` is any ideal of `G`, `𝔞/(𝔞 ∩ 𝔍')` is isomorphic to
`(𝔞 + 𝔍')/𝔍'`, hence to an ideal of `F`, and is therefore of finite type; on the other hand, `𝔞 ∩ 𝔍'` is isomorphic to
an ideal of `E` contained in `𝔍`, hence is also of finite type, which proves that `𝔞` is of finite type, so `G` is
Noetherian.

(ii) Let `𝔯`, `𝔰` be the maximal ideals of `E` and `F` respectively; the set `𝔪 = 𝔯 ×_A 𝔰` is evidently an ideal of `G`,
the inverse image of `𝔯` by the projection `G → E` and of `𝔰` by the projection `G → F`. One deduces at once that the
elements of `𝔪` are non-invertible in `G`. On the other hand, if `(x, y) ∉ 𝔪`, and if for instance `x ∉ 𝔯`, `f(x)` does
not belong to the maximal ideal of `A`, hence, since `g(y) = f(x)`, and since `g` is local, one has also `y ∉ 𝔰`; one

<!-- original page 195 -->

concludes that `x` and `y` are invertible, so the same holds for `(x, y)`, which completes the proof of (ii).

(iii) The image of `𝔪` by the surjective homomorphism `G → E` is the maximal ideal `𝔯` of `E`, so the image of
`𝔪 ∩ 𝔍'` is `𝔯 ∩ 𝔍`, and as `𝔍` is closed (hence complete) for the topology induced by the `𝔯`-adic topology of `E`
`(0_I, 7.3.5)`, `𝔍'` is complete for the topology induced by the `𝔪`-preadic topology of `G`. On the other hand,
`G/𝔍'`, endowed with the `𝔪`-preadic topology, is isomorphic to `F` endowed with the `𝔰`-adic topology, hence is
complete for the quotient topology of the `𝔪`-preadic topology of `G`. One deduces at once that `G` is complete for the
`𝔪`-preadic topology.

This lemma being established, it follows from Cohen's theorem `(0, 19.8.8)` that `B''` is isomorphic to a quotient of a
complete regular Noetherian local ring `C`. It then follows from `(19.1.2)` that the immersion `Spec(B') → Spec(C)` is
regular; as by hypothesis the same holds for the immersion `Spec(A) → Spec(B')`, one concludes from `(19.1.5, (iii))`
that the immersion `Spec(A) → Spec(C)` is regular; but this immersion is also written as the composite
`Spec(A) → Spec(B) → Spec(C)`. Now as `B` is regular by hypothesis, the immersion `Spec(B) → Spec(C)` is regular
`(19.1.2)`; so the same holds for `Spec(A) → Spec(B)` by `(19.1.5, (iv))`.

**Corollary (19.3.3).**

<!-- label: IV.19.3.3 -->

*Let `X` be a locally Noetherian prescheme locally immersible in a regular prescheme. Then the set of `x ∈ X` where `X`
is a complete intersection is open in `X` and contains the set of regular points of `X`.*

One may indeed restrict to the case where `X = Spec(B/𝔍)`, where `B` is a regular ring and `𝔍` an ideal of `B`. The set
of `𝔭 ⊃ 𝔍` in `Spec(B)` such that `𝔍_𝔭` is regular in `B_𝔭` is then open `(16.9.5)`, and it is the set of points of
`Spec(B)` where `X` is a complete intersection by virtue of `(19.3.2)`.

**Corollary (19.3.4).**

<!-- label: IV.19.3.4 -->

*Let `k` be a field, `X` a prescheme locally of finite type over `k`, `k'` an extension of `k`, `X' = X ⊗_k k'`. Let
`x'` be a point of `X'`, `x` its projection in `X`. For `X` to be a complete intersection at `x`, it is necessary and
sufficient that `X'` be a complete intersection at `x'`.*

The question being local on `X`, one may suppose that `X = Spec(A)`, where `A` is a `k`-algebra of finite type, hence a
quotient of a polynomial algebra `k[T_1, …, T_n]`, so that `X` is a closed sub-prescheme of the regular scheme
`Y = Spec(k[T_1, …, T_n])` `(0, 17.3.7)`. If one sets `Y' = Y ⊗_k k' = Spec(k'[T_1, …, T_n])`, `Y'` is also a regular
scheme. Now, since `Spec(k')` is faithfully flat over `Spec(k)`, it follows from `(19.1.5, (ii))` and from the fact that
these are Noetherian preschemes that the immersion `X → Y` is regular at the point `x` if and only if the immersion
`X' → Y'` is regular at the point `x'`. The conclusion therefore follows from the criterion `(19.3.2)`.

**Corollary (19.3.5).**

<!-- label: IV.19.3.5 -->

*Let `A`, `C` be two Noetherian local rings, `φ : A → C` a local homomorphism, `B = C/𝔍` a quotient ring of `C`, `k` the
residue field of `A`. Suppose that `φ` makes `C` a flat `A`-module, and that the ring `C ⊗_A k` is regular. Then the
following conditions are equivalent:*

*a) The ideal `𝔍` is transversally regular relatively to `A`.*

*b) `B` is a flat `A`-module, and `B ⊗_A k` is a complete intersection ring.*

<!-- original page 196 -->

By virtue of `(19.2.4)`, condition a) amounts to saying that `B` is a flat `A`-module and that `𝔍 ⊗_A k` (which is
identified with an ideal of `C ⊗_A k` by virtue of the flatness of `B` over `A` `(0_I, 6.1.2)`) is regular in this ring.
Since the ring `C ⊗_A k` is regular, it amounts to the same, by virtue of `(19.3.2)`, to say (when `B` is a flat
`A`-module) that `𝔍 ⊗_A k` is a regular ideal of `C ⊗_A k`, or that `B ⊗_A k = (C ⊗_A k)/(𝔍 ⊗_A k)` is a complete
intersection ring; whence the corollary.

**Definition (19.3.6).**

<!-- label: IV.19.3.6 -->

*Let `f : X → S` be a flat morphism locally of finite presentation. We say that `X` is a **relative complete
intersection relatively to `S`** at a point `x` if the fibre `f⁻¹(f(x))` is an (absolute) complete intersection at the
point `x`. We say that `X` is a **relative complete intersection relatively to `S`**, or that the morphism `f` is a
**complete intersection morphism**, if `X` is a relative complete intersection relatively to `S` at each of its
points.*

**Proposition (19.3.7).**

<!-- label: IV.19.3.7 -->

*Let `g : X → S`, `h : Y → S` be two flat morphisms locally of finite presentation, `f : Y → X` an `S`-immersion, `y`
a point of `Y`, `x = f(y)`, `s = g(x) = h(y)`. Suppose that the fibre `X_s = g⁻¹(s)` is regular at the point `x`. Then,
for `f` to be a transversally regular immersion at the point `y`, it is necessary and sufficient that `Y` be a relative
complete intersection relatively to `S` at the point `y`.*

To say that `f` is transversally regular at the point `y` amounts, by virtue of `(19.2.4)`, to saying that
`f_s : Y_s → X_s` is a regular immersion at the point `y`. But as `X_s` is regular at the point `x = f(y)`, this is
equivalent, by virtue of `(19.3.2)`, to saying that `Y_s` is an (absolute) complete intersection at the point `y`,
whence the proposition.

**Corollary (19.3.8).**

<!-- label: IV.19.3.8 -->

*Let `f : X → S` be a flat morphism locally of finite presentation. The set `U` of `x ∈ X` at which `X` is a relative
complete intersection relatively to `S` is open in `X`. If moreover `f` is proper, the set `E` of `s ∈ S` such that
`X_s = f⁻¹(s)` is a complete intersection at each of its points is an open part of `S`.*

As `E = S − f(X − U)`, the second assertion follows from the first and from the fact that `f` is a closed map. The first
assertion is local on `X`, so one may suppose that `X` is a closed sub-prescheme of a prescheme of the form
`Z = S[T_1, …, T_n]` (the `T_i` indeterminates). This latter being smooth over `S` `(17.3.8)`, its fibres `Z_s` are
regular, and it amounts to the same, by virtue of `(19.3.7)`, to say that `x ∈ U` or that the immersion `X → Z` is
transversally regular at the point `x`; one therefore concludes the corollary by `(19.2.2)`.

**Proposition (19.3.9).**

<!-- label: IV.19.3.9 -->

*(i) Every open immersion is a complete intersection morphism.*

*(ii) Let `f : X → S` be a flat morphism locally of finite presentation which is a complete intersection morphism. For
every base change `g : S' → S`, `f' = f_{(S')} : X_{(S')} → S'` is a complete intersection morphism. The converse is true
if `g` is faithfully flat and quasi-compact.*

*(iii) If `f : X → Y` and `g : Y → Z` are two flat morphisms locally of finite presentation which are complete
intersection morphisms, so is `g ∘ f : X → Z`.*

Assertion (i) follows at once from definition `(19.3.1)`. To prove (ii), let us note first that if `f` is flat and
locally of finite presentation, so is

<!-- original page 197 -->

`f'` `(2.1.4)`, the converse being true if `g` is faithfully flat `((2.5.1)` and `(2.7.1))`; furthermore, for every
`s' ∈ S'`, if `s = g(s')`, it is equivalent to say that `f⁻¹(s)` is a complete intersection or that `f'⁻¹(s')` is a
complete intersection `(19.3.4)`; whence (ii), taking into account that `g` is surjective when it is faithfully flat.

Finally, under the hypotheses of (iii), `g ∘ f` is flat and locally of finite presentation. By definition `(19.3.6)`,
one is therefore reduced to the case where `Z` is the spectrum of a field, and to proving then that the local rings
`𝒪_{X, x}` are complete intersection rings, which is contained in the more general following result:

**Corollary (19.3.10).**

<!-- label: IV.19.3.10 -->

*Let `f : X → S` be a flat morphism locally of finite presentation which is a complete intersection morphism, `x` a
point of `X`, `s = f(x)`. If `𝒪_{S, s}` is a Noetherian complete intersection ring, so is `𝒪_{X, x}`.*

One may obviously restrict to the case where `S = Spec(A)` with `A = 𝒪_{S, s}`. Let us show moreover that one may
restrict to the case where the local ring `A` is complete. Indeed, set `A' = Â`, `X' = X ⊗_A A'`, and let `s'` be the
unique closed point of `S' = Spec(A')`, which is the unique point of `S'` above `s`; if `f' = f_{(S')} : X' → S'`, the
fibre `f'⁻¹(s')` is canonically isomorphic to `f⁻¹(s)`, since `A` and `Â` have the same residue field `(I, 3.6.4)`;
there is thus a single point `x' ∈ X'` above `x` and above `s'`, and one consequently has
`𝒪_{X', x'} = 𝒪_{X, x} ⊗_A A'`. One deduces that the local rings `𝒪_{X, x}` and `𝒪_{X', x'}` have isomorphic
completions, for in general, if `E` is a local ring which is an `A`-algebra (the homomorphism `A → E` being local), the
separated completion `(E ⊗_A Â)^∧` of the ring `E ⊗_A Â` endowed with the tensor product topology is isomorphic to the
separated completion of `Ê ⊗_A Â = Ê`, hence to the separated completion `Ê` of `E` `(0_I, 7.7.1)`. By virtue of
definition `(19.3.1)`, it therefore amounts to the same to say that `𝒪_{X, x}` is a complete intersection ring, or that
`𝒪_{X', x'}` is a complete intersection ring.

Suppose then `A` complete; one may moreover suppose that `X = Spec(B)`, `B` being a quotient of a polynomial algebra
`C = A[T_1, …, T_r]`. As polynomial rings over a field are regular rings `(0, 17.3.7)`, it follows from `(19.3.7)` that
the immersion `X → Z = Spec(C)` may be supposed transversally regular relatively to `S`; so one may suppose that
`B = C/𝔍`, where `𝔍` is generated by a regular sequence `(g_i)_{1 ⩽ i ⩽ n}` of elements of `C`. On the other hand,
since `A` is complete, one may by hypothesis write it `A'/𝔎`, where `A'` is a regular local ring and `𝔎` an ideal of
`A'` `(0, 19.8.8)`, and the hypothesis that `A` is a complete intersection ring entails that `𝔎` is generated by a
regular sequence `(f_j)_{1 ⩽ j ⩽ m}` `(19.3.2)`. In the polynomial ring `C' = A'[T_1, …, T_r]`, the elements `f_j`
`(1 ⩽ j ⩽ m)` still form a regular sequence generating the ideal `𝔎' = 𝔎[T_1, …, T_r]` `(0, 15.1.4)`; as `C'/𝔎' = C`,
one sees that if, for each `i`, `g'_i` is an element of `C'` of image `g_i` in `C`, the sequence formed of the `f_j`
`(1 ⩽ j ⩽ m)` and of the `g'_i` `(1 ⩽ i ⩽ n)` is regular in `C'`; if `𝔍'` is the ideal of `C'` it generates, one has
`B = C'/𝔍'`, whence the conclusion by virtue of `(19.3.2)`, since `C'` is a regular ring `(0, 17.3.7)`.

### 19.4. Application: regularity and smoothness criteria for blow-up preschemes

**(19.4.1)**

<!-- label: IV.19.4.1 -->

Let `X` be a prescheme, `Y` a closed sub-prescheme of `X` defined by a quasi-coherent Ideal `𝓘` of `𝒪_X`. Recall
`(II, 8.1.3)` that the `X`-scheme `X'` obtained by blowing up `Y` is the prescheme

```text
  X' = Proj(𝒮),    where    𝒮 = ⊕_{n ⩾ 0} 𝓘^n.
```

If `𝓘` is of finite type, the structure morphism `f : X' → X` is projective. Without hypothesis on `𝓘`, the closed
sub-prescheme

```text
  Y' = f⁻¹(Y)
```

of `X'` is defined by the quasi-coherent Ideal `𝓘 𝒪_{X'}` of `𝒪_{X'}`, canonically isomorphic to `𝒪_{X'}(1)`; more
precisely, one has an exact sequence

```text
  (19.4.1.1)    0 → 𝓘 𝒪_{X'} → 𝒪_{X'} → 𝒪_{Y'} → 0
```

where `𝓘 𝒪_{X'}` is the image of `𝓘 ⊗_{𝒪_X} 𝒪_{X'}` `(II, 8.1.7 and 8.1.8)`. As in a neighbourhood of a point of `X'`,
the `𝒪_{X'}`-Module `𝒪_{X'}(1)` is free of rank `1`, `𝓘 𝒪_{X'}` is generated in such a neighbourhood by an invertible
section of `𝒪_{X'}`, and `𝓘 𝒪_{X'}/𝓘² 𝒪_{X'}` is consequently a free `(𝒪_{X'}/𝓘 𝒪_{X'})`-Module of rank `1`. This proves
the first assertion of the following lemma:

**Lemma (19.4.2).**

<!-- label: IV.19.4.2 -->

*The canonical immersion `Y' → X'` is regular and of codimension `1`, and `Y'` is canonically isomorphic to the
`Y`-scheme `Proj(gr_𝓘^•(𝒪_X))`.*

The second assertion follows from `(II, 3.5.3)` applied to the canonical injection `Y → X`, taking into account that
`𝒮 ⊗_{𝒪_X} (𝒪_X/𝓘) = gr_𝓘^•(𝒪_X)` by definition, since `𝓘 ⊗_{𝒪_X} (𝒪_X/𝓘)` is isomorphic to `𝒪_X/𝓘` and
`𝓘^n ⊗_{𝒪_X} (𝒪_X/𝓘)` to `𝓘^n/𝓘^{n+1}`.

**Corollary (19.4.3).**

<!-- label: IV.19.4.3 -->

*If the canonical immersion `Y → X` is quasi-regular, the restriction `g : Y' → Y` of the morphism `f` is smooth.*

Indeed, `𝒩_{Y/X} = 𝓘/𝓘²` is then a locally free `𝒪_Y`-Module of finite rank, and `gr_𝓘^•(𝒪_X)` is isomorphic to
`S_{𝒪_Y}^•(𝒩_{Y/X})` `(16.9.8)`, so `Y'` is `Y`-isomorphic to `P(𝒩_{Y/X})` and consequently is smooth over `Y`
`(17.3.9)`.

We shall see later (chap. V) that `g` is still smooth when the immersion `Y → X` "presents only ordinary singularities".

**Proposition (19.4.4).**

<!-- label: IV.19.4.4 -->

*With the notations of `(19.4.1)`, suppose that `X` is locally Noetherian and that the morphism `g : Y' → Y`,
restriction of `f`, is smooth. Let `x'` be a point of `Y'`, `x = g(x')`. For `Y` to be regular at the point `x`, it is
necessary and sufficient that `Y'` be regular at the point `x'`; when this holds, `X'` is then regular at the
point `x'`.*

The first assertion follows from `(17.5.8)` and the second from `(19.1.1)` and `(19.4.2)`.

**Corollary (19.4.5).**

<!-- label: IV.19.4.5 -->

*Under the general hypotheses of `(19.4.4)`, suppose that `Y` is regular at the point `x`. Then, for every generization
`x_1` of `x` in `X` not belonging to `Y`, `X` is regular at the point `x_1`. If `Reg(X)` is open (`(6.12)`, for example
if `X` is excellent `(7.8.6, (iii))`),*

<!-- original page 199 -->

*and if `Y` is regular, then there exists an open neighbourhood `U` of `Y` in `X` such that `X` is regular at the points
of `U − Y`.*

To prove the first assertion, one may restrict to the case where `X` is a local scheme of closed point `x`; the
hypothesis then entails that `Y` is regular `(0, 17.3.2)`, so the same holds for `Y'` by `(19.4.4)`. Note now that the
morphism `f : X' → X` is projective, hence proper, and therefore `(II, 7.2.1)`, if one considers the unique point `x'_1`
of `X'` above `x_1` `(II, 8.1.3)`, there exists a specialization of `x'_1` belonging to `Y'`; applying `(19.4.4)` and
using `(0, 17.3.2)` one concludes that `X'` is regular at the point `x'_1`, hence `X` regular at the point `x_1` since
`X' − Y'` is isomorphic to `X − Y` `(II, 8.1.3)`. If `Y` is regular, every point of `X − Y` which is a generization of a
point of `Y` therefore belongs to `Reg(X)`; if `Reg(X)` is open, `U = Y ∪ Reg(X)` is therefore locally constructible and
stable under generization, hence open `(0_III, 9.2.5)` and answers the question.

**Proposition (19.4.6).**

<!-- label: IV.19.4.6 -->

*Let `g : X → S` be a morphism, `Y` a closed sub-prescheme of `X` defined by a quasi-coherent Ideal `𝓘`, `X'` the
`X`-scheme obtained by blowing up `Y`, `Y'` the inverse image of `Y` in `X'`.*

*(i) The following conditions are equivalent:*

*a) `gr_𝓘^•(𝒪_X)` is a `g`-flat `𝒪_X`-Algebra.*

*b) For every `n ⩾ 0`, the `𝒪_X`-Module `𝒪_X/𝓘^{n+1}` is `g`-flat (in other words, the `n`-th infinitesimal neighbourhood
`Y_n` of `Y` in `X` `(16.1.2)` is `S`-flat).*

*c) For every base change `S_1 → S` and every integer `n ⩾ 1`, if one sets `X_1 = X ×_S S_1`, the canonical homomorphism
`𝓘^n ⊗_{𝒪_S} 𝒪_{S_1} → 𝓘_1^n` is bijective.*

*When this holds, `Y'` is `S`-flat, and for every base change `S_1 → S`, if `Y_1` is the inverse image of `Y` in `X_1`,
the prescheme `X'_1` obtained by blowing up `Y_1` in `X_1` is canonically isomorphic to `X' ×_S S_1`.*

*(ii) Suppose that the equivalent conditions of (i) are satisfied and moreover that the morphisms `X → S` and `Y → S`
are locally of finite presentation. Then `𝒮 = ⊕_{n ⩾ 0} 𝓘^n` is an `𝒪_X`-Algebra of finite presentation, the morphism
`X' → X` is of finite presentation, the canonical immersion `Y' → X'` is transversally regular of codimension `1`
relatively to `S`, and the set of points of `X` (resp. `X'`) where `X` (resp. `X'`) is `S`-flat is a neighbourhood of
`Y` (resp. `Y'`).*

(i) The equivalence of a) and b) follows at once from `(0_I, 6.1.2)`; to show the equivalence of b) and c), one may
restrict to the case where `S = Spec(A)` and `X = Spec(B)` are affine, with `𝓘 = 𝔍̃`, where `𝔍` is an ideal of `B`; the
exact sequence of `Tor` then shows that condition b) implies that `Tor_1^A(B/𝔍^{n+1}, A') = 0` for every integer `n ⩾ 0`
and every `A`-algebra `A'`; by taking for `A'` an algebra direct sum `A ⊕ M`, where `M` is an arbitrary `A`-module and
where multiplication is defined by `(a, m)(a', m') = (aa', am' + a'm)`, one sees that the preceding condition is
equivalent to `Tor_1^A(B/𝔍^{n+1}, M) = 0`, hence to the fact that `B/𝔍^{n+1}` is a flat `A`-module. If one sets
`𝔍_1 = 𝔍 ⊗_A A_1`, one then has `⊕_{n ⩾ 0} 𝔍_1^n = (⊕_{n ⩾ 0} 𝔍^n) ⊗_A A_1`

<!-- original page 200 -->

and the assertion relative to the blown-up prescheme `X'_1` follows at once. Finally, to prove that `Y'` is `S`-flat,
one may again restrict to the case where `S` and `X` are affine; if one sets `C = gr_𝓘^•(B)`, every point of `Y'` then
admits, by virtue of `(19.4.2)` and `(II, 2.3.6)`, an affine open neighbourhood whose ring is of the form `C_(t)`, where
`t` is a homogeneous element of degree `1` of `C`; since by hypothesis `C` is a flat `A`-module, so is its ring of
fractions `C_t` and the degree-`0` component `C_(t)` of this graded `A`-module, whence our assertion.

(ii) One may always restrict to the case where `S` and `X` are affine, `B` being therefore an `A`-algebra of finite
presentation, `𝔍` an ideal of finite type of `B`. By virtue of `(8.9.1)`, `(8.6.3)` and `(11.2.9)`, there exist a
Noetherian sub-ring `A_0` of `A`, an `A_0`-algebra of finite type `B_0`, an ideal `𝔍_0` of `B_0` such that
`B = B_0 ⊗_{A_0} A`, `𝔍 = 𝔍_0 ⊗_{A_0} A`, such that `gr_{𝔍_0}^•(B_0)` is an `A_0`-flat module, and that
`gr_𝔍^•(B) = gr_{𝔍_0}^•(B_0) ⊗_{A_0} A`. By virtue of (i), one then has
`⊕_{n ⩾ 0} 𝔍^n = (⊕_{n ⩾ 0} 𝔍_0^n) ⊗_{A_0} A = (⊕_{n ⩾ 0} 𝔍_0^n) ⊗_{B_0} B`; as `⊕_{n ⩾ 0} 𝔍_0^n` is a `B_0`-algebra
generated by the ideal of finite type `𝔍_0` of `B_0`, hence a `B_0`-algebra of finite type, and consequently of finite
presentation since `B_0` is Noetherian, one deduces that `⊕_{n ⩾ 0} 𝔍^n` is a `B`-algebra of finite presentation.
Likewise, the morphism `Proj(⊕_{n ⩾ 0} 𝔍_0^n) → Spec(B_0)` is of finite type `(II, 2.7.1)`, and consequently of finite
presentation; so the morphism `X' → X`, which is deduced from it by base change, is of finite presentation. One already
knows `(19.4.2)` that the canonical immersion `Y' → X'` is regular; as `Y'` is `S`-flat, as one saw in (i), one deduces
from `(19.2.4)` that the immersion `Y' → X'` is transversally regular relatively to `S` and that `X'` is `S`-flat at the
points of `Y'`, hence `(11.3.1)` in an open neighbourhood of `Y'`. On the other hand, the fact that `gr_𝓘^•(𝒪_X)` is
an `S`-flat `𝒪_X`-Algebra entails, by `(11.3.4)`, that `X` is `S`-flat at the points of `Y`, hence `(11.3.1)` in a
neighbourhood of `Y`.

**Corollary (19.4.7).**

<!-- label: IV.19.4.7 -->

*Let `g : X → S` be a morphism locally of finite presentation, `Y` a closed sub-prescheme of `X` such that the composite
morphism `Y → X → S` is locally of finite presentation; suppose moreover that `Y` is `S`-flat and that `𝒪_X` is normally
flat along `Y` `(11.3.4)`. Then (with the notations of `(19.4.1)`), the morphism `X' → X` is of finite presentation, the
canonical immersion `Y' → X'` is transversally regular of codimension `1` relatively to `S`, `Y'` is `Y`-flat (hence
`S`-flat), and the set of points of `X` (resp. `X'`) where `X` (resp. `X'`) is `S`-flat is a neighbourhood of `Y`
(resp. `Y'`).*

Indeed, by hypothesis `gr_𝓘^•(𝒪_X)` is a flat `(𝒪_X/𝓘)`-Module, hence is `S`-flat since `Y` is supposed to be `S`-flat.
One may consequently apply the results of `(19.4.6, (i) and (ii))`. As `Y'` is `Y`-isomorphic to `Proj(gr_𝓘^•(𝒪_X))`
`(19.4.2)`, the same reasoning as in `(19.4.6)` shows that `Y'` is `Y`-flat.

**Proposition (19.4.8).**

<!-- label: IV.19.4.8 -->

*Suppose the hypotheses of `(19.4.7)` are verified and moreover that the morphism `Y' → Y` is smooth. Let `x'` be a
point of `Y'`, `x` its image in `Y`. For `Y` to be smooth over `S` at the point `x`, it is necessary and sufficient that
`Y'` be smooth over `S` at the point `x'`; when this holds, `X'` is then smooth over `S` at the point `x'`.*

*The preceding conclusions are in particular verified when the morphism `X → S` is locally of finite presentation and
the immersion `Y → X` is transversally regular relatively to `S`.*

Taking into account the compatibility of the formation of a blow-up prescheme with base changes in the case considered
`(19.4.6, (i))` and `(17.5.1)` and `(17.7.1)`,

<!-- original page 201 -->

it suffices to consider the case where `S` is the spectrum of an algebraically closed field. But then it amounts to the
same to say that an `S`-prescheme locally of finite type is smooth over `S` at a point or that it is regular at this
point `(17.15.1)`, so the conclusions follow from `(19.4.4)`.

When `X → S` is locally of finite presentation and the canonical immersion `Y → X` is quasi-regular, this immersion is
by definition of finite presentation since the Ideal which defines `Y` is of finite type, so the composite morphism
`Y → X → S` is locally of finite presentation. One has already seen `(19.4.3)` that under these conditions the morphism
`Y' → Y` is smooth; moreover, one then knows that `𝒩_{Y/X}` is a locally free `𝒪_Y`-Module and that `gr_𝓘^•(𝒪_X)` is
isomorphic to `S_{𝒪_Y}^•(𝒩_{Y/X})`, hence is a flat `𝒪_Y`-Module. The hypotheses of `(19.4.7)` are therefore all
verified and one may apply the conclusions of the statement of `(19.4.8)`.

**Corollary (19.4.9).**

<!-- label: IV.19.4.9 -->

*Under the general hypotheses of `(19.4.8)`, suppose moreover that `Y` is smooth over `S` at the point `x`. Then, for
every generization `x_1` of `x` in `X` not belonging to `Y`, `X` is smooth over `S` at the point `x_1`. If `Y` is smooth
over `S`, then there exists an open neighbourhood `U` of `Y` in `X` such that `X` is smooth over `S` at the points of
`U − Y`.*

For the first assertion, one may restrict to the case where `X` is a local scheme of closed point `x`, and the
hypothesis then entails that `Y` is smooth over `S`, every neighbourhood of the closed point of `Y` in `Y` being
necessarily `Y` itself; one therefore concludes by the same reasoning as in `(19.4.5)`, using the fact that the set of
points where `X'` is smooth over `S` is open in `X'`. To prove the second assertion, one may reduce to the case where
`S` is Noetherian, and `X` of finite type over `S`, thanks to `(8.9.1)`, `(8.6.3)`, `(11.2.9)` and `(17.7.6)`; one then
concludes as in `(19.4.5)`, using the fact that the set of points where `X` is smooth over `S` is open in `X`.

**Remark (19.4.10).**

<!-- label: IV.19.4.10 -->

*In `(19.4.6, (ii))`, replace the hypothesis that `X` and `Y` are locally of finite presentation over `S` by the
hypothesis that `S` and `X` (hence also `Y`) are locally Noetherian. Then it is clear that `⊕_{n ⩾ 0} 𝓘^n` is an
`𝒪_X`-Algebra of finite type, that the morphism `X' → X` is of finite type, and it still follows from `(19.2.4)`
applied to locally Noetherian preschemes that the immersion `Y' → X'` is transversally regular relatively to `S` and
that `X'` is `S`-flat at the points of `Y'`.*

**Proposition (19.4.11).**

<!-- label: IV.19.4.11 -->

*Let `X` be a prescheme, `ℰ` a quasi-coherent `𝒪_X`-Module, `u : ℰ → 𝒪_X` a homomorphism of `𝒪_X`-Modules, `𝓘 = u(ℰ)`
the quasi-coherent Ideal of `𝒪_X` image of `u`, `Y` the closed sub-prescheme of `X` defined by `𝓘`. Let `X'` be the
`X`-scheme obtained by blowing up `Y`. On the other hand, let `P = P(ℰ)` be the projective bundle on `X` defined by `ℰ`
`(II, 4.1.1)`, `p : P → X` the structure morphism, `α♯ : p*(ℰ) → 𝒪_P(1)` the canonical homomorphism `(II, 4.1.5.1)` and
`ℋ` its kernel, so that one has the exact sequence*

```text
  0 → ℋ → p*(ℰ) →^{α♯} 𝒪_P(1) → 0.
```

*Finally, let `𝒥` be the quasi-coherent Ideal of `𝒪_P` image of the restriction `v : ℋ → 𝒪_P` of `p*(u)`, and let `Z`
be the closed sub-prescheme of `P` defined by `𝒥`.*

<!-- original page 202 -->

*(i) The image of `X'` by the closed immersion `j : X' → P` corresponding to the surjective homomorphism of graded
`𝒪_X`-Algebras `S_{𝒪_X}^•(ℰ) → S_{𝒪_X}^•(𝓘) → ⊕_{n ⩾ 0} 𝓘^n` `(II, 3.6.2)` is a closed sub-prescheme `j(X')` of `Z`.*

*(ii) If `ℰ` is a locally free `𝒪_X`-Module of rank `m`, `ℋ` is a locally free `𝒪_P`-Module of rank `m − 1`.*

*(iii) Suppose that `X` is locally Noetherian, that `ℰ` is locally free of finite type, that the sub-prescheme `Y` is
regular, and that at every point `x ∈ Y`, the canonical immersion `i : Y → X` is regular and of codimension equal to
`rg_{𝒪_x}(ℰ_x)` (which we shall express by saying that the **homomorphism `u` is regular at the point `x`** (cf.
chap. V)). Then the image of `X'` by the closed immersion `j : X' → P` is the closed sub-prescheme `Z` of `P`; at all
points of `X' − Y'` (with `Y'` the inverse image of `Y` in `X'`) the immersion `j` is transversally regular relatively
to `X`; finally at all points `x'` of `Y'`, `X'` is regular, the immersion `j` is regular and of codimension
`rg_{𝒪_z}(ℋ_z)` where `z = j(x')` (in other words the homomorphism `v : ℋ → 𝒪_P` is regular at `z`).*

All questions being local on `X`, one may restrict to the case where `X = Spec(A)` is affine and `ℰ = Ẽ`, where `E` is
an `A`-module. To do the verification of (i), one may moreover restrict to examining what happens in an affine open of
`P` of the form `D_+(t)`, where `t ∈ E = S_1(E)` `(II, 2.3.14)`. If one sets `S = S_A^•(E)`, one then has
`Γ(D_+(t), 𝒪_P) = S_(t)` `(II, 2.4.1)`, and `Γ(D_+(t), p*(ℰ)) = (E ⊗_A S_(t))`. If one refers to the definition of
`α♯` `(II, 2.6.2.2)`, one sees that to every element
`a = ∑_i x_i^{(1)} ⊗ ((x_i^{(2)} ⋯ x_i^{(n+1)})/t^n)` of `E ⊗_A S_(t)` (where `x_i^{(1)}` and the `x_i^{(j)}` are in
`E`) `α♯` makes correspond the element `∑_i (x_i^{(1)} x_i^{(2)} ⋯ x_i^{(n+1)})/t^{n+1}` of `(S(1))_(t)`. It is a matter
(to prove (i)) of showing that if this latter element is zero, then so is the image of
`a' = v(a) = ∑_i u(x_i^{(1)})((x_i^{(2)} ⋯ x_i^{(n+1)})/t^n)` by the canonical homomorphism
`S_(t) → (𝓘 ⊗_𝒪_X (𝒪_X/𝓘))_t` `(II, 3.6.2)`. Now, this image is none other than
`∑_i u(x_i^{(1)}) u(x_i^{(2)}) ⋯ u(x_i^{(n+1)}) / (u(t))^{n+1}` in the ring `A_{u(t)}`, that is, the product of the
element `u(t)` and the canonical image of `∑_i u(x_i^{(1)}) (x_i^{(2)} ⋯ x_i^{(n+1)})/t^{n+1}` by the algebra
homomorphism, canonical extension to `S_(t)` of the homomorphism `u : E → A` of `A`-modules. This therefore proves (i).

To establish (ii), let us note that one may suppose that `E` is a free `A`-module of rank `m`; since
`α♯ : E ⊗_A S(1) → S(1)` is surjective and `(S(1))_(t)` is a free `S_(t)`-module of rank `1`, the exact sequence

```text
  0 → H_(t) → (E ⊗_A S(1))_(t) → (S(1))_(t) → 0
```

is split, and `H_(t)` is therefore a projective `S_(t)`-module of rank `m − 1`, whence (ii).

To prove (iii), let us first examine what happens above a point `x ∈ X − Y`. Then `u : ℰ → 𝒪_X` restricted to a
neighbourhood of `x`, is surjective, so one may restrict to the case where `ℰ = 𝒪_X`, in which case `X'` is canonically
identified with `X`. To see then that the closed sub-prescheme of `P` image of `X'` is identified with `Z`, one may
restrict as at the beginning to the case where `X = Spec(A)` is affine, and moreover `E = A^m`, so that
`S = A[T_0, …, T_{m−1}]`. One may moreover, with the notations of the beginning, suppose `u(t) ≠ 0` (the values of
`t ∈ E`

<!-- original page 203 -->

verifying this condition generate the symmetric algebra `S_A^•(E)`); by changing if necessary the basis in `E`, one may
therefore suppose that `t = T_0`, and `u(T_i) = 0` for `1 ⩽ i ⩽ m − 1`, so that `S_(t)` is canonically identified with
`A[T_1, …, T_{m−1}]`. The elements of `H` are then the elements of the form `∑_α L_α T_0 T^α` of
`E ⊗_A A[T_1, …, T_{m−1}]` where the `L_α = λ_{α,0} + λ_{α,1} T_1 + ⋯ + λ_{α,m−1} T_{m−1}` must be such that
`∑_α L_α T^α = 0` in `A[T_1, …, T_{m−1}]`. One verifies easily that when the condition `λ_{α,0} = 0` is fulfilled, the
values of the `λ_{α,j}` may be taken arbitrarily for `|α| ⩾ 1`, and one may always then determine the `λ_{α,j}` for
`j ⩾ 1` so as to verify the condition `∑_α L_α T^α = 0`. The elements of the ideal `𝒥` corresponding are then the
polynomials `∑_α λ_α T^α` without constant term, and since `(S_A^•(A))_(t)` is identified with `A`, these polynomials
are exactly the elements of the kernel of the homomorphism `(S_A^•(E))_(t) → (S_A^•(A))_(t)`. One has thus proved
`(19.2.1)` that the immersion `j : X' → P` is transversally regular relatively to `X` at all points of `X' − Y'` and
that `j(X') = Z` at these points.

It remains to examine what happens above a point `x ∈ Y`. The hypothesis of "regularity" of `u` at this point made in
(iii) is equivalent (by virtue of `(19.1.1)` and `(0, 17.1.7)`) to saying that `X` is regular at the point `x` and that
the homomorphism `u_x ⊗ 1 : ℰ_x ⊗ k(x) → 𝔪_x/𝔪_x²` is injective. This being so, as the morphism `p : P → X` is smooth
`(17.3.9)`, for every `z ∈ P` above `x`, `P` is regular at the point `z` `(17.5.8)`; furthermore `(0, 17.3.3)` the
canonical homomorphism

```text
  (19.4.11.1)    (𝔪_x/𝔪_x²) ⊗_{k(x)} k(z) → 𝔪_z/𝔪_z²
```

is injective. One deduces that the homomorphism

```text
  (ℰ_x ⊗_{𝒪_{X, x}} 𝒪_{P, z}) ⊗_{𝒪_{P, z}} k(z) → 𝔪_z/𝔪_z²
```

deduced from `p*(u)` is also injective, for it is written as the composite

```text
  (ℰ_x ⊗_{𝒪_{X, x}} k(z)) → (𝔪_x/𝔪_x²) ⊗_{k(x)} k(z) → 𝔪_z/𝔪_z²
```

where the first arrow is injective by flatness and the second is the injective homomorphism `(19.4.11.1)`. As `ℋ` is
(in a neighbourhood of `z` in `P`) a direct factor of `ℰ ⊗_{𝒪_X} 𝒪_P = p*(ℰ)`, the homomorphism
`v_z ⊗ 1 : ℋ_z ⊗_{𝒪_{P, z}} k(z) → 𝔪_z/𝔪_z²` is also injective, which establishes the "regularity" of the homomorphism
`v` at the point `z`. It remains to see that the image by `j` of `X'` is identical to the closed sub-prescheme `Z`. It
will suffice for this to show on the one hand that the image by `j` of the points of `q⁻¹(x)` (where `q : X' → X` is the
structure morphism) are exactly those of `Z ∩ p⁻¹(x)` and on the other hand that at each of these points `z = j(x')`,
the surjective homomorphism `𝒪_{Z, z} → 𝒪_{X', x'}` is bijective. But, taking into account the hypothesis and
`(19.4.3)`, the restriction `Y' → Y` of `q` is smooth, and since `𝒪_{Y, x}` is supposed regular, the same holds for
`𝒪_{Y', x'}` `(17.5.8)`; furthermore, the immersion `Y' → X'` is regular `(19.4.2)`, so `𝒪_{X', x'}` is also a regular
ring `(19.1.1)`. To prove that the homomorphism `𝒪_{Z, z} → 𝒪_{X', x'}` is bijective, it suffices then to show that
the dimensions

<!-- original page 204 -->

of these two regular rings are equal `(0, 17.1.9)`. But by virtue of `(0, 17.3.3)`, this is also equivalent to the
equality of the dimensions of the two rings `𝒪_{X', x'} ⊗_{𝒪_X} k(x)` and `𝒪_{Z, z} ⊗_{𝒪_X} k(x)`, so that finally
one may replace `X'` and `Z` by the fibres `q⁻¹(x)` and `Z ∩ p⁻¹(x)` respectively, and reduce to proving that the
morphism `q⁻¹(x) → Z ∩ p⁻¹(x)` deduced from `j` is an isomorphism. Now, one may restrict here to the case where
`X = Spec(B)` with `B = 𝒪_{X, x}` and `E = B^m`, whence `S = B[T_1, …, T_m]`; furthermore, the hypothesis means that
there exists a regular system of parameters `(t_i)_{1 ⩽ i ⩽ m}` of `B` such that `u(T_i) = t_i` for `1 ⩽ i ⩽ m`
`(0, 17.1.7)`; let `𝔍` be the image of `u`; if `𝔪` is the maximal ideal of `B`, one has
`𝔍^n ⊗_B (B/𝔪) = 𝔍^n/𝔪 𝔍^n = (𝔍^n/𝔍^{n+1}) ⊗_B (B/𝔪)` and consequently `(16.9.4.1)`,
`(⊕_{n ⩾ 0} 𝔍^n) ⊗_B k(x)` is identified with `k(x)[T_1, …, T_m]`, whence it follows that the immersion
`q⁻¹(x) → p⁻¹(x)` is here an isomorphism; this implies *a fortiori* that the closed sub-prescheme `Z ∩ p⁻¹(x)` is
identical to the fibre `j(q⁻¹(x))`, and terminates the proof of `(19.4.11)`.

### 19.5. `M`-regularity criteria

We resume here, in completed form, the criteria for a sequence of elements of a ring `A` to be `M`-regular or
`M`-quasi-regular (`M` being an `A`-module), already studied in `(0, 15.1)`.

**Theorem (19.5.1).**

<!-- label: IV.19.5.1 -->

*Let `A` be a ring, `f = (f_i)_{1 ⩽ i ⩽ n}` a sequence of elements of `A`, `M` an `A`-module. Consider the following
properties:*

*a) The sequence `f` is `M`-regular.*

*b) One has `H_i(f, M) = 0` for every `i > 0` `(III, 1.1.3)`.*

*b') One has `H_1(f, M) = 0`.*

*c) The sequence `f` is `M`-quasi-regular.*

*Then one has the implications*

```text
  a) ⟹ b) ⟹ b')    and    a) ⟹ c).
```

*Set `𝔍 = ∑_{i=1}^n f_i A`. If the modules `M_i = M/(∑_{j ⩽ i} f_j M)` are separated for the `𝔍`-preadic topology (resp.
if every quotient module of a sub-module of `M` is separated for the `𝔍`-preadic topology), then one also has the
implication c) ⟹ a) (resp. b') ⟹ a)).*

The implications a) ⟹ c), and c) ⟹ a) when the `M_i` are separated for the `𝔍`-preadic topology, were already proved
`(0, 15.1.9)`, and are given only for the record. One has also shown `(III, 1.1.4 and 1.1.3.3)` that a) entails b), and
b) trivially implies b'). It therefore remains to see that when every quotient module of a sub-module of `M` is
separated for the `𝔍`-preadic topology, b') entails a). Let us reason by induction on `n`. For `n = 1`, the assertion
follows trivially from the definitions, since `H_1(f, M)` is none other than the kernel of the homothety `x ↦ f_1 x` in
`M` `(III, 1.1.1 and 1.1.2)`. Suppose then `n ⩾ 2`, and set `f' = (f_i)_{1 ⩽ i ⩽ n−1}`; with the notations of
`(III, 1.1.2)`, one has `K_•(f, M) = K_•(f_n) ⊗_A K_•(f', M)`. One therefore has `(III, 1.1.4.1)` the exact sequence

```text
  (19.5.1.1)    0 → H_0(f_n, H_1(f', M)) → H_1(f, M) → H_1(f_n, H_0(f', M)) → 0.
```

<!-- original page 205 -->

The relation `H_1(f, M) = 0` therefore implies `H_0(f_n, H_1(f', M)) = 0` and `H_1(f_n, H_0(f', M)) = 0`. The first of
these two relations means that one has `f_n H_1(f', M) = H_1(f', M)` `(III, 1.1.3.5)`. Now, by definition
`(III, 1.1.1)`, `H_1(f', M)` is isomorphic to a quotient of a sub-module `N` of `M^{n−1}`; taking on `M^{n−1}` the
filtration of the `M^j` for `j ⩽ n − 1`, on `N` the induced filtration, and on `H_1(f', M)` the quotient filtration of
that of `N`, one obtains on `H_1(f', M)` a finite filtration whose quotients are isomorphic to quotients of sub-modules
of `M`, hence are separated for the `𝔍`-preadic topology by hypothesis. As the relation `f_n H_1(f', M) = H_1(f', M)`
implies *a fortiori* `𝔍 H_1(f', M) = H_1(f', M)`, one deduces from the hypothesis and the preceding remarks that
`H_1(f', M) = 0`. The induction hypothesis then proves that the sequence `f'` is `M`-regular. On the other hand, the
relation `H_1(f_n, H_0(f', M)) = 0` means that `f_n` is `(M/(∑_{i=1}^{n−1} f_i M))`-regular, so the sequence `f` is
`M`-regular.

**Corollary (19.5.2).**

<!-- label: IV.19.5.2 -->

*Suppose that `A` is a Noetherian ring, that the `f_i` `(1 ⩽ i ⩽ n)` belong to the radical of `A`, and that `M` is an
`A`-module of finite type. Then the four conditions a), b), b'), c) of `(19.5.1)` are equivalent.*

One knows indeed `(0_I, 7.3.5)` that every `A`-module of finite type is separated for the `𝔍`-preadic topology.

**Proposition (19.5.3).**

<!-- label: IV.19.5.3 -->

*Let*

```text
  0 → M' → M → M'' → 0
```

*be an exact sequence of `A`-modules, `f = (f_i)_{1 ⩽ i ⩽ n}` an `M`-regular sequence, `𝔍 = ∑_{i=1}^n f_i A`. Consider
the following properties:*

*a) The sequence `f` is `M''`-regular.*

*b) The `𝔍`-preadic filtration of `M'` is induced on `M'` by the `𝔍`-preadic filtration of `M` (in other words, the
canonical homomorphism `gr_𝔍^•(M') → gr_𝔍^•(M)` is injective).*

*c) The canonical homomorphism `M'/(∑_{i=1}^n f_i M') → M/(∑_{i=1}^n f_i M)` is injective.*

*Then one has the implications a) ⟹ b) ⟹ c), and a) entails moreover that the sequence `f` is `M'`-regular.*

*If every quotient of a sub-module of `M''` is separated for the `𝔍`-preadic topology, the conditions a), b) and c) are
equivalent.*

It is clear that c) is a consequence of b). Let us prove that under the hypotheses of the last assertion, c) entails a):
since `f` is `M`-regular, one has, by `(19.5.1)`, `H_1(f, M) = 0`, whence an exact sequence, portion of the exact
homology sequence

```text
  0 → H_1(f, M'') → H_0(f, M') → H_0(f, M).
```

Now, condition c) expresses that the homomorphism `H_0(f, M') → H_0(f, M)` is injective `(III, 1.1.3.5)`; it therefore
amounts to `H_1(f, M'') = 0`, whence, by virtue of the separation hypothesis and `(19.5.1)`, the fact that `f` is
`M''`-regular.

Let us next show that a) entails c) and the fact that `f` is `M'`-regular, by induction on `n`. For `n = 1`, `f_1` being
`M`-regular is also `M'`-regular and property c) is none other than lemma `(3.4.1.4)`. For `n > 1`, the induction
hypothesis shows that

<!-- original page 206 -->

if one sets `f' = (f_1, …, f_{n−1})`, the sequence `f'` is `M'`-regular and one has, by virtue of c) applied to `f'`,
an exact sequence

```text
  0 → M'/(∑_{i=1}^{n−1} f_i M') → M/(∑_{i=1}^{n−1} f_i M) → M''/(∑_{i=1}^{n−1} f_i M'') → 0.
```

By hypothesis, `f_n` is `(M/(∑_{i=1}^{n−1} f_i M))`-regular and `(M''/(∑_{i=1}^{n−1} f_i M''))`-regular, so the same
reasoning shows on the one hand that `f_n` is `(M'/(∑_{i=1}^{n−1} f_i M'))`-regular, and on the other hand, by virtue of
`(3.4.1.4)`, that the sequence

```text
  0 → M'/(∑_{i=1}^n f_i M') → M/(∑_{i=1}^n f_i M) → M''/(∑_{i=1}^n f_i M'') → 0
```

is exact, whence c).

Let us finally show that a) entails b). As the sequence `f` is then `M`-regular and `M'`-regular, hence
`M`-quasi-regular and `M'`-quasi-regular, one has canonical isomorphisms

```text
  gr_𝔍^•(M') ≃ gr_𝔍^0(M')[T_1, …, T_n],    gr_𝔍^•(M) ≃ gr_𝔍^0(M)[T_1, …, T_n]
```

`(0, 15.1.7)`, and as one has seen above that `gr_𝔍^0(M') → gr_𝔍^0(M)` is injective, so is `gr_𝔍^•(M') → gr_𝔍^•(M)`.

One will note again that the equivalence of conditions a), b), c) of `(19.5.3)` is valid in particular when `A` is
Noetherian, `M''` an `A`-module of finite type and the `f_i` belong to the radical of `A`.

**(19.5.4)**

<!-- label: IV.19.5.4 -->

In the sequel of this number, we keep the notations `A`, `f`, `M`, `𝔍` of `(19.5.1)`, and we suppose moreover that `M`
is endowed with a decreasing filtration `(M_k)` formed of sub-`A`-modules, such that `M_0 = M`. Recall `(0, 15.1.5)` that
one then defines on `M` a second decreasing filtration formed of the sub-modules

```text
  M'_k = M_k + 𝔍 M_{k−1} + ⋯ + 𝔍^{k−1} M_1 + 𝔍^k M_0
```

and that, if `gr_•(M)` and `gr'_•(M)` are the graded `A`-modules associated with the filtrations `(M_k)` and `(M'_k)`
respectively, one defines a surjective graded homomorphism of degree `0` `(0, 15.1.5.2)`

```text
  ψ_M : (gr_•(M) ⊗_A (A/𝔍))[T_1, …, T_n] → gr'_•(M).
```

Recall also that one has the canonical surjective homomorphism `(0, 15.1.1.1)`

```text
  φ_{gr_•(M)} : (gr_•(M) ⊗_A (A/𝔍))[T_1, …, T_n] → gr_𝔍^•(gr_•(M)).
```

**Theorem (19.5.5).**

<!-- label: IV.19.5.5 -->

*With the notations of `(19.5.4)`, consider the following properties:*

*a) The sequence `f` is `gr_•(M)`-regular.*

*b) The canonical homomorphism `ψ_M` is bijective.*

*c) The canonical homomorphism `φ_{gr_•(M)}` is bijective (in other words `(0, 15.1.7)`, the sequence `f` is
`gr_•(M)`-quasi-regular).*

<!-- original page 207 -->

*One then has the implications*[^19.5.5-deligne]

```text
  a) ⟹ b) ⟹ c).
```

*Moreover, if, for every integer `k ⩾ 0`, the quotient modules of sub-modules of `gr_k(M)` are separated for the
`𝔍`-preadic topology (which is the case when `A` is Noetherian, the `f_i` are in the radical of `A` and the `gr_k(M)`
are `A`-modules of finite type), then the conditions a), b) and c) are equivalent.*

The implication a) ⟹ b) was proved in `(0, 15.1.8)`; the implication c) ⟹ a) under the separation hypothesis is a
particular case of `(0, 15.1.9)`. It remains to prove that b) implies c), which will be done in several steps.

We shall denote by `𝔍` the ideal `∑ f_i A` generated by the `f_i`; for every sequence `q = (q_1, …, q_n)` of `n`
integers `⩾ 0`, we shall set `|q| = ∑ q_i` and `f^q = f_1^{q_1} f_2^{q_2} ⋯ f_n^{q_n}`.

**(19.5.5.1)**

<!-- label: IV.19.5.5.1 -->

*For the map `ψ_M` to be injective (and consequently bijective), it is necessary and sufficient that for `p ⩾ 0` and
`q ⩾ 0` the following condition be verified:*

*(I_{q, p}) For every family `(x_q)_{|q| = q}` of elements of `M_p`, the relation*

```text
  ∑_{|q| = q} f^q x_q ∈ ∑_{i=1}^n 𝔍^{q−1} M_{p+1} + 𝔍^q M_{p+1}
```

*implies `x_q ∈ M_{p+1} + 𝔍 M_p` for every `q` such that `|q| = q`.*

One proceeds as in `(0, 15.1.6)` by considering the sub-`A`-module `Q_k` (resp. `Q'_k`) of terms of degree `k` in the
first (resp. second) member of `(0, 15.1.5.2)`. One endows `Q_k` with the filtration

```text
  (Q_k)_• = ⊕_{|q| ⩾ •} ((gr_{k−|q|}(M)) ⊗_A (A/𝔍)) T^q
```

and `Q'_k` with the image filtration formed by `ψ_M((Q_k)_•) = (Q'_k)_•`; it again suffices to prove that the
homomorphisms `(ψ_M)_k : gr_•(Q_k) → gr_•(Q'_k)` are injective. Now, one has

```text
  gr_•(Q_k)_i = ⊕_{|q| = i} ((M_{k−i}/M_{k−i+1}) ⊗_A (A/𝔍)) T^q = ⊕_{|q| = i} (M_{k−i}/(𝔍 M_{k−i} + M_{k−i+1})) T^q;
```

on the other hand, `(Q'_k)_i` is the image of `∑_{|q| = i} 𝔍^{|q|−1} M_{k−|q|+1}` in `M_k/M'_{k+1}`. To write that
`(ψ_M)_k` is injective therefore amounts to writing the condition `(I_{i, k−i})`; whence our assertion.

**(19.5.5.2)**

<!-- label: IV.19.5.5.2 -->

*For the sequence `f` to be `gr_•(M)`-quasi-regular, it suffices that, for `p ⩾ 0` and `q > 0`, the following condition
be verified:*

*(S_{q, p}) For every family `(x_q)_{|q| = q}` of elements of `M_p`, the relation `∑_{|q| = q} f^q x_q ∈ M_{p+q+1}`
implies `x_q ∈ M_{p+1} + 𝔍 M_p` for every `q` such that `|q| = q`.*

By definition, to say that `f` is `gr_•(M)`-quasi-regular means that, for every `p ⩾ 0` and every `q ⩾ 0`, a relation

```text
  ∑_{|q| = q} f^q x_q ∈ M_{p+q+1} + 𝔍 M_{p+q}
```

<!-- original page 208 -->

*for a family `(x_q)_{|q| = q}` of elements of `M_p` entails `x_q ∈ M_{p+1} + 𝔍 M_p` for every `q` such that `|q| = q`.*
Now, the hypothesis on the sequence `(x_q)` means that there exists a family `(y_q)_{|q| = q}` of elements of `𝔍 M_p`
such that one has `∑ f^q x_q − y_q ∈ M_{p+q+1}`. Condition `(S_{q, p})` then entails `x_q − y_q ∈ M_{p+1} + 𝔍 M_p` for
every `q` such that `|q| = q`, whence `x_q ∈ M_{p+1} + 𝔍 M_p`; one therefore sees that the conditions `(S_{q, p})` entail
that the sequence `f` is `gr_•(M)`-quasi-regular.

It therefore suffices to prove the following proposition:

**(19.5.5.3)**

<!-- label: IV.19.5.5.3 -->

*For every `r ⩾ 0`, if the conditions `(I_{q, p})` are verified for `p + q < r`, then the conditions `(S_{q, p})` are
verified for `p + q < r`.*

Let us introduce the conditions

*(A_{q, p}) For every family `(x_q)_{|q| = q}` of elements of `M_p`, the relation `∑_{|q| = q} f^q x_q ∈ M_{p+q+1}`
implies `∑_{|q| = q} f^q x_q ∈ ∑_{i=1}^n 𝔍^{q−1} M_{p+1}`.*

It is clear that the conjunction of `(A_{q, p})` and of `(I_{q, p})` implies `(S_{q, p})`. On the other hand, the
conditions `(A_{0, p})` and `(S_{0, p})` are trivial. Consequently, `(19.5.5.3)` will result from the following
proposition:

**(19.5.5.4)**

<!-- label: IV.19.5.5.4 -->

*Whatever `p ⩾ 0` and `q ⩾ 1`, the condition `(A_{q, p})` is entailed by the conjunction of the conditions
`(A_{q−1, p})`, `(I_{q−1, p+1})`, `(I_{q−2, p+2})`, …, `(I_{0, p+q})`.*

Indeed, once this proposition is proved, the conditions `(I_{q, p})` supposed verified for `p + q < r` will entail, for
each `p < r`, the condition `(A_{q, p})` for `1 ⩽ q < r − p`, by induction on `q`.

Let us therefore prove `(19.5.5.4)`. Note that condition `(A_{q, p})` is equivalent to

```text
  (19.5.5.5)    𝔍^q M_p ∩ M_{p+q+1} ⊂ ∑_{i=1}^n 𝔍^{q−1} M_{p+1}.
```

Let therefore `m ∈ 𝔍^q M_p ∩ M_{p+q+1} ⊂ 𝔍 M_p ∩ M_{p+1}`. Applying the condition `(A_{0, p+1})`, one sees that there
exists, for each `i` such that `1 ⩽ i ⩽ q`, a family `(y_q^{(i)})_{|q| = q − i}` of elements of `M_{p+i}` such that

```text
  m = ∑_{i=1}^q (∑_{|q| = q − i} f^q y_q^{(i)}).
```

Suppose that, for `1 ⩽ i < j` `(j ⩾ 1)`, one has proved that

```text
  y_q^{(i)} ∈ 𝔍 M_{p+i} + M_{p+i+1}    (for |q| = q − i).
```

One deduces by definition `(19.5.4)`

```text
  ∑_{|q| = q − j} f^q y_q^{(j)} ∈ M'_{p+q+1}.
```

Since by hypothesis `(I_{q−j, p+j})` is verified, one deduces

```text
  y_q^{(j)} ∈ 𝔍 M_{p+j} + M_{p+j+1}
```

<!-- original page 209 -->

and, by induction on `j`, one sees therefore that this condition is verified for every `j` such that `1 ⩽ j ⩽ q`. One
therefore has

```text
  m ∈ ∑_{i=1}^q 𝔍^{q−i}(𝔍 M_{p+i} + M_{p+i+1}) ⊂ ∑_{i=1}^{q+1} 𝔍^{q−i+1} M_{p+i}
```

and one has thus proved `(A_{q, p})` and terminated the proof of `(19.5.5)`.

[^19.5.5-deligne]: The proof, without separation hypothesis on the `gr_k(M)`, is due to P. Deligne, whom we thank.

**Remarks (19.5.6).**

<!-- label: IV.19.5.6 -->

*(i) The results of this number retain a meaning and are valid when, instead of an `A`-module `M` and elements
`f_i ∈ A`, one takes an object `M` of an arbitrary abelian category, and `n` endomorphisms `f_i` of `M` which commute
pairwise; the proofs adapt without difficulty to this more general case. The separation hypothesis for an `A`-module
`N` relative to the `𝔍`-preadic topology must here be replaced by the hypothesis that every sub-object of `N` contained

of `19.7`.*

*(ii) Suppose that `A` is a graded ring with degrees `⩾ 0`. If `M` (resp. each `gr_p(M)`) is a graded `A`-module with
degrees bounded below and if the `f_i` contain no homogeneous component of degree `0`, the separation hypothesis of
`(0, 15.1.9)` is ipso facto verified for `M` (resp. each `gr_p(M)`), and one therefore sees that in this case one has
the implication c) ⟹ a) in `(19.5.1)` (resp. `(19.5.5)`).*

*(iii) Recall that, without separation hypothesis, the implication c) ⟹ b) is no longer valid even for `n = 1`
`(0, 15.1.12, (iii))`.*

### 19.6. Regular sequences relative to a quotient filtered module

**(19.6.1).**

<!-- label: IV.19.6.1 -->

Let `A` be a ring, `f = (f_1, …, f_n)` a finite sequence of elements of `A`, `𝔍 = f_1 A + ⋯ + f_n A` the ideal it
generates, and consider an exact sequence of `A`-modules

```text
                       0 → R → N → M → 0
```

where one supposes that `N` is endowed with a decreasing filtration `(N_k)` formed of sub-`A`-modules, with `N_0 = N`.
One sets `R_k = R ∩ N_k` (filtration induced by `(N_k)`), `M_k = p(N_k)` (quotient filtration of `(N_k)`), and one
denotes by `gr_•(N)`, `gr_•(R)` and `gr_•(M)` the graded `A`-modules associated with these three filtrations. One sets
moreover, for every `k`,

```text
                       N'_k = N_k + 𝔍 N_{k-1} + ⋯ + 𝔍^{k-1} N_1 + 𝔍^k N_0
```

so that `M'_k = p(N'_k) = M_k + 𝔍 M_{k-1} + ⋯ + 𝔍^{k-1} M_1 + 𝔍^k M_0`; one sets moreover `R'_k = R ∩ N'_k` (filtration
induced by `(N'_k)`), and one denotes by `gr'_•(N)`, `gr'_•(M)` and `gr'_•(R)` the graded `A`-modules associated with
these three filtrations; one thus has a commutative diagram of exact sequences

```text
  (19.6.1.1)         0 ──→ gr_•(R) ──→ gr_•(N) ──→ gr_•(M) ──→ 0
                              │            │            │
                              ▼            ▼            ▼
                     0 ──→ gr'_•(R) ──→ gr'_•(N) ──→ gr'_•(M) ──→ 0
```

<!-- original page 210 -->

where the vertical arrows are the canonical homomorphisms from a graded module associated with a filtration into the
graded module associated with a coarser filtration.

One will note that, if one sets

```text
                       R''_k = R_k + 𝔍 R_{k-1} + ⋯ + 𝔍^{k-1} R_1 + 𝔍^k R_0
```

one evidently has `R''_k ⊂ R'_k`, but the filtrations `(R'_k)` and `(R''_k)` are in general distinct; one will denote
by `gr''_•(R)` the graded `A`-module associated with the filtration `(R''_k)`.

**(19.6.2).**

<!-- label: IV.19.6.2 -->

One has defined in `(0, 15.1.5.2)` the graded surjective homomorphisms of degree `0`

```text
                       ψ_N : (gr_•(N) ⊗_A (A/𝔍))[T_1, …, T_n] → gr'_•(N)
                       ψ_M : (gr_•(M) ⊗_A (A/𝔍))[T_1, …, T_n] → gr'_•(M)
                       ψ_R : (gr_•(R) ⊗_A (A/𝔍))[T_1, …, T_n] → gr''_•(R)
```

of which the first two are deduced from the last two vertical arrows of `(19.6.1.1)`.

As the filtration `(R'_k)` is finer than `(R''_k)` on `R`, one has a canonical homomorphism
`γ : gr''_•(R) → gr'_•(R)`; we shall denote by `ψ'_R` the composite homomorphism `γ ∘ ψ_R`. It then follows at once
from the definitions that one has a commutative diagram

```text
  (19.6.2.1)         (gr_•(R) ⊗_A (A/𝔍))[T_1, …, T_n]  ──ψ_R──→  gr''_•(R)
                                  │                                  │
                                  │ j'                               │ gr''_•(j)
                                  ▼                                  ▼
                     (gr_•(N) ⊗_A (A/𝔍))[T_1, …, T_n]  ──ψ_N──→  gr'_•(N)
                                  │                                  │
                                  │ p'                               │ gr'_•(p)
                                  ▼                                  ▼
                     (gr_•(M) ⊗_A (A/𝔍))[T_1, …, T_n]  ──ψ_M──→  gr'_•(M)
                                  │                                  │
                                  ▼                                  ▼
                                  0                                  0
```

where the columns are exact.

**Proposition (19.6.3).**

<!-- label: IV.19.6.3 -->

*With the preceding notation, suppose that the sequence `f` is `gr_•(N)`-regular. Consider the following properties:*

- *a) `f` is `gr_•(M)`-regular.*
- *b) `ψ_M` is bijective.*
- *c) `ψ_R` is surjective (in other words, `gr''_•(R)` is generated as a `gr'_•(A)`-module by
  `Im(gr_•(R) → gr''_•(R))`).*
- *d) `ψ'_R` is injective.*
- *d′) The homomorphism `(ψ'_R)_0 : gr_•(R) ⊗_A (A/𝔍) → gr'_•(R)` obtained by restricting `ψ'_R` to polynomials of
  degree `0` is injective.*
- *e) `ψ_R` is bijective.*
- *f) `j'` is injective.*
- *f′) The homomorphism `gr_•(R) ⊗_A (A/𝔍) → gr_•(N) ⊗_A (A/𝔍)` obtained by restricting `j'` to polynomials of
  degree `0` is injective.*
- *g) The `𝔍`-preadic filtration of `gr_•(R)` is induced by that of `gr_•(N)`.*
- *h) The filtrations `(R'_k)` and `(R''_k)` on `R` are identical.*

*One then has the following implications:*

```text
                              a) ⇒ e) ⇒ b) ⇔ c) ⇔ h)
                                    ⇕     ⇕
                                       g) ⇒ d) ⇔ d′) ⇔ f) ⇔ f′)
```

*Moreover, when every quotient module of a submodule of a `gr_k(M)` is separated for the `𝔍`-preadic topology (which
will be the case when `A` is Noetherian, the `f_i` belong to the radical of `A`, and the `gr_k(M)` are `A`-modules of
finite type), then the conditions a) through h) are all equivalent.*

Note that by `(19.5.5)`, from the hypothesis that `f` is `gr_•(N)`-regular, it follows that `ψ_N` is bijective; as
`gr'_•(j)` is injective, the equivalence of d) and f) follows from the diagram `(19.6.2.1)`, as does that of d′) and
f′); moreover, f) and f′) are trivially equivalent, which shows the equivalence of d), d′), f) and f′).

Since we already know that `ψ_R` is surjective and `ψ_N` bijective, the equivalence of b) and c) also follows from the
diagram `(19.6.2.1)` by a particular case of the five lemma.

The relation `ψ'_R = γ ∘ ψ_R` `(19.6.2)` and the fact that `ψ_R` is surjective show that condition c) is equivalent to
saying that `γ` is surjective, which is equivalent in turn to condition h); one has thus proved the equivalence of b),
c) and h).

It is trivial that e) is equivalent to the conjunction of c) and d), hence also of b) and d). Note now that a) implies
b), and that b) implies a) under the separation hypothesis `(19.5.5)`. On the other hand, the implications
a) ⇒ g) ⇒ f), and the implication f) ⇒ a) under the separation hypothesis, follow from `(19.5.3)`. One has thus
proved that a) entails e) (equivalent to the conjunction of b) and f′)), and also that all the conditions are
equivalent under the separation hypothesis. Q.E.D.

**Corollary (19.6.4).**

<!-- label: IV.19.6.4 -->

*Let `𝔎` be an ideal of `A`, `𝔏 = 𝔍 + 𝔎`. Suppose that the filtration `(N_k)` (hence also `(M_k)`) is the
`𝔎`-preadic filtration, so that the filtrations `(N'_k)` and `(M'_k)` are the `𝔏`-preadic filtrations. Consider
`gr_•(R)` (resp. `gr''_•(R)`) as a graded module over `gr_•(A)` (resp. `gr'_•(A)`).*

- *(i) Let `S` be a part of `gr_•(R)` which generates `gr_•(R)` as a `gr_•(A)`-module. Then condition c) of `(19.6.3)`
  is equivalent to the following condition:*
- *c′) The image of `S` in `gr''_•(R)` by `ψ_R` generates `gr''_•(R)` as a `gr'_•(A)`-module.*
- *(ii) Suppose that condition e) of `(19.6.3)` is satisfied, and moreover that the quotients of the `gr_k(R)` are
  separated for the `𝔍`-preadic topology (which will be the case when `A` is Noetherian, the `f_i` are in the radical
  of `A`, and the `gr_k(N)` are `A`-modules of finite type). Then, in order for a part `S` of `gr_•(R)` formed of
  homogeneous elements to generate `gr_•(R)` as a `gr_•(A)`-module, it is necessary and sufficient that its image
  by `ψ_R` generate `gr''_•(R)` as a `gr'_•(A)`-module.*

<!-- original page 212 -->

(i) Consider the homomorphism of degree `0`

```text
                       ψ_A : (gr_•(A) ⊗_A (A/𝔍))[T_1, …, T_n] → gr'_•(A) = gr_•^𝔏(A)
```

`(0, 15.1.5.2)`. The fact that this homomorphism is surjective entails that the sub-`gr'_•(A)`-algebra of `gr''_•(R)`
generated by `S' = ψ_R(S)` is none other than `Im(ψ_R)`, whence the equivalence of c) and c′).

(ii) Since e) is satisfied, the same is true of c), and by virtue of (i), it remains to prove the sufficiency of the
condition in the statement. Now, as `ψ_R` is bijective, saying that `S'` generates `gr''_•(R)` regarded as a
`gr'_•(A)`-module is equivalent to saying that `S` generates `(gr_•(R) ⊗_A (A/𝔍))[T_1, …, T_n]` as a `gr_•(A)`-module,
and by virtue of the surjectivity of `ψ_A`, one may, in this statement, replace the ring `gr_•(A)` by
`(gr_•(A) ⊗_A (A/𝔍))[T_1, …, T_n]`. It evidently amounts to the same to say that `S` generates `gr_•(R) ⊗_A (A/𝔍)` as
a module over the ring `gr_•(A) ⊗_A (A/𝔍)`, or equivalently as a `gr_•(A)`-module. Now, if `T` is the graded
sub-`gr_•(A)`-module of `gr_•(R)` generated by `S`, and if one sets `T' = gr_•(R)/T`, one has by hypothesis
`T'/𝔍 T' = 0`, that is `𝔍 T' = T'`; but `T'` is a graded module, and each `T'_p` is a quotient of `gr_p(R)`, hence
separated for the `𝔍`-preadic topology; the hypothesis `𝔍 T'_p = T'_p` therefore entails `T'_p = 0` for every
integer `p`, hence `T' = 0`, which completes the proof of the corollary.

### 19.7. Hironaka's normal flatness criterion

**Theorem (19.7.1) (Hironaka).**

<!-- label: IV.19.7.1 -->

*Let `A` be a ring, `𝔎` an ideal of `A`, `f = (f_1, …, f_n)` a sequence of elements of `A` which is `(A/𝔎)`-regular,
`𝔍 = f_1 A + ⋯ + f_n A` the ideal generated by `f`, `𝔏 = 𝔍 + 𝔎`, `M` an `A`-module.*

*One has in this case `(gr_𝔍^•(M)) ⊗_A (A/𝔎) = (gr_𝔍^•(M)) ⊗_A (A/𝔏) = (gr_𝔏^•(M)) ⊗_A (A/𝔏)`, because
`(𝔍^n M / 𝔍^{n+1} M) ⊗_A (A/𝔎) = 𝔍^n M / (𝔍^{n+1} + 𝔎 𝔍^n) M = 𝔏^n M / (𝔏^{n+1} + 𝔎 𝔏^n) M`, the last equality
following from Bourbaki, *Alg.*, chap. II, 3rd ed., §3, n° 7, cor. 2 of prop. 6.*

*Consider the following conditions:*

- *a) `gr_𝔏^•(M)` is a flat `(A/𝔏)`-module.*
- *b) `gr_𝔍^•(M)` is a flat `(A/𝔎)`-module, and the canonical homomorphism `(0, 15.1.5.2)`*

```text
                       ψ_M : ((gr_𝔍^•(M)) ⊗_A (A/𝔏))[T_1, …, T_n] → gr_𝔏^•(M)
```

  *is bijective.*

- *c) `(gr_𝔍^•(M)) ⊗_A (A/𝔏)` is a flat `(A/𝔏)`-module, and the sequence `f` is `(gr_𝔍^•(M))`-regular.*
- *d) `gr_𝔏^•(M)` is a flat `(A/𝔏)`-module, and for every `𝔭 ∈ Ass_A(A/𝔎)`, `(gr_𝔎^•(M))_𝔭` is a flat
  `(A_𝔭/𝔎_𝔭)`-module.*

*One then has the implications*

```text
                              a) ⇒ c) ⇒ b)
                                       ⇑
                                       d)
```

*When every quotient of a submodule of a `gr_𝔍^k(M)` `(k ≥ 0)` is ideally separated for `𝔍` (Bourbaki, *Alg. comm.*,
chap. III, §5, n° 1), the conditions a), b) and c) are equivalent.*

<!-- original page 213 -->

*Finally, when `A` is Noetherian, the `f_i` belong to the radical of `A`, the sequence `f` is `gr_𝔎^•(A)`-regular, and
`M` is an `A`-module of finite type, the conditions a), b), c), d) are equivalent.*

The implication a) ⇒ c) is immediate `(0, 15.1.13)`. If c) is satisfied, it follows from `(19.5.5)` that `ψ_M` is
bijective; moreover, `gr_𝔍^•(M) ⊗_A (A/𝔏) = gr_𝔍^•(M) ⊗_A (A/𝔎)` is a flat `(A/𝔏)`-module, hence so is
`(gr_𝔍^•(M) ⊗_A (A/𝔏))[T_1, …, T_n]`, and consequently so is `gr_𝔏^•(M)` since `ψ_M` is an `(A/𝔏)`-isomorphism; thus
c) entails b). The fact that a) implies b) shows at once that a) also implies d).

When every quotient of a `gr_𝔍^k(M)` is ideally separated for `𝔍`, it follows from `(19.5.5)` that condition b)
entails that `f` is `(gr_𝔍^•(M))`-regular; moreover, since `(gr_𝔍^•(M) ⊗_A (A/𝔏))[T_1, …, T_n]`, isomorphic to
`gr_𝔏^•(M)`, is then a flat `(A/𝔏)`-module, the same is true of `gr_𝔍^•(M) ⊗_A (A/𝔏)`, which is a direct factor of
it; this proves therefore that b) then entails c). On the other hand, under the same hypothesis, c) entails a) by
virtue of `(0, 15.1.21)` (where one may replace the Noetherian hypothesis by the hypothesis that `M` is ideally
separated for `𝔍`, by virtue of `(III, 10.2.1)`).

It remains therefore to prove that when `A` is Noetherian, `M` of finite type and the `f_i` belong to the radical
of `A`, d) entails a).

By hypothesis there exists a free `A`-module of finite type `N` and an exact sequence `0 → R → N → M → 0`. It suffices
to prove that d) entails b), since every quotient of a submodule of a `gr_𝔍^k(M)` is then ideally separated for `𝔍`,
by virtue of the fact that `gr_𝔍^k(M)` is an `A`-module of finite type, that `𝔍` is contained in the radical of `A`,
and that `A` is Noetherian (Bourbaki, *Alg. comm.*, chap. III, §5, n° 1); in other words, the question is to see
that `ψ_M` is bijective.

As by hypothesis the sequence `f` is `gr_𝔎^•(A)`-regular, it is also `gr_𝔎^•(N)`-regular; one may therefore
apply `(19.6.3)`, for every quotient module of a submodule of a `gr_𝔎^k(M)` is then separated for the `𝔍`-preadic
topology, since the `f_i` belong to the radical of `A` by hypothesis. The commutative diagram `(19.6.2.1)` is
therefore written here

```text
                                                          0
                                                          │
                                                          ▼
  (19.7.1.1)   (gr'_•(R, N) ⊗_{A/𝔎} (A/𝔏))[T_1, …, T_n] ──ψ_R──→ gr''_•(R, N)
                              │                                       │
                              ▼                                       ▼
               (gr'_•(N) ⊗_{A/𝔎} (A/𝔏))[T_1, …, T_n]  ──ψ_N──→  gr'_•(N)
                              │                                       │
                              ▼                                       ▼
               (gr'_•(M) ⊗_{A/𝔎} (A/𝔏))[T_1, …, T_n]  ──ψ_M──→  gr'_•(M)
                              │                                       │
                              ▼                                       ▼
                              0                                       0
```

where `gr'_•(R, N)` (resp. `gr''_•(R, N)`) is the graded module associated with `R` for the filtration of the
`R ∩ 𝔎^k N` (resp. `R ∩ 𝔏^k N`); recall that in this diagram the columns are exact and that `ψ_N`

<!-- original page 214 -->

is bijective. The question is, by virtue of `(19.6.3)`, to prove that `ψ_R` is surjective, which will be done in
several steps.

**(19.7.1.2).**

<!-- label: IV.19.7.1.2 -->

Set `B = gr_𝔎^•(A) ⊗_A (A/𝔏)`, `P = gr_𝔎^•(N) ⊗_A (A/𝔏)`, `Q = gr''_•(R, N)`; since `ψ_N` is bijective,
`P[T_1, …, T_n]` is identified with `gr'_•(N)`, and `Q` with a sub-`B[T_1, …, T_n]`-module of `P[T_1, …, T_n]`; we
shall first see that one has

```text
  (19.7.1.3)                  Q = (Q ∩ P)[T_1, …, T_n].
```

For this, set `Z = Q / ((Q ∩ P)[T_1, …, T_n])`; this is a sub-`B[T_1, …, T_n]`-module of
`P[T_1, …, T_n] / ((Q ∩ P)[T_1, …, T_n]) = (P/(Q ∩ P))[T_1, …, T_n]`. As an `(A/𝔏)`-module, it is therefore
isomorphic to a submodule of a direct sum of `(A/𝔏)`-modules isomorphic to `P/(Q ∩ P) = (P + Q)/Q`. But `(P + Q)/Q` is
an `(A/𝔏)`-module isomorphic to a submodule of `P[T_1, …, T_n]/Q`, which is none other than `gr'_•(M)` by virtue of
the diagram `(19.7.1.1)`. One has therefore

```text
  (19.7.1.4)                  Ass_A(Z) ⊂ Ass_A(gr'_•(M)).
```

But each of the `gr_𝔏^k(M)` is, by hypothesis, a flat `(A/𝔏)`-module of finite type, hence projective since `A/𝔏` is
Noetherian, and one has consequently `Ass_A(gr_𝔏^•(M)) ⊂ Ass_A(A/𝔏)`. To see that `Z = 0`, it suffices therefore
(Bourbaki, *Alg. comm.*, chap. IV, §1, n° 1, cor. 1 of prop. 2 and n° 3, prop. 7) to see that for every
`𝔮 ∈ Ass_{A/𝔎}(A/𝔏)`, one has `Z_𝔮 = 0`. But the ideals of `Ass_{A/𝔎}(A/𝔏)` are the ideals of the form `𝔭/(𝔎/𝔎)`,
where `𝔭 ∈ Ass_A(A/𝔏)`, and it amounts therefore to the same to see that `Z_𝔭 = 0` for every `𝔭 ∈ Ass_A(A/𝔏)`. Now,
if `𝔭 = 𝔯/𝔎`, where `𝔯` is a prime ideal of `A`, one has `(gr_𝔍^•(M))_𝔭 = gr_𝔍^•(M_𝔯)` and `(A/𝔎)_𝔭 = A_𝔯/𝔎_𝔯`, and
the images of the `f_i` in `A_𝔯` form a `gr_𝔎^•(A_𝔯)`-regular sequence `(0, 15.1.14)`; applying to `A_𝔯`, `𝔎_𝔯`
and `M_𝔯` the implication a) ⇒ b) of the statement, one sees that the hypothesis d) entails that `ψ_M` is
bijective, hence also `ψ_R` by virtue of `(19.6.3)`; in other words, `Q_𝔭 = Q'_𝔭[T_1, …, T_n]`, where one has
set `Q' = gr'_•(R, N) ⊗_A (A/𝔏)`, which is here a sub-`B`-module of `P`; in particular `Q'_𝔭 = Q_𝔭 ∩ P_𝔭`, hence
finally one has indeed `Z_𝔯 = Z_𝔭 = 0`, which completes the proof of `(19.7.1.3)`.

**(19.7.1.5).**

<!-- label: IV.19.7.1.5 -->

By virtue of `(19.7.1.3)`, to prove that `ψ_R` is surjective, it suffices to show that every homogeneous element of
degree `m` of `Q ∩ P` is the image of an element of the same degree of `gr'_•(R, N) ⊗_A (A/𝔏)`. Now, the elements of
degree `m` of `P` are identified (by `ψ_N`) with the elements of `gr_𝔎^m(N)` belonging to
`(𝔎^m N + 𝔏^{m+1} N) / 𝔏^{m+1} N`; those which are images of elements of `gr'_•(R, N) ⊗_A (A/𝔏)` are identified with
the elements of `gr_𝔎^m(N)` belonging to `((R ∩ 𝔎^m N) + 𝔏^{m+1} N) / 𝔏^{m+1} N`. It will therefore suffice in the
end to prove, for every integer `m > 0`, the inclusion

```text
                  ((R ∩ 𝔏^m N) + 𝔏^{m+1} N) ∩ (𝔎^m N + 𝔏^{m+1} N) ⊂ (R ∩ 𝔎^m N) + 𝔏^{m+1} N.
```

As `𝔎 ⊂ 𝔏`, one verifies at once that the first member of this relation is equal to
`(R ∩ (𝔎^m N + 𝔏^{m+1} N)) + 𝔏^{m+1} N`, so that everything reduces to proving

```text
  (19.7.1.6)              R ∩ (𝔎^m N + 𝔏^{m+1} N) ⊂ (R ∩ 𝔎^m N) + 𝔏^{m+1} N.
```

<!-- original page 215 -->

We shall proceed by induction on `m`, supposing therefore `(19.7.1.6)` satisfied when one replaces `m` by an
integer `m' < m`. We shall consider on the other hand, for `m` fixed and an integer `d ≥ 0`, the relation

```text
  (*_d)                  R ∩ (𝔎^m N + 𝔏^{m+1} N) ⊂ (R ∩ 𝔎^m N ∩ 𝔏^d N) + 𝔏^{m+1} N.
```

It is clear that it suffices to prove `(*_d)` for `d = m`, and on the other hand `(*_0)` is trivially true. We shall
prove `(*_d)` by induction on `d`; in other words, we suppose, for a `d ≥ 0` fixed (with `d < m`), that `(*_d)` is
true, and we want to prove

```text
  (*_{d+1})              R ∩ (𝔎^m N + 𝔏^{m+1} N) ⊂ (R ∩ 𝔎^m N ∩ 𝔏^{d+1} N) + 𝔏^{m+1} N.
```

Consider for this, for an `h ≥ 0`, the relation

```text
  (**_{d+1, h})  R ∩ (𝔎^m N + 𝔏^{m+1} N) ⊂ (R ∩ (𝔏^{d+1} N + 𝔍^h 𝔎^d N) ∩ 𝔎^m N) + 𝔏^{m+1} N.
```

The hypothesis `(*_d)` implies that `(**_{d+1, 0})` is true. We shall prove by induction on `h` that `(**_{d+1, h})`
is true for every `h ≥ 0`. But one has the

**Lemma (19.7.1.7).**

<!-- label: IV.19.7.1.7 -->

*Let `A` be a Noetherian ring, `N` an `A`-module of finite type, `E`, `F` two submodules of `N`, `𝔏` an ideal of `A`.
For every `k > 0` there exists `h > 0` such that*

```text
                       E ∩ (F + 𝔏^h N) ⊂ E ∩ F + 𝔏^k N.
```

Indeed, let `φ : N → N/(E ∩ F) = N_1` be the canonical homomorphism, and set `E_1 = φ(E)`, `F_1 = φ(F)`, so
that `E_1 ∩ F_1 = 0` and `φ(E ∩ (F + 𝔏^h N)) = E_1 ∩ (F_1 + 𝔏^h N_1)`. One knows `(0_I, 7.3.2)` that there exists `h`
such that `(E_1 + F_1) ∩ 𝔏^h N_1 ⊂ 𝔏^k (E_1 + F_1)`; if `h` is so chosen and if `x_1 ∈ E_1` is such that there
exists `y_1 ∈ F_1` for which `x_1 − y_1 ∈ 𝔏^h N_1`, one deduces from the foregoing that there
exist `u_1 ∈ 𝔏^k E_1` and `v_1 ∈ 𝔏^k F_1` such that `x_1 = u_1` and `y_1 = v_1`; hence
`E_1 ∩ (F_1 + 𝔏^h N_1) ⊂ 𝔏^k N_1`, which proves the lemma.

It suffices then to apply this lemma with `E = R ∩ 𝔎^m N`, `F = 𝔏^{d+1} N` and to take `k = m + 1` to deduce that,
with the corresponding value of `h`, `(**_{d+1, h})` entails `(*_{d+1})`.

We suppose therefore in all that follows that `(**_{d+1, h})` is satisfied.

**(19.7.1.8). Proof of `(**_{d+1, h+1})`: First case: `h ≤ m − d`.**

<!-- label: IV.19.7.1.8 -->

Start therefore from an element

```text
  (19.7.1.9)              g ∈ R ∩ (𝔏^{d+1} N + 𝔍^h 𝔎^d N) ∩ 𝔎^m N
```

congruent mod. `𝔏^{m+1} N` to an element `g_1 ∈ R ∩ (𝔎^m N + 𝔏^{m+1} N)`; one has therefore

```text
  (19.7.1.10)             g ∈ 𝔎^m N + 𝔏^{m+1} N.
```

It will suffice to prove that `g` is also congruent mod. `𝔏^{m+1} N` to an element
`g_2 ∈ R ∩ (𝔏^{d+1} N + 𝔍^{h+1} 𝔎^d N)`, for that will entail `g_2 ∈ 𝔎^m N`, since one will have `g_2 ∈ 𝔎^d N`
and `g_2 − g ∈ 𝔏^{m+1} N`. In the case `h ≤ m − d` which we are considering, it suffices to demonstrate the relation

```text
  (19.7.1.11)            (𝔏^{d+1} N + 𝔍^h 𝔎^d N) ∩ 𝔎^m N ⊂ 𝔍^{m-d+1} 𝔎^d N + 𝔏^{m+1} N
```

<!-- original page 216 -->

for the relations `(19.7.1.9)` and `(19.7.1.10)` entail that `g` belongs to the first member of `(19.7.1.11)`,
and as `h ≤ m − d`, one has `𝔍^{m-d+1} ⊂ 𝔍^{h+1}`, which will establish `(**_{d+1, h+1})`. Moreover one
has `𝔎^d N ⊂ 𝔏^d N`, and the relation

```text
  (19.7.1.12)            𝔍^a N ∩ 𝔏^b N ⊂ 𝔏^{a+b-d} N
```

will entail `(19.7.1.11)`. As `N` is a free `A`-module of finite type, one sees that it therefore suffices to prove

```text
  (19.7.1.13)            𝔏^{m+1} ∩ 𝔎^d ⊂ 𝔍^{m-d+1} 𝔎^d.
```

It will suffice to prove that, in general, for `d < m`, one has

```text
  (19.7.1.14)            𝔏^{m+1} ∩ 𝔎^d ⊂ 𝔍^{m-d+1} 𝔎^d + 𝔎^{d+1}.
```

Indeed, as `𝔎 ⊂ 𝔏`, hence `𝔍^{m-d+1} 𝔎^d ⊂ 𝔏^{m+1}`, the preceding relation will entail

```text
                       𝔏^{m+1} ∩ 𝔎^{d+1} ⊂ 𝔍^{m-d} 𝔎^{d+1} + 𝔎^{d+2}
```

and by induction on `d ≤ m`, one will conclude

```text
                       𝔏^{m+1} ∩ 𝔎^d ⊂ 𝔍^{m-d+1} 𝔎^d + 𝔎^{m+1}
```

whence `(19.7.1.13)`, since `𝔎^{m+1} ⊂ 𝔍^{m-d+1} 𝔎^{m-d+1}`.

As `𝔏 = 𝔍 + 𝔎`, the relation `(19.7.1.14)` is also written

```text
  (19.7.1.15)        (𝔍^{m+1} + 𝔍^m 𝔎 + ⋯ + 𝔍 𝔎^m + 𝔎^{m+1}) ∩ 𝔎^d ⊂
                              𝔍^{m-d+1} 𝔎^d + 𝔍^{m-d} 𝔎^{d+1} + ⋯ + 𝔍 𝔎^m + 𝔎^{m+1} = 𝔍^{m-d+1} 𝔎^d + 𝔎^{d+1}.
```

We shall see that this inclusion is itself a consequence of

```text
  (19.7.1.16)            𝔍^a 𝔎^b ∩ 𝔎^{a+1} ⊂ 𝔍^{a+1} 𝔎^{b-1}
```

valid for `a ≥ 0`, `b ≥ 1`. Indeed, to prove `(19.7.1.15)` it suffices to show that for `0 < q ≤ d`, one has

```text
  (19.7.1.17)        (𝔍^{m+1} + 𝔍^m 𝔎 + ⋯ + 𝔍^{m-q+1} 𝔎^{q-1}) ∩ 𝔎^d ⊂
                              𝔍^{m+1-q} 𝔎^{q-1} 𝔎^{d-q+1} + ⋯ + 𝔎^{m+1} ⊂ 𝔍^{m+1-q} 𝔎^d
```

for, taking `q = d`, this will entail `(19.7.1.15)`. Now, to prove `(19.7.1.17)`, it suffices to proceed by induction
on `q` supposing the relation true for `q ≤ d`; an element of the first member is written, by hypothesis,
as `y + z` with `y ∈ 𝔍^{m+1-q} 𝔎^q`, `z ∈ 𝔍^{m-q} 𝔎^{q+1} + ⋯ + 𝔎^{m+1}`; as `y + z ∈ 𝔎^d ⊂ 𝔎^{q+1}`, one deduces
that `y ∈ 𝔎^{q+1}`, since `z ∈ 𝔎^{q+1}`; taking account of `(19.7.1.16)`, one has `y ∈ 𝔍^{m-q} 𝔎^{q+1}`, hence

```text
                       y + z ∈ 𝔍^{m-q} 𝔎^{q+1} + ⋯ + 𝔍 𝔎^m + 𝔎^{m+1}, and y + z ∈ 𝔎^d
```

by hypothesis, which proves `(19.7.1.17)` where `q` has been replaced by `q + 1`.

It remains then to establish `(19.7.1.16)`. An element of the first member is written
`a_1 f_1 + ⋯ + a_n f_n`, where the `a_i` belong to `𝔍^{a-1} 𝔎^b`; taking account of the fact that the
sequence `f` is `(A/𝔎^{b+1})`-regular by hypothesis, hence also `(A/𝔎^{b+1})`-quasi-regular `(0, 15.1.9)`, it follows
from the definition `(0, 15.1.7)` that the relation `a_1 f_1 + ⋯ + a_n f_n ∈ 𝔎^{a+1}`, where
the `a_i ∈ 𝔍^a`, entails necessarily `a_i ∈ 𝔎^{a+1}`, hence `a_i ∈ 𝔍^{a-1} 𝔎^b ∩ 𝔎^{a+1}`; it suffices to reason by

<!-- original page 217 -->

induction on `b` for `b ≥ 1` (the case `b = 1` being trivial) to complete the proof of `(19.7.1.16)` and consequently
also of `(**_{d+1, h+1})` for `h ≤ m − d`.

**(19.7.1.18). Proof of `(**_{d+1, h+1})`: Second case: `h > m − d`.**

<!-- label: IV.19.7.1.18 -->

We shall first demonstrate that, for every `h ≥ 0`, one has

```text
  (19.7.1.19)        R ∩ (𝔏^{d+1} N + 𝔍^h 𝔎^d N) ⊂ 𝔍^h (R ∩ (𝔎^{d+1} N + 𝔍 𝔎^d N)) + 𝔏^{d+h+1} N.
```

Consider for this an element `g` of the first member of `(19.7.1.19)`. Note that one has
`𝔏^{d+1} N + 𝔍^h 𝔎^d N = 𝔍^{d+1} N + 𝔎^{d+1} N` since `𝔏 = 𝔎 + 𝔍`; we shall consider the class `ẑ` of `g` in
`gr_𝔍^h(gr_𝔎^d(N))`, which, by virtue of `(19.5.5)` and the hypothesis on `f`, is identified with a
sub-`(A/𝔏)`-module of `gr_𝔍^h(N)`. From the fact that `g ∈ R`, we shall show that one has even (with the foregoing
identification)

```text
  (19.7.1.20)            ẑ ∈ gr_𝔍^h(R, N).
```

Indeed, one has noted, in the proof of `(19.7.1.3)`, that for every prime ideal `𝔯` of `A` such
that `𝔭 = 𝔯/𝔎 ∈ Ass_A(A/𝔏)`, the map `ψ_R` is bijective, and consequently the relation `(19.7.1.6)`, where one
replaces all the modules by their localizations at `𝔯`, is true. As `g` belongs to `R ∩ (𝔎^m N + 𝔏^{m+1} N)`, its
image `g/1` in `R_𝔯` belongs to `(R_𝔯 ∩ 𝔎^m N_𝔯) + 𝔏^{m+1} N_𝔯`, hence the image `ẑ/1` of `ẑ` in `gr_𝔍^h(N_𝔯)`
belongs to `gr_𝔍^h(R_𝔯, N_𝔯) = (gr'_•(R, N))_𝔯 = (gr''_•(R, N))_𝔯`, where `𝔮 = 𝔯/𝔏`. In other words the image of `ẑ`
by the canonical map `(gr_𝔍^h(N))_𝔮 → (gr_𝔍^h(M))_𝔮` is zero for every `𝔮 ∈ Ass_{A/𝔏}(A/𝔏)`. As one has seen in the
proof of `(19.7.1.3)` that `Ass_{A/𝔏}(gr_𝔍^h(M)) ⊂ Ass_A(A/𝔏)`, one deduces (Bourbaki, *Alg. comm.*, chap. IV, §1,
n° 1, cor. 1 of prop. 2 and n° 3, prop. 7) that the image of `ẑ` by the canonical map
`gr_𝔍^h(N) → gr_𝔍^h(M)` is zero, that is, one has the relation `(19.7.1.20)`.

This being so, one may write by definition

```text
  (19.7.1.21)            ẑ = Σ_{|p| = h} c_p f^p mod. 𝔏^{h+1} N
```

where one sets as usual `p = (p_1, …, p_n)`, `f^p = f_1^{p_1} f_2^{p_2} ⋯ f_n^{p_n}`, `|p| = p_1 + ⋯ + p_n`, and
where `c_p ∈ 𝔎^d N`. Moreover, since `ψ_N` identifies `gr'_•(N)` with `(gr_𝔎^•(N) ⊗_A (A/𝔏))[T_1, …, T_n]`,
the `c_p` are determined mod. `𝔎^{d+1} N + 𝔏 N`, and if `c̄_p` is the class of `c_p` mod. `𝔎^{d+1} N + 𝔏 N`, one has,
by the preceding identification,

```text
  (19.7.1.22)            ẑ = Σ c̄_p f^p.
```

Using `(19.7.1.3)`, one deduces that one has necessarily, for every `p` such that `|p| = h` (`c̄_p` being this
time identified by `ψ_R` with an element of `gr_𝔎^d(N)`)

```text
  (19.7.1.23)            c̄_p ∈ gr_𝔎^d(R, N).
```

But since `d < m`, the hypothesis that `(*_d)` is true implies that `c̄_p` belongs to the image of `ψ_R`, hence one
may suppose that `c_p ∈ (R ∩ 𝔎^d N) + 𝔎^{d+1} N + 𝔏 N`. The relation `(19.7.1.21)` then gives

```text
                       ẑ ∈ 𝔍^h (R ∩ 𝔎^d N) + 𝔍^h 𝔎^{d+1} N + 𝔏^{h+1} N
```

and since `𝔍 ⊂ 𝔏`, this proves `(19.7.1.19)`.

<!-- original page 218 -->

In particular, one may therefore write

```text
                       g = t + g_2
```

with `t ∈ 𝔍^h (R ∩ 𝔎^d N)` and `g_2 ∈ 𝔎^{d+1} N + 𝔏^{d+h+1} N`; moreover, as `t ∈ R` and `g ∈ R`, one has `g_2 ∈ R`.
On the other hand, using the hypothesis `h ≥ m − d + 1`, one sees that `t ∈ 𝔏^{m+1} N`. The element `g_2` therefore
answers to all the conditions stated at the beginning of `(19.7.1.8)`, which completes the proof of `(19.7.1)`.

**Corollary (19.7.3).**

<!-- label: IV.19.7.3 -->

*Let `A` be a Noetherian local ring, `𝔪` its maximal ideal, `𝔎` an ideal of `A` such that the ring `A/𝔎` is regular
and of dimension `n`, `M` an `A`-module of finite type. The following conditions are equivalent:*

- *a) `gr_𝔎^•(M)` is a flat `(A/𝔎)`-module.*
- *b) If `P(T)` and `Q(T)` are the Poincaré series of the `(A/𝔪)`-graded modules `gr_𝔪^•(M)` and
  `gr_𝔎^•(M) ⊗_A (A/𝔪)`, one has*

```text
  (19.7.3.1)             Q(T) = P(T)(1 − T)^n.
```

Let `f = (f_1, …, f_n)` be a sequence of elements of `A` such that the images of the `f_i` in `A/𝔎` form a regular
system of parameters of `A/𝔎` `(0, 17.1.6)`, so that `f` is an `(A/𝔎)`-regular sequence; moreover, if `𝔍` is the
ideal generated by `f`, one has `𝔍 + 𝔎 = 𝔪`, and as `A/𝔪` is a field, `gr_𝔎^•(M)` is a flat `(A/𝔪)`-module. As `A` is
Noetherian and `M` an `A`-module of finite type, one may apply the equivalence of a) and b) in `(19.7.1)`. Moreover,
as `A/𝔪` is a field, and `ψ_M` is surjective, the fact that `ψ_M` is bijective is equivalent to saying that for
every `h ≥ 0`, `gr_𝔪^h(M)` and the submodule of elements of degree `h` of `(gr_𝔎^•(M) ⊗_A (A/𝔪))[T_1, …, T_n]` have
the same rank over `A/𝔪`. But for the second of these modules, the rank in question is evidently equal to

```text
                       Σ_{i+j = h} binom(n + j − 1, j) rank_{A/𝔪}(gr_𝔎^i(M) ⊗_A (A/𝔪)).
```

As `(1 − T)^{-n} = Σ_j binom(n + j − 1, j) T^j`, this proves that the relation `(19.7.3.1)` is necessary and
sufficient for `ψ_M` to be bijective.

**Corollary (19.7.4).**

<!-- label: IV.19.7.4 -->

*Let `X` be a locally Noetherian prescheme, `Y` a regular and connected closed subprescheme of `X`, `ℱ` a coherent
`𝒪_X`-Module normally flat `(6.10.1)` along `Y`. Then there exists a formal series `R ∈ ℚ[[T]]` (independent of
`x ∈ Y`) such that, for every `x ∈ Y`, the Poincaré series of `gr_𝔪^•(ℱ_x)` is given by the formula*

```text
  (19.7.4.1)             P_{ℱ(x)}(T) = R(T)(1 − T)^{-n}    where n = dim(𝒪_x).
```

<!-- original page 219 -->

Let `𝒥` be the coherent Ideal of `𝒪_X` defining `Y`; the hypothesis on `ℱ` signifies that `gr_𝒥^•(ℱ)` is a
flat `𝒪_Y`-Module. One may therefore, for every `x ∈ Y`, apply `(19.7.3)` taking `A = 𝒪_{X, x}`, `𝔎 = 𝒥_x`,
`M = ℱ_x`; the hypothesis a) being fulfilled by hypothesis, the Poincaré series `P_{ℱ(x)}(T)` is therefore equal
to `R_x(T)(1 − T)^{-n}`, where `R_x(T)` is the Poincaré series of `gr_𝒥^•(ℱ) ⊗_{𝒪_Y} k(x)`. Now, each of
the `𝒪_Y`-Modules `𝒥^k ℱ / 𝒥^{k+1} ℱ` is flat and coherent by hypothesis, hence locally free `(2.1.12)`, and
since `Y` is supposed connected, each `𝒥^k ℱ / 𝒥^{k+1} ℱ` is of constant rank on `Y`, which proves that the Poincaré
series `R_x(T)` is independent of `x ∈ Y`.

**Corollary (19.7.5).**

<!-- label: IV.19.7.5 -->

*Let `X` be a locally Noetherian prescheme, `Y` a closed subprescheme of `X`, `Z` a closed subprescheme of `Y`; one
supposes `Y` and `Z` regular. Let `ℱ` be a coherent `𝒪_X`-Module.*

*(i) If `ℱ` is normally flat along `Y` at the points of `Z` (cf. `(11.3.4)` for the definition of normal flatness at
a point), then `ℱ` is normally flat along `Z`.*

*(ii) Suppose moreover that `Z` is connected and that `𝒪_X` is normally flat along `Y` at the points of `Z` (which
will hold in particular if `X` is regular at the points of `Z`). If `ℱ` is normally flat along `Z` and if there
exists a point `z ∈ Z` such that `ℱ` is normally flat along `Y` at the point `z`, then `ℱ` is normally flat along `Y`
at the points of `Z`.*

Let `𝒥_Y` and `𝒥_Z` be the coherent Ideals of `𝒪_X` defining respectively `Y` and `Z`. For every point `z ∈ Z`, the
canonical immersion `Z → Y` is regular at the point `z`, since `Y` and `Z` are regular at that point `(19.1.1)`; in
other words, there exists a sequence `(f_i)_{1 ≤ i ≤ n}` of elements of `𝒥_{Y, z}` which is `𝒪_{Z, z}`-regular and
is such that `𝒥_{Z, z} = 𝒥_{Y, z} + Σ f_i 𝒪_{X, z}`.

(i) The hypothesis signifies that `gr_{𝒥_Y}^•(ℱ_z)` is a flat `𝒪_{Y, z}`-module; it therefore entails that
`gr_{𝒥_Z}^•(ℱ_z)` is a flat `𝒪_{Z, z}`-module, by virtue of the fact that in `(19.7.1)`, a) entails b).

(ii) The supplementary hypothesis on `X` entails that the sequence `(f_i)` is `gr_{𝒥_Y}^•(ℱ_z)`-regular at every
point `z ∈ Z` `(19.7.2)`. On the other hand, `Z` being regular and connected, is integral, and if `gr_{𝒥_Y}^•(ℱ_z)`
is `𝒪_{Y, z}`-flat at a point `z ∈ Z`, then, as the generic point `ξ` of `Z` is a generization of `z`, one concludes
that `gr_{𝒥_Y}^•(ℱ_ξ)` is `𝒪_{Y, ξ}`-flat `(0_I, 6.3.1)`. One deduces that if at every point `z'` of `Z`,
`gr_{𝒥_Y}^•(ℱ_{z'})` is a flat `𝒪_{Y, z'}`-module, then `gr_{𝒥_Y}^•(ℱ_z)` is a flat `𝒪_{Y, z}`-module, for one may
apply the equivalence of a) and d) in `(19.7.1)`.

### 19.8. Properties of passage to projective limit

In this number, the notation and conventions on projective limits are those of `(8.5.1)` and `(8.8.1)`.

**Proposition (19.8.1).**

<!-- label: IV.19.8.1 -->

*Suppose that the transition morphisms `S_μ → S_λ` (`λ ≤ μ`) are flat, and moreover that one of the two following
hypotheses is satisfied:*

- *1° The preschemes `S_λ` are locally Noetherian.*
- *2° The transition morphisms `S_μ → S_λ` are surjective (hence faithfully flat).*

<!-- original page 220 -->

*Under these conditions:*

*(i) Suppose `S_α` quasi-compact. Let `ℱ_α` be a quasi-coherent `𝒪_{S_α}`-Module, which is moreover supposed of
finite type when hypothesis 1° is satisfied. Let `(f_{i, α})_{1 ≤ i ≤ n}` be a finite sequence of sections of `𝒪_{S_α}`
above `S_α`. In order for the sequence of sections `f_i` of the `𝒪_S`-Module `ℱ` above `S`, corresponding to
the `f_{i, α}` (`1 ≤ i ≤ n`), to be `ℱ`-regular, it is necessary and sufficient that there exist `λ ≥ α` such that
the sequence of sections `f_{i, λ}` of `ℱ_λ` above `S_λ` be `ℱ_λ`-regular.*

*(ii) Suppose `X_α` quasi-compact, and let `j_α : X_α → S_α` be an immersion, which one supposes locally of finite
presentation when hypothesis 2° is satisfied. Then, in order for the corresponding immersion `j : X → S` to be
regular, it is necessary and sufficient that there exist `λ ≥ α` such that `j_λ : X_λ → S_λ` be regular.*

(i) If `p_α : S → S_α` is the canonical projection, one has `ℱ/(Σ f_i ℱ) = p_α^*(ℱ_α/(Σ f_{i, α} ℱ_α))`, hence one is
reduced to the case `n = 1`, in which case one suppresses the index `i`. The fact that the condition is sufficient
follows from the fact that `p_α` is flat `(8.3.8)` and from `(0, 15.1.5)`. In case 2°, `p_α` is faithfully
flat `(8.3.8)` and the necessity of the condition still follows from `(0, 15.2.5)`, with `λ = α`. In case 1°, denote
by `𝒩_α` (resp. `𝒩`) the kernel of the homomorphism `ℱ_α → ℱ_α` (resp. `ℱ → ℱ`), multiplication
by `f_α` (resp. by `f`); since `ℱ_α` is coherent by hypothesis, so is `𝒩_α`, hence the hypothesis `𝒩 = 0`
entails `𝒩_λ = 0` for a `λ ≥ α` by virtue of `(8.5.8, (ii))`.

(ii) Since `p_α` is flat, the sufficiency of the condition follows from `(19.1.5, (ii))`. To prove its necessity,
note that, since `j_α(X_α)` is quasi-compact, it is contained in a quasi-compact open of `S_α` and one may therefore
limit oneself to the case where `S_α` is also quasi-compact and `j_α` a closed immersion, so that the image
of `X_α` is defined by a quasi-coherent Ideal `𝒥_α` of `𝒪_{S_α}`, which one may moreover suppose of finite type in
cases 1° and 2° (`j_α` being locally of finite presentation in case 2°); the image of `X_α` (resp. `X`) in `S_α`
(resp. `S`) is then defined by `𝒥_λ = (𝒥_α)_λ` (resp. `𝒥 = (𝒥_α)`) which is still of finite type. One may moreover,
taking account of `(8.2.11)`, suppose that `𝒥` is generated by a regular sequence `(f_i)_{1 ≤ i ≤ n}` of sections
above `S`, which therefore define a surjective homomorphism `u : 𝒪_S^n → 𝒥`. Taking account of `(8.5.2, (i))` and
`(8.5.7)`, there exist `λ ≥ α` and a surjective homomorphism `u_λ : 𝒪_{S_λ}^n → 𝒥_λ` such that `u = p_λ^*(u_λ)`,
hence the `f_i` are the canonical images of sections `f_{i, λ}` of `𝒥_λ` above `S_λ` generating that Ideal. By virtue
of (i), there exists therefore `μ ≥ λ` such that the sequence `(f_{i, μ})` is `𝒪_{S_μ}`-regular, hence the
immersion `j_μ` is regular.

**Proposition (19.8.2).**

<!-- label: IV.19.8.2 -->

*Let `X_α` be a quasi-compact `S_α`-prescheme, locally of finite presentation.*

*(i) Let `ℱ_α` be a quasi-coherent `𝒪_{X_α}`-Module of finite presentation, `(f_{i, α})_{1 ≤ i ≤ n}` a sequence of
sections of `𝒪_{X_α}` above `X_α`. In order for the sequence of sections `f_i` of `𝒪_X` above `X`, corresponding to
the `f_{i, α}`, to be `ℱ`-transversally regular relative to `S` `(19.2.1)`, it is necessary and sufficient that there
exist `λ ≥ α` such that the sequence of sections `f_{i, λ}` of `𝒪_{X_λ}` above `X_λ` be `ℱ_λ`-transversally regular
relative to `S_λ`.*

*(ii) Let `Y_α` be a quasi-compact `S_α`-prescheme, `j_α : Y_α → X_α` an `S_α`-immersion locally of finite

<!-- original page 221 -->

presentation. In order for the corresponding `S`-immersion `j : Y → X` to be transversally regular relative to `S`,
it is necessary and sufficient that there exist `λ ≥ α` such that `j_λ : Y_λ → X_λ` be transversally regular
relative to `S_λ`.*

(i) The sufficiency of the condition follows from `(0, 15.1.15)`. To prove that it is necessary, it will suffice to
show that every point `x ∈ X` has an open neighbourhood `V(x)` for which there exist an index `λ = λ(x) ≥ α` and an
open neighbourhood `V_λ` of the image `x_λ` of `x` in `X_λ` such that `V(x)` is the inverse image of `V_λ` and that
the sequence of the restrictions to `V_λ` of the `f_{i, λ}` is `(ℱ_λ | V_λ)`-transversally regular relative to `S_λ`:
indeed, it will then suffice to apply `(8.3.4)` to the open set `U_λ` of `X_λ`, defined by the condition of being the
largest open such that the restrictions to `U_λ` of the `f_{i, λ}` form a sequence `(ℱ_λ | U_λ)`-transversally
regular relative to `S_λ`. Let `s` be the image of `x` in `S`, and for every `λ ≥ α`, let `s_λ` be the image of `s`
in `S_λ`. Then the fibre `X_s` is the projective limit of the fibres `(X_λ)_{s_λ}` and is locally Noetherian since
the morphism `X → S` is locally of finite presentation; as moreover `(X_α)_{s_α}` is quasi-compact and the transition
morphisms `(X_μ)_{s_μ} → (X_λ)_{s_λ}` are flat (since this is so of
`Spec(k(s_μ)) → Spec(k(s_λ))`), one may apply to the sections `f_i ⊗ 1` of `ℱ_s = ℱ ⊗_𝒪 k(s)` above `X_s` the result
of `(19.8.1, (i))`; whence the existence of `λ ≥ α` such that the sections `f_{i, λ} ⊗ 1` of
`(ℱ_λ)_{s_λ} = ℱ_λ ⊗_𝒪 k(s_λ)` above `(X_λ)_{s_λ}` form a `(ℱ_λ)_{s_λ}`-regular sequence. Moreover `(11.2.6)`, one
may suppose that `ℱ_λ` is `S_λ`-flat at the point `x_λ`. It then follows from `(11.3.8)` that there exists an open
neighbourhood `V_λ` of `x_λ` in `X_λ` satisfying the required conditions.

(ii) The sufficiency of the condition follows from `(19.2.7, (ii))`. To prove its necessity, it again suffices to
show that every point `x ∈ j(Y)` admits an open neighbourhood `V(x)` for which there exist an
index `λ = λ(x) ≥ α` and an open neighbourhood `V_λ` of the image `x_λ` of `x` in `X_λ` such that `V(x)` is the
inverse image of `V_λ` and that the restriction `j_λ^{-1}(V_λ) → V_λ` of `j_λ` is transversally regular relative
to `S_λ`. Let `s` be the image of `x` in `S`, and for every `λ ≥ α`, let `s_λ` be the image of `s` in `S_λ`.
As `X_s` (resp. `Y_s`) is the projective limit of the fibres `(X_λ)_{s_λ}` (resp. `(Y_λ)_{s_λ}`), one may, as in
(i), use `(19.8.1, (ii))`, and as by hypothesis the immersion `Y_s → X_s` is regular, there exists an
index `λ(s)` such that for `λ ≥ λ(s)` the immersion `(Y_λ)_{s_λ} → (X_λ)_{s_λ}` be regular. Moreover, by virtue
of `(19.2.4)` there exists a quasi-compact open neighbourhood `W(x)` of `x` in `X` such that the structure
morphisms `W(x) → S` and `Y ∩ W(x) → S` are flat; applying `(11.2.6)` and `(8.2.11)`, one may suppose that there
exist `λ ≥ λ(s)` and a quasi-compact open neighbourhood `W(x_λ)` of `x_λ` in `X_λ`, such that `W(x)` is the inverse
image of `W(x_λ)` and that the restrictions to `W(x_λ)` and to `Y_λ ∩ W(x_λ)` respectively of the structure
morphisms `X_λ → S_λ` and `Y_λ → S_λ` be flat. One concludes therefore from `(19.2.4)` that there exists an open
neighbourhood `V_λ` of `x_λ` answering the question.

### 19.9. `ℱ`-regular sequences and depth

**(19.9.1).**

<!-- label: IV.19.9.1 -->

Recall that if `X` is a locally Noetherian prescheme, `ℱ` a coherent `𝒪_X`-Module, `T` a part of `X`, one
sets `(5.10.1)`

```text
  (19.9.1.1)             prof_T(ℱ) = inf_{t ∈ T} prof(ℱ_t).
```

One sets moreover, for every point `t ∈ T`,

```text
  (19.9.1.2)             prof_{T, t}(ℱ) = inf_{z ∈ T ∩ Spec(𝒪_{X, t})} prof(ℱ_z),
```

and one says that `prof_{T, t}(ℱ)` is the *`T`-depth* of `ℱ` at the point `t`; it is clear that one has

```text
  (19.9.1.3)             prof_T(ℱ) = inf_{t ∈ T} prof_{T, t}(ℱ).
```

**Lemma (19.9.2).**

<!-- label: IV.19.9.2 -->

*Let `A` be a Noetherian ring, `M` an `A`-module of finite type, `𝔍` an ideal of `A`. In order that, for every prime
ideal `𝔭 ⊃ 𝔍`, one have `prof(M_𝔭) ≥ r`, it is necessary and sufficient that there exist an `M`-regular sequence of
`r` elements belonging to `𝔍`.*

The sufficiency of the condition follows at once from `(0, 16.4.5)`; let us prove its necessity.

We reason by induction on `r` (there is nothing to prove if `r = 0`): since `prof(M_𝔭) ≥ r − 1` for every `𝔭 ⊃ 𝔍`,
there exists by hypothesis an `M`-regular sequence `(g_i)_{1 ≤ i ≤ r-1}` formed of elements of `𝔍`. Set
`N = M / (g_1 M + ⋯ + g_{r-1} M)`; for every `𝔭 ⊃ 𝔍`, the images of the `g_i` in `A_𝔭` therefore belong to the
maximal ideal `𝔭 A_𝔭`, and form an `M_𝔭`-regular sequence `(0, 15.1.14)`; one concludes therefore from `(0, 16.4.6)`
that one has `prof(N_𝔭) = prof(M_𝔭) − (r − 1) ≥ 1`, and one sees that everything reduces to proving the lemma
for `r = 1`. Now the hypothesis then signifies that, for every `𝔭 ⊃ 𝔍`, `𝔭 A_𝔭` is not associated
to `M_𝔭` `(0, 16.4.6)`, hence (Bourbaki, *Alg. comm.*, chap. IV, §1, n° 2, prop. 5), `𝔭` is not associated to `M`.
In other words, `𝔍` is not contained in any of the prime ideals of `Ass(M)`, hence it is not contained in their
union (Bourbaki, *Alg. comm.*, chap. II, §1, n° 1, prop. 2). But as this union is the set of elements which are
not `M`-regular (Bourbaki, *Alg. comm.*, chap. IV, §1, n° 1, cor. 2 of prop. 2), this proves the lemma.

**Proposition (19.9.3).**

<!-- label: IV.19.9.3 -->

*Let `X` be a locally Noetherian prescheme, `ℱ` a coherent `𝒪_X`-Module, `Y` a closed part of `X`, `y` a point of `Y`,
`n` an integer `> 0`. The following conditions are equivalent:*

- *a) There exists an open neighbourhood `U` of `y` in `X` such that `prof_{Y ∩ U}(ℱ | U) ≥ n`.*
- *b) There exists an open neighbourhood `U` of `y` in `X` and a sequence `(f_i)_{1 ≤ i ≤ n}` of `n` sections of `𝒪_X`
  above `U`, which is `(ℱ | U)`-regular, such that `f_i(x) = 0` `(0_I, 5.5.1)` for `1 ≤ i ≤ n` at every
  point `x ∈ U ∩ Y`.*
- *c) One has `prof_{Y, y}(ℱ) ≥ n`.*

*Moreover, the set of `y ∈ Y` satisfying these conditions is open in `Y`.*

The last assertion follows trivially from a). The equivalence of a) and b) follows from `(19.9.2)` applied to an
affine open neighbourhood of `y` in `X` and to `(0, 15.1.14)`. It is clear that a) entails c), every open
neighbourhood of `y` in `X` containing `Spec(𝒪_{X, y})`; let us show conversely that c) implies b). It follows
from c) and from the definition `(19.9.1.1)` that one may

<!-- original page 223 -->

apply `(19.9.2)` to the ring `𝒪_{X, y}`, to the `𝒪_{X, y}`-module `ℱ_y` and to the ideal `𝒥_y` of `𝒪_{X, y}`, hence
there exists an `ℱ_y`-regular sequence `(g_i)_{1 ≤ i ≤ n}` of `n` elements of `𝒥_y`. There exists therefore an open
neighbourhood `U` of `y` in `X` and a sequence `(f_i)_{1 ≤ i ≤ n}` of sections of `𝒥` above `U` such that
the `g_i` are the germs of the `f_i` at `y`; using `(0, 15.2.4)`, one deduces that there exists an open
neighbourhood `U' ⊂ U` of `y` in `X` such that the `f_i | U'` form an `(ℱ | U')`-regular sequence.

**Corollary (19.9.4).**

<!-- label: IV.19.9.4 -->

*With the notation of `(19.9.3)`, the function `y ↦ prof_{Y, y}(ℱ)` is lower semi-continuous in `Y`.*

**Proposition (19.9.5).**

<!-- label: IV.19.9.5 -->

*With the notation of `(19.9.3)`, let `X'` be a locally Noetherian prescheme, `g : X' → X` a flat morphism,
`Y' = g^{-1}(Y)`, `ℱ' = g^*(ℱ)`. Then, for every point `y' ∈ Y'` above `y`, one has
`prof_{Y', y'}(ℱ') = prof_{Y, y}(ℱ)`.*

Indeed, for every `z' ∈ Spec(𝒪_{X', y'})`, it follows from `(0_I, 6.3.1)` that one has
`prof(ℱ'_{z'}) ≥ prof(ℱ_{g(z')})`, and if `z''` is a maximal point of the fibre `g^{-1}(g(z'))`, generization
of `z'` (hence belonging also to `Spec(𝒪_{X', y'})`), one has `(0_I, 6.3.1)` `prof(ℱ'_{z''}) = prof(ℱ_{g(z'')})`.
The proposition follows from the fact that one has `g(Spec(𝒪_{X', y'})) = Spec(𝒪_{X, g(y')})` since `g` is
flat `(2.3.4)`.

**Proposition (19.9.6).**

<!-- label: IV.19.9.6 -->

*Let `f : X → S` be a morphism locally of finite presentation, `Y` a closed part of `X`, `ℱ` an `𝒪_X`-Module of finite
presentation and `f`-flat, `n` an integer `> 0`. For every point `y ∈ Y`, the following conditions are equivalent
(denoting by `X_{f(y)}` the fibre of `f` at the point `f(y)`, by `ℱ_{f(y)}` the inverse image of `ℱ` in `X_{f(y)}`,
and setting `Y_{f(y)} = X_{f(y)} ∩ Y`):*

- *a) One has `prof_{Y_{f(y)}, y}(ℱ_{f(y)}) ≥ n`.*
- *b) There exist an open neighbourhood `U` of `y` in `X` and a sequence `(g_i)_{1 ≤ i ≤ n}` of `n` sections of `𝒪_X`
  above `U`, which is transversally `ℱ`-regular relative to `S` (cf. `(19.2.1)` for the terminology) and such
  that `g_i(x) = 0` for `1 ≤ i ≤ n` and for every `x ∈ U ∩ Y`.*

*The set `V_n` of `y ∈ Y` satisfying these conditions is open in `Y`; if moreover `Y` is locally constructible
in `X`, `V_n` is retrocompact in `X` `(0_III, 9.1.1)`.*

By virtue of `(19.9.3)`, condition a) is equivalent to the existence of an open neighbourhood `U` of `y` in `X` and
of an `(ℱ_{f(y)} | (U ∩ X_{f(y)}))`-regular sequence `(h_i)_{1 ≤ i ≤ n}` of sections of `𝒪_X` above `U ∩ X_{f(y)}`
such that `h_i(x) = 0` for every `x ∈ U ∩ Y_{f(y)}`. If one again denotes by `Y` a closed subprescheme of `X` having
`Y` as underlying space, by `𝒥` the quasi-coherent Ideal of `𝒪_X` defining `Y`, one may again say that one
has `h_i ∈ 𝒥_{f(y)}` for `1 ≤ i ≤ n`. As one may replace `U` by a smaller affine neighbourhood of `y`, one may
suppose that the `h_i` are the images of a sequence `(g_i)_{1 ≤ i ≤ n}` of sections of `𝒥` above `U`, so that the
germs `(g_i)_y` belong to the maximal ideal `𝔪_y` of `𝒪_{X, y}`. The equivalence of a) and b) then follows from
`(11.3.8)`, which also proves that the set `V_n` is open in `Y`. It remains to prove the last assertion; as it is
again a question of local properties on `X`, one may suppose `S = Spec(A)` affine and `X` of finite presentation
over `S`; there exists then a Noetherian subring `A_0` of `A`, a prescheme `X_0` of finite type over
`S_0 = Spec(A_0)` and a coherent `𝒪_{X_0}`-Module `ℱ_0` such that `X = X_0 ×_{S_0} S` and
`ℱ = ℱ_0 ⊗_{𝒪_{X_0}} 𝒪_X` `(8.9.1)`; one may moreover suppose that, if `f_0 : X_0 → S_0` is the structure morphism,
`ℱ_0` is `f_0`-flat `(11.2.6)`. Since `Y` is constructible, one may moreover `(8.3.11)` suppose

<!-- original page 224 -->

that `Y = p^{-1}(Y_0)`, where `p : X → X_0` is the canonical projection. Then, for every `y ∈ Y`, it follows
from `(19.9.5)` and the transitivity of fibres that one has

```text
                       prof_{Y_{f(y)}, y}(ℱ_{f(y)}) = prof_{(Y_0)_{f_0(y_0)}, y_0}((ℱ_0)_{f_0(y_0)})
```

setting `y_0 = p(y)`; if `V_n^0` is the set of `y_0 ∈ Y_0` for which the second member of this relation is `≥ n`, one
has therefore `V_n = p^{-1}(V_n^0)`. As `X_0` is Noetherian, `V_n^0` is a retrocompact open in `X_0`, and
consequently (cf. proof of `(1.8.2)`) `V_n` is retrocompact in `X`.

**Corollary (19.9.7).**

<!-- label: IV.19.9.7 -->

*Under the general hypotheses of `(19.9.6)`, the function `y ↦ prof_{Y_{f(y)}, y}(ℱ_{f(y)})` is lower semi-continuous
in `Y`. If moreover `Y` is locally constructible, this function is locally constructible.*

**Proposition (19.9.8).**

<!-- label: IV.19.9.8 -->

*Let `f : X → S` be a morphism locally of finite presentation, `Z` a closed part of `X`, `ℱ` an `𝒪_X`-Module of
finite presentation and `f`-flat, `𝒢` a quasi-coherent `𝒪_S`-Module. Suppose that, for every `x ∈ Z`, one has
`prof((ℱ_{f(x)})_x) ≥ 1` (resp. `prof((ℱ_{f(x)})_x) ≥ 2`). Then, if `j : X − Z → X` is the canonical injection, and
if one sets `ℋ = ℱ ⊗_𝒪 f^*(𝒢)`, the canonical homomorphism `ρ_𝒢 : ℋ → j_*(j^*(ℋ))` relative to `j` is injective
(resp. bijective).*

The question is evidently local on `X` and `S`, and it suffices to demonstrate the proposition in a neighbourhood of
a point `x ∈ Z`. In other words, one may restrict to the case where `X` and `S` are affine. Thanks to `(19.9.6)`,
one may suppose that there exists a transversally `ℱ`-regular section `g_1` of `𝒪_X` above `X` relative to `S`
(resp. a transversally `ℱ`-regular sequence `(g_1, g_2)` of two sections of `𝒪_X` above `X` relative to `S`) such
that, if `Z'` is the set of `x' ∈ X` for which `g_1(x') = 0` (resp. `g_1(x') = g_2(x') = 0`), one has `Z ⊂ Z'`.

This being so, let us return to the proof of `(19.9.8)`; by virtue of `(19.9.6)`, one still has
`prof((ℱ_{f(x)})_x) ≥ 1` (resp. `prof((ℱ_{f(x)})_x) ≥ 2`) for every `x ∈ Z'`. If the proposition is proved replacing
the pair `(X, Z)` by `(X, Z')` and `(X − Z, Z' ∩ (X − Z))`, it is immediate that it will also be so for `(X, Z)`; one
may therefore restrict to proving it for `X` and `Z'`. In other words, one may restrict to demonstrating `(19.9.8)`
when `S = Spec(A)`, `X = Spec(B)`, where `B` is an `A`-algebra of finite presentation, `Z = V(𝔍)`, where `𝔍` is an
ideal of finite type of the ring `B`, `𝒢 = Ñ` and `ℱ = M̃`, where `N` is an `A`-module and `M` a `B`-module of finite
presentation which is a flat `A`-module. One may moreover reduce to the case where `N` is an `A`-module of finite
presentation. Indeed, `N` is the inductive limit of a filtered inductive system of `A`-modules of finite
presentation `N_α` (by double inductive limit); if one sets `ℋ_α = ℱ ⊗_𝒪 f^*(Ñ_α)`, `ℋ` is the inductive limit of
the `ℋ_α = ℱ ⊗_𝒪 f^*(Ñ_α)`; as `j^*` and `j_*` commute with inductive limits and `lim` is an exact functor in the
category of quasi-coherent Modules, if the proposition is proved for each of the `ℋ_α`, it will be so for `ℋ`.

It suffices moreover to prove that the canonical homomorphism

```text
                       Γ(X, ℋ) → Γ(X − Z, ℋ)
```

is injective (resp. bijective). There exists then a Noetherian subring `A_0` of `A`, an `A_0`-algebra of finite
type `B_0`, an ideal `𝔍_0` of `B_0` and a `B_0`-module of finite type `M_0` which is

<!-- original page 225 -->

a flat `A_0`-module and an `A_0`-module `N_0` such that `B = B_0 ⊗_{A_0} A`, `𝔍 = 𝔍_0 B`, `M = M_0 ⊗_{A_0} A` and
`N = N_0 ⊗_{A_0} A` `(8.9.1, 8.5.11 and 11.2.7)`. Moreover, let `(A_λ)` be the family of sub-`A_0`-algebras of finite
type of `A`, so that `A = lim A_λ`; set `B_λ = B_0 ⊗_{A_0} A_λ`, `𝔍_λ = 𝔍_0 B_λ`, `M_λ = M_0 ⊗_{A_0} A_λ`,
`N_λ = N_0 ⊗_{A_0} A_λ`, `S_λ = Spec(A_λ)`, `X_λ = Spec(B_λ)`, `Z_λ = V(𝔍_λ)`; if `p_λ : X → X_λ` is the canonical
projection, one has therefore `Z = p_λ^{-1}(Z_λ)`, and `X − Z = p_λ^{-1}(X_λ − Z_λ)`. As `X_λ − Z_λ` is
quasi-compact and quasi-separated, one concludes `(8.5.2)` that if one sets `ℋ_λ = M̃_λ ⊗ Ñ_λ` and
`ℋ_λ = (M_λ ⊗ N_λ)^∼`, the homomorphisms `lim Γ(X_λ, ℋ_λ) → Γ(X, ℋ)` and
`lim Γ(X_λ − Z_λ, ℋ_λ) → Γ(X − Z, ℋ)` are bijective; by virtue of the exactness of the functor `lim`, it will
therefore suffice to prove that for every sufficiently large `λ`, the canonical
homomorphism `Γ(X_λ, ℋ_λ) → Γ(X_λ − Z_λ, ℋ_λ)` is injective (resp. bijective). But for every `x ∈ Z`, if `x_λ` is
the projection of `x` in `Z_λ`, one has `prof((ℱ_{f(x)})_x) = prof(((ℱ_λ)_{f_λ(x_λ)})_{x_λ})` by virtue
of `(4.2.7)` and `(6.7.1)`; to be reduced to demonstrating `(19.9.8)` in the case where `A` is Noetherian, one must
prove that, for `λ` sufficiently large, one has `prof(((ℱ_λ)_{f_λ(x_λ)})_{x_λ}) ≥ 1` (resp. `≥ 2`) for every
point `x_λ ∈ Z_λ`. Now, let `Z_λ^1` be the set of points `x_λ ∈ Z_λ` such that, for every generization `ξ`
of `x_λ` in `Z_λ ∩ (X_λ)_{f_λ(x_λ)}`, one has `prof(((ℱ_λ)_{f_λ(ξ)})_ξ) ≥ 1`; if `p_{μ λ} : Z_μ → Z_λ` is the
canonical projection for `λ ≤ μ`, it follows from the hypothesis and from `(19.9.5)` that one has
`Z_λ = p_{μ λ}^{-1}(Z_μ^1)` and `Z_λ^1 = p_{μ λ}^{-1}(Z_λ^1)`; on the other hand, it follows from `(19.9.6)`
that `Z_λ^1` is open in `Z_λ`, hence our assertion follows from `(8.3.4)`.

To finish the proof when `A` is Noetherian, note that by virtue of the flatness hypothesis and of `(6.3.1)`, one then
has `prof_Z(ℋ) ≥ 1` (resp. `prof_Z(ℋ) ≥ 2`); but the conclusion of `(19.9.8)` follows in this case from `(5.10.2)`
(resp. `(5.10.4)`).

**Remark (19.9.9).**

<!-- label: IV.19.9.9 -->

The same proof, joined to the results of Chap. III, 3rd part on depth and local cohomology, allows one to establish
the following generalization of `(19.9.8)`:

Under the general conditions of `(19.9.8)`, suppose that, for every `x ∈ Z`, one has `prof((ℱ_{f(x)})_x) ≥ k`; then
the canonical homomorphism

```text
                       H^i(X, ℋ) → H^i(X − Z, ℋ | (X − Z))
```

is bijective for `i ≤ k − 2` and injective for `i = k − 1` (which, expressed by means of the general cohomological
notion of depth introduced in Chap. III, is also written `prof_Z(ℋ) ≥ k`).
