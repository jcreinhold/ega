### 15.1. `M`-regular sequences and `M`-quasi-regular sequences

**(15.1.1)** Let `A` be a ring, `N` an `A`-module; in what follows, we shall denote by `N[T_1, ÔÇĞ, T_n]`, for short, the

the ideal of `A` generated by the `f_i`, and equip `M` with the `­Øöı`-preadic filtration; one then defines a *surjective
graded homomorphism of graded `A`-modules*, of degree `0`,

```text
(15.1.1.1)              ¤å : (gr_0(M))[T_1, ÔÇĞ, T_n] ÔåÆ gr_ÔÇó(M)
```

in the following way: if `╬¥ Ôêê gr_0(M)` is the class mod `­ØöıM` of an element `x Ôêê M`, then to the pair formed by `╬¥` and a
polynomial `P[T_1, ÔÇĞ, T_n]` homogeneous of degree `k`, one associates the class mod `­Øöı^{k+1}M` of `P(f_1, ÔÇĞ, f_n)x`; it
is immediate that this class depends only on `╬¥` and `P`, and if `S_k` is the set of homogeneous polynomials of degree
`k` in `A[T_1, ÔÇĞ, T_n]`, one has thus defined an `A`-linear map


which is surjective by the definition of `­Øöı^k M`.

We shall say in what follows that an element `f Ôêê A` is *not a zero-divisor* in an `A`-module `M` if the homothety
`f_M : x ÔåĞ fx` of `M` is injective.

**Lemma (15.1.2).**

<!-- label: 0_IV.15.1.2 -->

*Let `M` be an `A`-module, `f` an element of `A`, and equip `M` with the `(fA)`-preadic filtration. If `f` is not a
zero-divisor in `M`, the canonical homomorphism defined in `(15.1.1)`:*

```text
(15.1.2.1)                      ¤å : (M/fM)[T] ÔåÆ gr_ÔÇó(M)
```

*is bijective. The converse holds if `M` is separated for the `(fA)`-preadic topology.*

One has here `gr_k(M) = f^k M / f^{k+1} M`; to say that `¤å` is injective means that for every `x Ôêê M` and every `k`, the
relation `f^k x Ôêê f^{k+1} M` implies `x Ôêê fM`; it is clear that this is so if `f` is not a zero-divisor in `M`.
Conversely, observe that the homothety `f_M : x ÔåĞ fx` defines by passage to the quotients homomorphisms
`gr_k(M) ÔåÆ gr_{k+1}(M)`, hence a graded homomorphism `g` of degree `1` of `gr_ÔÇó(M)` into itself; and to say that `¤å` is
injective is to say that `g` is injective. If one assumes the filtration of `M` to be separated, it follows that `f_M`
is injective `(Bourbaki, Alg. comm., chap. III, ┬ğ2, n┬░ 8, cor. 1 of th. 1)`.

**Corollary (15.1.3).**

<!-- label: 0_IV.15.1.3 -->

*Let `A` be a Noetherian ring, `M` an `A`-module of finite type. For `f` to be not a zero-divisor in `M`, it is
necessary and sufficient that `¤å` be bijective.*

One has only to prove that the condition is sufficient; to show that `f_M` is injective, it suffices to show that for
every prime ideal `­Øö¡` of `A`, the homothety of ratio `f' = f/1 Ôêê A' = A_­Øö¡` in the `A_­Øö¡`-module `M_­Øö¡ = M'` is injective
`(Bourbaki, Alg. comm., chap. II, ┬ğ3, n┬░ 4, th. 1)`. Now one has `f'^k M'/f'^{k+1} M' Ôëà (f^k M / f^{k+1} M) Ôèù_A A'`, and
it is immediate that the canonical homomorphism `¤å' : (M'/f'M')[T] ÔåÆ gr_ÔÇó(M')` is equal to `¤å Ôèù 1_{A'}`; if `¤å` is
injective, then so is `¤å'` `(0_I, 1.3.2)`. One is thus reduced to the case where `A` is a Noetherian local ring, whose
maximal ideal we denote by `­Øö¬`. If `f Ôêë ­Øö¬`, then `f` is invertible and there is nothing to prove. If `f Ôêê ­Øö¬`, one knows
that `M` is separated for the `(fA)`-preadic topology `(0_I, 7.3.5)`, and it therefore suffices to apply `(15.1.2)`.

**Definition (15.1.4).**

<!-- label: 0_IV.15.1.4 -->

*Let `A` be a ring, `M` an `A`-module. One says that an element `f Ôêê A` is **`M`-regular** (resp. **`M`-quasi-regular**)
if `f` is not a zero-divisor in `M` (resp. if the homomorphism `(15.1.2.1)` is bijective).*

We have thus just proved that every `M`-regular element is `M`-quasi-regular, and that the converse holds if `A` is
Noetherian and `M` an `A`-module of finite type. When one no longer assumes `M` of finite type, this converse may fail:
for example, if `A` is a principal ring which is not a field, `K` its field of fractions and `M` the `A`-module `K/A`,
one has `fM = M` for every `f Ôëá 0` in `A`, hence both members of `(15.1.2.1)` reduce to `0`, but `f` is then a
zero-divisor in `M`.

**Proposition (15.1.8).**

*Let `M` be an `A`-module, `(f_i)_{1 Ôëñ i Ôëñ n}` a sequence of elements of `A`. If the sequence `(f_i)` is `M`-regular, it

`(15.1.1.1)`. Conversely, suppose the homomorphism `¤å` of `(15.1.1.1)` is bijective. Note that if one sets
`­Øöı'' = Ôêæ_{i=1}^{n-1} f_i A`,

**Remarks (15.1.12).**

<!-- label: 0_IV.15.1.12 -->

*(i) When `A` is a Noetherian local ring, `M` an `A`-module of finite type, and when the `f_i` are contained in the
maximal ideal of `A`, the notion of `M`-regular sequence was introduced by J.-P. Serre under the name `M`-sequence
`[17]`.*

*(ii) Even if `A` is Noetherian and `M = A`, a sequence `(f, g)` of two elements of `A` which is quasi-regular is not
necessarily regular. For example, the sequence `(0, 1)` is quasi-regular, since then `­Øöı = fA + gA = A`, hence
`gr_ÔÇó(A) = 0`; but if `A Ôëá 0` it is not regular; one will note however that in this case the sequence `(1, 0)` is
regular, since `1` is not a zero-divisor in `A`, and since `A/A = 0`, `0` is not a zero-divisor in this module (cf.
`(15.1.1)`).*

<!-- original page 112 -->

*(iii) In `(15.1.6)`, one cannot in general replace the hypothesis that `f` is `gr_ÔÇó(M)`-regular by the hypothesis that
it is `gr_ÔÇó(M)`-quasi-regular. To see this, take up again the example `(15.1.4)` where `A` is a principal ring with
field of fractions `K Ôëá A`, and take `M = K`, the filtration `(M_k)` of `M` being defined by `M_0 = M = K`, `M_1 = A`,
`M_k = 0` for `k ÔëÑ 2`, whence `gr_0(M) = K/A`, `gr_1(M) = A`, `gr_k(M) = 0` for `k ÔëÑ 2`. If `f Ôëá 0` in `A`, one has seen
that `f` is not `gr_ÔÇó(M)`-regular; but since one has `f^k gr_ÔÇó(M)/f^{k+1} gr_ÔÇó(M) = f^k A / f^{k+1} A`, it is immediate
that the homomorphism `(15.1.2.1)` (where `M` is replaced by `gr_ÔÇó(M)`) is injective, hence `f` is
`gr_ÔÇó(M)`-quasi-regular. But with the notations of `(15.1.6)`, one then has `M'_k = K` for every `k ÔëÑ 0`, hence
`gr'_ÔÇó(M) = 0`, while the first member of `(15.1.5.2)` is not reduced to `0`.*

**Proposition (15.1.15).**

**Proposition (15.1.16).**

<!-- label: 0_IV.15.1.16 -->

*Let `A`, `B` be two Noetherian local rings, `k` the residue field of `A`, `¤å : A ÔåÆ B` a local homomorphism, `M` a
`B`-module of finite type. Let `(f_i)_{1 Ôëñ i Ôëñ n}` be a sequence of elements of the maximal ideal of `B`, and for every
`i`, let `g_i` be the image of `f_i` in `B Ôèù_A k`. The following conditions are equivalent:*

*a) The sequence `(f_i)` is `M`-regular and the quotients `M_i = M/(Ôêæ_{j=1}^{i} f_j M)` are flat `A`-modules for
`1 Ôëñ i Ôëñ n`.*

*b) The sequence `(f_i)` is `M`-regular and the `A`-module `M_n` is flat.*

*c) The `A`-module `M` is flat and the sequence `(g_i)_{1 Ôëñ i Ôëñ n}` is `(M Ôèù_A k)`-regular.*

*d) The `A`-module `M` is flat, and for every ring homomorphism `¤ü : A ÔåÆ A'`, if one sets `B' = B Ôèù_A A'`,

It is clear that a) implies b); to show that b) implies d), it suffices, by virtue of `(15.1.15)`, to show that b)
implies that the `M_i` are flat `A`-modules for `0 Ôëñ i Ôëñ n - 1` (setting `M_0 = M`). Since `M_{i+1} = M_i/f_{i+1} M_i`,
one is reduced, by descending induction, to proving that if `h` is an element of the maximal ideal of `B` such that the
homothety of ratio `h` in `M` is injective and `M/hM` is a flat `A`-module, then `M` is a flat `A`-module; but this is
none other than `(0_III, 10.2.7)`. It is clear that c) is the particular case of d) obtained by taking `A' = k`. It
therefore remains to prove that c) implies a). Since `M` is a flat `A`-module and a `B`-module of finite type, it
follows from c) and `(0_III, 10.2.4)` that the homothety of ratio `f_1` in `M` is injective and that its cokernel

`A`-module; since the homothety of ratio `g_{i+1}` in `M_i Ôèù_A k` is injective by hypothesis, it again follows from
`(0_III, 10.2.4)` that the homothety of ratio `f_{i+1}` in `M_i` is injective and that `M_i/f_{i+1} M_i = M_{i+1}` is a
flat `A`-module, which completes the proof. One will note that the proof that c) implies a) does not use the fact that
the `f_i` belong to the maximal ideal of `B`.

**Lemma (15.1.18).**

<!-- label: 0_IV.15.1.18 -->

*Let `A` be a ring, `M` an `A`-module equipped with a separated and exhaustive filtration `(M_k)_{k ÔëÑ 0}`, and let
`f Ôêê A` be a `gr_ÔÇó(M)`-regular element. Then:*

*(i) `f` is `M`-regular.*

*(ii) For every `k`, one has `M_k Ôê® fM = fM_k`, and the canonical image `M''_k` of `M_k` in `M'' = M/fM` is therefore
canonically isomorphic to `M_k/fM_k`.*

*(iii) The sequence*

```text
        0 ÔåÆ M_{k+1}/fM_{k+1} ÔåÆ M_k/fM_k ÔåÆ gr_k(M)/f ┬À gr_k(M) ÔåÆ 0
```

*is exact, and if one equips `M''` with the quotient filtration `(M''_k)`, `gr_ÔÇó(M'')` is therefore canonically
isomorphic to `gr_ÔÇó(M)/f ┬À gr_ÔÇó(M)`.*

Assertion (i) follows from the hypothesis on `f` and from `Bourbaki, Alg. comm., chap. III, ┬ğ2, n┬░ 8, cor. 1 of th. 1`.
Since `gr_ÔÇó(M/M_k)` is a direct summand of `gr_ÔÇó(M)`, `f` is `gr_ÔÇó(M/M_k)`-regular for every `k`, hence
`(M/M_k)`-regular by (i), which establishes (ii). To prove (iii), it suffices to see that the map
`M_{k+1}/fM_{k+1} ÔåÆ M_k/fM_k` is injective: now, if `x Ôêê M_{k+1}` is such that `x Ôêê fM_k`, it follows from (ii) that
`x Ôêê fM_{k+1}`, which completes the proof of the lemma.

**Proposition (15.1.19).**

<!-- label: 0_IV.15.1.19 -->

*Let `A` be a ring, `M` an `A`-module equipped with a filtration `(M_k)_{k ÔëÑ 0}` such that `M_0 = M`,
`(f_i)_{1 Ôëñ i Ôëñ n}` a `gr_ÔÇó(M)`-regular sequence of elements of `A`. Suppose in addition that for `0 Ôëñ i Ôëñ n - 1`, the

`N = M/(Ôêæ_{i Ôëñ n-1} f_i M)` and denoting by `(N_k)` the quotient filtration of `(M_k)` on `N`, which is separated by

hypotheses:
