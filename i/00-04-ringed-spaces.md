# Chapter 0 — Preliminaries

## §4. Ringed Spaces

<!-- label: 0.4 -->

### 4.1. Ringed spaces; `𝒜`-Modules; `𝒜`-Algebras

<!-- label: 0.4.1 -->

**(4.1.1)** A _ringed space_ (resp. _topologically ringed space_) is a pair `(X, 𝒜)` consisting of a topological space
`X` and a sheaf of rings (not necessarily commutative) (resp. a sheaf of topological rings) `𝒜` on `X`; we call `X` the
topological space _underlying_ the ringed space `(X, 𝒜)`, and `𝒜` the _structure sheaf_. The latter will be written
`𝒪_X`, and its stalk at `x ∈ X` will be written `𝒪_{X,x}` or simply `𝒪_x` when no confusion can arise.

We shall write `1` or `e` for the _unit section_ of `𝒪_X` over `X` (the unit element of `Γ(X, 𝒪_X)`).

Since in this treatise we shall mainly consider sheaves of _commutative_ rings, when we speak of a ringed space `(X, 𝒜)`
without further qualification it will be understood that `𝒜` is a sheaf of commutative rings.

Ringed spaces with structure sheaf not necessarily commutative (resp. topologically ringed spaces) form a _category_,
with morphisms `(X, 𝒜) → (Y, ℬ)` defined as pairs `(ψ, θ) = Ψ` consisting of a continuous map `ψ : X → Y` and a
_ψ-morphism_ `θ : ℬ → 𝒜` (3.5.1) of sheaves of rings (resp. sheaves of topological rings). The _composite_ of a second
morphism `Ψ′ = (ψ′, θ′) : (Y, ℬ) → (Z, 𝒞)` with `Ψ`, written `Ψ″ = Ψ′ ∘ Ψ`, is `(ψ″, θ″)` with `ψ″ = ψ′ ∘ ψ` and `θ″`
the composite of `θ` and `θ′` (equal to `ψ′_*(θ) ∘ θ′`; cf. 3.5.2). For ringed spaces, recall that
`θ″^♯ = θ^♯ ∘ ψ*(θ′^♯)` (3.5.5); hence if `θ^♯` and `θ′^♯` are _injective_ (resp. _surjective_) homomorphisms, so is
`θ″^♯`, using that `ψ_x ∘ ρ_{ψ(x)}` is an isomorphism for every `x ∈ X` (3.7.2). One checks at once that when `ψ` is
_injective_ and `θ^♯` is _surjective_, the morphism `(ψ, θ)` is a _monomorphism_ (T, 1.1) in the category of ringed
spaces.

By abuse of language, we shall often replace `ψ` by `Ψ` in the notation — for instance writing `Ψ⁻¹(U)` in place of
`ψ⁻¹(U)` for `U ⊂ Y` — when no confusion can arise.

**(4.1.2)** For every subset `M ⊂ X`, the pair `(M, 𝒜|M)` is plainly a ringed space, called _induced_ on `M` by `(X, 𝒜)`
(or the _restriction_ of `(X, 𝒜)` to `M`). If `j : M → X` is the canonical injection and `ω` the identity of `𝒜|M`, then
`(j, ω^♭)` is a monomorphism `(M, 𝒜|M) → (X, 𝒜)` of ringed spaces, called the _canonical injection_. The composite of a
morphism `Ψ : (X, 𝒜) → (Y, ℬ)` with this injection is called the _restriction_ of `Ψ` to `M`.

**(4.1.3)** We shall not return to the definition of `𝒜`-_Modules_ or _algebraic sheaves_ on a ringed space `(X, 𝒜)` (G,
II, 2.2); when `𝒜` is a sheaf of rings not necessarily commutative, by `𝒜`-Module we shall always mean "left `𝒜`-Module"
unless explicit mention is made to the contrary. The `𝒜`-submodules of `𝒜` will be called _sheaves of (left, right, or
two-sided) ideals_ in `𝒜`, or `𝒜`-_Ideals_.

When `𝒜` is a sheaf of commutative rings, and the _module_ structure is replaced everywhere by an _algebra_ structure in
the definition of `𝒜`-Modules, one obtains the definition of an `𝒜`-_Algebra_ on `X`. Equivalently, an `𝒜`-Algebra (not
necessarily commutative) is an `𝒜`-Module `𝒞` together with an `𝒜`-Module homomorphism `φ : 𝒞 ⊗_𝒜 𝒞 → 𝒞` and a section
`e` over `X` such that:

1° the diagram

```
𝒞 ⊗_𝒜 𝒞 ⊗_𝒜 𝒞 ──φ⊗1──→ 𝒞 ⊗_𝒜 𝒞
        │                         │
      1⊗φ                         φ
        │                         │
        ↓                         ↓
   𝒞 ⊗_𝒜 𝒞 ─────φ────→         𝒞
```

commutes;

2° for every open `U ⊂ X` and every section `s ∈ Γ(U, 𝒞)`, `φ((e|U) ⊗ s) = φ(s ⊗ (e|U)) = s`.

To say that `𝒞` is a _commutative_ `𝒜`-Algebra is to say in addition that the diagram

```
𝒞 ⊗_𝒜 𝒞 ──σ──→ 𝒞 ⊗_𝒜 𝒞
       ↘φ      ↙φ
          𝒞
```

commutes, where `σ` is the canonical symmetry of the tensor product `𝒞 ⊗_𝒜 𝒞`.

Homomorphisms of `𝒜`-Algebras are defined as homomorphisms of `𝒜`-Modules as in (G, II, 2.2), but of course no longer
form an abelian group.

If `ℳ` is an `𝒜`-submodule of an `𝒜`-Algebra `𝒞`, the _sub-`𝒜`-Algebra of `𝒞` generated by_ `ℳ` is the sum of the images
of the homomorphisms `⊗ⁿℳ → 𝒞` (for `n ≥ 0`). It is also the sheaf associated with the presheaf of algebras `U ↦ ℬ(U)`,
where `ℬ(U)` is the subalgebra of `Γ(U, 𝒞)` generated by the submodule `Γ(U, ℳ)`.

**(4.1.4)** A sheaf of rings `𝒜` on a topological space `X` is said to be _reduced at a point_ `x ∈ X` if the stalk
`𝒜_x` is a _reduced_ ring (1.1.1); `𝒜` is _reduced_ if it is reduced at every point. Recall that a ring `A` is said to
be _regular_ if each local ring `A_𝔭` (`𝔭` ranging over the prime ideals of `A`) is a regular local ring; we say that a
sheaf of rings `𝒜` on `X` is _regular at a point_ `x` (resp. _regular_) if the stalk `𝒜_x` is a regular ring (resp. if
`𝒜` is regular at every point). Finally, we say that `𝒜` is _normal at a point_ `x` (resp. _normal_) if `𝒜_x` is
_integral and integrally closed_ (resp. if `𝒜` is normal at every point). A ringed space `(X, 𝒜)` has one of these
properties if `𝒜` does.

A sheaf of _graded rings_ `𝒜` is by definition a sheaf of rings that is the direct sum (G, II, 2.7) of a family
`(𝒜_n)_{n ∈ ℤ}` of sheaves of abelian groups with `𝒜_m · 𝒜_n ⊂ 𝒜_{m+n}`. A _graded_ `𝒜`-_Module_ is an `𝒜`-Module `ℱ`
that is a direct sum of a family `(ℱ_n)_{n ∈ ℤ}` of sheaves of abelian groups satisfying `𝒜_m · ℱ_n ⊂ ℱ_{m+n}`.
Equivalently, `(𝒜_m)_x (𝒜_n)_x ⊂ (𝒜_{m+n})_x` (resp. `(𝒜_m)_x (ℱ_n)_x ⊂ (ℱ_{m+n})_x`) at every point `x`.

**(4.1.5)** For a ringed space `(X, 𝒜)` (not necessarily commutative), we shall not recall the definitions of the
bifunctors `ℱ ⊗_𝒜 𝒢`, `ℋom_𝒜(ℱ, 𝒢)`, and `Hom_𝒜(ℱ, 𝒢)` (G, II, 2.8 and 2.2) on the categories of left or right
`𝒜`-Modules (as the case may be), with values in the category of sheaves of abelian groups (or more generally
`𝒞`-Modules, where `𝒞` is the center of `𝒜`). The stalk `(ℱ ⊗_𝒜 𝒢)_x` at any `x ∈ X` is canonically identified with
`ℱ_x ⊗_{𝒜_x} 𝒢_x`, and there is a canonical functorial homomorphism `(ℋom_𝒜(ℱ, 𝒢))_x → Hom_{𝒜_x}(ℱ_x, 𝒢_x)` which in
general is neither injective nor surjective. The bifunctors above are additive and in particular commute with finite
direct sums; `ℱ ⊗_𝒜 𝒢` is right exact in `ℱ` and in `𝒢`, commutes with inductive limits, and `𝒜 ⊗ 𝒢` (resp. `ℱ ⊗_𝒜 𝒜`)
is canonically identified with `𝒢` (resp. `ℱ`). The functors `ℋom_𝒜(ℱ, 𝒢)` and `Hom_𝒜(ℱ, 𝒢)` are _left exact_ in `ℱ` and
`𝒢`; precisely, given an exact sequence `0 → 𝒢′ → 𝒢 → 𝒢″`, the sequence

```
0 → ℋom_𝒜(ℱ, 𝒢′) → ℋom_𝒜(ℱ, 𝒢) → ℋom_𝒜(ℱ, 𝒢″)
```

is exact; given an exact sequence `ℱ′ → ℱ → ℱ″ → 0`, the sequence

```
0 → ℋom_𝒜(ℱ″, 𝒢) → ℋom_𝒜(ℱ, 𝒢) → ℋom_𝒜(ℱ′, 𝒢)
```

is exact; analogous properties hold for `Hom`. Moreover, `ℋom_𝒜(𝒜, 𝒢)` is canonically identified with `𝒢`; finally, for
every open `U ⊂ X`,

```
Γ(U, ℋom_𝒜(ℱ, 𝒢)) = Hom_{𝒜|U}(ℱ|U, 𝒢|U).
```

For any left (resp. right) `𝒜`-Module `ℱ`, the _dual_ of `ℱ`, written `ℱ̌`, is the right (resp. left) `𝒜`-Module
`ℋom_𝒜(ℱ, 𝒜)`.

Finally, if `𝒜` is a sheaf of commutative rings and `ℱ` is an `𝒜`-Module, then `U ↦ ⋀^p Γ(U, ℱ)` is a presheaf whose
associated sheaf is an `𝒜`-Module written `⋀^p ℱ`, called the _`p`-th exterior power of_ `ℱ`. One checks easily that the
canonical map of the presheaf `U ↦ ⋀^p Γ(U, ℱ)` into `⋀^p ℱ` is _injective_, and that for every `x ∈ X`,
`(⋀^p ℱ)_x = ⋀^p (ℱ_x)`. Plainly `⋀^p ℱ` is a covariant functor in `ℱ`.

**(4.1.6)** Suppose `𝒜` is a sheaf of rings not necessarily commutative, `𝒥` a sheaf of left ideals of `𝒜`, and `ℱ` a
left `𝒜`-Module. We write `𝒥ℱ` for the sub-`𝒜`-Module of `ℱ` that is the image of `𝒥 ⊗_ℤ ℱ` (where `ℤ` is the sheaf
associated with the constant presheaf `U ↦ ℤ`) under the canonical map `𝒥 ⊗_ℤ ℱ → ℱ`; plainly `(𝒥ℱ)_x = 𝒥_x ℱ_x` for
every `x ∈ X`. When `𝒜` is commutative, `𝒥ℱ` is also the canonical image of `𝒥 ⊗_𝒜 ℱ → ℱ`. It is immediate that `𝒥ℱ` is
also the `𝒜`-Module associated with the presheaf `U ↦ Γ(U, 𝒥) Γ(U, ℱ)`. If `𝒥_1, 𝒥_2` are two sheaves of left ideals,
then `𝒥_1(𝒥_2 ℱ) = (𝒥_1 𝒥_2) ℱ`.

**(4.1.7)** Let `(X_λ, 𝒜_λ)_{λ ∈ L}` be a family of ringed spaces; for each pair `(λ, μ)`, suppose given an open subset
`V_{μλ} ⊂ X_λ` and an isomorphism of ringed spaces `φ_{λμ} : (V_{μλ}, 𝒜_μ|V_{μλ}) ⥲ (V_{λμ}, 𝒜_λ|V_{λμ})`, with
`V_{λλ} = X_λ` and `φ_{λλ}` the identity. Suppose moreover that for every triple `(λ, μ, ν)`, writing `φ′_{λμ}` for the
restriction of `φ_{λμ}` to `V_{μλ} ∩ V_{μν}`, the map `φ′_{λμ}` is an isomorphism of
`(V_{μλ} ∩ V_{μν}, 𝒜_μ|(V_{μλ} ∩ V_{μν}))` onto `(V_{λμ} ∩ V_{λν}, 𝒜_λ|(V_{λμ} ∩ V_{λν}))`, and that
`φ′_{λν} = φ′_{λμ} ∘ φ′_{μν}` (_gluing condition_ for the `φ_{λμ}`). One first forms the topological space obtained by
gluing the `X_λ` along the `V_{λμ}` via the `φ_{λμ}`; identifying `X_λ` with the open subset `X′_λ` of `X`, the
hypotheses imply that the three subsets `V_{λμ} ∩ V_{λν}`, `V_{μν} ∩ V_{μλ}`, `V_{νλ} ∩ V_{νμ}` are identified with
`X′_λ ∩ X′_μ ∩ X′_ν`. One then transports the ringed-space structure of `X_λ` to `X′_λ`; the resulting `𝒜′_λ` satisfy
the gluing condition (3.3.1) and hence define a sheaf of rings `𝒜` on `X`. One says that `(X, 𝒜)` is the ringed space
obtained by _gluing_ the `(X_λ, 𝒜_λ)` along the `V_{λμ}` via the `φ_{λμ}`.

### 4.2. Direct image of an `𝒜`-Module

<!-- label: 0.4.2 -->

**(4.2.1)** Let `(X, 𝒜)`, `(Y, ℬ)` be two ringed spaces and `Ψ = (ψ, θ)` a morphism `(X, 𝒜) → (Y, ℬ)`. Then `ψ_*(𝒜)` is
a sheaf of rings on `Y` and `θ : ℬ → ψ_*(𝒜)` is a sheaf-of-rings homomorphism. Let `ℱ` be an `𝒜`-Module; its direct
image `ψ_*(ℱ)` is a sheaf of abelian groups on `Y`. Moreover, for every open `U ⊂ Y`,

```
Γ(U, ψ_*(ℱ)) = Γ(ψ⁻¹(U), ℱ)
```

is equipped with a module structure over the ring `Γ(U, ψ_*(𝒜)) = Γ(ψ⁻¹(U), 𝒜)`; the bilinear maps defining these
structures are compatible with restriction, giving `ψ_*(ℱ)` a structure of `ψ_*(𝒜)`-Module. The homomorphism
`θ : ℬ → ψ_*(𝒜)` then equips `ψ_*(ℱ)` with a `ℬ`-Module structure as well; this `ℬ`-Module is called the _direct image
of_ `ℱ` _by_ `Ψ` and is written `Ψ_*(ℱ)`. If `ℱ_1, ℱ_2` are two `𝒜`-Modules on `X` and `u : ℱ_1 → ℱ_2` is an
`𝒜`-homomorphism, it is immediate (considering sections over open subsets of `Y`) that `ψ_*(u)` is a
`ψ_*(𝒜)`-homomorphism `ψ_*(ℱ_1) → ψ_*(ℱ_2)`, and _a fortiori_ a `ℬ`-homomorphism `Ψ_*(ℱ_1) → Ψ_*(ℱ_2)`; as such, it is
written `Ψ_*(u)`. Thus `Ψ_*` is a _covariant functor_ from `𝒜`-Modules to `ℬ`-Modules; it is _left exact_ (G, II, 2.12).

On `ψ_*(𝒜)`, the `ℬ`-Module structure together with the sheaf-of-rings structure gives a structure of `ℬ`-Algebra; this
`ℬ`-Algebra is written `Ψ_*(𝒜)`.

**(4.2.2)** Let `ℳ`, `𝒩` be two `𝒜`-Modules. For every open `U ⊂ Y`, there is a canonical map

```
Γ(ψ⁻¹(U), ℳ) × Γ(ψ⁻¹(U), 𝒩) → Γ(ψ⁻¹(U), ℳ ⊗_𝒜 𝒩)
```

which is bilinear over `Γ(ψ⁻¹(U), 𝒜) = Γ(U, ψ_*(𝒜))`, and _a fortiori_ over `Γ(U, ℬ)`; it defines a homomorphism

```
Γ(U, Ψ_*(ℳ)) ⊗_{Γ(U, ℬ)} Γ(U, Ψ_*(𝒩)) → Γ(U, Ψ_*(ℳ ⊗_𝒜 𝒩))
```

which is compatible with restriction; the result is a canonical functorial homomorphism of `ℬ`-Modules

```
(4.2.2.1)    Ψ_*(ℳ) ⊗_ℬ Ψ_*(𝒩) → Ψ_*(ℳ ⊗_𝒜 𝒩),
```

in general neither injective nor surjective. If `𝒫` is a third `𝒜`-Module, the diagram

```
(4.2.2.2)    Ψ_*(ℳ) ⊗_ℬ Ψ_*(𝒩) ⊗_ℬ Ψ_*(𝒫) → Ψ_*(ℳ ⊗_𝒜 𝒩) ⊗_ℬ Ψ_*(𝒫)
                        │                                    │
                        ↓                                    ↓
             Ψ_*(ℳ) ⊗_ℬ Ψ_*(𝒩 ⊗_𝒜 𝒫) ─────────────→  Ψ_*(ℳ ⊗_𝒜 𝒩 ⊗_𝒜 𝒫)
```

commutes.

**(4.2.3)** Let `ℳ`, `𝒩` be two `𝒜`-Modules. For every open `U ⊂ Y`, by definition
`Γ(ψ⁻¹(U), ℋom_𝒜(ℳ, 𝒩)) = Hom_{𝒜|V}(ℳ|V, 𝒩|V)` with `V = ψ⁻¹(U)`. The map `u ↦ ψ_*(u)` is a homomorphism

```
Hom_{𝒜|V}(ℳ|V, 𝒩|V) → Hom_{ℬ|U}(Ψ_*(ℳ)|U, Ψ_*(𝒩)|U)
```

for the `Γ(U, ℬ)`-module structures. These homomorphisms are compatible with restriction, so they define a canonical
functorial homomorphism of `ℬ`-Modules

```
(4.2.3.1)    Ψ_*(ℋom_𝒜(ℳ, 𝒩)) → ℋom_ℬ(Ψ_*(ℳ), Ψ_*(𝒩)).
```

**(4.2.4)** If `𝒞` is an `𝒜`-Algebra, the composite

```
Ψ_*(𝒞) ⊗_ℬ Ψ_*(𝒞) → Ψ_*(𝒞 ⊗_𝒜 𝒞) → Ψ_*(𝒞)
```

equips `Ψ_*(𝒞)` with a `ℬ`-Algebra structure, by (4.2.2.2). Similarly, if `ℳ` is a `𝒞`-Module, `Ψ_*(ℳ)` is canonically
equipped with a `Ψ_*(𝒞)`-Module structure.

**(4.2.5)** Consider the special case in which `X` is a _closed_ subspace of `Y` and `ψ` is the canonical injection
`j : X → Y`. Let `ℬ′ = ℬ|X = j*(ℬ)` be the restriction of the sheaf of rings `ℬ` to `X`; an `𝒜`-Module `ℳ` may be viewed
as a `ℬ′`-Module via `θ^♯ : ℬ′ → 𝒜`. Then `Ψ_*(ℳ)` is the `ℬ`-Module that induces `ℳ` on `X` and `0` elsewhere. If `𝒩`
is a second `𝒜`-Module, `Ψ_*(ℳ) ⊗_ℬ Ψ_*(𝒩)` is identified with `Ψ_*(ℳ ⊗_{ℬ′} 𝒩)`, and `ℋom_ℬ(Ψ_*(ℳ), Ψ_*(𝒩))` with
`Ψ_*(ℋom_{ℬ′}(ℳ, 𝒩))`.

**(4.2.6)** Let `(Z, 𝒞)` be a third ringed space and `Ψ′ = (ψ′, θ′) : (Y, ℬ) → (Z, 𝒞)` a morphism; if `Ψ″ = Ψ′ ∘ Ψ`,
then plainly `Ψ″_* = Ψ′_* ∘ Ψ_*`.

### 4.3. Inverse image of a `ℬ`-Module

<!-- label: 0.4.3 -->

**(4.3.1)** Under the hypotheses and notation of (4.2.1), let `𝒢` be a `ℬ`-Module and `ψ*(𝒢)` its inverse image (3.7.1)
— a sheaf of abelian groups on `X`. The construction of sections of `ψ*(𝒢)` and `ψ*(ℬ)` (3.7.1) shows that `ψ*(𝒢)` is
canonically a `ψ*(ℬ)`-Module. The homomorphism `θ^♯ : ψ*(ℬ) → 𝒜` makes `𝒜` a `ψ*(ℬ)`-Module, written `𝒜_{[θ]}` when
confusion threatens; the tensor product `ψ*(𝒢) ⊗_{ψ*(ℬ)} 𝒜_{[θ]}` is then an `𝒜`-Module. We call this `𝒜`-Module the
_inverse image of_ `𝒢` _by_ `Ψ` and write it `Ψ*(𝒢)`. If `𝒢_1, 𝒢_2` are `ℬ`-Modules and `v : 𝒢_1 → 𝒢_2` is a
`ℬ`-homomorphism, one checks at once that `v` is a `ψ*(ℬ)`-homomorphism `ψ*(𝒢_1) → ψ*(𝒢_2)`; hence `ψ*(v) ⊗ 1` is an
`𝒜`-homomorphism `Ψ*(𝒢_1) → Ψ*(𝒢_2)`, written `Ψ*(v)`. Thus `Ψ*` is a _covariant functor_ from `ℬ`-Modules to
`𝒜`-Modules. Unlike `ψ*`, this functor is not exact in general but only _right exact_, tensoring with `𝒜` being a
right-exact functor on `ψ*(ℬ)`-Modules.

For every `x ∈ X`, by (3.7.2),

```
(Ψ*(𝒢))_x = 𝒢_{ψ(x)} ⊗_{ℬ_{ψ(x)}} 𝒜_x.
```

The support of `Ψ*(𝒢)` is therefore contained in `ψ⁻¹(Supp(𝒢))`.

**(4.3.2)** Let `(𝒢_λ)` be an inductive system of `ℬ`-Modules with `𝒢 = lim⃗ 𝒢_λ`. The canonical homomorphisms `𝒢_λ → 𝒢`
give `ψ*(ℬ)`-homomorphisms `ψ*(𝒢_λ) → ψ*(𝒢)`, and so a canonical homomorphism `lim⃗ ψ*(𝒢_λ) → ψ*(𝒢)`. Since the stalk of
an inductive limit of sheaves is the inductive limit of stalks (G, II, 1.11), this map is _bijective_ (3.7.2). Tensor
product also commutes with inductive limits, so there is a _canonical functorial isomorphism_ of `𝒜`-Modules

```
lim⃗ Ψ*(𝒢_λ) ⥲ Ψ*(lim⃗ 𝒢_λ).
```

For a finite direct sum `⊕_i 𝒢_i` of `ℬ`-Modules, plainly `ψ*(⊕_i 𝒢_i) = ⊕_i ψ*(𝒢_i)`, so tensoring with `𝒜_{[θ]}` gives

```
(4.3.2.1)    Ψ*(⊕_i 𝒢_i) = ⊕_i Ψ*(𝒢_i).
```

By passage to inductive limit, the equality extends to arbitrary direct sums.

**(4.3.3)** Let `𝒢_1, 𝒢_2` be `ℬ`-Modules. From the construction of inverse images of sheaves of abelian groups (3.7.1)
one obtains at once a canonical homomorphism

```
ψ*(𝒢_1) ⊗_{ψ*(ℬ)} ψ*(𝒢_2) → ψ*(𝒢_1 ⊗_ℬ 𝒢_2)
```

of `ψ*(ℬ)`-Modules; since the stalk of a tensor product of sheaves is the tensor product of stalks (G, II, 2.8), (3.7.2)
shows that this map is an _isomorphism_. Tensoring with `𝒜` gives a canonical functorial isomorphism

```
(4.3.3.1)    Ψ*(𝒢_1) ⊗_𝒜 Ψ*(𝒢_2) ⥲ Ψ*(𝒢_1 ⊗_ℬ 𝒢_2).
```

**(4.3.4)** Let `𝒞` be a `ℬ`-Algebra; giving `𝒞` an algebra structure amounts to giving a `ℬ`-homomorphism `𝒞 ⊗_ℬ 𝒞 → 𝒞`
satisfying associativity and commutativity (verified stalkwise). The isomorphism above transports this into an
`𝒜`-Module homomorphism `Ψ*(𝒞) ⊗_𝒜 Ψ*(𝒞) → Ψ*(𝒞)` with the same properties, equipping `Ψ*(𝒞)` with an `𝒜`-Algebra
structure. In particular, the `𝒜`-Algebra `Ψ*(ℬ)` is equal to `𝒜` (up to canonical isomorphism).

Similarly, if `ℳ` is a `𝒞`-Module, giving the module structure amounts to a `ℬ`-homomorphism `𝒞 ⊗_ℬ ℳ → ℳ` satisfying
associativity; transport gives a `Ψ*(𝒞)`-Module structure on `Ψ*(ℳ)`.

**(4.3.5)** Let `𝒥` be a sheaf of ideals of `ℬ`; since `ψ*` is exact, the `ψ*(ℬ)`-Module `ψ*(𝒥)` is canonically
identified with a sheaf of ideals of `ψ*(ℬ)`. The canonical injection `ψ*(𝒥) → ψ*(ℬ)` then gives an `𝒜`-Module
homomorphism `Ψ*(𝒥) = ψ*(𝒥) ⊗_{ψ*(ℬ)} 𝒜_{[θ]} → 𝒜`; we write `Ψ*(𝒥) · 𝒜`, or `𝒥𝒜` when no confusion threatens, for the
image of `Ψ*(𝒥)` under this map. Thus by definition `𝒥𝒜 = θ^♯(ψ*(𝒥)) · 𝒜`, and in particular
`(𝒥𝒜)_x = θ^♯_x(𝒥_{ψ(x)}) · 𝒜_x` using the canonical identification of stalks of `ψ*(𝒥)` with those of `𝒥` (3.7.2). If
`𝒥_1, 𝒥_2` are two ideals of `ℬ`, `(𝒥_1 𝒥_2) 𝒜 = 𝒥_1(𝒥_2 𝒜) = (𝒥_1 𝒜)(𝒥_2 𝒜)`. For an `𝒜`-Module `ℱ`, set `𝒥ℱ = (𝒥𝒜) ℱ`.

**(4.3.6)** Let `(Z, 𝒞)` be a third ringed space and `Ψ′ = (ψ′, θ′) : (Y, ℬ) → (Z, 𝒞)` a morphism. If `Ψ″ = Ψ′ ∘ Ψ`,
then by the definition (4.3.1) and (4.3.3.1), `Ψ″* = Ψ* ∘ Ψ′*`.

### 4.4. Relations between direct and inverse images

<!-- label: 0.4.4 -->

**(4.4.1)** Under the hypotheses and notation of (4.2.1), let `𝒢` be a `ℬ`-Module. A `ℬ`-Module homomorphism
`u : 𝒢 → Ψ_*(ℱ)` is also called a `Ψ`-_homomorphism_ of `𝒢` into `ℱ` — or simply a _homomorphism of `𝒢` into `ℱ`_,
written `u : 𝒢 → ℱ`, when no confusion threatens. To give such a homomorphism is to give, for every pair `(U, V)` with
`U ⊂ X` open, `V ⊂ Y` open, `ψ(U) ⊂ V`, a homomorphism `u_{U,V} : Γ(V, 𝒢) → Γ(U, ℱ)` of `Γ(V, ℬ)`-modules — `Γ(U, ℱ)`
viewed as a `Γ(V, ℬ)`-module via `θ_{U,V} : Γ(V, ℬ) → Γ(U, 𝒜)` — with the `u_{U,V}` making the diagrams (3.5.1.1)
commute. It suffices to give the `u_{U,V}` for `U` (resp. `V`) ranging over a basis `𝔅` of the topology of `X` (resp.
`𝔅′` of `Y`), provided one checks (3.5.1.1) for those restrictions.

**(4.4.2)** Under the hypotheses of (4.2.1) and (4.2.6), let `ℋ` be a `𝒞`-Module and `v : ℋ → Ψ′_*(𝒢)` a `Ψ′`-morphism;
then

```
w : ℋ ──v──→ Ψ′_*(𝒢) ──Ψ′_*(u)──→ Ψ′_*(Ψ_*(ℱ))
```

is a `Ψ″`-morphism, called the _composite_ of `u` and `v`.

**(4.4.3)** We now show that there is a _canonical isomorphism of bifunctors in_ `ℱ` _and_ `𝒢`

```
(4.4.3.1)    Hom_𝒜(Ψ*(𝒢), ℱ) ⥲ Hom_ℬ(𝒢, Ψ_*(ℱ)),
```

written `v ↦ v^♭` (or simply `v ↦ v^♭`); the inverse is written `u ↦ u^♯`. The definition is as follows: composing
`v : Ψ*(𝒢) → ℱ` with the canonical map `ψ*(𝒢) → Ψ*(𝒢)` gives a sheaf-of-groups homomorphism `v′ : ψ*(𝒢) → ℱ`, which is
also a `ψ*(ℬ)`-module homomorphism. From it (3.7.1) one deduces a homomorphism `v′^♭ : 𝒢 → ψ_*(ℱ) = Ψ_*(ℱ)`, also a
`ℬ`-Module homomorphism (as one checks); set `v^♭ = v′^♭`. Similarly, from `u : 𝒢 → Ψ_*(ℱ)` one deduces (3.7.1) a
`ψ*(ℬ)`-Module homomorphism `u^♯ : ψ*(𝒢) → ℱ`, and by tensoring with `𝒜` an `𝒜`-Module homomorphism `Ψ*(𝒢) → ℱ`, which
we write `u^♯`. One verifies at once that `(u^♯)^♭ = u` and `(v^♭)^♯ = v`, and the functoriality in `ℱ` of `v ↦ v^♭`.
The functoriality in `𝒢` of `u ↦ u^♯` then follows formally as in (3.5.4) (this reasoning would also give the
functoriality of `Ψ*` established directly in (4.3.1)).

Taking `v` to be the identity of `Ψ*(𝒢)`, `v^♭` is a homomorphism

```
(4.4.3.2)    ρ_𝒢 : 𝒢 → Ψ_*(Ψ*(𝒢));
```

taking `u` to be the identity of `Ψ_*(ℱ)`, `u^♯` is a homomorphism

```
(4.4.3.3)    σ_ℱ : Ψ*(Ψ_*(ℱ)) → ℱ;
```

these will be called _canonical_. In general they are neither injective nor surjective. Canonical factorizations
analogous to (3.5.3.3) and (3.5.4.4) hold.

Note that if `s` is a section of `𝒢` over an open `V ⊂ Y`, then `ρ_𝒢(s)` is the section `s′ ⊗ 1` of `Ψ*(𝒢)` over
`ψ⁻¹(V)`, where `s′_x = s_{ψ(x)}` for `x ∈ ψ⁻¹(V)`. Note also that any homomorphism `u : 𝒢 → ψ_*(ℱ)` defines for each
`x ∈ X` a stalk homomorphism `u_x : 𝒢_{ψ(x)} → ℱ_x`, factoring through the canonical map `s_x ↦ s_x ⊗ 1` of `𝒢_{ψ(x)}`
into `(Ψ*(𝒢))_x = 𝒢_{ψ(x)} ⊗_{ℬ_{ψ(x)}} 𝒜_x`. The map `u_x` is also the inductive limit of
`Γ(V, 𝒢) ──u──→ Γ(ψ⁻¹(V), ℱ) → ℱ_x` over neighborhoods `V` of `ψ(x)`.

**(4.4.4)** Let `ℱ_1, ℱ_2` be `𝒜`-Modules and `𝒢_1, 𝒢_2` be `ℬ`-Modules, with `u_i : 𝒢_i → ℱ_i` a homomorphism
(`i = 1, 2`). We write `u_1 ⊗ u_2` for the homomorphism `u : 𝒢_1 ⊗_ℬ 𝒢_2 → ℱ_1 ⊗_𝒜 ℱ_2` with `u^♯ = (u_1)^♯ ⊗ (u_2)^♯`
(using (4.3.3.1)); one checks that `u` is also the composite

```
𝒢_1 ⊗_ℬ 𝒢_2 → Ψ_*(ℱ_1) ⊗_ℬ Ψ_*(ℱ_2) → Ψ_*(ℱ_1 ⊗_𝒜 ℱ_2),
```

where the first arrow is the ordinary tensor product `u_1 ⊗ u_2` and the second is the canonical map (4.2.2.1).

**(4.4.5)** Let `(𝒢_λ)_{λ ∈ L}` be an inductive system of `ℬ`-Modules, and for each `λ` let `u_λ : 𝒢_λ → Ψ_*(ℱ)` be a
homomorphism, forming an inductive system. Set `𝒢 = lim⃗ 𝒢_λ` and `u = lim⃗ u_λ`; then the `(u_λ)^♯` form an inductive
system of homomorphisms `Ψ*(𝒢_λ) → ℱ`, whose inductive limit is `u^♯`.

**(4.4.6)** Let `ℳ`, `𝒩` be `ℬ`-Modules, `V ⊂ Y` open, `U = ψ⁻¹(V)`. The map `v ↦ Ψ*(v)` is a homomorphism

```
Hom_{ℬ|V}(ℳ|V, 𝒩|V) → Hom_{𝒜|U}(Ψ*(ℳ)|U, Ψ*(𝒩)|U)
```

for the `Γ(V, ℬ)`-module structures (`Hom_{𝒜|U}(Ψ*(ℳ)|U, Ψ*(𝒩)|U)` is naturally a `Γ(U, ψ*(ℬ))`-module, hence a
`Γ(V, ℬ)`-module via the canonical homomorphism `Γ(V, ℬ) → Γ(U, ψ*(ℬ))` of (3.7.2)). These homomorphisms are compatible
with restriction, so they define a canonical functorial homomorphism

```
γ : ℋom_ℬ(ℳ, 𝒩) → Ψ_*(ℋom_𝒜(Ψ*(ℳ), Ψ*(𝒩))),
```

corresponding to a homomorphism

```
γ^♯ : Ψ*(ℋom_ℬ(ℳ, 𝒩)) → ℋom_𝒜(Ψ*(ℳ), Ψ*(𝒩)),
```

both functorial in `ℳ` and `𝒩`.

**(4.4.7)** Suppose `ℱ` (resp. `𝒢`) is an `𝒜`-Algebra (resp. a `ℬ`-Algebra). If `u : 𝒢 → Ψ_*(ℱ)` is a `ℬ`-Algebra
homomorphism, then `u^♯ : Ψ*(𝒢) → ℱ` is an `𝒜`-Algebra homomorphism; this follows from the commutativity of

```
𝒢 ⊗_ℬ 𝒢 ────────────→ 𝒢
    │                    │
    ↓                    ↓ u
Ψ_*(ℱ ⊗_𝒜 ℱ) ────→ Ψ_*(ℱ)
```

and (4.4.4). Similarly, if `v : Ψ*(𝒢) → ℱ` is an `𝒜`-Algebra homomorphism, then `v^♭ : 𝒢 → Ψ_*(ℱ)` is a `ℬ`-Algebra
homomorphism.

**(4.4.8)** Let `(Z, 𝒞)` be a third ringed space, `Ψ′ = (ψ′, θ′) : (Y, ℬ) → (Z, 𝒞)` a morphism, and
`Ψ″ = Ψ′ ∘ Ψ : (X, 𝒜) → (Z, 𝒞)`. Let `ℋ` be a `𝒞`-Module and `v′ : ℋ → Ψ′_*(𝒢)` a homomorphism. The composite
`v″ = v ∘ v′` is defined as the homomorphism

```
ℋ ──v′──→ Ψ′_*(𝒢) ──Ψ′_*(v)──→ Ψ′_*(Ψ_*(ℱ));
```

one checks that `v″^♯` is

```
Ψ*(Ψ′*(ℋ)) ──Ψ*(v′^♯)──→ Ψ*(𝒢) ──v^♯──→ ℱ.
```
