# Chapter 0 — Preliminaries

## §1. Rings of fractions

<!-- label: 0.1 -->

### 1.0. Rings and algebras

<!-- label: 0.1.0 -->

**(1.0.1)** Every ring considered in this treatise will possess a _unit element_; every module over such a ring will be
assumed _unitary_; every ring homomorphism will be assumed to _send the unit element to the unit element_; unless
explicitly stated otherwise, a subring of a ring `A` is taken to _contain the unit element of_ `A`. We shall consider
above all _commutative_ rings, and when we speak of a ring without further qualification, it is to be understood that we
mean a commutative ring. If `A` is a ring, not necessarily commutative, by an `A`-module we shall always mean a _left_
module, unless explicit mention to the contrary is made.

**(1.0.2)** Let `A`, `B` be two rings, not necessarily commutative, and let `φ : A → B` be a homomorphism. Every left
(resp. right) `B`-module `M` can be equipped with the structure of a left (resp. right) `A`-module by setting
`a · m = φ(a) · m` (resp. `m · a = m · φ(a)`); when it is necessary to distinguish the `A`-module and `B`-module
structures on `M`, we shall denote by `M_[φ]` the left (resp. right) `A`-module so defined. If `L` is an `A`-module, a
homomorphism `u : L → M_[φ]` is therefore a homomorphism of abelian groups such that `u(a · x) = φ(a) · u(x)` for
`a ∈ A`, `x ∈ L`; one also calls such a `u` a _φ-homomorphism_ `L → M`, and one calls the pair `(φ, u)` (or, by abuse of
language, `u` itself) a _di-homomorphism_ from `(A, L)` to `(B, M)`. The pairs `(A, L)` consisting of a ring `A` and an
`A`-module `L` therefore form a _category_, whose morphisms are the di-homomorphisms.

**(1.0.3)** Under the hypotheses of (1.0.2), if `𝔍` is a left (resp. right) ideal of `A`, we shall denote by `B𝔍` (resp.
`𝔍B`) the left (resp. right) ideal `Bφ(𝔍)` (resp. `φ(𝔍)B`) of `B` generated by `φ(𝔍)`; this is also the image of the
canonical homomorphism `B ⊗_A 𝔍 → B` (resp. `𝔍 ⊗_A B → B`) of left (resp. right) `B`-modules.

**(1.0.4)** If `A` is a (commutative) ring and `B` is a ring not necessarily commutative, giving a structure of
`A`-_algebra_ on `B` amounts to giving a ring homomorphism `φ : A → B` such that `φ(A)` is contained in the center of
`B`. For every ideal `𝔍` of `A`, the ideal `𝔍B = B𝔍` is then a two-sided ideal of `B`, and for every `B`-module `M`,
`𝔍M` is a `B`-submodule equal to `(B𝔍)M`.

**(1.0.5)** We shall not return to the notions of _module of finite type_ and _(commutative) algebra of finite type_; to
say that an `A`-module `M` is of finite type means that there is an exact sequence `A^p → M → 0`. An `A`-module `M` is
said to admit a _finite presentation_ if it is isomorphic to the cokernel of a homomorphism `A^p → A^q`; in other words,
if there is an exact sequence `A^p → A^q → M → 0`. Note that over a _Noetherian_ ring `A`, every `A`-module of finite
type admits a finite presentation.

Recall that an `A`-algebra `B` is said to be _integral over_ `A` if every element of `B` is a root in `B` of a monic
polynomial with coefficients in `A`; this is the same as saying that every element of `B` is contained in a subalgebra
of `B` that is an `A`-module of finite type. When this is so and `B` is commutative, the subalgebra of `B` generated by
a finite subset is an `A`-module of finite type; the commutative algebra `B` is therefore integral and of finite type
over `A` if and only if `B` is an `A`-module of finite type. One then also says that `B` is a _finite integral
`A`-algebra_ (or simply _finite_, if no confusion results). We note that in these definitions the homomorphism `A → B`
defining the `A`-algebra structure is not assumed to be injective.

**(1.0.6)** An _integral ring_ [modern: integral domain] is a ring in which the product of a finite family of nonzero
elements is nonzero; equivalently, in such a ring `0 ≠ 1` and the product of two nonzero elements is nonzero. A _prime_
ideal of a ring `A` is an ideal `𝔭` such that `A/𝔭` is integral; this therefore implies `𝔭 ≠ A`. For a ring `A` to have
at least one prime ideal, it is necessary and sufficient that `A ≠ {0}`.

**(1.0.7)** A _local_ ring is a ring `A` in which there is a single maximal ideal, which is then the complement of the
set of invertible elements and contains every ideal `≠ A`. If `A` and `B` are local rings with maximal ideals `𝔪` and
`𝔫`, a homomorphism `φ : A → B` is called _local_ if `φ(𝔪) ⊂ 𝔫` (equivalently, if `φ⁻¹(𝔫) = 𝔪`). By passage to the
quotients, such a homomorphism then defines a monomorphism of the _residue field_ `A/𝔪` into the _residue field_ `B/𝔫`.
The composite of two local homomorphisms is local.

### 1.1. Radical of an ideal. Nilradical and radical of a ring

<!-- label: 0.1.1 -->

**(1.1.1)** Let `𝔞` be an ideal of a ring `A`; the _radical of_ `𝔞`, written `r(𝔞)`, is the set of `x ∈ A` such that
`xⁿ ∈ 𝔞` for some integer `n > 0`; it is an ideal containing `𝔞`. One has `r(r(𝔞)) = r(𝔞)`; the relation `𝔞 ⊂ 𝔟` entails
`r(𝔞) ⊂ r(𝔟)`; the radical of a finite intersection of ideals is the intersection of their radicals. If `φ` is a
homomorphism from a ring `A′` to `A`, one has `r(φ⁻¹(𝔞)) = φ⁻¹(r(𝔞))` for every ideal `𝔞 ⊂ A`. For an ideal to be the
radical of an ideal, it is necessary and sufficient that it be an intersection of prime ideals. The radical of an ideal
`𝔞` is the intersection of those prime ideals _minimal_ among the prime ideals containing `𝔞`; if `A` is Noetherian,
these minimal primes are finite in number.

The radical of the zero ideal `(0)` is also called the _nilradical_ of `A`; it is the set `𝔑` of nilpotent elements of
`A`. A ring `A` is said to be _reduced_ if `𝔑 = (0)`; for every ring `A`, the quotient `A/𝔑` is reduced.

**(1.1.2)** Recall that the _radical_ `ℜ(A)` of a ring `A` (not necessarily commutative) is the intersection of the
maximal left ideals of `A` (and also of the maximal right ideals). The radical of `A/ℜ(A)` is `(0)`.

### 1.2. Modules and rings of fractions

<!-- label: 0.1.2 -->

**(1.2.1)** A subset `S` of a ring `A` is called _multiplicative_ if `1 ∈ S` and the product of any two elements of `S`
lies in `S`. The examples that will matter most in what follows are:

1. the set `S_f` of powers `fⁿ` (`n ≥ 0`) of an element `f ∈ A`;
1. the complement `A − 𝔭` of a prime ideal `𝔭` of `A`.

**(1.2.2)** Let `S` be a multiplicative subset of a ring `A` and `M` an `A`-module. On the set `M × S`, the relation
between pairs `(m₁, s₁)` and `(m₂, s₂)`

> "there exists `s ∈ S` such that `s(s₁m₂ − s₂m₁) = 0`"

is an equivalence relation. We write `S⁻¹M` for the quotient set, and `m/s` for the canonical image in `S⁻¹M` of the
pair `(m, s)`; the _canonical map_ of `M` into `S⁻¹M` is the map `iˢ_M : m ↦ m/1` (also written `iˢ`). This map is in
general neither injective nor surjective; its kernel is the set of `m ∈ M` such that `sm = 0` for some `s ∈ S`.

On `S⁻¹M` we define an additive group law by

```
(m₁/s₁) + (m₂/s₂) = (s₂m₁ + s₁m₂)/(s₁s₂)
```

(one checks that this is independent of the representatives chosen). On `S⁻¹A` we define in addition a multiplicative
law by `(a₁/s₁)(a₂/s₂) = (a₁a₂)/(s₁s₂)`, and an external law on `S⁻¹M` with ring of operators `S⁻¹A` by
`(a/s)(m/s′) = (am)/(ss′)`. One checks that `S⁻¹A` is thereby a ring (called the _ring of fractions of `A` with
denominators in `S`_) and `S⁻¹M` an `S⁻¹A`-module (called the _module of fractions of `M` with denominators in `S`_);
for every `s ∈ S`, the element `s/1` is invertible in `S⁻¹A`, with inverse `1/s`. The canonical map `iˢ_A` (resp.
`iˢ_M`) is a ring homomorphism (resp. an `A`-module homomorphism, where `S⁻¹M` is viewed as an `A`-module via
`iˢ_A : A → S⁻¹A`).

**(1.2.3)** If `S_f = {fⁿ}_{n≥0}` for some `f ∈ A`, we write `A_f` and `M_f` in place of `S_f⁻¹A` and `S_f⁻¹M`; viewing
`A_f` as an algebra over `A`, we may write `A_f = A[1/f]`. The ring `A_f` is isomorphic to the quotient algebra
`A[T]/(fT − 1)A[T]`. When `f = 1`, `A_f` and `M_f` are canonically identified with `A` and `M`; if `f` is nilpotent,
`A_f` and `M_f` reduce to `0`.

When `S = A − 𝔭` for a prime ideal `𝔭` of `A`, we write `A_𝔭` and `M_𝔭` in place of `S⁻¹A` and `S⁻¹M`; here `A_𝔭` is a
_local ring_, whose maximal ideal `𝔮` is generated by `iˢ_A(𝔭)`, and one has `(iˢ_A)⁻¹(𝔮) = 𝔭`. By passage to the
quotients, `iˢ_A` gives a monomorphism of the integral ring `A/𝔭` into the field `A_𝔭/𝔮`, which is canonically
identified with the field of fractions of `A/𝔭`.

**(1.2.4)** The ring of fractions `S⁻¹A` and the canonical homomorphism `iˢ_A` solve a _universal mapping problem_:
every homomorphism `u` from `A` to a ring `B` such that `u(S)` consists of invertible elements of `B` factors uniquely
as

```
u : A ──iˢ_A──→ S⁻¹A ──u*──→ B
```

where `u*` is a ring homomorphism. Under the same hypotheses, let `M` be an `A`-module, `N` a `B`-module, and
`v : M → N` an `A`-module homomorphism (for the `B`-module structure on `N` defined by `u : A → B`); then `v` factors
uniquely as

```
v : M ──iˢ_M──→ S⁻¹M ──v*──→ N
```

where `v*` is an `S⁻¹A`-module homomorphism (for the `S⁻¹A`-module structure on `N` defined by `u*`).

**(1.2.5)** One defines a canonical isomorphism `S⁻¹A ⊗_A M ⥲ S⁻¹M` of `S⁻¹A`-modules by sending `(a/s) ⊗ m` to
`(am)/s`; the inverse isomorphism sends `m/s` to `(1/s) ⊗ m`.

**(1.2.6)** For every ideal `𝔞′` of `S⁻¹A`, `𝔞 = (iˢ_A)⁻¹(𝔞′)` is an ideal of `A`, and `𝔞′` is the ideal of `S⁻¹A`
generated by `iˢ_A(𝔞)`, which is canonically identified with `S⁻¹𝔞` (1.3.2). The map `𝔭′ ↦ (iˢ_A)⁻¹(𝔭′)` is an
order-isomorphism from the set of _prime_ ideals of `S⁻¹A` onto the set of prime ideals `𝔭` of `A` such that
`𝔭 ∩ S = ∅`. Moreover, the local rings `A_𝔭` and `(S⁻¹A)_{S⁻¹𝔭}` are then canonically (1.5.1) isomorphic.

**(1.2.7)** When `A` is an integral ring with field of fractions `K`, the canonical map `iˢ_A : A → S⁻¹A` is _injective_
for every multiplicative subset `S` not containing `0`, and `S⁻¹A` is canonically identified with a subring of `K`
containing `A`. In particular, for every prime ideal `𝔭` of `A`, `A_𝔭` is a local ring containing `A`, with maximal
ideal `𝔭A_𝔭`, and one has `𝔭A_𝔭 ∩ A = 𝔭`.

**(1.2.8)** If `A` is a reduced ring (1.1.1), so is `S⁻¹A`: indeed, if `(x/s)ⁿ = 0` for `x ∈ A`, `s ∈ S`, this means
that there is some `s′ ∈ S` with `s′xⁿ = 0`, whence `(s′x)ⁿ = 0`; the hypothesis then gives `s′x = 0`, so `x/s = 0`.

### 1.3. Functorial properties

<!-- label: 0.1.3 -->

**(1.3.1)** Let `M`, `N` be two `A`-modules and `u : M → N` an `A`-homomorphism. If `S` is a multiplicative subset of
`A`, one defines an `S⁻¹A`-homomorphism `S⁻¹M → S⁻¹N`, written `S⁻¹u`, by `(S⁻¹u)(m/s) = u(m)/s`; if `S⁻¹M` and `S⁻¹N`
are canonically identified with `S⁻¹A ⊗_A M` and `S⁻¹A ⊗_A N` (1.2.5), `S⁻¹u` is identified with `1 ⊗ u`. If `P` is a
third `A`-module and `v : N → P` an `A`-homomorphism, then `S⁻¹(v ∘ u) = (S⁻¹v) ∘ (S⁻¹u)`; in other words, `S⁻¹M` is a
_covariant functor in_ `M` from the category of `A`-modules to the category of `S⁻¹A`-modules (`A` and `S` being fixed).

**(1.3.2)** The functor `S⁻¹M` is _exact_; that is, if

```
M ──u──→ N ──v──→ P
```

is exact, so is

```
S⁻¹M ──S⁻¹u──→ S⁻¹N ──S⁻¹v──→ S⁻¹P.
```

In particular, if `u : M → N` is injective (resp. surjective), so is `S⁻¹u`; if `N` and `P` are submodules of `M`, then
`S⁻¹N` and `S⁻¹P` are canonically identified with submodules of `S⁻¹M`, and one has

```
S⁻¹(N + P) = S⁻¹N + S⁻¹P    and    S⁻¹(N ∩ P) = (S⁻¹N) ∩ (S⁻¹P).
```

**(1.3.3)** Let `(M_α, φ_{βα})` be an inductive limit [modern: direct limit] system of `A`-modules; then
`(S⁻¹M_α, S⁻¹φ_{βα})` is an inductive system of `S⁻¹A`-modules. Expressing `S⁻¹M_α` and `S⁻¹φ_{βα}` via tensor products
(1.2.5 and 1.3.1), and using the fact that tensor product commutes with inductive limits, one obtains a canonical
isomorphism

```
S⁻¹(lim⃗ M_α) ⥲ lim⃗ S⁻¹M_α,
```

which one also expresses by saying that the functor `S⁻¹M` (in `M`) _commutes with inductive limits_.

**(1.3.4)** Let `M`, `N` be two `A`-modules; there is a canonical _functorial_ isomorphism (in `M` and `N`)

```
(S⁻¹M) ⊗_{S⁻¹A} (S⁻¹N) ⥲ S⁻¹(M ⊗_A N)
```

sending `(m/s) ⊗ (n/t)` to `(m ⊗ n)/(st)`.

**(1.3.5)** Similarly one has a _functorial_ homomorphism (in `M` and `N`)

```
S⁻¹Hom_A(M, N) → Hom_{S⁻¹A}(S⁻¹M, S⁻¹N)
```

sending `u/s` to the homomorphism `m/t ↦ u(m)/(st)`. When `M` has a _finite presentation_, this homomorphism is an
_isomorphism_: this is immediate when `M` is of the form `A^r`, and one reduces from there to the general case by using
the exact sequence `A^p → A^q → M → 0`, the exactness of the functor `S⁻¹M`, and the left exactness of `Hom_A(M, N)` in
`M`. Note that this situation always obtains when `A` is _Noetherian_ and `M` is of finite type.

### 1.4. Change of multiplicative subset

<!-- label: 0.1.4 -->

**(1.4.1)** Let `S`, `T` be multiplicative subsets of a ring `A` with `S ⊂ T`; there is a canonical homomorphism
`ρ^{T,S}_A` (or simply `ρ^{T,S}`) from `S⁻¹A` to `T⁻¹A` sending the element `a/s` of `S⁻¹A` to the element `a/s` of
`T⁻¹A`; one has `iᵀ_A = ρ^{T,S}_A ∘ iˢ_A`. For every `A`-module `M`, one similarly has a map `ρ^{T,S}_M : S⁻¹M → T⁻¹M`
(the latter viewed as an `S⁻¹A`-module via `ρ^{T,S}_A`) sending `m/s` to `m/s`; this map is written `ρ^{T,S}_M`, or
simply `ρ^{T,S}`, and one has `iᵀ_M = ρ^{T,S}_M ∘ iˢ_M`. Under the canonical identification (1.2.5), `ρ^{T,S}_M` is
identified with `ρ^{T,S}_A ⊗ 1`. The map `ρ^{T,S}_M` is a _functorial morphism_ (or _natural transformation_) from the
functor `S⁻¹M` to the functor `T⁻¹M`; that is, the diagram

```
S⁻¹M ─S⁻¹u─→ S⁻¹N
  │            │
ρ^{T,S}_M     ρ^{T,S}_N
  │            │
  ↓            ↓
T⁻¹M ─T⁻¹u─→ T⁻¹N
```

commutes for every homomorphism `u : M → N`. Note moreover that `T⁻¹u` is entirely determined by `S⁻¹u`, since for
`m ∈ M` and `t ∈ T` one has

```
(T⁻¹u)(m/t) = (t/1)⁻¹ · ρ^{T,S}((S⁻¹u)(m/1)).
```

**(1.4.2)** With the same notation, for two `A`-modules `M`, `N`, the diagrams (cf. (1.3.4) and (1.3.5))

```
(S⁻¹M) ⊗_{S⁻¹A} (S⁻¹N) ⥲ S⁻¹(M ⊗_A N)       S⁻¹Hom_A(M, N) → Hom_{S⁻¹A}(S⁻¹M, S⁻¹N)
        │                       │                       │                            │
        ↓                       ↓                       ↓                            ↓
(T⁻¹M) ⊗_{T⁻¹A} (T⁻¹N) ⥲ T⁻¹(M ⊗_A N)       T⁻¹Hom_A(M, N) → Hom_{T⁻¹A}(T⁻¹M, T⁻¹N)
```

commute.

**(1.4.3)** There is an important case in which the homomorphism `ρ^{T,S}` is _bijective_: namely, when every element of
`T` divides some element of `S`; one then identifies `S⁻¹M` and `T⁻¹M` via `ρ^{T,S}`. A multiplicative set `S` is called
_saturated_ if every divisor in `A` of an element of `S` is itself in `S`; replacing `S` by the set `T` of all divisors
of elements of `S` (a multiplicative and saturated set), one sees that one may always, if one wishes, restrict to
modules of fractions `S⁻¹M` with `S` saturated.

**(1.4.4)** If `S`, `T`, `U` are three multiplicative subsets of `A` with `S ⊂ T ⊂ U`, one has

```
ρ^{U,S} = ρ^{U,T} ∘ ρ^{T,S}.
```

**(1.4.5)** Consider an increasing filtered family `(S_α)` of multiplicative subsets of `A` (we write `α ≤ β` for
`S_α ⊂ S_β`), and let `S = ⋃_α S_α`, again multiplicative. Set `ρ_{βα} = ρ^{S_β, S_α}_A` for `α ≤ β`; by (1.4.4), the
homomorphisms `ρ_{βα}` define a ring `A′` as the _inductive limit_ of the inductive system `(S_α⁻¹A, ρ_{βα})`. Let
`ρ_α : S_α⁻¹A → A′` be the canonical map, and set `φ_α = ρ^{S, S_α}_A`; since `φ_α = φ_β ∘ ρ_{βα}` for `α ≤ β` by
(1.4.4), there is a unique homomorphism `φ : A′ → S⁻¹A` such that the diagram

```
                  S_α⁻¹A
                ρ_α ↙ ↘ φ_α
              A′ ────φ──── S⁻¹A
                ↖  ρ_β    φ_β ↗
                  S_β⁻¹A
                    (α ≤ β)
```

commutes. In fact `φ` is an _isomorphism_: it is plainly surjective by construction. On the other hand, if
`ρ_α(a/s_α) ∈ A′` satisfies `φ(ρ_α(a/s_α)) = 0`, then `a/s_α = 0` in `S⁻¹A`, so there is `s ∈ S` with `sa = 0`; pick
`β ≥ α` with `s ∈ S_β`; then `ρ_α(a/s_α) = ρ_β(sa/(ss_α)) = 0`, showing `φ` injective. One handles an `A`-module `M` in
the same way, obtaining canonical isomorphisms

```
lim⃗_α S_α⁻¹A ⥲ (lim⃗ S_α)⁻¹A,    lim⃗_α S_α⁻¹M ⥲ (lim⃗ S_α)⁻¹M,
```

the second functorial in `M`.

**(1.4.6)** Let `S₁`, `S₂` be multiplicative subsets of `A`; then `S₁S₂` is also multiplicative. Let `S₂′` denote the
canonical image of `S₂` in `S₁⁻¹A`, a multiplicative subset of that ring. For every `A`-module `M` there is a functorial
isomorphism

```
S₂′⁻¹(S₁⁻¹M) ⥲ (S₁S₂)⁻¹M
```

sending `(m/s₁)/(s₂/1)` to `m/(s₁s₂)`.

### 1.5. Change of ring

<!-- label: 0.1.5 -->

**(1.5.1)** Let `A`, `A′` be two rings, `φ : A′ → A` a homomorphism, and let `S` (resp. `S′`) be a multiplicative subset
of `A` (resp. `A′`) such that `φ(S′) ⊂ S`. The composite `A′ → A → S⁻¹A` factors as `A′ → S′⁻¹A′ ──φ^{S′}──→ S⁻¹A` by
(1.2.4), with `φ^{S′}(a′/s′) = φ(a′)/φ(s′)`. If `A = φ(A′)` and `S = φ(S′)`, then `φ^{S′}` is _surjective_. If `A′ = A`
and `φ` is the identity, then `φ^{S′}` is none other than the homomorphism `ρ^{S, S′}_A` of (1.4.1).

**(1.5.2)** Under the hypotheses of (1.5.1), let `M` be an `A`-module. There is a canonical _functorial_ homomorphism

```
σ : S′⁻¹(M_[φ]) → (S⁻¹M)_[φ^{S′}]
```

of `S′⁻¹A′`-modules sending `m/s′ ∈ S′⁻¹(M_[φ])` to `m/φ(s′) ∈ (S⁻¹M)_[φ^{S′}]`; one checks at once that this is
well-defined. _When_ `S = φ(S′)`, this homomorphism `σ` is _bijective_. When `A′ = A` and `φ` is the identity, `σ` is
the homomorphism `ρ^{S, S′}_M` of (1.4.1).

When in particular `M = A`, the homomorphism `φ` makes `A` an `A′`-algebra; `S′⁻¹A` is then a ring (identified with
`(φ(S′))⁻¹A`), and `σ : S′⁻¹(A_[φ]) → S⁻¹A` is a homomorphism of `S′⁻¹A′`-algebras.

**(1.5.3)** Let `M`, `N` be two `A`-modules. Composing (1.3.4) with (1.5.2) yields a homomorphism

```
(S⁻¹M ⊗_{S⁻¹A} S⁻¹N)_[φ^{S′}] ← S′⁻¹((M ⊗_A N)_[φ])
```

which is an isomorphism when `φ(S′) = S`. Similarly, composing (1.3.5) with (1.5.2) gives a homomorphism

```
S′⁻¹((Hom_A(M, N))_[φ]) → (Hom_{S⁻¹A}(S⁻¹M, S⁻¹N))_[φ^{S′}]
```

which is an isomorphism when `φ(S′) = S` and `M` admits a finite presentation.

**(1.5.4)** Now consider an `A′`-module `N′`, and form the tensor product `N′ ⊗_{A′} A_[φ]`, which becomes an `A`-module
via `a · (n′ ⊗ b) = n′ ⊗ (ab)`. There is a functorial isomorphism of `S⁻¹A`-modules

```
τ : (S′⁻¹N′) ⊗_{S′⁻¹A′} (S⁻¹A)_[φ^{S′}] ⥲ S⁻¹(N′ ⊗_{A′} A_[φ])
```

sending `(n′/s′) ⊗ (a/s)` to `(n′ ⊗ a)/(φ(s′)s)`; one checks separately that replacing `n′/s′` (resp. `a/s`) by another
expression of the same element leaves `(n′ ⊗ a)/(φ(s′)s)` unchanged. An inverse for `τ` is given by sending `(n′ ⊗ a)/s`
to `(n′/1) ⊗ (a/s)`; one uses the canonical isomorphism
`S⁻¹(N′ ⊗_{A′} A_[φ]) ≃ (N′ ⊗_{A′} A_[φ]) ⊗_A S⁻¹A ≃ N′ ⊗_{A′} (S⁻¹A)_[ψ]` (1.2.5), where `ψ` is the composite
`a′ ↦ φ(a′)/1` of `A′ → S⁻¹A`.

**(1.5.5)** If `M′` and `N′` are two `A′`-modules, composing (1.3.4) with (1.5.4) yields an isomorphism

```
S′⁻¹M′ ⊗_{S′⁻¹A′} S′⁻¹N′ ⊗_{S′⁻¹A′} S⁻¹A ⥲ S⁻¹(M′ ⊗_{A′} N′ ⊗_{A′} A).
```

Similarly, if `M′` admits a finite presentation, then by (1.3.5) and (1.5.4) one has an isomorphism

```
Hom_{S′⁻¹A′}(S′⁻¹M′, S′⁻¹N′) ⊗_{S′⁻¹A′} S⁻¹A ⥲ S⁻¹(Hom_{A′}(M′, N′) ⊗_{A′} A).
```

**(1.5.6)** Under the hypotheses of (1.5.1), let `T` (resp. `T′`) be a second multiplicative subset of `A` (resp. `A′`)
such that `S ⊂ T` (resp. `S′ ⊂ T′`) and `φ(T′) ⊂ T`. Then the diagram

```
S′⁻¹A′ ──φ^{S′}──→ S⁻¹A
  │                  │
ρ^{T′,S′}          ρ^{T,S}
  │                  │
  ↓                  ↓
T′⁻¹A′ ──φ^{T′}──→ T⁻¹A
```

commutes. For an `A`-module `M`, the diagram

```
S′⁻¹(M_[φ]) ──σ──→ (S⁻¹M)_[φ^{S′}]
    │                    │
 ρ^{T′,S′}             ρ^{T,S}
    │                    │
    ↓                    ↓
T′⁻¹(M_[φ]) ──σ──→ (T⁻¹M)_[φ^{T′}]
```

commutes. Finally, for an `A′`-module `N′`, the diagram

```
(S′⁻¹N′) ⊗_{S′⁻¹A′} (S⁻¹A)_[φ^{S′}] ──τ──→ S⁻¹(N′ ⊗_{A′} A_[φ])
    │                                              │
    ↓                                            ρ^{T,S}
    ↓                                              ↓
(T′⁻¹N′) ⊗_{T′⁻¹A′} (T⁻¹A)_[φ^{T′}] ──τ──→ T⁻¹(N′ ⊗_{A′} A_[φ])
```

commutes, where the left-hand vertical arrow is obtained by applying `ρ^{T′,S′}_{N′}` to `S′⁻¹N′` and `ρ^{T,S}_A` to
`S⁻¹A`.

**(1.5.7)** Let `A″` be a third ring, `φ′ : A″ → A′` a ring homomorphism, and `S″` a multiplicative subset of `A″` with
`φ′(S″) ⊂ S′`. Set `φ″ = φ ∘ φ′`; then

```
φ″^{S″} = φ^{S′} ∘ φ′^{S″}.
```

For an `A`-module `M`, one has `M_[φ″] = (M_[φ])_[φ′]`; if `σ′` and `σ″` are defined from `φ′` and `φ″` as `σ` is
defined from `φ` in (1.5.2), one has the _transitivity formula_

```
σ″ = σ ∘ σ′.
```

Finally, for an `A″`-module `N″`, the `A`-module `N″ ⊗_{A″} A_[φ″]` is canonically identified with
`(N″ ⊗_{A″} A′_[φ′]) ⊗_{A′} A_[φ]`, and likewise the `S⁻¹A`-module `(S″⁻¹N″) ⊗_{S″⁻¹A″} (S⁻¹A)_[φ″^{S″}]` is canonically
identified with `((S″⁻¹N″) ⊗_{S″⁻¹A″} (S′⁻¹A′)_[φ′^{S″}]) ⊗_{S′⁻¹A′} (S⁻¹A)_[φ^{S′}]`. Under these identifications, if
`τ′` and `τ″` are defined from `φ′` and `φ″` as `τ` is defined from `φ` in (1.5.4), one has the _transitivity formula_

```
τ″ = τ ∘ (τ′ ⊗ 1).
```

**(1.5.8)** Let `A` be a subring of a ring `B`; for every _minimal_ prime ideal `𝔭` of `A`, there exists a minimal prime
ideal `𝔮` of `B` with `𝔭 = A ∩ 𝔮`. Indeed, `A_𝔭` is a subring of `B_𝔭` (1.3.2) and has _exactly one_ prime ideal `𝔭′`
(1.2.6); since `B_𝔭 ≠ 0`, it has at least one prime ideal `𝔮′`, and necessarily `𝔮′ ∩ A_𝔭 = 𝔭′`. The prime ideal `𝔮₁` of
`B` lying over `𝔮′` then satisfies `𝔮₁ ∩ A = 𝔭`, and _a fortiori_ `𝔮 ∩ A = 𝔭` for every minimal prime `𝔮` of `B`
contained in `𝔮₁`.

### 1.6. Identification of `M_f` with an inductive limit

<!-- label: 0.1.6 -->

**(1.6.1)** Let `M` be an `A`-module and `f` an element of `A`. Consider a sequence `(M_n)` of `A`-modules, all equal to
`M`, and for `m ≤ n` let `φ_{nm} : M_m → M_n` be the homomorphism `z ↦ f^{n−m}z`. The pair `((M_n), (φ_{nm}))` is an
_inductive system_ of `A`-modules; let `N = lim⃗ M_n`. We shall define a canonical _functorial_ `A`-isomorphism of `N`
onto `M_f`. To do so, note that for each `n`, `θ_n : z ↦ z/fⁿ` is an `A`-homomorphism `M_n = M → M_f`, and that
`θ_n ∘ φ_{nm} = θ_m` for `m ≤ n`. There is therefore an `A`-homomorphism `θ : N → M_f` with `θ_n = θ ∘ φ_n` for every
`n`, where `φ_n : M_n → N` is the canonical map. Since by hypothesis every element of `M_f` has the form `z/fⁿ` for some
`n`, `θ` is surjective. On the other hand, if `θ(φ_n(z)) = 0`, that is `z/fⁿ = 0`, then `f^k z = 0` for some integer
`k > 0`, so `φ_{n+k,n}(z) = 0`, whence `φ_n(z) = 0`. Thus `M_f` and `lim⃗ M_n` are identified via `θ`.

**(1.6.2)** Write `M_{f,n}`, `φ^f_{nm}`, and `φ^f_n` in place of `M_n`, `φ_{nm}`, and `φ_n`. Let `g` be a second element
of `A`. Since `fⁿ` divides `fⁿgⁿ`, there is a functorial homomorphism

```
ρ_{fg, f} : M_f → M_{fg}    (1.4.1 and 1.4.3);
```

if `M_f` and `M_{fg}` are identified with `lim⃗ M_{f,n}` and `lim⃗ M_{fg,n}` respectively, then `ρ_{fg, f}` is the
_inductive limit_ of the maps `ρⁿ_{fg, f} : M_{f,n} → M_{fg,n}` defined by `ρⁿ_{fg, f}(z) = gⁿz`. Indeed, this follows
at once from commutativity of the diagram

```
M_{f,n} ──ρⁿ_{fg,f}──→ M_{fg,n}
   │                       │
 φ^f_n                  φ^{fg}_n
   │                       │
   ↓                       ↓
  M_f ───ρ_{fg,f}───→  M_{fg}
```

### 1.7. Support of a module

<!-- label: 0.1.7 -->

**(1.7.1)** Given an `A`-module `M`, the _support_ of `M`, written `Supp(M)`, is the set of prime ideals `𝔭` of `A` such
that `M_𝔭 ≠ 0`. One has `M = 0` if and only if `Supp(M) = ∅`: indeed, if `M_𝔭 = 0` for every `𝔭`, the annihilator of any
element `x ∈ M` is not contained in any prime ideal, hence equals `A`.

**(1.7.2)** If `0 → N → M → P → 0` is an exact sequence of `A`-modules, then

```
Supp(M) = Supp(N) ∪ Supp(P),
```

since for every prime `𝔭` of `A` the sequence `0 → N_𝔭 → M_𝔭 → P_𝔭 → 0` is exact (1.3.2), and `M_𝔭 = 0` iff
`N_𝔭 = P_𝔭 = 0`.

**(1.7.3)** If `M` is the sum of a family `(M_λ)` of submodules, then `M_𝔭` is the sum of the `(M_λ)_𝔭` for every prime
`𝔭` of `A` (1.3.3 and 1.3.2), so `Supp(M) = ⋃_λ Supp(M_λ)`.

**(1.7.4)** If `M` is an `A`-module _of finite type_, then `Supp(M)` is the set of prime ideals _containing the
annihilator of_ `M`. Indeed, if `M` is monogenic, generated by `x`, then `M_𝔭 = 0` iff there is `s ∉ 𝔭` with `sx = 0`,
that is, iff `𝔭` does not contain the annihilator of `x`. If now `M` has a finite generating system `(x_i)_{1 ≤ i ≤ n}`
and `𝔞_i` is the annihilator of `x_i`, then by (1.7.3) `Supp(M)` is the set of `𝔭` containing some `𝔞_i`, equivalently
the set of `𝔭` containing `𝔞 = ⋂_i 𝔞_i`, the annihilator of `M`.

**(1.7.5)** If `M` and `N` are two `A`-modules _of finite type_, then

```
Supp(M ⊗_A N) = Supp(M) ∩ Supp(N).
```

It suffices to see, using (1.3.4), that for every prime `𝔭` of `A`, the condition `M_𝔭 ⊗_{A_𝔭} N_𝔭 ≠ 0` is equivalent to
"`M_𝔭 ≠ 0` and `N_𝔭 ≠ 0`". In other words, we must show that if `P`, `Q` are nonzero modules of finite type over a
_local_ ring `B`, then `P ⊗_B Q ≠ 0`. Let `𝔪` be the maximal ideal of `B`. By Nakayama, the vector spaces `P/𝔪P` and
`Q/𝔪Q` are nonzero, so their tensor product `(P/𝔪P) ⊗_{B/𝔪} (Q/𝔪Q) = (P ⊗_B Q) ⊗_B (B/𝔪)` is nonzero, whence the
conclusion.

In particular, if `M` is an `A`-module of finite type and `𝔞` is an ideal of `A`, then `Supp(M/𝔞M)` is the set of prime
ideals containing both `𝔞` and the annihilator `𝔫` of `M` (1.7.4) — equivalently, the set of prime ideals containing
`𝔞 + 𝔫`.
