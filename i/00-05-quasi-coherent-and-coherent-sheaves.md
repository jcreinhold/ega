# Chapter 0 — Preliminaries

## §5. Quasi-coherent and Coherent Sheaves

<!-- label: 0.5 -->

### 5.1. Quasi-coherent sheaves

<!-- label: 0.5.1 -->

**(5.1.1)** Let `(X, 𝒪_X)` be a ringed space and `ℱ` an `𝒪_X`-Module. To give a homomorphism `u : 𝒪_X → ℱ` of
`𝒪_X`-Modules is the same as to give the section `s = u(1) ∈ Γ(X, ℱ)`. Indeed, once `s` is given, for every section
`t ∈ Γ(U, 𝒪_X)` one necessarily has `u(t) = t · (s|U)`; we say that `u` is _defined by the section_ `s`. If `I` is an
arbitrary index set, consider the direct sum sheaf `𝒪_X^{(I)}`, and for `i ∈ I` let `h_i` be the canonical injection of
the `i`-th factor. The map `u ↦ (u ∘ h_i)` is an isomorphism of `Hom_{𝒪_X}(𝒪_X^{(I)}, ℱ)` onto the product
`(Hom_{𝒪_X}(𝒪_X, ℱ))^I`. There is thus a canonical bijection between homomorphisms `u : 𝒪_X^{(I)} → ℱ` and _families of
sections_ `(s_i)_{i ∈ I}` of `ℱ` over `X`. The homomorphism `u` corresponding to `(s_i)` sends
`(a_i) ∈ (Γ(U, 𝒪_X))^{(I)}` to `∑_{i ∈ I} a_i · (s_i|U)`.

`ℱ` is said to be _generated by the family_ `(s_i)` if the corresponding homomorphism `𝒪_X^{(I)} → ℱ` is _surjective_ —
equivalently, if for every `x ∈ X`, `ℱ_x` is the `𝒪_x`-module generated by the `(s_i)_x`. `ℱ` is _generated by its
sections over_ `X` if it is generated by the family of all such sections (or a subfamily); equivalently, if there is a
surjective homomorphism `𝒪_X^{(I)} → ℱ` for some `I`.

Note that an `𝒪_X`-Module `ℱ` may be such that some point `x_0 ∈ X` has the property that `ℱ|U` is not generated by its
sections over `U` for _any_ open neighborhood `U` of `x_0`: take `X = ℝ`, `𝒪_X` the simple sheaf `ℤ`, and `ℱ` the
sub-sheaf with `ℱ_0 = {0}` and `ℱ_x = ℤ` for `x ≠ 0`, and `x_0 = 0`. The only section of `ℱ|U` over `U` is `0` for a
neighborhood `U` of `0`.

**(5.1.2)** Let `f : X → Y` be a morphism of ringed spaces. If `ℱ` is an `𝒪_X`-Module generated by its sections over
`X`, then the canonical homomorphism `f*(f_*(ℱ)) → ℱ` (4.4.3.3) is _surjective_: with the notation of (5.1.1), `s_i ⊗ 1`
is a section of `f*(f_*(ℱ))` over `X`, and its image in `ℱ` is `s_i`. The example of (5.1.1) with `f` the identity shows
that the converse fails in general.

**(5.1.3)** An `𝒪_X`-Module `ℱ` is said to be _quasi-coherent_ if for every `x ∈ X` there is an open neighborhood `U`
such that `ℱ|U` is isomorphic to the _cokernel_ of a homomorphism `𝒪_X^{(I)}|U → 𝒪_X^{(J)}|U` for some index sets
`I, J`. Clearly `𝒪_X` itself is quasi-coherent, and every direct sum of quasi-coherent `𝒪_X`-Modules is quasi-coherent.
An `𝒪_X`-Algebra `𝒜` is _quasi-coherent_ if it is quasi-coherent as an `𝒪_X`-Module.

**(5.1.4)** Let `f : X → Y` be a morphism of ringed spaces. If `𝒢` is a quasi-coherent `𝒪_Y`-Module, then `f*(𝒢)` is a
quasi-coherent `𝒪_X`-Module. Indeed, for every `x ∈ X` there is an open neighborhood `V` of `f(x)` in `Y` such that
`𝒢|V` is the cokernel of `𝒪_Y^{(I)}|V → 𝒪_Y^{(J)}|V`. Set `U = f⁻¹(V)` and let `f_U = f|U`; then `f*(𝒢)|U = f_U*(𝒢|V)`.
Since `f_U*` is right exact and commutes with direct sums, `f_U*(𝒢|V)` is the cokernel of `𝒪_X^{(I)}|U → 𝒪_X^{(J)}|U`.

### 5.2. Sheaves of finite type

<!-- label: 0.5.2 -->

**(5.2.1)** An `𝒪_X`-Module `ℱ` is said to be _of finite type_ if every `x ∈ X` admits an open neighborhood `U` such
that `ℱ|U` is generated by _finitely many_ sections over `U`; equivalently, `ℱ|U` is a quotient sheaf of `(𝒪_X|U)^p` for
some finite `p`. Every quotient of an `𝒪_X`-Module of finite type is of finite type; so is every finite direct sum and
every finite tensor product of `𝒪_X`-Modules of finite type. An `𝒪_X`-Module of finite type need not be quasi-coherent,
as one sees for `𝒪_X / ℱ` where `ℱ` is the sheaf of (5.1.1). If `ℱ` is of finite type, then `ℱ_x` is an `𝒪_x`-module of
finite type for every `x ∈ X`; the example of (5.1.1) shows that this necessary condition is not in general sufficient.

**(5.2.2)** Let `ℱ` be an `𝒪_X`-Module _of finite type_. If `s_i` (`1 ≤ i ≤ n`) are sections of `ℱ` over an open
neighborhood `U` of a point `x ∈ X` and the `(s_i)_x` generate `ℱ_x`, then there is an open neighborhood `V ⊂ U` of `x`
such that the `(s_i)_y` generate `ℱ_y` for every `y ∈ V` (FAC, I, §2, no. 12, Prop. 1). In particular, the support of
`ℱ` is _closed_.

Similarly, if `u : ℱ → 𝒢` is a homomorphism with `u_x = 0`, there is a neighborhood `U` of `x` such that `u_y = 0` for
every `y ∈ U`.

**(5.2.3)** Suppose `X` is _quasi-compact_ and let `ℱ`, `𝒢` be `𝒪_X`-Modules with `𝒢` of _finite type_, `u : ℱ → 𝒢` a
_surjective_ homomorphism. Suppose moreover that `ℱ = lim⃗ ℱ_λ` is the inductive limit of a system of `𝒪_X`-Modules.
Then there exists an index `μ` such that the homomorphism `ℱ_μ → 𝒢` is _surjective_. Indeed, for every `x ∈ X` there is
a finite system of sections `s_i` of `𝒢` over an open neighborhood `U(x)` of `x` whose germs generate `𝒢_y` for every
`y ∈ U(x)`; there is also an open `V(x) ⊂ U(x)` and sections `t_i` of `ℱ` over `V(x)` with `s_i|V(x) = u(t_i)`. We may
assume the `t_i` are canonical images of sections of some `ℱ_{λ(x)}` over `V(x)`. Cover `X` by finitely many `V(x_k)`
and let `μ` exceed all the `λ(x_k)`; plainly `μ` answers the question.

Still assuming `X` quasi-compact, if `ℱ` is of finite type and generated by its sections over `X` (5.1.1), then `ℱ` is
generated by a _finite_ subfamily of those sections: cover `X` by finitely many open `U_k` such that for each `k` there
are finitely many sections `s_{ik}` of `ℱ` over `X` whose restrictions to `U_k` generate `ℱ|U_k`; plainly the `s_{ik}`
then generate `ℱ`.

**(5.2.4)** Let `f : X → Y` be a morphism of ringed spaces. If `𝒢` is an `𝒪_Y`-Module of finite type, then `f*(𝒢)` is an
`𝒪_X`-Module of finite type. Indeed, for every `x ∈ X` there is an open `V` containing `f(x)` and a surjective
homomorphism `v : 𝒪_Y^p|V → 𝒢|V`. Set `U = f⁻¹(V)` and `f_U = f|U`; then `f*(𝒢)|U = f_U*(𝒢|V)`. Since `f_U*` is right
exact (4.3.1) and commutes with direct sums, `f_U*(v)` is a surjective homomorphism `𝒪_X^p|U → f*(𝒢)|U`.

**(5.2.5)** An `𝒪_X`-Module `ℱ` _admits a finite presentation_ if for every `x ∈ X` there is an open neighborhood `U`
such that `ℱ|U` is isomorphic to the cokernel of a `(𝒪_X|U)`-homomorphism `𝒪_X^p|U → 𝒪_X^q|U` with `p, q > 0` finite.
Such an `𝒪_X`-Module is of finite type and quasi-coherent. If `f : X → Y` is a morphism of ringed spaces and `𝒢` admits
a finite presentation, so does `f*(𝒢)`, by the argument of (5.1.4).

**(5.2.6)** Let `ℱ` be an `𝒪_X`-Module admitting a finite presentation (5.2.5). Then for every `𝒪_X`-Module `ℋ`, the
canonical functorial homomorphism

```
(ℋom_{𝒪_X}(ℱ, ℋ))_x → Hom_{𝒪_x}(ℱ_x, ℋ_x)
```

is _bijective_ (T, 4.1.1).

**(5.2.7)** Let `ℱ`, `𝒢` be `𝒪_X`-Modules admitting finite presentations. If for some `x ∈ X` the `𝒪_x`-modules `ℱ_x`
and `𝒢_x` are _isomorphic_, then there is an open neighborhood `U` of `x` such that `ℱ|U` and `𝒢|U` are _isomorphic_.
Indeed, if `φ : ℱ_x → 𝒢_x` and `ψ : 𝒢_x → ℱ_x` are inverse isomorphisms, then by (5.2.6) there is an open `V ∋ x` and
sections `u` (resp. `v`) of `ℋom_{𝒪_X}(ℱ, 𝒢)` (resp. `ℋom_{𝒪_X}(𝒢, ℱ)`) over `V` with `u_x = φ` (resp. `v_x = ψ`). Since
`(u ∘ v)_x` and `(v ∘ u)_x` are the identity automorphisms, there is an open `U ⊂ V` containing `x` on which `(u ∘ v)|U`
and `(v ∘ u)|U` are identities, whence the assertion.

### 5.3. Coherent sheaves

<!-- label: 0.5.3 -->

**(5.3.1)** An `𝒪_X`-Module `ℱ` is _coherent_ if:

> a) `ℱ` is of finite type; b) For every open `U ⊂ X`, every integer `n > 0`, and every homomorphism
> `u : 𝒪_X^n|U → ℱ|U`, the kernel of `u` is of finite type.

Both conditions are _local_.

For most of the proofs of the properties of coherent sheaves recalled below, see (FAC), I, §2.

**(5.3.2)** Every coherent `𝒪_X`-Module admits a finite presentation (5.2.5); the converse is not in general true, since
`𝒪_X` itself need not be coherent.

Every sub-`𝒪_X`-Module _of finite type_ of a coherent `𝒪_X`-Module is coherent; every _finite_ direct sum of coherent
`𝒪_X`-Modules is coherent.

**(5.3.3)** In an exact sequence `0 → ℱ → 𝒢 → ℋ → 0` of `𝒪_X`-Modules, if two are coherent then so is the third.

**(5.3.4)** If `ℱ` and `𝒢` are coherent `𝒪_X`-Modules and `u : ℱ → 𝒢` is a homomorphism, then `Im(u)`, `Ker(u)`, and
`Coker(u)` are coherent. In particular, sub-`𝒪_X`-Modules `ℱ`, `𝒢` of a coherent `𝒪_X`-Module have `ℱ + 𝒢` and `ℱ ∩ 𝒢`
coherent.

**(5.3.5)** If `ℱ` and `𝒢` are coherent `𝒪_X`-Modules, so are `ℱ ⊗_{𝒪_X} 𝒢` and `ℋom_{𝒪_X}(ℱ, 𝒢)`.

**(5.3.6)** Let `ℱ` be coherent and `𝒥` a coherent sheaf of ideals of `𝒪_X`. Then `𝒥ℱ` is coherent, being the image of
`𝒥 ⊗_{𝒪_X} ℱ` under the canonical map `𝒥 ⊗_{𝒪_X} ℱ → ℱ` (5.3.4 and 5.3.5).

**(5.3.7)** An `𝒪_X`-Algebra `𝒜` is _coherent_ if it is a coherent `𝒪_X`-Module. In particular, `𝒪_X` is a _coherent
sheaf of rings_ if and only if, for every open `U ⊂ X` and every `u : 𝒪_X^n|U → 𝒪_X|U`, the kernel of `u` is a
`(𝒪_X|U)`-Module of finite type.

If `𝒪_X` is a coherent sheaf of rings, every `𝒪_X`-Module `ℱ` admitting a finite presentation (5.2.5) is coherent, by
(5.3.4).

The _annihilator_ of an `𝒪_X`-Module `ℱ` is the kernel `𝒥` of the canonical homomorphism `𝒪_X → ℋom_{𝒪_X}(ℱ, ℱ)` sending
`s ∈ Γ(U, 𝒪_X)` to multiplication by `s` in `Hom(ℱ|U, ℱ|U)`. If `𝒪_X` is coherent and `ℱ` coherent, then `𝒥` is coherent
(5.3.4 and 5.3.5), and `𝒥_x` is the annihilator of `ℱ_x` for every `x ∈ X` (5.2.6).

**(5.3.8)** Suppose `𝒪_X` is coherent. Let `ℱ` be a coherent `𝒪_X`-Module, `x ∈ X`, and `M` a finite-type submodule of
`ℱ_x`. There is an open neighborhood `U` of `x` and a coherent sub-`(𝒪_X|U)`-Module `𝒢` of `ℱ|U` with `𝒢_x = M` (T, 4.1,
Lemma 1).

This, together with the properties of sub-`𝒪_X`-Modules of a coherent `𝒪_X`-Module, imposes necessary conditions on the
rings `𝒪_x` for `𝒪_X` to be coherent. For example (5.3.4), the intersection of two finite-type ideals of `𝒪_x` must
still be a finite-type ideal.

**(5.3.9)** Suppose `𝒪_X` is coherent, and let `M` be an `𝒪_x`-module admitting a finite presentation, so the cokernel
of some `φ : 𝒪_x^p → 𝒪_x^q`. There is an open neighborhood `U` of `x` and a coherent `(𝒪_X|U)`-Module `ℱ` with
`ℱ_x ≅ M`. Indeed, by (5.2.6) there is a section `u` of `ℋom_{𝒪_X}(𝒪_X^p, 𝒪_X^q)` over some `U` with `u_x = φ`; the
cokernel of `u : 𝒪_X^p|U → 𝒪_X^q|U` answers the question (5.3.4).

**(5.3.10)** Suppose `𝒪_X` is coherent and `𝒥` is a coherent sheaf of ideals of `𝒪_X`. For an `(𝒪_X / 𝒥)`-Module `ℱ` to
be coherent, it is necessary and sufficient that it be coherent as an `𝒪_X`-Module. In particular `𝒪_X / 𝒥` is a
coherent sheaf of rings.

**(5.3.11)** Let `f : X → Y` be a morphism of ringed spaces, and suppose `𝒪_X` is coherent. Then for every coherent
`𝒪_Y`-Module `𝒢`, `f*(𝒢)` is coherent. Indeed, with the notation of (5.2.4), one may assume `𝒢|V` is the cokernel of
`v : 𝒪_Y^q|V → 𝒪_Y^p|V`. Since `f_U*` is right exact, `f*(𝒢)|U = f_U*(𝒢|V)` is the cokernel of
`f_U*(v) : 𝒪_X^q|U → 𝒪_X^p|U`.

**(5.3.12)** Let `Y ⊂ X` be a closed subset, `j : Y → X` the canonical injection, `𝒪_Y` a sheaf of rings on `Y`, and
`𝒪_X = j_*(𝒪_Y)`. An `𝒪_Y`-Module `𝒢` is of finite type (resp. quasi-coherent, coherent) if and only if `j_*(𝒢)` is of
finite type (resp. quasi-coherent, coherent) as an `𝒪_X`-Module.

### 5.4. Locally free sheaves

<!-- label: 0.5.4 -->

**(5.4.1)** Let `X` be a ringed space. An `𝒪_X`-Module `ℱ` is _locally free_ if for every `x ∈ X` there is an open
neighborhood `U` such that `ℱ|U` is isomorphic to `𝒪_X^{(I)}|U` for some `I` (which may depend on `U`). If `I` may
always be taken finite, `ℱ` is _of finite rank_; if `I` may always be taken finite with the same number of elements `n`,
`ℱ` is _of rank_ `n`. A locally free `𝒪_X`-Module of rank 1 is also called _invertible_ (cf. (5.4.3)). If `ℱ` is locally
free of finite rank, then for every `x ∈ X`, `ℱ_x` is a free `𝒪_x`-module of finite rank `n(x)`, and there is a
neighborhood `U` of `x` on which `ℱ|U` has rank `n(x)`; if `X` is connected, `n(x)` is _constant_.

Every locally free sheaf is quasi-coherent; if `𝒪_X` is a coherent sheaf of rings, every locally free `𝒪_X`-Module of
finite rank is coherent.

If `ℒ` is locally free, then `ℒ ⊗_{𝒪_X} ℱ` is _exact_ in `ℱ` on `𝒪_X`-Modules.

We shall mostly consider locally free `𝒪_X`-Modules of finite rank; when we speak of locally free sheaves without
qualification, _of finite rank_ is to be understood.

**(5.4.2)** If `ℒ`, `ℱ` are two `𝒪_X`-Modules, there is a canonical functorial homomorphism

```
(5.4.2.1)    ℒ̌ ⊗_{𝒪_X} ℱ = ℋom_{𝒪_X}(ℒ, 𝒪_X) ⊗_{𝒪_X} ℱ → ℋom_{𝒪_X}(ℒ, ℱ)
```

defined as follows: for `U` open and `(u, t)` with `u ∈ Γ(U, ℋom_{𝒪_X}(ℒ, 𝒪_X)) = Hom(ℒ|U, 𝒪_X|U)` and `t ∈ Γ(U, ℱ)`,
send `(u, t)` to the element of `Hom(ℒ|U, ℱ|U)` carrying `s_x ∈ ℒ_x` to `u_x(s_x) · t_x ∈ ℱ_x`. If `ℒ` is locally free
of finite rank, this map is _bijective_: the question being local, reduce to `ℒ = 𝒪_X^n`; since
`ℋom_{𝒪_X}(𝒪_X^n, 𝒢) ≅ 𝒢^n` canonically for every `𝒪_X`-Module `𝒢`, the assertion reduces to `ℒ = 𝒪_X`, which is
immediate.

**(5.4.3)** If `ℒ` is invertible, so is its dual `ℒ̌ = ℋom_{𝒪_X}(ℒ, 𝒪_X)`, as one sees locally by reducing to `ℒ = 𝒪_X`.
Moreover, there is a canonical isomorphism

```
(5.4.3.1)    ℋom_{𝒪_X}(ℒ, 𝒪_X) ⊗_{𝒪_X} ℒ ⥲ 𝒪_X.
```

Indeed, by (5.4.2) it suffices to define a canonical isomorphism `ℋom_{𝒪_X}(ℒ, ℒ) ⥲ 𝒪_X`. For _any_ `𝒪_X`-Module `ℱ`
there is a canonical homomorphism `𝒪_X → ℋom_{𝒪_X}(ℱ, ℱ)` (5.3.7); when `ℱ = ℒ` is invertible, this homomorphism is
bijective — locally the question reduces to `ℒ = 𝒪_X`, which is immediate.

Accordingly, write `ℒ⁻¹ = ℋom_{𝒪_X}(ℒ, 𝒪_X)`, called the _inverse_ of `ℒ`. The terminology "invertible sheaf" is
motivated as follows for `X` a single point and `𝒪_X` a _local_ ring `A` with maximal ideal `𝔪`. If `M`, `M′` are
`A`-modules with `M` of finite type and `M ⊗_A M′ ≅ A`, then `(A/𝔪) ⊗_A (M ⊗_A M′) ≅ (M/𝔪M) ⊗_{A/𝔪} (M′/𝔪M′)`; this
tensor product of `A/𝔪`-vector spaces is `≅ A/𝔪`, forcing `M/𝔪M` and `M′/𝔪M′` to be 1-dimensional. For any `z ∈ M ∖ 𝔪M`,
`M = Az + 𝔪M`, so `M = Az` by Nakayama (since `M` is of finite type). The annihilator of `z` annihilates `M ⊗_A M′ ≅ A`,
hence is `(0)`; so `M ≅ A`. In general, this shows: if `ℒ` is an `𝒪_X`-Module of finite type with some `𝒪_X`-Module `ℱ`
satisfying `ℒ ⊗_{𝒪_X} ℱ ≅ 𝒪_X`, and the rings `𝒪_x` are local, then `ℒ_x ≅ 𝒪_x` for every `x ∈ X`. If `𝒪_X` and `ℒ` are
coherent, one concludes that `ℒ` is invertible by (5.2.7).

**(5.4.4)** If `ℒ` and `ℒ′` are invertible `𝒪_X`-Modules, so is `ℒ ⊗_{𝒪_X} ℒ′` (reducing locally to `ℒ = ℒ′ = 𝒪_X`). For
`n ≥ 1`, write `ℒ^{⊗n}` for the tensor product of `n` copies of `ℒ`; set `ℒ^{⊗0} = 𝒪_X` and `ℒ^{⊗(−n)} = (ℒ⁻¹)^{⊗n}`.
There is a canonical functorial isomorphism

```
(5.4.4.1)    ℒ^{⊗m} ⊗_{𝒪_X} ℒ^{⊗n} ⥲ ℒ^{⊗(n+m)}
```

for any integers `m, n`: by the definitions one reduces to `m = −1, n = 1`, the case treated in (5.4.3).

**(5.4.5)** Let `f : Y → X` be a morphism of ringed spaces. If `ℒ` is a locally free (resp. invertible) `𝒪_X`-Module,
then `f*(ℒ)` is a locally free (resp. invertible) `𝒪_Y`-Module — using that inverse images of locally isomorphic sheaves
are locally isomorphic, that `f*` commutes with finite direct sums, and that `f*(𝒪_X) = 𝒪_Y` (4.3.4). There is a
canonical functorial homomorphism `f*(ℒ̌) → (f*(ℒ))̌` (4.4.6) which is _bijective_ when `ℒ` is locally free (reducing
again to `ℒ = 𝒪_X`). Consequently, for `ℒ` invertible, `f*(ℒ^{⊗n})` is canonically identified with `(f*(ℒ))^{⊗n}` for
every integer `n`.

**(5.4.6)** Let `ℒ` be an invertible `𝒪_X`-Module. Write `Γ_*(X, ℒ)` (or simply `Γ_*(ℒ)`) for the abelian group
`⊕_{n ∈ ℤ} Γ(X, ℒ^{⊗n})`, made a _graded ring_ by the multiplication sending `(s_n, s_m) ∈ Γ(X, ℒ^{⊗n}) × Γ(X, ℒ^{⊗m})`
to the section of `ℒ^{⊗(n+m)}` corresponding canonically (5.4.4.1) to `s_n ⊗ s_m`. Associativity is immediate. Plainly
`Γ_*(X, ℒ)` is a covariant functor in `ℒ` with values in graded rings.

For any `𝒪_X`-Module `ℱ`, set

```
Γ_*(ℒ, ℱ) = ⊕_{n ∈ ℤ} Γ(X, ℱ ⊗_{𝒪_X} ℒ^{⊗n}),
```

made a _graded module_ over `Γ_*(ℒ)` by sending `(s_n, u_m) ∈ Γ(X, ℒ^{⊗n}) × Γ(X, ℱ ⊗ ℒ^{⊗m})` to the section of
`ℱ ⊗ ℒ^{⊗(n+m)}` corresponding canonically (5.4.4.1) to `s_n ⊗ u_m`. The module axioms hold by inspection. For `X, ℒ`
fixed, `Γ_*(ℒ, ℱ)` is a covariant functor in `ℱ` with values in graded `Γ_*(ℒ)`-modules; for `X, ℱ` fixed, a covariant
functor in `ℒ`.

If `f : Y → X` is a morphism of ringed spaces, the canonical homomorphism (4.4.3.2) `ρ : ℒ^{⊗n} → f_*(f*(ℒ^{⊗n}))`
defines `Γ(X, ℒ^{⊗n}) → Γ(Y, f*(ℒ^{⊗n}))`; since `f*(ℒ^{⊗n}) = (f*(ℒ))^{⊗n}`, the canonical homomorphisms (4.4.3.2) and
(5.4.4.1) yield a _functorial homomorphism of graded rings_ `Γ_*(ℒ) → Γ_*(f*(ℒ))`. The same canonical homomorphism
(4.4.3) similarly yields `Γ(X, ℱ ⊗ ℒ^{⊗n}) → Γ(Y, f*(ℱ ⊗ ℒ^{⊗n}))`; using

```
f*(ℱ ⊗ ℒ^{⊗n}) = f*(ℱ) ⊗ (f*(ℒ))^{⊗n}    (4.3.3.1),
```

these (for varying `n`) define a _di-homomorphism of graded modules_ `Γ_*(ℒ, ℱ) → Γ_*(f*(ℒ), f*(ℱ))`.

**(5.4.7)** One can show that there is a _set_ `𝔐` (also written `𝔐(X)`) of invertible `𝒪_X`-Modules such that every
invertible `𝒪_X`-Module is isomorphic to a unique element of `𝔐`.[^5-1] Endow `𝔐` with the composition law sending
`(ℒ, ℒ′)` to the unique element of `𝔐` isomorphic to `ℒ ⊗ ℒ′`. With this law, `𝔐` is a _group_ isomorphic to the
cohomology group `H¹(X, 𝒪_X^*)`, where `𝒪_X^*` is the subsheaf of `𝒪_X` whose sections over `U` are the invertible
elements of `Γ(U, 𝒪_X)` (so `𝒪_X^*` is a sheaf of _multiplicative_ abelian groups).

For this, note that for every open `U ⊂ X`, `Γ(U, 𝒪_X^*)` is canonically identified with the _automorphism group_ of the
`(𝒪_X|U)`-Module `𝒪_X|U`: a section `ε` corresponds to the automorphism `u` with `u_x(s_x) = ε_x · s_x`. Given an open
cover `𝔘 = (U_λ)` of `X`, giving an automorphism `θ_{λμ}` of `𝒪_X|(U_λ ∩ U_μ)` for each pair amounts to giving a
1-_cochain_ on `𝔘` with values in `𝒪_X^*`; the gluing condition (3.3.1) for the `θ_{λμ}` says exactly that the cochain
is a _cocycle_. Likewise, giving an automorphism `ω_λ` of `𝒪_X|U_λ` for each `λ` is a 0-cochain, and its _coboundary_ is
the family `(ω_λ|U_λ ∩ U_μ) ∘ (ω_μ|U_λ ∩ U_μ)⁻¹`. To each 1-cocycle of `𝔘` we associate the element of `𝔐` isomorphic to
the invertible `𝒪_X`-Module obtained by gluing via the `(θ_{λμ})`; cohomologous cocycles yield the same element of `𝔐`
(3.3.2). This defines `φ_𝔘 : H¹(𝔘, 𝒪_X^*) → 𝔐`. If `𝔙` refines `𝔘`, the diagram

```
H¹(𝔘, 𝒪_X^*) ──φ_𝔘──→ 𝔐
     │              ↗
     ↓           φ_𝔙
H¹(𝔙, 𝒪_X^*)
```

(left arrow the canonical homomorphism (G, II, 5.7)) commutes, by (3.3.3). Passage to the inductive limit gives
`H¹(X, 𝒪_X^*) → 𝔐`, since Čech `Ȟ¹` agrees with derived `H¹` here (G, II, 5.9, Cor. of Th. 5.9.1). This map is
_surjective_: every invertible `ℒ` is obtained by gluing copies of `𝒪_X|U_λ` for some cover. It is _injective_: this
reduces to injectivity of each `φ_𝔘`, which is (3.3.2). To see it is a group homomorphism: for invertible `ℒ, ℒ′`,
choose a cover `(U_λ)` and `a_λ ∈ Γ(U_λ, ℒ)`, `a′_λ ∈ Γ(U_λ, ℒ′)` with `Γ(U_λ, ℒ) = Γ(U_λ, 𝒪_X) · a_λ`,
`Γ(U_λ, ℒ′) = Γ(U_λ, 𝒪_X) · a′_λ`. The corresponding cocycles `(ε_{λμ})`, `(ε′_{λμ})` satisfy `s_λ · a_λ = s_μ · a_μ`
iff `s_λ = ε_{λμ} s_μ` (similarly for `ℒ′`). Sections of `ℒ ⊗_{𝒪_X} ℒ′` over `U_λ` are finite sums of
`s_λ s′_λ · (a_λ ⊗ a′_λ)`; hence `(ε_{λμ} ε′_{λμ})` is the cocycle of `ℒ ⊗_{𝒪_X} ℒ′`, completing the proof.[^5-2]

**(5.4.8)** Let `f = (ψ, ω) : Y → X` be a morphism of ringed spaces. The functor `f*(ℒ)` on invertible `𝒪_X`-Modules
defines (by abuse of language, still written `f*`) a map `𝔐(X) → 𝔐(Y)`. There is also a canonical homomorphism (T,
3.2.2)

```
(5.4.8.1)    H¹(X, 𝒪_X^*) → H¹(Y, 𝒪_Y^*).
```

Under the canonical identifications (5.4.7) `𝔐(X) ≅ H¹(X, 𝒪_X^*)` (resp. `𝔐(Y) ≅ H¹(Y, 𝒪_Y^*)`), the homomorphism
(5.4.8.1) _is identified with_ `f*`. Indeed, if `ℒ` comes from a cocycle `(ε_{λμ})` for a cover `(U_λ)` of `X`, it
suffices to show `f*(ℒ)` comes from a cocycle whose cohomology class is the image under (5.4.8.1) of that of `(ε_{λμ})`.
If `θ_{λμ}` is the automorphism corresponding to `ε_{λμ}`, then `f*(ℒ)` is obtained by gluing the `𝒪_Y|ψ⁻¹(U_λ)` via the
`f*(θ_{λμ})`; one checks these correspond to the cocycle `(ω^♯(ε_{λμ}))` (after identifying `ε_{λμ}` with its canonical
image under `ρ` (3.7.2), a section of `ψ*(𝒪_X^*)` over `ψ⁻¹(U_λ ∩ U_μ)`).

**(5.4.9)** Let `ℰ`, `ℱ` be `𝒪_X`-Modules with `ℱ` _locally free_, and let `𝒢` be an _extension of_ `ℱ` _by_ `ℰ` — an
exact sequence `0 → ℰ ──j──→ 𝒢 ──p──→ ℱ → 0`. Then for every `x ∈ X` there is an open `U ∋ x` such that
`𝒢|U ≅ (ℰ|U) ⊕ (ℱ|U)`. One reduces to `ℱ = 𝒪_X^n`; let `e_i` (`1 ≤ i ≤ n`) be the canonical sections of `𝒪_X^n` (5.5.5).
There is an open `U` and sections `s_i ∈ Γ(U, 𝒢)` with `p(s_i|U) = e_i|U` (`1 ≤ i ≤ n`). Let `f : ℱ|U → 𝒢|U` be the
homomorphism defined by the `s_i|U` (5.1.1). For open `V ⊂ U` and `s ∈ Γ(V, 𝒢)`, `s − f(p(s)) ∈ Γ(V, ℰ)`, whence the
assertion.

**(5.4.10)** Let `f : X → Y` be a morphism of ringed spaces, `ℱ` an `𝒪_X`-Module, and `ℒ` a locally free `𝒪_Y`-Module of
finite rank. There is a canonical isomorphism

```
(5.4.10.1)    f_*(ℱ) ⊗_{𝒪_Y} ℒ ⥲ f_*(ℱ ⊗_{𝒪_X} f*(ℒ)).
```

Indeed, for any `𝒪_Y`-Module `ℒ` there is a canonical homomorphism

```
f_*(ℱ) ⊗_{𝒪_Y} ℒ ──1⊗ρ──→ f_*(ℱ) ⊗_{𝒪_Y} f_*(f*(ℒ)) ──α──→ f_*(ℱ ⊗_{𝒪_X} f*(ℒ))
```

(`ρ` from (4.4.3.2), `α` from (4.2.2.1)). To see this is an isomorphism when `ℒ` is locally free, the question being
local on `Y`, reduce to `ℒ = 𝒪_Y^n`; since `f_*` and `f*` commute with finite direct sums, reduce to `n = 1`, where the
assertion follows immediately from the definitions and `f*(𝒪_Y) = 𝒪_X`.

### 5.5. Sheaves on a space ringed in local rings

<!-- label: 0.5.5 -->

**(5.5.1)** A ringed space `(X, 𝒪_X)` is _ringed in local rings_ if for every `x ∈ X`, `𝒪_x` is a local ring; these are
by far the most common ringed spaces we shall encounter. We then write `𝔪_x` for the maximal ideal of `𝒪_x` and `𝑘(x)`
for the residue field `𝒪_x / 𝔪_x`. For an `𝒪_X`-Module `ℱ`, an open `U ⊂ X`, a point `x ∈ U`, and a section
`f ∈ Γ(U, ℱ)`, we write `f(x)` for the class of the germ `f_x ∈ ℱ_x` mod `𝔪_x ℱ_x`, and call it the _value of_ `f` _at_
`x`. The relation `f(x) = 0` thus means `f_x ∈ 𝔪_x ℱ_x`; when it holds, we say (by abuse of language) that `f` _vanishes
at_ `x`. Do not confuse this with `f_x = 0`.

**(5.5.2)** Let `X` be a space ringed in local rings, `ℒ` an invertible `𝒪_X`-Module, and `f` a section of `ℒ` over `X`.
At a point `x ∈ X`, the following are equivalent:

> a) `f_x` is a generator of `ℒ_x`; b) `f_x ∉ 𝔪_x ℒ_x` (i.e., `f(x) ≠ 0`); c) There is a section `g` of `ℒ⁻¹` over some
> open `V ∋ x` such that the canonical image of `f ⊗ g` in `Γ(V, 𝒪_X)` (5.4.3) is the unit section.

Indeed, the question being local, reduce to `ℒ = 𝒪_X`. Equivalence of a) and b) is then immediate, and c) ⟹ b). For b) ⟹
c): if `f_x ∉ 𝔪_x`, `f_x` is invertible in `𝒪_x`, say `f_x · g_x = 1_x`; by the definition of germs, there is a
neighborhood `V ∋ x` and a section `g` over `V` with `fg = 1` on `V`.

By c), the set `X_f` of `x` satisfying the equivalent conditions a)–c) is _open_ in `X`; in the terminology of (5.5.1),
it is the set of `x` _where_ `f` _does not vanish_.

**(5.5.3)** Under the hypotheses of (5.5.2), let `ℒ′` be a second invertible `𝒪_X`-Module. For `f ∈ Γ(X, ℒ)`,
`g ∈ Γ(X, ℒ′)`,

```
X_f ∩ X_g = X_{f ⊗ g}.
```

Reduce locally to `ℒ = ℒ′ = 𝒪_X`; then `f ⊗ g` is canonically identified with the product `fg`, and the assertion is
obvious.

**(5.5.4)** Let `ℱ` be a locally free `𝒪_X`-Module of rank `n`. Then `⋀^p ℱ` is locally free of rank `(n choose p)` if
`p ≤ n`, and zero if `p > n` — the question being local, reduce to `ℱ = 𝒪_X^n`. For every `x ∈ X`,
`(⋀^p ℱ)_x / 𝔪_x (⋀^p ℱ)_x` is a `𝑘(x)`-vector space of dimension `(n choose p)`, canonically identified with
`⋀^p (ℱ_x / 𝔪_x ℱ_x)`. For sections `s_1, …, s_p` of `ℱ` over an open `U`, set `s = s_1 ∧ … ∧ s_p ∈ Γ(U, ⋀^p ℱ)`
(4.1.5); then `s(x) = s_1(x) ∧ … ∧ s_p(x)`, so `s_1(x), …, s_p(x)` are _linearly dependent_ iff `s(x) = 0`. Hence _the
set of_ `x ∈ X` _at which_ `s_1(x), …, s_p(x)` _are linearly independent is open in_ `X`: reducing to `ℱ = 𝒪_X^n` and
applying (5.5.2) to the image of `s` under a projection of `⋀^p ℱ = 𝒪_X^{(n choose p)}` onto one of its `(n choose p)`
factors.

In particular, if `s_1, …, s_n` are `n` sections of `ℱ` over `U` with `s_1(x), …, s_n(x)` linearly independent at every
`x ∈ U`, then the homomorphism `u : 𝒪_X^n|U → ℱ|U` defined by the `s_i` (5.1.1) is an _isomorphism_: reduce to
`ℱ = 𝒪_X^n` and identify `⋀^n ℱ` with `𝒪_X`; then `s = s_1 ∧ … ∧ s_n` is an invertible section of `𝒪_X` over `U`, and an
inverse to `u` is given by Cramer's rule.

**(5.5.5)** Let `ℰ`, `ℱ` be locally free `𝒪_X`-Modules (of finite rank), and let `u : ℰ → ℱ` be a homomorphism. For
there to exist an open `U ∋ x` such that `u|U` is _injective_ and `ℱ|U` is the _direct sum of_ `u(ℰ)|U` _and a locally
free sub-`(𝒪_X|U)`-Module_, it is necessary and sufficient that `u_x : ℰ_x → ℱ_x` induce an _injective_ `𝑘(x)`-linear
map `ℰ_x / 𝔪_x ℰ_x → ℱ_x / 𝔪_x ℱ_x`. The condition is _necessary_: `ℱ_x` is then the direct sum of the free
`𝒪_x`-modules `u_x(ℰ_x)` and `𝒢_x`, so `ℱ_x / 𝔪_x ℱ_x` is the direct sum of `u_x(ℰ_x) / 𝔪_x u_x(ℰ_x)` and
`𝒢_x / 𝔪_x 𝒢_x`. The condition is _sufficient_: reduce to `ℰ = 𝒪_X^m`; let `s_1, …, s_m` be the images by `u` of the
canonical sections `e_i` of `𝒪_X^m` (the `e_i` are the sections with `(e_i)_y` equal to the `i`-th element of the
canonical basis of `𝒪_y^m` for `y ∈ X` — _canonical sections_ of `𝒪_X^m`). By hypothesis `s_1(x), …, s_m(x)` are
linearly independent, so if `ℱ` has rank `n`, there are `n − m` sections `s_{m+1}, …, s_n` of `ℱ` over some neighborhood
`V` of `x` with `s_i(x)` (`1 ≤ i ≤ n`) a basis of `ℱ_x / 𝔪_x ℱ_x`. By (5.5.4) there is an open `U ⊂ V` with the `s_i(y)`
(`1 ≤ i ≤ n`) a basis of `ℱ_y / 𝔪_y ℱ_y` for every `y ∈ U`. By (5.5.4), there is an isomorphism `ℱ|U ⥲ 𝒪_X^n|U` sending
`s_i|U` (`1 ≤ i ≤ m`) to `e_i|U`, finishing the proof.
