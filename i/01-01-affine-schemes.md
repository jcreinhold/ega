# Chapter I — The Language of Schemes

## §1. Affine Schemes

<!-- label: I.1 -->

### 1.1. The prime spectrum of a ring

<!-- label: I.1.1 -->

**(1.1.1) Notation.** Let `A` be a (commutative) ring and `M` an `A`-module. In this chapter and the following ones we
shall constantly use the notation:

- `Spec(A)` = the set of _prime ideals_ of `A`, also called the _prime spectrum_ of `A`; for `x ∈ X = Spec(A)`, it is
    often convenient to write `𝔧_x` in place of `x`. For `Spec(A)` to be _empty_, it is necessary and sufficient that
    `A = 0`.

- `A_x = A_{𝔧_x} = S⁻¹A`, where `S = A ∖ 𝔧_x` — the _(local) ring of fractions_.

- `𝔪_x = 𝔧_x A_{𝔧_x}` = maximal ideal of `A_x`.

- `κ(x) = A_x / 𝔪_x` = _residue field_ of `A_x`, canonically isomorphic to the field of fractions of `A / 𝔧_x`, with
    which we identify it.

- `f(x)` = class of `f` mod `𝔧_x` in `A / 𝔧_x ⊂ κ(x)`, for `f ∈ A` and `x ∈ X`; we also call `f(x)` the _value_ of `f`
    at the point `x ∈ Spec(A)`. The relations `f(x) = 0` and `f ∈ 𝔧_x` are equivalent.

- `M_x = M ⊗_A A_x` = module of fractions with denominators in `A ∖ 𝔧_x`.

- `r(E)` = _radical of the ideal of `A` generated by_ `E`, for `E ⊂ A`.

- `V(E)` = `{x ∈ X : E ⊂ 𝔧_x}` (equivalently, `{x ∈ X : f(x) = 0 for all f ∈ E}`), for `E ⊂ A`. Thus

    ```
    r(E) = ⋂_{x ∈ V(E)} 𝔧_x.    (1.1.1.1)
    ```

- `V(f) = V({f})` for `f ∈ A`.

- `D(f) = X ∖ V(f)` = `{x ∈ X : f(x) ≠ 0}`.

**Proposition (1.1.2).**

> (i) `V(0) = X`, `V(1) = ∅`. (ii) `E ⊂ E′` implies `V(E) ⊃ V(E′)`. (iii) For every family `(E_λ)` of subsets,
> `V(⋃_λ E_λ) = V(∑_λ E_λ) = ⋂_λ V(E_λ)`. (iv) `V(EE′) = V(E) ∪ V(E′)`. (v) `V(E) = V(r(E))`.

**Proof.** (i), (ii), (iii) are trivial; (v) follows from (ii) and (1.1.1.1). For (iv), `V(EE′) ⊃ V(E) ∪ V(E′)` is
clear; conversely, if `x ∉ V(E)` and `x ∉ V(E′)`, pick `f ∈ E`, `f′ ∈ E′` with `f(x), f′(x) ≠ 0` in `κ(x)`; then
`f(x) f′(x) ≠ 0`, so `x ∉ V(EE′)`.

Proposition (1.1.2) shows the sets `V(E)` are the _closed sets_ of a topology on `X`, called the _spectral
topology_[^I-1-1]; unless otherwise stated, `X = Spec(A)` is always equipped with this topology.

**(1.1.3)** For a subset `Y ⊂ X`, write `𝔧(Y) = {f ∈ A : f(y) = 0 for all y ∈ Y} = ⋂_{y ∈ Y} 𝔧_y`. Clearly
`Y ⊂ Y′ ⟹ 𝔧(Y) ⊃ 𝔧(Y′)`, and

```
𝔧(⋃_λ Y_λ) = ⋂_λ 𝔧(Y_λ);    𝔧({x}) = 𝔧_x.    (1.1.3.1, 1.1.3.2)
```

**Proposition (1.1.4).**

> (i) For every `E ⊂ A`, `𝔧(V(E)) = r(E)`. (ii) For every `Y ⊂ X`, `V(𝔧(Y)) = Ȳ`, the closure of `Y`.

**Corollary (1.1.5).** The closed subsets of `X = Spec(A)` and the ideals of `A` equal to their radicals correspond
bijectively under the inclusion-reversing maps `Y ↦ 𝔧(Y)` and `𝔞 ↦ V(𝔞)`. To `Y_1 ∪ Y_2` corresponds `𝔧(Y_1) ∩ 𝔧(Y_2)`;
to `⋂_λ Y_λ` corresponds the radical of `∑_λ 𝔧(Y_λ)`.

**Corollary (1.1.6).** If `A` is Noetherian, `X = Spec(A)` is a Noetherian space.

The converse fails: any non-Noetherian integral ring with a unique nonzero prime ideal (e.g. a non-discrete rank-1
valuation ring) gives a counterexample. For an example whose spectrum is not Noetherian, take `A = 𝒞(Y)`, the ring of
continuous real-valued functions on an infinite compact space `Y`; then `Y` (as a set) corresponds to the maximal ideals
of `A`, and the topology of `Y` is induced from the spectral topology of `X = Spec(A)`.

**Corollary (1.1.7).** For `x ∈ X`, `{x}̄ = {y ∈ X : 𝔧_x ⊂ 𝔧_y}`. `{x}` is closed iff `𝔧_x` is maximal.

**Corollary (1.1.8).** `X = Spec(A)` is a Kolmogorov space.

**(1.1.9)** For `f, g ∈ A`, by (1.1.2)(iv),

```
D(fg) = D(f) ∩ D(g).    (1.1.9.1)
```

Also `D(f) = D(g)` iff `r(f) = r(g)`, iff the minimal primes containing `(f)` and `(g)` agree. In particular
`D(f) = D(g)` whenever `f = u g` with `u` invertible.

**Proposition (1.1.10).**

> (i) The `D(f)` (`f ∈ A`) form a basis of the topology of `X`. (ii) For every `f ∈ A`, `D(f)` is quasi-compact. In
> particular, `X = D(1)` is quasi-compact.

**Proof.** (i) follows from `U = X ∖ V(E) = ⋃_{f ∈ E} D(f)`. For (ii): if `D(f) ⊂ ⋃_{λ ∈ L} D(f_λ)`, let `𝔞 ⊂ A` be the
ideal generated by the `f_λ`; then `V(f) ⊃ V(𝔞)`, so `r(f) ⊂ r(𝔞)`, hence `fⁿ ∈ 𝔞` for some `n`. Then `fⁿ` lies in the
ideal `𝔟` generated by a finite subfamily `(f_λ)_{λ ∈ J}`, so `D(f) = D(fⁿ) ⊂ ⋃_{λ ∈ J} D(f_λ)`.

**Proposition (1.1.11).** For every ideal `𝔞 ⊂ A`, `Spec(A/𝔞)` is canonically identified with the closed subspace `V(𝔞)`
of `Spec(A)`.

Recall (0.1.1.1) that the _nilradical_ `𝔑 = r(0)` is the intersection of all prime ideals of `A`.

**Corollary (1.1.12).** `Spec(A)` and `Spec(A/𝔑)` are canonically homeomorphic.

**Proposition (1.1.13).** `X = Spec(A)` is irreducible (0.2.1.1) if and only if `A/𝔑` is integral (equivalently, `𝔑` is
prime).

**Corollary (1.1.14).**

> (i) In the bijection of (1.1.5), the irreducible closed subsets of `X` correspond to the prime ideals of `A`; the
> irreducible components correspond to minimal primes. (ii) `x ↦ {x}̄` is a bijection between `X` and the set of closed
> irreducible subsets — that is, every closed irreducible subset of `X` admits a unique generic point.

**Proposition (1.1.15).** If `𝔍 ⊂ A` contains the radical of `A`, then the only neighborhood of `V(𝔍)` in `X = Spec(A)`
is `X` itself.

**Proof.** Every maximal ideal belongs to `V(𝔍)`. Every ideal `𝔞 ≠ A` is in some maximal ideal, so `V(𝔞) ∩ V(𝔍) ≠ ∅`.

### 1.2. Functorial properties of prime spectra

<!-- label: I.1.2 -->

**(1.2.1)** For a ring homomorphism `φ : A′ → A` and `x = 𝔧_x ∈ Spec(A)`, `A′ / φ⁻¹(𝔧_x)` injects into `A / 𝔧_x`, so
`φ⁻¹(𝔧_x)` is prime. Write `^aφ(x) = φ⁻¹(𝔧_x)`, defining

```
^aφ : X = Spec(A) → X′ = Spec(A′)
```

(also written `Spec(φ)`) — the map _associated_ to `φ`. Let `φ^x` be the injective homomorphism `A′/φ⁻¹(𝔧_x) → A/𝔧_x`
induced by `φ`, and also its extension to fields `κ(^aφ(x)) → κ(x)`. For `f′ ∈ A′`,

```
φ^x(f′(^aφ(x))) = (φ(f′))(x)    for x ∈ X.    (1.2.1.1)
```

**Proposition (1.2.2).**

> (i) For every `E′ ⊂ A′`, `^aφ⁻¹(V(E′)) = V(φ(E′))`; in particular for `f′ ∈ A′`, `^aφ⁻¹(D(f′)) = D(φ(f′))`. (1.2.2.1,
> 1.2.2.2) (ii) For every ideal `𝔞 ⊂ A`, `^aφ(V(𝔞))̄ = V(φ⁻¹(𝔞))`. (1.2.2.3)

**Corollary (1.2.3).** `^aφ` is continuous.

If `A″` is a third ring and `φ′ : A″ → A′` a homomorphism, `^a(φ ∘ φ′) = ^aφ′ ∘ ^aφ`. So `Spec(A)` is a _contravariant
functor_ in `A` from rings to topological spaces.

**Corollary (1.2.4).** If every `f ∈ A` is of the form `h φ(f′)` with `h` invertible (e.g., if `φ` is surjective), then
`^aφ` is a homeomorphism from `X` onto `^aφ(X)`.

**(1.2.5)** When `φ : A → A/𝔞` is the canonical quotient, `^aφ` is the canonical injection of `V(𝔞) ≅ Spec(A/𝔞)` into
`X = Spec(A)`.

**Corollary (1.2.6).** If `S ⊂ A` is multiplicative, `Spec(S⁻¹A)` is canonically identified (with topology) with
`{x ∈ Spec(A) : 𝔧_x ∩ S = ∅}`.

**Corollary (1.2.7).** `^aφ(X)` is dense in `X′` iff every element of `Ker φ` is nilpotent.

### 1.3. Sheaf associated to a module

<!-- label: I.1.3 -->

**(1.3.1)** Let `A` be a commutative ring, `M` an `A`-module, `f ∈ A`, `S_f = {fⁿ : n ≥ 0}`. Write `A_f = S_f⁻¹A`,
`M_f = S_f⁻¹M`. The saturated multiplicative subset `S_f′` of divisors of elements of `S_f` gives `A_f = S_f′⁻¹A`,
`M_f = S_f′⁻¹M` (0.1.4.3).

**Lemma (1.3.2).** The following are equivalent: (a) `g ∈ S_f′`; (b) `S_g′ ⊂ S_f′`; (c) `f ∈ r(g)`; (d) `r(f) ⊂ r(g)`;
(e) `V(g) ⊂ V(f)`; (f) `D(f) ⊂ D(g)`.

**(1.3.3)** If `D(f) ⊃ D(g)`, there is a canonical functorial homomorphism `ρ_{g,f} : M_f → M_g`, satisfying
`ρ_{h,g} ∘ ρ_{g,f} = ρ_{h,f}` (1.3.3.1) when `D(f) ⊃ D(g) ⊃ D(h)`.

For fixed `x ∈ X`, the `S_f′` (with `f ∈ A ∖ 𝔧_x`) form a directed family with union `A ∖ 𝔧_x`, so `M_x = lim⃗ M_f` is
the inductive limit along `(ρ_{g,f})`. Write `ρ_x^f : M_f → M_x` for the canonical map (`f ∈ A ∖ 𝔧_x`, i.e. `x ∈ D(f)`).

**Definition (1.3.4).** The _structure sheaf_ of `X = Spec(A)`, written `Ã` or `𝒪_X`, and the _sheaf associated to_ `M`,
written `M̃`, are the sheaf of rings (resp. `Ã`-module) associated with the presheaf `D(f) ↦ A_f` (resp. `D(f) ↦ M_f`)
on the basis `𝔅 = {D(f) : f ∈ A}` (using (1.1.10), (0.3.2.1), and (0.3.5.6)).

By (0.3.2.4), the stalk `Ã_x` (resp. `M̃_x`) is identified with `A_x` (resp. `M_x`). Write `θ_f : A_f → Γ(D(f), Ã)`
(resp. `θ_f : M_f → Γ(D(f), M̃)`) for the canonical map; for `x ∈ D(f)` and `ξ ∈ M_f`, `(θ_f(ξ))_x = ρ_x^f(ξ)`.

**Proposition (1.3.5).** `M̃` is an _exact covariant functor_ from `A`-modules to `Ã`-modules.

**Proof.** A homomorphism `u : M → N` yields `u_f : M_f → N_f` compatible with restrictions, hence `ũ : M̃ → Ñ`;
stalkwise `ũ_x = u_x`, so functoriality and exactness follow from (0.1.3.2). Plainly `Supp(M̃) = Supp(M)`.

**Proposition (1.3.6).** For every `f ∈ A`, `D(f) ⊂ X` is canonically identified with `Spec(A_f)`, and `M̃_f` is
canonically identified with `M̃ | D(f)`.

**Theorem (1.3.7).** For every `A`-module `M` and `f ∈ A`, the map `θ_f : M_f → Γ(D(f), M̃)` is bijective. In
particular, `M ≅ Γ(X, M̃)` via `θ_1`.

**Proof sketch.** _Injectivity:_ if `θ_f(ξ) = 0` and `ξ ∈ M_f`, then for every prime `𝔭 ⊂ A_f`, some `h ∉ 𝔭` kills `ξ`;
the annihilator is contained in no prime, hence equals `A_f`, so `ξ = 0`. _Surjectivity (case `f = 1`):_ given a section
`s ∈ Γ(X, M̃)`, by (1.1.10) cover `X` by finitely many `D(f_i)` with `s|D(f_i) = θ_{f_i}(ξ_i)`, `ξ_i ∈ M_{f_i}`. After
multiplying through by powers, write `ξ_i = z_i / f_i^n` with all `n` equal and compatibility relations
`f_j^n z_i = f_i^n z_j`. Since `(f_i^n)` generate `A`, pick `g_i ∈ A` with `∑_i g_i f_i^n = 1`; set `z = ∑_i g_i z_i`.
Then `f_i^n z = z_i`, so `θ_1(z)|D(f_i) = s|D(f_i)`. The general case `f ≠ 1` follows by passing to `D(f) ≅ Spec(A_f)`.

**Corollary (1.3.8).** `u ↦ ũ` is a bijection `Hom_A(M, N) ⥲ Hom_Ã(M̃, Ñ)`. In particular `M = 0 ⟺ M̃ = 0`.

**Corollary (1.3.9).**

> (i) For `u : M → N`, the sheaves associated with `Ker u`, `Im u`, `Coker u` are `Ker ũ`, `Im ũ`, `Coker ũ`. In
> particular, `ũ` is injective (resp. surjective, bijective) iff `u` is. (ii) The functor `M ↦ M̃` commutes with
> inductive limits and direct sums.

So sheaves isomorphic to associated sheaves of `A`-modules form an _abelian category_.

**(1.3.10)** A submodule `N ⊂ M` gives an injective `Ñ → M̃`; we identify `Ñ` with an `Ã`-submodule of `M̃`. For
submodules `N, P ⊂ M`,

```
(N + P)~ = Ñ + P̃,    (N ∩ P)~ = Ñ ∩ P̃.    (1.3.10.1, 1.3.10.2)
```

So `Ñ = P̃` ⟹ `N = P`.

**Corollary (1.3.11).** `Γ` is exact on the category of sheaves isomorphic to associated sheaves of `A`-modules.

**Corollary (1.3.12).** Let `M, N` be `A`-modules.

> (i) `(M ⊗_A N)~ ≅ M̃ ⊗_Ã Ñ` canonically. (ii) If `M` admits a finite presentation, `(Hom_A(M, N))~ ≅ ℋom_Ã(M̃, Ñ)`
> canonically.

**(1.3.13)** An `A`-algebra structure on `B` is the same as `A`-module structure plus `A`-homomorphism `φ : B ⊗_A B → B`
and unit `e ∈ B` satisfying associativity, commutativity, and unit axioms. By (1.3.12), `φ̃ : B̃ ⊗_Ã B̃ → B̃` makes `B̃`
an `Ã`-algebra. Similarly, `B`-module structure on `N` corresponds to `Ã`-module homomorphism `ψ̃ : B̃ ⊗_Ã Ñ → Ñ`; `Ñ`
is thus a `B̃`-module.

For `B`-modules `M, N`, `M̃ ⊗_B̃ Ñ ≅ (M ⊗_B N)~`, and `ℋom_B̃(M̃, Ñ) ≅ (Hom_B(M, N))~` when `M` has a finite
presentation. For `𝔍 ⊂ B`, `(𝔍 N)~ = 𝔍̃ · Ñ`. Graded `A`-algebras carry over to graded `Ã`-algebras.

### 1.4. Quasi-coherent sheaves over a prime spectrum

<!-- label: I.1.4 -->

**Theorem (1.4.1).** Let `X = Spec(A)`, `V ⊂ X` a quasi-compact open subset, and `ℱ` an `(𝒪_X | V)`-module. The
following are equivalent:

> (a) `ℱ ≅ M̃ | V` for some `A`-module `M`. (b) `V` is covered by finitely many `V_i = D(f_i) ⊂ V` with `ℱ | V_i ≅ M̃_i`
> for some `A_{f_i}`-module `M_i`. (c) `ℱ` is quasi-coherent (0.5.1.3). (d) The following hold: (d1) For every
> `D(f) ⊂ V` and `s ∈ Γ(D(f), ℱ)`, `fⁿ s` extends to a section over `V` for some `n ≥ 0`. (d2) For every `D(f) ⊂ V` and
> `t ∈ Γ(V, ℱ)` with `t|D(f) = 0`, `fⁿ t = 0` for some `n ≥ 0`.

**Corollary (1.4.2).** Every quasi-coherent sheaf on a quasi-compact open subset of `X` is induced by a quasi-coherent
sheaf on `X`.

**Corollary (1.4.3).** Every quasi-coherent `𝒪_X`-algebra on `X = Spec(A)` is `≅ B̃` for some `A`-algebra `B`; every
quasi-coherent `B̃`-module is `≅ Ñ` for some `B`-module `N`.

### 1.5. Coherent sheaves over a prime spectrum

<!-- label: I.1.5 -->

**Theorem (1.5.1).** Let `A` be Noetherian, `X = Spec(A)`, `V ⊂ X` open, `ℱ` an `(𝒪_X | V)`-module. Then the following
are equivalent:

> (a) `ℱ` is coherent; (b) `ℱ` is of finite type and quasi-coherent; (c) `ℱ ≅ M̃ | V` for some `A`-module `M` of finite
> type.

**Corollary (1.5.2).** Under (1.5.1), `𝒪_X` is a quasi-coherent sheaf of rings.

**Corollary (1.5.3).** Under (1.5.1), every coherent sheaf on an open subset of `X` is induced by a coherent sheaf on
`X`.

**Corollary (1.5.4).** Under (1.5.1), every quasi-coherent `𝒪_X`-module is the inductive limit of its coherent
sub-`𝒪_X`-modules.

### 1.6. Functorial properties of quasi-coherent sheaves

<!-- label: I.1.6 -->

**(1.6.1)** Let `φ : A′ → A` be a ring homomorphism and `^aφ : X = Spec(A) → X′ = Spec(A′)` the associated map. There is
a canonical homomorphism `φ̃ : 𝒪_{X′} → ^aφ_*(𝒪_X)` of sheaves of rings: for each `f′ ∈ A′`, `f = φ(f′)`, and
`^aφ⁻¹(D(f′)) = D(f)`; identifying `Γ(D(f′), 𝒪_{X′}) = A′_{f′}` and `Γ(D(f), 𝒪_X) = A_f`, `φ` induces a ring map
`φ_{f′} : A′_{f′} → A_f` (0.1.5.1), and these are compatible with restrictions. The pair `Φ = (^aφ, φ̃)` is a morphism
of ringed spaces `(X, 𝒪_X) → (X′, 𝒪_{X′})`.

For `x ∈ X` with `x′ = ^aφ(x)`, the stalk homomorphism `φ̃_x^♯ : A′_{x′} → A_x` is the canonical map induced by `φ`.

**Example (1.6.2).** For `S ⊂ A` multiplicative and `φ : A → S⁻¹A`, `^aφ : Y = Spec(S⁻¹A) → X = Spec(A)` is a
homeomorphism onto `{x : 𝔧_x ∩ S = ∅}`, and `𝒪_Y` is identified with the induced sheaf `𝒪_X | Y` (since the stalk maps
are isomorphisms).

**Proposition (1.6.3).** For every `A`-module `M`, there is a canonical functorial isomorphism `(M_{[φ]})~ ⥲ Φ_*(M̃)` of
`𝒪_{X′}`-modules.

This also holds for algebras (with module by an algebra structure preserved).

**Corollary (1.6.4).** `Φ_*` is exact on quasi-coherent `𝒪_X`-modules.

**Proposition (1.6.5).** For every `A′`-module `N′`, setting `N = N′ ⊗_{A′} A_{[φ]}`, there is a canonical functorial
isomorphism `Φ*(Ñ′) ⥲ Ñ` of `𝒪_X`-modules.

**Proof sketch.** The map `j : z′ ↦ z′ ⊗ 1` is an `A′`-homomorphism `N′ → N_{[φ]}`; by (1.3.8), `j̃ : Ñ′ → (N_{[φ]})~`
is an `𝒪_{X′}`-module homomorphism, hence `j̃ : Ñ′ → Φ_*(Ñ)` via (1.6.3). The corresponding `h = j̃^♯ : Φ*(Ñ′) → Ñ` is
bijective stalkwise — at any `x` with `x′ = ^aφ(x)`, `h_x` is the canonical isomorphism
`N′_{x′} ⊗_{A′_{x′}} (A_x)_{[φ_{x′}]} ⥲ N_x`.

For `B′` an `A′`-algebra, `Φ*(B̃′) ⥲ (B′ ⊗_{A′} A_{[φ]})~` is an `𝒪_X`-algebra isomorphism.

**Corollary (1.6.6).** Sections of `Φ*(Ñ′)` are generated, as `A`-module, by the canonical images of sections of `Ñ′`.

**(1.6.7)** The canonical map `ρ : Ñ′ → Φ_*(Φ*(Ñ′))` is `j̃`; the canonical `σ : Φ*(Φ_*(M̃)) → M̃` is `p̃` where
`p : M_{[φ]} ⊗_{A′} A_{[φ]} → M` is `z ⊗ a ↦ a · z`.

**(1.6.8)** For `N′_1, N′_2` `A′`-modules with `N′_1` admitting a finite presentation, the canonical homomorphism
`Φ*(ℋom_{Ã′}(Ñ′_1, Ñ′_2)) → ℋom_Ã(Φ*(Ñ′_1), Φ*(Ñ′_2))` is `γ̃`, where
`γ : Hom_{A′}(N′_1, N′_2) ⊗_{A′} A → Hom_A(N′_1 ⊗_{A′} A, N′_2 ⊗_{A′} A)` is canonical.

**(1.6.9)** For `𝔍′ ⊂ A′` and `M` an `A`-module, `𝔍̃′ M̃ ≅ (𝔍′ M)~`; in particular `Φ*(𝔍̃′) · Ã ≅ (𝔍′ A)~`, and
`Φ*((A′ / 𝔍′)~) ≅ (A / 𝔍′ A)~`.

**(1.6.10)** For a third ring `A″` and `φ′ : A″ → A′`, with `φ″ = φ ∘ φ′`, one has `^aφ″ = ^aφ′ ∘ ^aφ` and
`φ̃″ = φ̃ ∘ φ̃′`, so `Φ″ = Φ′ ∘ Φ`. Thus `(Spec(A), Ã)` is a functor from rings to ringed spaces.

### 1.7. Characterization of morphisms of affine schemes

<!-- label: I.1.7 -->

**Definition (1.7.1).** A ringed space `(X, 𝒪_X)` is an _affine scheme_ if it is isomorphic to `(Spec(A), Ã)` for some
ring `A`; we then call `Γ(X, 𝒪_X) ≅ A` the _ring_ of the affine scheme, written `A(X)`.

By abuse of language, the _affine scheme_ `Spec(A)` always denotes the ringed space `(Spec(A), Ã)`.

**(1.7.2)** For affine schemes `X = Spec(A)` and `Y = Spec(B)`, every ring homomorphism `φ : B → A` gives a morphism
`Φ = (^aφ, φ̃) = Spec(φ) : X → Y`. Moreover, `φ = Γ(φ̃)` recovers `φ` from `Φ`.

**Theorem (1.7.3).** Let `(X, 𝒪_X)`, `(Y, 𝒪_Y)` be affine schemes. For a morphism `(ψ, θ) : (X, 𝒪_X) → (Y, 𝒪_Y)` of
ringed spaces to be of the form `(^aφ, φ̃)` for some `φ : A(Y) → A(X)`, it is necessary and sufficient that for every
`x ∈ X`, `θ_x^♯ : 𝒪_{ψ(x)} → 𝒪_x` be a _local_ homomorphism.

**Proof.** _Necessity:_ `φ̃_x^♯` is induced from `φ` by passing to fractions, so is local. _Sufficiency:_ let
`A = A(X)`, `B = A(Y)`, `φ = Γ(θ) : B → A`. The local hypothesis on `θ_x^♯` means it induces a monomorphism
`θ^x : κ(ψ(x)) → κ(x)` with `θ^x(f(ψ(x))) = φ(f)(x)`. Hence `f(ψ(x)) = 0 ⟺ φ(f)(x) = 0`, so `𝔧_{ψ(x)} = 𝔧_{^aφ(x)}`,
i.e. `ψ = ^aφ`. The commutativity of stalks then forces `θ_x^♯ = φ̃_x^♯` (0.1.5.1), hence `θ = φ̃` (by (0.3.7.1)).

A morphism satisfying (1.7.3) is called a _morphism of affine schemes_.

**Corollary (1.7.4).** `Hom((X, 𝒪_X), (Y, 𝒪_Y))` (morphisms of affine schemes) is canonically in bijection with
`Hom_{ring}(A(Y), A(X))`. Thus the functors `A ↦ (Spec(A), Ã)` and `(X, 𝒪_X) ↦ Γ(X, 𝒪_X)` give an _equivalence_ between
commutative rings and the opposite category of affine schemes.

**Corollary (1.7.5).** If `φ : B → A` is surjective, `(^aφ, φ̃)` is a monomorphism of ringed spaces.

### 1.8. Morphisms from locally ringed spaces to affine schemes

<!-- label: I.1.8 -->

A remark of J. Tate generalizes (1.7.3) and (2.2.4):

**Proposition (1.8.1).** Let `(S, 𝒪_S)` be an affine scheme and `(X, 𝒪_X)` a locally ringed space. There is a canonical
bijection between ring homomorphisms `Γ(S, 𝒪_S) → Γ(X, 𝒪_X)` and morphisms of ringed spaces
`(ψ, θ) : (X, 𝒪_X) → (S, 𝒪_S)` such that for every `x ∈ X`, `θ_x^♯ : 𝒪_{ψ(x)} → 𝒪_x` is _local_.

**Proof sketch.** A morphism of ringed spaces always gives `ρ : Hom((X, 𝒪_X), (S, 𝒪_S)) → Hom(Γ(S, 𝒪_S), Γ(X, 𝒪_X))` via
`(ψ, θ) ↦ Γ(θ)`. Conversely, set `A = Γ(S, 𝒪_S)` and let `φ : A → Γ(X, 𝒪_X)` be a ring homomorphism. For `x ∈ X`,
`{f ∈ A : φ(f)(x) = 0}` is prime (since `κ(x)` is a field), giving `^aφ(x) ∈ S = Spec(A)`. `^aφ⁻¹(D(f)) = X_{φ(f)}` (by
(0.5.5.2)), so `^aφ` is continuous. Define `φ̃ : 𝒪_S → ^aφ_*(𝒪_X)` by sending `s/f ∈ A_f = Γ(D(f), 𝒪_S)` to
`(φ(s) | X_{φ(f)}) (φ(f) | X_{φ(f)})⁻¹`. Then `φ̃_x^♯(s_y / f_y) = (φ(s)_x)(φ(f)_x)⁻¹` where `y = ^aφ(x)`;
`s_y ∈ 𝔪_y ⟺ φ(s)_x ∈ 𝔪_x`, so `φ̃_x^♯` is local. The two maps `ρ` and `σ` are mutually inverse.

**(1.8.2)** For locally ringed spaces `X, Y`, we always consider morphisms `(ψ, θ)` whose stalk maps `θ_x^♯` are local;
with this definition, locally ringed spaces form a category. Write `Hom(X, Y)` for morphisms in this category and
`Hom_{rs}(X, Y)` for morphisms of ringed spaces (without the locality condition). The map
`ρ : Hom_{rs}(X, Y) → Hom(Γ(Y, 𝒪_Y), Γ(X, 𝒪_X))` restricts to a functorial `ρ′ : Hom(X, Y) → Hom(Γ(Y, 𝒪_Y), Γ(X, 𝒪_X))`.

**Corollary (1.8.3).** A locally ringed space `Y` is an affine scheme if and only if
`ρ′ : Hom(X, Y) → Hom(Γ(Y, 𝒪_Y), Γ(X, 𝒪_X))` is bijective for every locally ringed space `X`.

**(1.8.4)** Let `S = Spec(A)` and `(S′, A′)` the ringed space whose underlying space is a point and whose sheaf is the
simple sheaf defined by `A`. Let `π : S → S′` be the unique map, and `ι : A′ → 𝒪_S` the `π`-morphism from the constant
sections. This gives a canonical morphism `i = (π, ι) : (S, 𝒪_S) → (S′, A′)`. For an `A`-module `M`, write `M′` for the
simple sheaf on `S′`; then `i_*(M̃) = M′`.

**Lemma (1.8.5).** Under (1.8.4), the canonical homomorphism `i*(i_*(M̃)) → M̃` is an isomorphism for every `A`-module
`M`.

**Corollary (1.8.6).** For a ringed space `(X, 𝒪_X)` and `u : X → S`, there is a canonical functorial isomorphism
`u*(M̃) ⥲ u*(i*(M′))` of `𝒪_X`-modules.

**Corollary (1.8.7).** Under (1.8.6), there is a canonical functorial isomorphism

```
Hom_{𝒪_S}(M̃, u_*(ℱ)) ⥲ Hom_A(M, Γ(X, ℱ)).    (1.8.7.1)
```

**(1.8.8)** With the notation of (1.8.4), a morphism `(ψ, θ) : X → S′` of ringed spaces is the same as a ring
homomorphism `A → Γ(X, 𝒪_X)`. By (1.8.1), there is a canonical bijection `Hom(X, S) ⥲ Hom(X, S′)` (the right-hand side
meaning morphisms of ringed spaces). For locally ringed spaces `X, Y`, the analogous setup yields
`ρ : Hom_{rs}(X, Y) → Hom(X, Y′)`. By (1.8.3), affine schemes are characterized among locally ringed spaces as those for
which the restriction `ρ′ : Hom(X, Y) → Hom(X, Y′)` is bijective for every locally ringed space `X`.

In the next chapter, this is generalized to associate to _any_ ringed space `Z` a locally ringed space `Spec(Z)`, the
starting point for a "relative" theory of preschemes over a ringed space.

**(1.8.9)** Pairs `(X, ℱ)` with `X` a locally ringed space and `ℱ` an `𝒪_X`-module form a category, with morphisms
`(u, h) : (X, ℱ) → (Y, 𝒢)` where `u : X → Y` is a morphism of locally ringed spaces and `h : 𝒢 → ℱ` a `u`-morphism. The
map `(u, h) ↦ (ρ′(u), Γ(h))` is functorial.

**Corollary (1.8.10).** For `Y` a locally ringed space and `𝒢` an `𝒪_Y`-module, `Y` is an affine scheme and `𝒢` is
quasi-coherent if and only if for every pair `(X, ℱ)`, the canonical map (1.8.9.1) is bijective.

**Remark (1.8.11).** Theorem (1.7.3), Corollary (1.7.4), and Proposition (2.2.4) are special cases of (1.8.1); the
definition in (1.6.1) likewise. Corollary (1.8.7) implies (1.6.3) and (1.6.4); (1.6.5) and (1.6.6) follow from (1.8.6).
