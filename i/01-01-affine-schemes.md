# Chapter I — The Language of Schemes

## §1. Affine Schemes

<!-- label: I.1 -->

### 1.1. The prime spectrum of a ring

<!-- label: I.1.1 -->

**(1.1.1) Notation.** Let $A$ be a (commutative) ring and $M$ an $A$-module. In this chapter and the following ones we
shall constantly use the notation:

- $\operatorname{Spec}(A)$ = the set of _prime ideals_ of $A$, also called the _prime spectrum_ of $A$; for $x \in X =
  \operatorname{Spec}(A)$, it is often convenient to write $\mathfrak{j}_{x}$ in place of $x$. For
  $\operatorname{Spec}(A)$ to be _empty_, it is necessary and sufficient that $A = 0$.

- $A_{x} = A_{\mathfrak{j}_{x}} = S^{-1}A$, where $S = A \setminus \mathfrak{j}_{x}$ — the _(local) ring of fractions_.

- $\mathfrak{m}_{x} = \mathfrak{j}_{x} A_{\mathfrak{j}_{x}}$ = maximal ideal of $A_{x}$.

- $\kappa(x) = A_{x} / \mathfrak{m}_{x}$ = _residue field_ of $A_{x}$, canonically isomorphic to the field of fractions
  of $A / \mathfrak{j}_{x}$, with which we identify it.

- $f(x)$ = class of $f$ mod $\mathfrak{j}_{x}$ in $A / \mathfrak{j}_{x} \subset \kappa(x)$, for $f \in A$ and $x \in X$;
  we also call $f(x)$ the _value_ of $f$ at the point $x \in \operatorname{Spec}(A)$. The relations $f(x) = 0$ and $f
  \in \mathfrak{j}_{x}$ are equivalent.

- $M_{x} = M \otimes_{A} A_{x}$ = module of fractions with denominators in $A \setminus \mathfrak{j}_{x}$.

- $r(E)$ = _radical of the ideal of $A$ generated by_ $E$, for $E \subset A$.

- $V(E) = \{x \in X : E \subset \mathfrak{j}_{x}\}$ (equivalently, $\{x \in X : f(x) = 0 \text{ for all } f \in E\}$),
  for $E \subset A$. Thus

    $$ r(E) = \bigcap_{x \in V(E)} \mathfrak{j}_{x}. \tag{1.1.1.1} $$

- $V(f) = V(\{f\})$ for $f \in A$.

- $D(f) = X \setminus V(f) = \{x \in X : f(x) \neq 0\}$.

**Proposition (1.1.2).**

> (i) $V(0) = X$, $V(1) = \emptyset$. (ii) $E \subset E'$ implies $V(E) \supset V(E')$. (iii) For every family
> $(E_{\lambda})$ of subsets, $V(\bigcup_{\lambda} E_{\lambda}) = V(\sum_{\lambda} E_{\lambda}) = \bigcap_{\lambda}
> V(E_{\lambda})$. (iv) $V(EE') = V(E) \cup V(E')$. (v) $V(E) = V(r(E))$.

**Proof.** (i), (ii), (iii) are trivial; (v) follows from (ii) and (1.1.1.1). For (iv), $V(EE') \supset V(E) \cup V(E')$
is clear; conversely, if $x \notin V(E)$ and $x \notin V(E')$, pick $f \in E$, $f' \in E'$ with $f(x), f'(x) \neq 0$ in
$\kappa(x)$; then $f(x) f'(x) \neq 0$, so $x \notin V(EE')$.

Proposition (1.1.2) shows the sets $V(E)$ are the _closed sets_ of a topology on $X$, called the _spectral
topology_[^I-1-1]; unless otherwise stated, $X = \operatorname{Spec}(A)$ is always equipped with this topology.

**(1.1.3)** For a subset $Y \subset X$, write
$\mathfrak{j}(Y) = \{f \in A : f(y) = 0 \text{ for all } y \in Y\} = \bigcap_{y \in Y} \mathfrak{j}_{y}$. Clearly
$Y \subset Y' \Longrightarrow \mathfrak{j}(Y) \supset \mathfrak{j}(Y')$, and

$$ \mathfrak{j}\!\left(\bigcup_{\lambda} Y_{\lambda}\right) = \bigcap_{\lambda} \mathfrak{j}(Y_{\lambda}) \tag{1.1.3.1} $$

$$ \mathfrak{j}(\{x\}) = \mathfrak{j}_{x}. \tag{1.1.3.2} $$

**Proposition (1.1.4).**

> (i) For every $E \subset A$, $\mathfrak{j}(V(E)) = r(E)$. (ii) For every $Y \subset X$, $V(\mathfrak{j}(Y)) =
> \bar{Y}$, the closure of $Y$.

**Corollary (1.1.5).** The closed subsets of $X = \operatorname{Spec}(A)$ and the ideals of $A$ equal to their radicals
correspond bijectively under the inclusion-reversing maps $Y \mapsto \mathfrak{j}(Y)$ and $\mathfrak{a} \mapsto
V(\mathfrak{a})$. To $Y_{1} \cup Y_{2}$ corresponds $\mathfrak{j}(Y_{1}) \cap \mathfrak{j}(Y_{2})$; to
$\bigcap_{\lambda} Y_{\lambda}$ corresponds the radical of $\sum_{\lambda} \mathfrak{j}(Y_{\lambda})$.

**Corollary (1.1.6).** If $A$ is Noetherian, $X = \operatorname{Spec}(A)$ is a Noetherian space.

The converse fails: any non-Noetherian integral ring with a unique nonzero prime ideal (e.g. a non-discrete rank-1
valuation ring) gives a counterexample. For an example whose spectrum is not Noetherian, take $A = \mathcal{C}(Y)$, the
ring of continuous real-valued functions on an infinite compact space $Y$; then $Y$ (as a set) corresponds to the
maximal ideals of $A$, and the topology of $Y$ is induced from the spectral topology of $X = \operatorname{Spec}(A)$.

**Corollary (1.1.7).** For $x \in X$, $\bar{x} = \{y \in X : \mathfrak{j}_{x} \subset \mathfrak{j}_{y}\}$. $\{x\}$ is
closed iff $\mathfrak{j}_{x}$ is maximal.

**Corollary (1.1.8).** $X = \operatorname{Spec}(A)$ is a Kolmogorov space.

**(1.1.9)** For $f, g \in A$, by (1.1.2)(iv),

$$ D(fg) = D(f) \cap D(g). \tag{1.1.9.1} $$

Also $D(f) = D(g)$ iff $r(f) = r(g)$, iff the minimal primes containing $(f)$ and $(g)$ agree. In particular $D(f) =
D(g)$ whenever $f = u g$ with $u$ invertible.

**Proposition (1.1.10).**

> (i) The $D(f)$ ($f \in A$) form a basis of the topology of $X$. (ii) For every $f \in A$, $D(f)$ is quasi-compact. In
> particular, $X = D(1)$ is quasi-compact.

**Proof.** (i) follows from $U = X \setminus V(E) = \bigcup_{f \in E} D(f)$. For (ii): if $D(f) \subset \bigcup_{\lambda
\in L} D(f_{\lambda})$, let $\mathfrak{a} \subset A$ be the ideal generated by the $f_{\lambda}$; then $V(f) \supset
V(\mathfrak{a})$, so $r(f) \subset r(\mathfrak{a})$, hence $f^{n} \in \mathfrak{a}$ for some $n$. Then $f^{n}$ lies in
the ideal $\mathfrak{b}$ generated by a finite subfamily $(f_{\lambda})_{\lambda \in J}$, so $D(f) = D(f^{n}) \subset
\bigcup_{\lambda \in J} D(f_{\lambda})$.

**Proposition (1.1.11).** For every ideal $\mathfrak{a} \subset A$, $\operatorname{Spec}(A/\mathfrak{a})$ is canonically
identified with the closed subspace $V(\mathfrak{a})$ of $\operatorname{Spec}(A)$.

Recall (0.1.1.1) that the _nilradical_ $\mathfrak{N} = r(0)$ is the intersection of all prime ideals of $A$.

**Corollary (1.1.12).** $\operatorname{Spec}(A)$ and $\operatorname{Spec}(A/\mathfrak{N})$ are canonically homeomorphic.

**Proposition (1.1.13).** $X = \operatorname{Spec}(A)$ is irreducible (0.2.1.1) if and only if $A/\mathfrak{N}$ is
integral (equivalently, $\mathfrak{N}$ is prime).

**Corollary (1.1.14).**

> (i) In the bijection of (1.1.5), the irreducible closed subsets of $X$ correspond to the prime ideals of $A$; the
> irreducible components correspond to minimal primes. (ii) $x \mapsto \bar{x}$ is a bijection between $X$ and the set
> of closed irreducible subsets — that is, every closed irreducible subset of $X$ admits a unique generic point.

**Proposition (1.1.15).** If $\mathfrak{J} \subset A$ contains the radical of $A$, then the only neighborhood of
$V(\mathfrak{J})$ in $X = \operatorname{Spec}(A)$ is $X$ itself.

**Proof.** Every maximal ideal belongs to $V(\mathfrak{J})$. Every ideal $\mathfrak{a} \neq A$ is in some maximal ideal,
so $V(\mathfrak{a}) \cap V(\mathfrak{J}) \neq \emptyset$.

### 1.2. Functorial properties of prime spectra

<!-- label: I.1.2 -->

**(1.2.1)** For a ring homomorphism $\phi : A' \to A$ and $x = \mathfrak{j}_{x} \in \operatorname{Spec}(A)$, $A' /
\phi^{-1}(\mathfrak{j}_{x})$ injects into $A / \mathfrak{j}_{x}$, so $\phi^{-1}(\mathfrak{j}_{x})$ is prime. Write
${}^{a}\phi(x) = \phi^{-1}(\mathfrak{j}_{x})$, defining

$$ {}^{a}\phi : X = \operatorname{Spec}(A) \to X' = \operatorname{Spec}(A') $$

(also written $\operatorname{Spec}(\phi)$) — the map _associated_ to $\phi$. Let $\phi^{x}$ be the injective
homomorphism $A'/\phi^{-1}(\mathfrak{j}_{x}) \to A/\mathfrak{j}_{x}$ induced by $\phi$, and also its extension to fields
$\kappa({}^{a}\phi(x)) \to \kappa(x)$. For $f' \in A'$,

$$ \phi^{x}\!\left(f'({}^{a}\phi(x))\right) = (\phi(f'))(x) \quad \text{for } x \in X. \tag{1.2.1.1} $$

**Proposition (1.2.2).**

> (i) For every $E' \subset A'$, ${}^{a}\phi^{-1}(V(E')) = V(\phi(E'))$; in particular for $f' \in A'$,
> ${}^{a}\phi^{-1}(D(f')) = D(\phi(f'))$. (1.2.2.1, 1.2.2.2) (ii) For every ideal $\mathfrak{a} \subset A$,
> ${}^{a}\phi\bar{V(\mathfrak{a})} = V(\phi^{-1}(\mathfrak{a}))$. (1.2.2.3)

**Corollary (1.2.3).** ${}^{a}\phi$ is continuous.

If $A''$ is a third ring and $\phi' : A'' \to A'$ a homomorphism, ${}^{a}(\phi \circ \phi') = {}^{a}\phi' \circ
{}^{a}\phi$. So $\operatorname{Spec}(A)$ is a _contravariant functor_ in $A$ from rings to topological spaces.

**Corollary (1.2.4).** If every $f \in A$ is of the form $h \phi(f')$ with $h$ invertible (e.g., if $\phi$ is
surjective), then ${}^{a}\phi$ is a homeomorphism from $X$ onto ${}^{a}\phi(X)$.

**(1.2.5)** When $\phi : A \to A/\mathfrak{a}$ is the canonical quotient, ${}^{a}\phi$ is the canonical injection of
$V(\mathfrak{a}) \cong \operatorname{Spec}(A/\mathfrak{a})$ into $X = \operatorname{Spec}(A)$.

**Corollary (1.2.6).** If $S \subset A$ is multiplicative, $\operatorname{Spec}(S^{-1}A)$ is canonically identified
(with topology) with $\{x \in \operatorname{Spec}(A) : \mathfrak{j}_{x} \cap S = \emptyset\}$.

**Corollary (1.2.7).** ${}^{a}\phi(X)$ is dense in $X'$ iff every element of $\operatorname{Ker} \phi$ is nilpotent.

### 1.3. Sheaf associated to a module

<!-- label: I.1.3 -->

**(1.3.1)** Let $A$ be a commutative ring, $M$ an $A$-module, $f \in A$, $S_{f} = {f^{n} : n \geq 0}$. Write $A_{f} =
S^{-1}_{f}A$, $M_{f} = S^{-1}_{f}M$. The saturated multiplicative subset $S_{f}'$ of divisors of elements of $S_{f}$
gives $A_{f} = S_{f}'^{-1}A$, $M_{f} = S_{f}'^{-1}M$ (0.1.4.3).

**Lemma (1.3.2).** The following are equivalent: (a) $g \in S_{f}'$; (b) $S_{g}' \subset S_{f}'$; (c) $f \in r(g)$; (d)
$r(f) \subset r(g)$; (e) $V(g) \subset V(f)$; (f) $D(f) \subset D(g)$.

**(1.3.3)** If $D(f) \supset D(g)$, there is a canonical functorial homomorphism $\rho_{g,f} : M_{f} \to M_{g}$,
satisfying $\rho_{h,g} \circ \rho_{g,f} = \rho_{h,f}$ (1.3.3.1) when $D(f) \supset D(g) \supset D(h)$.

For fixed $x \in X$, the $S_{f}'$ (with $f \in A \setminus \mathfrak{j}_{x}$) form a directed family with union $A
\setminus \mathfrak{j}_{x}$, so $M_{x} = \varinjlim M_{f}$ is the inductive limit along $(\rho_{g,f})$. Write
$\rho^{f}_{x} : M_{f} \to M_{x}$ for the canonical map ($f \in A \setminus \mathfrak{j}_{x}$, i.e. $x \in D(f)$).

**Definition (1.3.4).** The _structure sheaf_ of $X = \operatorname{Spec}(A)$, written $\tilde{A}$ or $\mathcal{O}_{X}$,
and the _sheaf associated to_ $M$, written $\tilde{M}$, are the sheaf of rings (resp. $\tilde{A}$-module) associated
with the presheaf $D(f) \mapsto A_{f}$ (resp. $D(f) \mapsto M_{f}$) on the basis
$\mathfrak{B} = \{D(f) : f \in A\}$ (using (1.1.10), (0.3.2.1), and (0.3.5.6)).

By (0.3.2.4), the stalk $\tilde{A}_{x}$ (resp. $\tilde{M}_{x}$) is identified with $A_{x}$ (resp. $M_{x}$). Write
$\theta_{f} : A_{f} \to \Gamma(D(f), \tilde{A})$ (resp. $\theta_{f} : M_{f} \to \Gamma(D(f), \tilde{M})$) for the
canonical map; for $x \in D(f)$ and $\xi \in M_{f}$, $(\theta_{f}(\xi))_{x} = \rho^{f}_{x}(\xi)$.

**Proposition (1.3.5).** $\tilde{M}$ is an _exact covariant functor_ from $A$-modules to $\tilde{A}$-modules.

**Proof.** A homomorphism $u : M \to N$ yields $u_{f} : M_{f} \to N_{f}$ compatible with restrictions, hence
$\tilde{u} : \tilde{M} \to \tilde{N}$; stalkwise $\tilde{u}_{x} = u_{x}$, so functoriality and exactness follow from
(0.1.3.2). Plainly $\operatorname{Supp}(\tilde{M}) = \operatorname{Supp}(M)$.

**Proposition (1.3.6).** For every $f \in A$, $D(f) \subset X$ is canonically identified with
$\operatorname{Spec}(A_{f})$, and $\tilde{M}_{f}$ is canonically identified with $\tilde{M} | D(f)$.

**Theorem (1.3.7).** For every $A$-module $M$ and $f \in A$, the map $\theta_{f} : M_{f} \to \Gamma(D(f), \tilde{M})$ is
bijective. In particular, $M \cong \Gamma(X, \tilde{M})$ via $\theta_{1}$.

**Proof sketch.** _Injectivity:_ if $\theta_{f}(\xi) = 0$ and $\xi \in M_{f}$, then for every prime $\mathfrak{p}
\subset A_{f}$, some $h \notin \mathfrak{p}$ kills $\xi$; the annihilator is contained in no prime, hence equals
$A_{f}$, so $\xi = 0$. _Surjectivity (case $f = 1$):_ given a section $s \in \Gamma(X, \tilde{M})$, by (1.1.10) cover
$X$ by finitely many $D(f_{i})$ with $s|D(f_{i}) = \theta_{f_{i}}(\xi_{i})$, $\xi_{i} \in M_{f_{i}}$. After multiplying
through by powers, write $\xi_{i} = z_{i} / f^{n}_{i}$ with all $n$ equal and compatibility relations $f^{n}_{j} z_{i} =
f^{n}_{i} z_{j}$. Since $(f^{n}_{i})$ generate $A$, pick $g_{i} \in A$ with $\sum_{i} g_{i} f^{n}_{i} = 1$; set $z =
\sum_{i} g_{i} z_{i}$. Then $f^{n}_{i} z = z_{i}$, so $\theta_{1}(z)|D(f_{i}) = s|D(f_{i})$. The general case $f \neq 1$
follows by passing to $D(f) \cong \operatorname{Spec}(A_{f})$.

**Corollary (1.3.8).** $u \mapsto \tilde{u}$ is a bijection $\operatorname{Hom}_{A}(M, N) \xrightarrow{\sim}
\operatorname{Hom}_{\tilde{A}}(\tilde{M}, \tilde{N})$. In particular $M = 0 \Longleftrightarrow \tilde{M} = 0$.

**Corollary (1.3.9).**

> (i) For $u : M \to N$, the sheaves associated with $\operatorname{Ker} u$, $\operatorname{Im} u$,
> $\operatorname{Coker} u$ are $\operatorname{Ker} \tilde{u}$, $\operatorname{Im} \tilde{u}$,
> $\operatorname{Coker} \tilde{u}$. In particular, $\tilde{u}$ is injective (resp. surjective, bijective) iff $u$ is.
> (ii) The functor $M \mapsto \tilde{M}$ commutes with inductive limits and direct sums.

So sheaves isomorphic to associated sheaves of $A$-modules form an _abelian category_.

**(1.3.10)** A submodule $N \subset M$ gives an injective $\tilde{N} \to \tilde{M}$; we identify $\tilde{N}$ with an
$\tilde{A}$-submodule of $\tilde{M}$. For submodules $N, P \subset M$,

$$ \widetilde{N + P} = \tilde{N} + \tilde{P}, \tag{1.3.10.1} $$

$$ \widetilde{N \cap P} = \tilde{N} \cap \tilde{P}. \tag{1.3.10.2} $$

So $\tilde{N} = \tilde{P} \Longrightarrow N = P$.

**Corollary (1.3.11).** $\Gamma$ is exact on the category of sheaves isomorphic to associated sheaves of $A$-modules.

**Corollary (1.3.12).** Let $M$, $N$ be $A$-modules.

> (i) $\widetilde{M \otimes_{A} N} \cong \tilde{M} \otimes_{\tilde{A}} \tilde{N}$ canonically. (ii) If $M$ admits a
> finite presentation, $\widetilde{\operatorname{Hom}_{A}(M, N)} \cong \mathcal{H}om_{\tilde{A}}(\tilde{M}, \tilde{N})$
> canonically.

**(1.3.13)** An $A$-algebra structure on $B$ is the same as $A$-module structure plus $A$-homomorphism
$\phi : B \otimes_{A} B \to B$ and unit $e \in B$ satisfying associativity, commutativity, and unit axioms. By
(1.3.12), $\tilde{\phi} : \tilde{B} \otimes_{\tilde{A}} \tilde{B} \to \tilde{B}$ makes $\tilde{B}$ an
$\tilde{A}$-algebra. Similarly, $B$-module structure on $N$ corresponds to $\tilde{A}$-module homomorphism
$\tilde{\psi} : \tilde{B} \otimes_{\tilde{A}} \tilde{N} \to \tilde{N}$; $\tilde{N}$ is thus a $\tilde{B}$-module.

For $B$-modules $M$, $N$, $\tilde{M} \otimes_{\tilde{B}} \tilde{N} \cong \widetilde{M \otimes_{B} N}$, and
$\mathcal{H}om_{\tilde{B}}(\tilde{M}, \tilde{N}) \cong \widetilde{\operatorname{Hom}_{B}(M, N)}$ when $M$ has a finite
presentation. For $\mathfrak{J} \subset B$, $\widetilde{\mathfrak{J} N} = \tilde{\mathfrak{J}} \cdot \tilde{N}$. Graded
$A$-algebras carry over to graded $\tilde{A}$-algebras.

### 1.4. Quasi-coherent sheaves over a prime spectrum

<!-- label: I.1.4 -->

**Theorem (1.4.1).** Let $X = \operatorname{Spec}(A)$, $V \subset X$ a quasi-compact open subset, and $\mathcal{F}$ an
$(\mathcal{O}_{X} | V)$-module. The following are equivalent:

> (a) $\mathcal{F} \cong \tilde{M} | V$ for some $A$-module $M$. (b) $V$ is covered by finitely many $V_{i} = D(f_{i})
> \subset V$ with $\mathcal{F} | V_{i} \cong \tilde{M}_{i}$ for some $A_{f_{i}}$-module $M_{i}$. (c) $\mathcal{F}$ is
> quasi-coherent (0.5.1.3). (d) The following hold: (d1) For every $D(f) \subset V$ and $s \in \Gamma(D(f),
> \mathcal{F})$, $f^{n} s$ extends to a section over $V$ for some $n \geq 0$. (d2) For every $D(f) \subset V$ and $t \in
> \Gamma(V, \mathcal{F})$ with $t|D(f) = 0$, $f^{n} t = 0$ for some $n \geq 0$.

**Corollary (1.4.2).** Every quasi-coherent sheaf on a quasi-compact open subset of $X$ is induced by a quasi-coherent
sheaf on $X$.

**Corollary (1.4.3).** Every quasi-coherent $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-algebra on $X = \operatorname{Spec}(A)$ is $\cong
\tilde{B}$ for some $A$-algebra $B$; every quasi-coherent $\tilde{B}$-module is $\cong \tilde{N}$ for some $B$-module
$N$.

### 1.5. Coherent sheaves over a prime spectrum

<!-- label: I.1.5 -->

**Theorem (1.5.1).** Let $A$ be Noetherian, $X = \operatorname{Spec}(A)$, $V \subset X$ open, $\mathcal{F}$ an
$(\mathcal{O}_{X} | V)$-module. Then the following are equivalent:

> (a) $\mathcal{F}$ is coherent; (b) $\mathcal{F}$ is of finite type and quasi-coherent; (c) $\mathcal{F} \cong
> \tilde{M} | V$ for some $A$-module $M$ of finite type.

**Corollary (1.5.2).** Under (1.5.1), $\mathcal{O}_{X}$ is a quasi-coherent sheaf of rings.

**Corollary (1.5.3).** Under (1.5.1), every coherent sheaf on an open subset of $X$ is induced by a coherent sheaf on
$X$.

**Corollary (1.5.4).** Under (1.5.1), every quasi-coherent $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-module is the inductive limit of its
coherent sub-$\mathcal{O}_{X}$-modules.

### 1.6. Functorial properties of quasi-coherent sheaves

<!-- label: I.1.6 -->

**(1.6.1)** Let $\phi : A' \to A$ be a ring homomorphism and ${}^{a}\phi : X = \operatorname{Spec}(A) \to X' =
\operatorname{Spec}(A')$ the associated map. There is a canonical homomorphism $\tilde{\phi} : \mathcal{O}_{X'} \to
{}^{a}\phi_{*}(\mathcal{O}_{X})$ of sheaves of rings: for each $f' \in A'$, $f = \phi(f')$, and ${}^{a}\phi^{-1}(D(f'))
= D(f)$; identifying $\Gamma(D(f'), \mathcal{O}_{X'}) = A'_{f'}$ and $\Gamma(D(f), \mathcal{O}_{X}) = A_{f}$, $\phi$
induces a ring map $\phi_{f'} : A'_{f'} \to A_{f}$ (0.1.5.1), and these are compatible with restrictions. The pair $\Phi
= ({}^{a}\phi, \tilde{\phi})$ is a morphism of ringed spaces $(X, \mathcal{O}_{X}) \to (X', \mathcal{O}_{X'})$.

For $x \in X$ with $x' = {}^{a}\phi(x)$, the stalk homomorphism $\tilde{\phi}^{\sharp}_{x} : A'_{x'} \to A_{x}$ is the
canonical map induced by $\phi$.

**Example (1.6.2).** For $S \subset A$ multiplicative and $\phi : A \to S^{-1}A$, ${}^{a}\phi : Y =
\operatorname{Spec}(S^{-1}A) \to X = \operatorname{Spec}(A)$ is a homeomorphism onto
$\{x : \mathfrak{j}_{x} \cap S = \emptyset\}$, and $\mathcal{O}_{Y}$ is identified with the induced sheaf
$\mathcal{O}_{X} | Y$ (since the stalk maps are isomorphisms).

**Proposition (1.6.3).** For every $A$-module $M$, there is a canonical functorial isomorphism
$\widetilde{M_{[\phi]}} \xrightarrow{\sim} \Phi_{*}(\tilde{M})$ of $\mathcal{O}_{X'}$-modules.

This also holds for algebras (with module by an algebra structure preserved).

**Corollary (1.6.4).** $\Phi_{*}$ is exact on quasi-coherent $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-modules.

**Proposition (1.6.5).** For every $A'$-module $N'$, setting $N = N' \otimes_{A'} A_{[\phi]}$, there is a canonical
functorial isomorphism $\Phi^{*}(\tilde{N}') \xrightarrow{\sim} \tilde{N}$ of $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-modules.

**Proof sketch.** The map $j : z' \mapsto z' \otimes 1$ is an $A'$-homomorphism $N' \to N_{[\phi]}$; by (1.3.8),
$\tilde{j} : \tilde{N}' \to \widetilde{N_{[\phi]}}$ is an $\mathcal{O}_{X'}$-module homomorphism, hence
$\tilde{j} : \tilde{N}' \to \Phi_{*}(\tilde{N})$ via (1.6.3). The corresponding
$h = \tilde{j}^{\sharp} : \Phi^{*}(\tilde{N}') \to \tilde{N}$ is bijective stalkwise — at any $x$ with
$x' = {}^{a}\phi(x)$, $h_{x}$ is the canonical isomorphism
$N'_{x'} \otimes_{A'_{x'}} (A_{x})_{[\phi_{x'}]} \xrightarrow{\sim} N_{x}$.

For $B'$ an $A'$-algebra, $\Phi^{*}(\tilde{B}') \xrightarrow{\sim} \widetilde{B' \otimes_{A'} A_{[\phi]}}$ is an
$\mathcal{O}_{X}$-algebra isomorphism.

**Corollary (1.6.6).** Sections of $\Phi^{*}(\tilde{N}')$ are generated, as $A$-module, by the canonical images of
sections of $\tilde{N}'$.

**(1.6.7)** The canonical map $\rho : \tilde{N}' \to \Phi_{*}(\Phi^{*}(\tilde{N}'))$ is $\tilde{j}$; the canonical
$\sigma : \Phi^{*}(\Phi_{*}(\tilde{M})) \to \tilde{M}$ is $\tilde{p}$ where
$p : M_{[\phi]} \otimes_{A'} A_{[\phi]} \to M$ is $z \otimes a \mapsto a \cdot z$.

**(1.6.8)** For $N'_{1}, N'_{2}$ $A'$-modules with $N'_{1}$ admitting a finite presentation, the canonical homomorphism
$\Phi^{*}(\mathcal{H}om_{\tilde{A}'}(\tilde{N}'_{1}, \tilde{N}'_{2})) \to
\mathcal{H}om_{\tilde{A}}(\Phi^{*}(\tilde{N}'_{1}), \Phi^{*}(\tilde{N}'_{2}))$ is $\tilde{\gamma}$, where
$\gamma : \operatorname{Hom}_{A'}(N'_{1}, N'_{2}) \otimes_{A'} A \to \operatorname{Hom}_{A}(N'_{1} \otimes_{A'} A, N'_{2}
\otimes_{A'} A)$ is canonical.

**(1.6.9)** For $\mathfrak{J}' \subset A'$ and $M$ an $A$-module, $\tilde{\mathfrak{J}}' \tilde{M} \cong
\widetilde{\mathfrak{J}' M}$; in particular $\Phi^{*}(\tilde{\mathfrak{J}}') \cdot \tilde{A} \cong
\widetilde{\mathfrak{J}' A}$, and $\Phi^{*}(\widetilde{A' / \mathfrak{J}'}) \cong \widetilde{A / \mathfrak{J}' A}$.

**(1.6.10)** For a third ring $A''$ and $\phi' : A'' \to A'$, with $\phi'' = \phi \circ \phi'$, one has ${}^{a}\phi'' =
{}^{a}\phi' \circ {}^{a}\phi$ and $\tilde{\phi}'' = \tilde{\phi} \circ \tilde{\phi}'$, so $\Phi'' = \Phi' \circ \Phi$.
Thus $(\operatorname{Spec}(A), \tilde{A})$ is a functor from rings to ringed spaces.

### 1.7. Characterization of morphisms of affine schemes

<!-- label: I.1.7 -->

**Definition (1.7.1).** A ringed space $(X, \mathcal{O}_{X})$ is an _affine scheme_ if it is isomorphic to
$(\operatorname{Spec}(A), \tilde{A})$ for some ring $A$; we then call $\Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_{X}) \cong A$ the _ring_ of
the affine scheme, written $A(X)$.

By abuse of language, the _affine scheme_ $\operatorname{Spec}(A)$ always denotes the ringed space
$(\operatorname{Spec}(A), \tilde{A})$.

**(1.7.2)** For affine schemes $X = \operatorname{Spec}(A)$ and $Y = \operatorname{Spec}(B)$, every ring homomorphism
$\phi : B \to A$ gives a morphism $\Phi = ({}^{a}\phi, \tilde{\phi}) = \operatorname{Spec}(\phi) : X \to Y$. Moreover,
$\phi = \Gamma(\tilde{\phi})$ recovers $\phi$ from $\Phi$.

**Theorem (1.7.3).** Let $(X, \mathcal{O}_{X})$, $(Y, \mathcal{O}_{Y})$ be affine schemes. For a morphism $(\psi,
\theta) : (X, \mathcal{O}_{X}) \to (Y, \mathcal{O}_{Y})$ of ringed spaces to be of the form $({}^{a}\phi, \tilde{\phi})$
for some $\phi : A(Y) \to A(X)$, it is necessary and sufficient that for every $x \in X$, $\theta^{\sharp}_{x} :
\mathcal{O}_{\psi(x)} \to \mathcal{O}_{x}$ be a _local_ homomorphism.

**Proof.** _Necessity:_ $\tilde{\phi}^{\sharp}_{x}$ is induced from $\phi$ by passing to fractions, so is local.
_Sufficiency:_ let $A = A(X)$, $B = A(Y)$, $\phi = \Gamma(\theta) : B \to A$. The local hypothesis on
$\theta^{\sharp}_{x}$ means it induces a monomorphism $\theta^{x} : \kappa(\psi(x)) \to \kappa(x)$ with
$\theta^{x}(f(\psi(x))) = \phi(f)(x)$. Hence $f(\psi(x)) = 0 \Longleftrightarrow \phi(f)(x) = 0$, so
$\mathfrak{j}_{\psi(x)} = \mathfrak{j}_{{}^{a}\phi(x)}$, i.e. $\psi = {}^{a}\phi$. The commutativity of stalks then
forces $\theta^{\sharp}_{x} = \tilde{\phi}^{\sharp}_{x}$ (0.1.5.1), hence $\theta = \tilde{\phi}$ (by (0.3.7.1)).

A morphism satisfying (1.7.3) is called a _morphism of affine schemes_.

**Corollary (1.7.4).** $\operatorname{Hom}((X, \mathcal{O}_{X}), (Y, \mathcal{O}_{Y}))$ (morphisms of affine schemes) is
canonically in bijection with $\operatorname{Hom}_{ring}(A(Y), A(X))$. Thus the functors $A \mapsto
(\operatorname{Spec}(A), \tilde{A})$ and $(X, \mathcal{O}_{X}) \mapsto \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_{X})$ give an _equivalence_
between commutative rings and the opposite category of affine schemes.

**Corollary (1.7.5).** If $\phi : B \to A$ is surjective, $({}^{a}\phi, \tilde{\phi})$ is a monomorphism of ringed
spaces.

### 1.8. Morphisms from locally ringed spaces to affine schemes

<!-- label: I.1.8 -->

A remark of J. Tate generalizes (1.7.3) and (2.2.4):

**Proposition (1.8.1).** Let $(S, \mathcal{O}_{S})$ be an affine scheme and $(X, \mathcal{O}_{X})$ a locally ringed
space. There is a canonical bijection between ring homomorphisms $\Gamma(S, \mathcal{O}_{S}) \to \Gamma(X,
\mathcal{O}_{X})$ and morphisms of ringed spaces $(\psi, \theta) : (X, \mathcal{O}_{X}) \to (S, \mathcal{O}_{S})$ such
that for every $x \in X$, $\theta^{\sharp}_{x} : \mathcal{O}_{\psi(x)} \to \mathcal{O}_{x}$ is _local_.

**Proof sketch.** A morphism of ringed spaces always gives $\rho : \operatorname{Hom}((X, \mathcal{O}_{X}), (S,
\mathcal{O}_{S})) \to \operatorname{Hom}(\Gamma(S, \mathcal{O}_{S}), \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_{X}))$ via $(\psi, \theta)
\mapsto \Gamma(\theta)$. Conversely, set $A = \Gamma(S, \mathcal{O}_{S})$ and let $\phi : A \to \Gamma(X,
\mathcal{O}_{X})$ be a ring homomorphism. For $x \in X$, $\{f \in A : \phi(f)(x) = 0\}$ is prime (since $\kappa(x)$ is
a field), giving ${}^{a}\phi(x) \in S = \operatorname{Spec}(A)$. ${}^{a}\phi^{-1}(D(f)) = X_{\phi(f)}$ (by (0.5.5.2)), so
${}^{a}\phi$ is continuous. Define $\tilde{\phi} : \mathcal{O}_{S} \to {}^{a}\phi_{*}(\mathcal{O}_{X})$ by sending $s/f
\in A_{f} = \Gamma(D(f), \mathcal{O}_{S})$ to $(\phi(s) | X_{\phi(f)}) (\phi(f) | X_{\phi(f)})^{-1}$. Then
$\tilde{\phi}^{\sharp}_{x}(s_{y} / f_{y}) = (\phi(s)_{x})(\phi(f)_{x})^{-1}$ where $y = {}^{a}\phi(x)$; $s_{y} \in
\mathfrak{m}_{y} \Longleftrightarrow \phi(s)_{x} \in \mathfrak{m}_{x}$, so $\tilde{\phi}^{\sharp}_{x}$ is local. The two
maps $\rho$ and $\sigma$ are mutually inverse.

**(1.8.2)** For locally ringed spaces $X$, $Y$, we always consider morphisms $(\psi, \theta)$ whose stalk maps
$\theta^{\sharp}_{x}$ are local; with this definition, locally ringed spaces form a category. Write
$\operatorname{Hom}(X, Y)$ for morphisms in this category and $\operatorname{Hom}_{rs}(X, Y)$ for morphisms of ringed
spaces (without the locality condition). The map $\rho : \operatorname{Hom}_{rs}(X, Y) \to \operatorname{Hom}(\Gamma(Y,
\mathcal{O}_{Y}), \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_{X}))$ restricts to a functorial $\rho' : \operatorname{Hom}(X, Y) \to
\operatorname{Hom}(\Gamma(Y, \mathcal{O}_{Y}), \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_{X}))$.

**Corollary (1.8.3).** A locally ringed space $Y$ is an affine scheme if and only if $\rho' : \operatorname{Hom}(X, Y)
\to \operatorname{Hom}(\Gamma(Y, \mathcal{O}_{Y}), \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_{X}))$ is bijective for every locally ringed
space $X$.

**(1.8.4)** Let $S = \operatorname{Spec}(A)$ and $(S', A')$ the ringed space whose underlying space is a point and whose
sheaf is the simple sheaf defined by $A$. Let $\pi : S \to S'$ be the unique map, and $\iota : A' \to \mathcal{O}_{S}$
the $\pi$-morphism from the constant sections. This gives a canonical morphism $i = (\pi, \iota) : (S, \mathcal{O}_{S})
\to (S', A')$. For an $A$-module $M$, write $M'$ for the simple sheaf on $S'$; then $i_{*}(\tilde{M}) = M'$.

**Lemma (1.8.5).** Under (1.8.4), the canonical homomorphism $i^{*}(i_{*}(\tilde{M})) \to \tilde{M}$ is an isomorphism
for every $A$-module $M$.

**Corollary (1.8.6).** For a ringed space $(X, \mathcal{O}_{X})$ and $u : X \to S$, there is a canonical functorial
isomorphism $u^{*}(\tilde{M}) \xrightarrow{\sim} u^{*}(i^{*}(M'))$ of $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-modules.

**Corollary (1.8.7).** Under (1.8.6), there is a canonical functorial isomorphism

$$ \operatorname{Hom}_{\mathcal{O}_{S}}(\tilde{M}, u_{*}(\mathcal{F})) \xrightarrow{\sim} \operatorname{Hom}_{A}(M,
\Gamma(X, \mathcal{F})). \tag{1.8.7.1} $$

**(1.8.8)** With the notation of (1.8.4), a morphism $(\psi, \theta) : X \to S'$ of ringed spaces is the same as a ring
homomorphism $A \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_{X})$. By (1.8.1), there is a canonical bijection $\operatorname{Hom}(X, S)
\xrightarrow{\sim} \operatorname{Hom}(X, S')$ (the right-hand side meaning morphisms of ringed spaces). For locally
ringed spaces `X, Y`, the analogous setup yields $\rho : \operatorname{Hom}_{rs}(X, Y) \to \operatorname{Hom}(X, Y')$.
By (1.8.3), affine schemes are characterized among locally ringed spaces as those for which the restriction $\rho' :
\operatorname{Hom}(X, Y) \to \operatorname{Hom}(X, Y')$ is bijective for every locally ringed space $X$.

In the next chapter, this is generalized to associate to _any_ ringed space $Z$ a locally ringed space
$\operatorname{Spec}(Z)$, the starting point for a "relative" theory of preschemes over a ringed space.

**(1.8.9)** Pairs $(X, \mathcal{F})$ with $X$ a locally ringed space and $\mathcal{F}$ an $\mathcal{O}_{X}$-module form
a category, with morphisms $(u, h) : (X, \mathcal{F}) \to (Y, \mathcal{G})$ where $u : X \to Y$ is a morphism of locally
ringed spaces and $h : \mathcal{G} \to \mathcal{F}$ a $u$-morphism. The map $(u, h) \mapsto (\rho'(u), \Gamma(h))$ is
functorial.

**Corollary (1.8.10).** For $Y$ a locally ringed space and $\mathcal{G}$ an $\mathcal{O}_{Y}$-module, $Y$ is an affine
scheme and $\mathcal{G}$ is quasi-coherent if and only if for every pair $(X, \mathcal{F})$, the canonical map (1.8.9.1)
is bijective.

**Remark (1.8.11).** Theorem (1.7.3), Corollary (1.7.4), and Proposition (2.2.4) are special cases of (1.8.1); the
definition in (1.6.1) likewise. Corollary (1.8.7) implies (1.6.3) and (1.6.4); (1.6.5) and (1.6.6) follow from (1.8.6).
