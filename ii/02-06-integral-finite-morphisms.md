# ¬ß6. Integral morphisms and finite morphisms

## 6.1. Preschemes integral over another

**Definition.**

<!-- label: II.6.1.1 -->

Let `X` be an `S`-prescheme and `f : X ‚Üí S` its structure morphism. We say that
`X` is _integral over_ `S`, or that `f` is an _integral morphism_, if there
exists a cover `(S_őĪ)` of `S` by affine opens such that, for every `őĪ`, the
induced prescheme `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(S_őĪ)` is an affine scheme whose ring `B_őĪ` is an
_integral algebra_ over the ring `A_őĪ` of `S_őĪ`. We say that `X` is _finite
over_ `S`, or that `f` is a _finite morphism_, if `X` is integral and of finite
type over `S`.

When `S` is affine of ring `A`, we shall also say "integral (resp. finite) over
`A`" instead of "integral (resp. finite) over `S`".

**(6.1.2)**

<!-- label: II.6.1.2 -->

It is clear that if `X` is integral over `S`, then it is affine over `S`. For an
affine prescheme `X` over `S` to be integral (resp. finite) over `S`, it is
necessary and sufficient that the associated quasi-coherent `ūĚí™_S`-algebra
`ūĚíú(X)` be such that there exists a cover `(S_őĪ)` of `S` by affine opens with
the property that, for every `őĪ`, `őď(S_őĪ, ūĚíú(X))` is an integral (resp.
integral and of finite type) algebra over `őď(S_őĪ, ūĚí™_S)`. A quasi-coherent
`ūĚí™_S`-algebra with this property is said to be _integral_ (resp. _finite_)
over `ūĚí™_S`. To give an integral (resp. finite) prescheme over `S` is therefore
the same (1.3.1) as to give a quasi-coherent `ūĚí™_S`-algebra that is integral
(resp. finite) over `ūĚí™_S`. Note that a quasi-coherent `ūĚí™_S`-algebra `‚Ą¨` is
finite if and only if it is an `ūĚí™_S`-module of finite type `(I, 1.3.9)`; this
is equivalent to saying that `‚Ą¨` is an integral `ūĚí™_S`-algebra of finite type,
since an algebra that is integral and of finite type over a ring `A` is an
`A`-module of finite type.

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.6.1.3 -->

Let `S` be a locally Noetherian prescheme. For an affine prescheme `X` over `S`
to be finite over `S`, it is necessary and sufficient that the
`ūĚí™_S`-algebra `ūĚíú(X)` be coherent.

**Proof.** Taking the preceding remark into account, this comes down to noting
that, if `S` is locally Noetherian, the quasi-coherent `ūĚí™_S`-modules of finite
type are exactly the coherent `ūĚí™_S`-modules `(I, 1.5.1)`.

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.6.1.4 -->

Let `X` be an integral (resp. finite) prescheme over `S` and `f : X ‚Üí S` its
structure morphism. Then, for every affine open `U ‚äā S` of ring `A`, `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(U)`
is an affine scheme whose ring `B` is an integral (resp. finite) algebra over
`A`.

<!-- original page 111 -->

**Proof.** We first prove the following lemma.

**Lemma.**

<!-- label: II.6.1.4.1 -->

Let `A` be a ring, `M` an `A`-module, and `(g_i)_{1 ‚Č§ i ‚Č§ m}` a finite system
of elements of `A` such that the `D(g_i)` (`1 ‚Č§ i ‚Č§ m`) cover `Spec(A)`. If, for
every `i`, `M_{g_i}` is an `A_{g_i}`-module of finite type, then `M` is an
`A`-module of finite type.

**Proof.** We may assume that each `M_{g_i}` admits a finite system of
generators `(m_{ij}/g_i^n)` with `m_{ij} ‚ąą M`, with `n` the same for all indices
`i`. We show that the `m_{ij}` form a system of generators of `M`. Let `M'`
be the sub-`A`-module of `M` generated by these elements, and let `m` be an
element of `M`. By hypothesis, for each `i`, there exist `a_{ij} ‚ąą A` and an
integer `p` (independent of `i`) such that, in `M_{g_i}`,
`m/1 = (‚ąĎ_j a_{ij} m_{ij})/g_i^p`; this implies that there exists an integer
`r ‚Č• p` such that, for every `i`, `g_i^r m ‚ąą M'`. Now, since the
`D(g_i^r) = D(g_i)` cover `Spec(A)`, the ideal of `A` generated by the `g_i^r`
is equal to `A`; in other words, there exist elements `a_i ‚ąą A` such that
`‚ąĎ_i a_i g_i^r = 1`, and consequently `m = (‚ąĎ_i a_i g_i^r) m ‚ąą M'`, whence
the lemma.

This being so, we already know `(1.3.2)` that `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(U)` is affine. If `ŌÜ` is the
homomorphism `A ‚Üí B` corresponding to `f`, there exists a finite cover of `U`
by opens `D(g_i)` (`g_i ‚ąą A`) such that, if `h_i = ŌÜ(g_i)`, then `B_{h_i}` is
an integral (resp. integral and finite) algebra over `A_{g_i}`. Indeed, there
is a cover of `U` by affine opens `V_őĪ ‚äā U` such that, if `A_őĪ = ūĚíú(V_őĪ)`,
`B_őĪ = ūĚíú(f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(V_őĪ))` is an integral (resp. finite) algebra over `A_őĪ`. Every
`x ‚ąą U` belongs to some `V_őĪ`, so there exists `g ‚ąą A` such that
`x ‚ąą D(g) ‚äā V_őĪ`; if `g_őĪ` is the image of `g` in `A_őĪ`, then
`ūĚíú(D(g)) = A_g = (A_őĪ)_{g_őĪ}`. Let `h = ŌÜ(g)` and let `h_őĪ` be the image of
`g_őĪ` in `B_őĪ`; we have

```text
  ūĚíú(D(h)) = B_h = (B_őĪ)_{h_őĪ}
```

and, since `B_őĪ` is integral (resp. finite) over `A_őĪ`, `(B_őĪ)_{h_őĪ}` is
integral (resp. finite) over `(A_őĪ)_{g_őĪ}`. It now suffices to use the fact
that `U` is quasi-compact to obtain the desired cover.

If we first suppose that the `B_{h_i}` are integral and finite over the
`A_{g_i}`, then since `B_{h_i}`, viewed as an `A_{g_i}`-module, also coincides
with `B_{g_i}`, Lemma (6.1.4.1) shows that in this case `B` is an `A`-module
of finite type.

Now suppose only that each `B_{h_i}` is integral over `A_{g_i}`; let `b ‚ąą B`,
and let `C` be the sub-`A`-algebra of `B` generated by `b`. For every `i`,
`C_{h_i}` is the algebra over `A_{g_i}` generated by `b/1` in `B_{h_i}`; it
follows from the hypothesis that each `C_{h_i}` is an `A_{g_i}`-module of
finite type, hence (6.1.4.1) `C` is an `A`-module of finite type, which proves
that `B` is integral over `A`.

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.6.1.5 -->

- (i) A closed immersion is finite (and _a fortiori_ integral).
- (ii) The composition of two finite (resp. integral) morphisms is finite
    (resp. integral).
- (iii) If `f : X ‚Üí Y` is a finite (resp. integral) `S`-morphism, then
    `f_{(S')} : X_{(S')} ‚Üí Y_{(S')}` is finite (resp. integral) for every base
    extension `S' ‚Üí S`.
- (iv) If `f : X ‚Üí Y` and `g : X' ‚Üí Y'` are two finite (resp. integral)
    `S`-morphisms, then so is `f √ó_S g : X √ó_S X' ‚Üí Y √ó_S Y'`.
- (v) If `f : X ‚Üí Y` and `g : Y ‚Üí Z` are morphisms such that `g ‚ąė f` is finite
    (resp. integral) and `g` is separated, then `f` is finite (resp. integral).
- (vi) If `f : X ‚Üí Y` is a finite (resp. integral) morphism, then so is
    `f_red`.

<!-- original page 112 -->

**Proof.** By `(I, 5.5.12)`, it suffices to prove (i), (ii), and (iii). To
prove that a closed immersion `X ‚Üí S` is finite, we may restrict to the case
`S = Spec(A)`, and everything then comes down to noting that a quotient ring
`A/ūĚĒß` is a monogeneous `A`-module. To prove that the composition of two finite
(resp. integral) morphisms `X ‚Üí Y`, `Y ‚Üí Z` is finite (resp. integral), we may
again assume that `Z` (and hence `X` and `Y` `(1.3.4)`) is affine, and the
claim then amounts to: if `B` is a finite (resp. integral) `A`-algebra and `C`
a finite (resp. integral) `B`-algebra, then `C` is a finite (resp. integral)
`A`-algebra, which is immediate. Finally, to prove (iii), we may restrict to
the case `S = Y`, since `X_{(S')}` is identified with `X √ó_Y Y_{(S')}`
`(I, 3.3.11)`; we may further assume that `S = Spec(A)` and `S' = Spec(A')`;
then `X` is affine of ring `B` `(1.3.4)`, `X_{(S')}` is affine of ring
`A' ‚äó_A B`, and it suffices to remark that if `B` is a finite (resp. integral)
`A`-algebra, then `A' ‚äó_A B` is a finite (resp. integral) `A'`-algebra.

We also note that if `X` and `Y` are two `S`-preschemes that are finite (resp.
integral) over `S`, their sum `X ‚äĒ Y` is a finite (resp. integral) prescheme
over `S`, since this comes down to seeing that if `B` and `C` are finite
(resp. integral) `A`-algebras, then so is `B √ó C`.

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.6.1.6 -->

If `X` is an integral (resp. finite) prescheme over `S`, then, for every open
`U ‚äā S`, `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(U)` is integral (resp. finite) over `U`.

**Proof.** This is a particular case of (6.1.5, (iii)).

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.6.1.7 -->

Let `f : X ‚Üí Y` be a finite morphism. Then, for every `y ‚ąą Y`, the fibre
`f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(y)` is a finite algebraic scheme over `őļ(y)`, and _a fortiori_ its
underlying space is discrete and finite.

**Proof.** Indeed, as a `őļ(y)`-prescheme, `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(y)` is identified with
`X √ó_Y Spec(őļ(y))` `(I, 3.6.1)`, which is finite over `Spec(őļ(y))`
(6.1.5, (iii)); it is thus an affine scheme whose ring is an algebra of finite
rank over `őļ(y)` (6.1.4). The corollary then follows from `(I, 6.4.4)`.

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.6.1.8 -->

Let `X`, `S` be two integral preschemes and `f : X ‚Üí S` a _dominant_ morphism.
If `f` is integral (resp. finite), then the field `R(X)` of rational functions
on `X` is algebraic (resp. algebraic of finite degree) over the field `R(S)`
of rational functions on `S`.

**Proof.** Let `s` be the generic point of `S`; the `őļ(s)`-prescheme `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(s)`
is integral (resp. finite) over `Spec(őļ(s))` (6.1.5, (iii)) and contains, by
hypothesis, the generic point `x` of `X`; the local ring of `x` in `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(s)`,
being equal to `őļ(x)` `(I, 3.6.5)`, is a local ring of an integral (resp.
finite) algebra over `őļ(s)` (6.1.4), whence the corollary.

**Remark.**

<!-- label: II.6.1.9 -->

The hypothesis that `g` is separated is essential for the validity of
(6.1.5, (v)): indeed, if `Y` is not separated over `Z`, then the identity
`1_Y` is the composite morphism
`Y ‚Üí^{őĒ_Y} Y √ó_Z Y ‚Üí^{p_1} Y`, but `őĒ_Y` is not an integral morphism, as
follows from (6.1.10):

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.6.1.10 -->

Every integral morphism is universally closed.

**Proof.** Let `f : X ‚Üí Y` be an integral morphism; by (6.1.5, (iii)), it
suffices to show that `f` is closed. Let `Z` be a closed part of `X`; there
exists a subprescheme of `X` whose

underlying space is `Z` `(I, 5.2.1)`, and it therefore follows from
(6.1.5, (i) and (ii)) that we may restrict to proving that `f(X)` is closed in

**Corollary.**

**Proof.** Since `f` is affine, `ūĚí™_X` is a very ample `ūĚí™_X`-module relative to

finite type (6.1.2); finally, `f` is separated, of finite type, and

**Proof.** It is clear that if `f_*(‚ĄĪ')|U` is isomorphic to `‚Ą¨^r|U` (`U` open

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.6.1.13 -->

Let `g : X' ‚Üí X` be an integral morphism of preschemes, `Y` a normal locally
integral prescheme, and `f` a rational map from `Y` to `X'` such that `g ‚ąė f`
is an everywhere defined rational map `(I, 7.2.1)`. Then `f` is everywhere

**Proof.** If `f‚āĀ` and `f‚āā` are two morphisms (from dense opens of `Y` to

morphisms, which justifies the notation `g ‚ąė f` for their equivalence class.

the hypothesis that `Y` is normal already implies that `Y` is locally integral
`(I, 6.1.13)`.

To prove (6.1.13), let us first note that the question is local on `Y`, and so
we may assume that there exists in the class `g ‚ąė f` a morphism `h : Y ‚Üí X`.
Consider the inverse image `Y' = X'_{(h)} = X'_{(Y)}`, and note that the
morphism `g' = g_{(Y)} : Y' ‚Üí Y` is integral (6.1.5, (iii)). In view of the
correspondence between rational maps from `Y` to `X'` and rational `Y`-sections
of `Y'` `(I, 7.1.2)`, we see that we are reduced to proving the particular
case of (6.1.13) in which `X = Y`; in other words:

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.6.1.14 -->

Let `X` be a normal locally integral prescheme, `g : X' ‚Üí X` an integral
morphism, and `f` a rational `X`-section of `X'`. Then `f` is everywhere
defined.

**Proof.** Since the question is local on `X`, we may assume `X` integral, and
then `f` is identified with a morphism from an open `U` of `X` to `X'`
`(I, 7.2.2)` which is a `U`-section of `g‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(U)`. Since `g` is separated, `f`
is a closed immersion of `U` into `g‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(U)` `(I, 5.4.6)`; let `Z` be the closed
subprescheme of `g‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(U)` associated to `f` `(I, 4.2.1)`, which is isomorphic
to `U`, hence integral; let `X‚āĀ` be the reduced subprescheme of `X'` whose
underlying space is the closure `ZŐĄ` of `Z` in `X'` `(I, 5.2.1)`; then `Z` is
a subprescheme induced on an open of `X‚āĀ` `(I, 5.2.3)`, and, being
irreducible, so is `X‚āĀ`, which is therefore integral. The morphism `f` can
then be considered as a rational `X`-section of `X‚āĀ`; since the restriction of
`g` to `X‚āĀ` is an integral morphism (6.1.5, (i) and (ii)), we are finally
reduced to proving (6.1.14) in the particular case `X' = X‚āĀ`; in other words:

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.6.1.15 -->

Let `X` be a normal integral prescheme, `X'` an integral prescheme, and
`g : X' ‚Üí X` an integral morphism. If there exists a rational `X`-section `f`
of `X'`, then `g` is an isomorphism.

**Proof.** Since the question is local on `X`, we may assume `X` affine of
integral ring `A`, and then `X'` is affine of ring `A'` integral over `A`
(6.1.4) and integral; furthermore, the argument of (6.1.14) shows that there
exists a dense open in `X` isomorphic to a dense open of `X'`, hence `A` and
`A'` have the same field of fractions. On the other hand, by `(I, 8.2.1.1)`
and the hypothesis that the `ūĚí™_x` are integrally closed, the ring `A` is
integrally closed, hence `A' = A`, which completes the proof of (6.1.13).

## 6.2. Quasi-finite morphisms

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.6.2.1 -->

Let `f : X ‚Üí Y` be a morphism locally of finite type, `x` a point of `X`.
The following conditions are equivalent:

<!-- original page 115 -->

- a) The point `x` is isolated in its fibre `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(f(x))`.
- b) The ring `ūĚí™_x` is a quasi-finite `ūĚí™_{f(x)}`-module `(0, 7.4.1)`.

**Proof.** Since the question is evidently local on `X` and on `Y`, we may
assume `X = Spec(A)` and `Y = Spec(B)` affine, with `A` a `B`-algebra of
finite type `(I, 6.3.3)`. Moreover, we may replace `X` by
`X √ó_Y Spec(ūĚí™_{f(x)})` without changing the fibre `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(f(x))` or the local
ring `ūĚí™_x` `(I, 3.6.5)`; we may therefore assume that `B` is a local ring
(equal to `ūĚí™_{f(x)}`); if `ūĚĒę` is the maximal ideal of `B`, then `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(f(x))`
is an affine scheme of ring `A/ūĚĒęA`, of finite type over `őļ(f(x)) = B/ūĚĒę`
`(I, 6.4.11)`. This being so, if a) is satisfied, we may further assume that
`f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(f(x))` is reduced to the point `x`; then `A/ūĚĒęA` is of finite rank over
`B/ūĚĒę` `(I, 6.4.4)`, that is, `A` is a quasi-finite `B`-module. Conversely,
if this is so, `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(f(x))` is an Artinian affine scheme, hence discrete
`(I, 6.4.4)`; consequently `x` is isolated in its fibre, which shows that b)
implies a).

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.6.2.2 -->

Let `f : X ‚Üí Y` be a morphism of finite type. The following conditions are
equivalent:

- a) Every point `x ‚ąą X` is isolated in its fibre `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(f(x))` (in other
    words, the subspace `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(f(x))` is discrete).
- b) For every `x ‚ąą X`, the prescheme `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(f(x))` is a finite
    `őļ(f(x))`-prescheme.
- c) For every `x ‚ąą X`, the ring `ūĚí™_x` is a quasi-finite `ūĚí™_{f(x)}`-module.

**Proof.** The equivalence of a) and c) follows from (6.2.1). On the other
hand, since `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(f(x))` is a `őļ(f(x))`-algebraic prescheme `(I, 6.4.11)`, the
equivalence of a) and b) follows from `(I, 6.4.4)`.

**Definition.**

<!-- label: II.6.2.3 -->

When `f : X ‚Üí Y` is a morphism of finite type satisfying the equivalent
conditions of (6.2.2), we say that `f` is _quasi-finite_, or that `X` is
_quasi-finite over_ `Y`.

It is clear that every finite morphism is quasi-finite (6.1.8).

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.6.2.4 -->

- (i) An immersion `X ‚Üí Y` which is closed, or such that `X` is Noetherian,
    is a quasi-finite morphism.
- (ii) If `f : X ‚Üí Y` and `g : Y ‚Üí Z` are quasi-finite morphisms, then
    `g ‚ąė f` is quasi-finite.
- (iii) If `X` and `Y` are `S`-preschemes and `f : X ‚Üí Y` a quasi-finite
    `S`-morphism, then `f_{(S')} : X_{(S')} ‚Üí Y_{(S')}` is quasi-finite for
    every base extension `g : S' ‚Üí S`.
- (iv) If `f : X ‚Üí Y` and `g : X' ‚Üí Y'` are two quasi-finite `S`-morphisms,
    then
    ```text
      f √ó_S g : X √ó_S X' ‚Üí Y √ó_S Y'
    ```
    is quasi-finite.
- (v) Let `f : X ‚Üí Y` and `g : Y ‚Üí Z` be morphisms such that `g ‚ąė f` is
    quasi-finite; if, further, `g` is separated, or `X` is Noetherian, or
    `X √ó_Z Y` is locally Noetherian, then `f` is quasi-finite.
- (vi) If `f` is quasi-finite, so is `f_red`.

**Proof.** If `f : X ‚Üí Y` is an immersion, then every fibre is reduced to a
point, and assertion (i) therefore follows from `(I, 6.3.4 (i)` and `6.3.5)`.
To prove (ii), we remark first that `h = g ‚ąė f` is of finite type
`(I, 6.3.4 (ii))`; furthermore, if `z = h(x)` and `y = f(x)`, then `y` is
isolated in `g‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(z)`, so there exists an open neighbourhood `V` of `y` in `Y`
meeting no point of `g‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(z)` other than `y`; therefore `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(V)` is an open
neighbourhood of `x` meeting

<!-- original page 116 -->

none of the `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(y')`, where `y' ‚Č† y` is in `g‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(z)`; since `x` is isolated
in `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(y)`, it is isolated in `h‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(z) = f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(g‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(z))`. To prove (iii), we may
restrict to the case `Y = S` `(I, 3.3.11)`; we remark again first that
`f' = f_{(S')}` is of finite type `(I, 6.3.4, (iii))`; on the other hand, if
`x' ‚ąą X' = X_{(S')}` and if we set `y' = f'(x')` and `y = g(y')`, then
`f'‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(y')` is identified with `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(y) ‚äó_{őļ(y)} őļ(y')` `(I, 3.6.5)`; since
`f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(y)` is of finite rank over `őļ(y)` by hypothesis, `f'‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(y')` is of finite
rank over `őļ(y')`, hence discrete. The assertions (iv), (v), (vi) follow from
(i), (ii), (iii) by the general method `(I, 5.5.12)`, except when the
hypotheses in (v) are other than "`g` separated"; to treat this case, we
remark first that if `x` is isolated in `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(g‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(g(f(x))))`, it is _a fortiori_
isolated in `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(f(x))`; the fact that `f` is of finite type then follows
from `(I, 6.3.6)`.

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.6.2.5 -->

Let `A` be a complete local Noetherian ring, `X` an `A`-scheme locally of
finite type, `x` a point of `X` over the closed point `y` of `Y = Spec(A)`,
and suppose that `x` is isolated in its fibre `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(y)` (where `f` is the
structure morphism `X ‚Üí Y`). Then `ūĚí™_x` is an `A`-module of finite type, and
`X` is `Y`-isomorphic to the sum `(I, 3.1)` of `X' = Spec(ūĚí™_x)` (which is a
finite `Y`-scheme) and an `A`-scheme `X''`.

**Proof.** It follows from (6.2.1) that `ūĚí™_x` is a quasi-finite `A`-module.
Since `ūĚí™_x` is Noetherian `(I, 6.3.7)` and the homomorphism `A ‚Üí ūĚí™_x` is
local, the hypothesis that `A` is complete implies that `ūĚí™_x` is an
`A`-module of finite type `(0, 7.4.3)`. Let `X' = Spec(ūĚí™_x)` be the local
scheme of `X` at the point `x` `(I, 2.4.1)`, and let `g : X' ‚Üí X` be the
canonical morphism. Since the composite `X' ‚Üí X ‚Üí Y` is finite (6.1.1) and
`f` is separated, `g` is finite (6.1.5, (v)), hence `g(X')` is closed in `X`
(6.1.10); on the other hand, since `g` is of finite type, `g` is a local
isomorphism at the closed point `x'` of `X'` by the definition of `g` and
`(I, 6.5.4)`; but since `X'` is the only open neighbourhood of `x'`, this
means that `g` is an open immersion, hence `g(X')` is also open in `X`, which
completes the proof.

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.6.2.6 -->

separated quasi-finite morphism. Then `X` is `Y`-isomorphic to a sum
`X' ‚äĒ X''`, where `X'` is a finite `Y`-scheme and `X''` a quasi-finite
`Y`-scheme such that, if `y` is the closed point of `Y`,
`X'' ‚ą© f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(y) = ‚ąÖ`.

**Proof.** Indeed, the fibre `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(y)` is finite and discrete by hypothesis,
and the corollary therefore follows, by induction on the number of points of
this fibre, from (6.2.5).

**Remark.**

<!-- label: II.6.2.7 -->

## 6.3. Integral closure of a prescheme

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.6.3.1 -->

Let `(X, ūĚíú)` be a ringed space, `‚Ą¨` a (commutative) `ūĚíú`-algebra, `f` a section
of `‚Ą¨` over `X`. The following properties are equivalent:

- a) The sub-`ūĚíú`-module of `‚Ą¨` generated by the `f^n` for `n ‚Č• 0`
    `(0, 5.1.1)` is of finite type.
- b) There exists a sub-`ūĚíú`-algebra `ūĚíě` of `‚Ą¨` that is an `ūĚíú`-module of
    finite type, such that `f ‚ąą őď(X, ūĚíě)`.
- c) For every `x ‚ąą X`, `f_x` is integral over the fibre `ūĚíú_x`.

<!-- original page 117 -->

**Proof.** Since the sub-`ūĚíú`-module of `‚Ą¨` generated by the `f^n` is an
`ūĚíú`-algebra, it is clear that a) implies b). On the other hand, b) implies
that for every `x ‚ąą X`, the `ūĚíú_x`-module `ūĚíě_x` is of finite type, which
implies that every element of the algebra `ūĚíě_x`, and in particular `f_x`, is
integral over `ūĚíú_x`. Finally, if for some `x ‚ąą X` we have a relation of the
form

```text
  f_x^n + (a‚āĀ)_x f_x^{n‚ąí1} + ‚Ä¶ + (a_n)_x = 0
```

where the `a_i` (`1 ‚Č§ i ‚Č§ n`) are sections of `ūĚíú` over an open neighbourhood
`U` of `x`, then the section `f^n|U + a‚āĀ ¬∑ f^{n‚ąí1}|U + ‚Ä¶ + a_n` is zero over
a neighbourhood `V ‚äā U` of `x`, whence it follows immediately that all the
`f^k|V` (`k ‚Č• 0`) are linear combinations with coefficients in `őď(V, ūĚíú)` of
the `f^j|V` for `0 ‚Č§ j ‚Č§ n ‚ąí 1`; we conclude that c) implies a).

When the equivalent conditions of (6.3.1) are satisfied, we say that the
section `f` is _integral over_ `ūĚíú`.

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.6.3.2 -->

Under the hypotheses of (6.3.1), there exists a (unique) sub-`ūĚíú`-module `ūĚíú'`
of `‚Ą¨` such that, for every `x ‚ąą X`, `ūĚíú'_x` is the set of germs `f_x ‚ąą ‚Ą¨_x`
that are integral over `ūĚíú_x`. For every open `U ‚äā X`, the sections of `ūĚíú'`
over `U` are the sections `f ‚ąą őď(U, ‚Ą¨)` that are integral over `ūĚíú|U`.

**Proof.** The existence of `ūĚíú'` is immediate, by taking `őď(U, ūĚíú')` to be the
set of `f ‚ąą őď(U, ‚Ą¨)` such that `f_x` is integral over `ūĚíú_x` for every
`x ‚ąą U`. The second assertion follows immediately from (6.3.1).

It is clear that `ūĚíú'` is a sub-`ūĚíú`-algebra of `‚Ą¨`; we say that it is the
_integral closure of_ `ūĚíú` _in_ `‚Ą¨`.

**(6.3.3)**

<!-- label: II.6.3.3 -->

Let `(X, ūĚíú)`, `(Y, ‚Ą¨)` be two ringed spaces and

```text
  g = (Ōą, őł) : X ‚Üí Y
```

a morphism. Let `ūĚíě` (resp. `ūĚíü`) be an `ūĚíú`-algebra (resp. a `‚Ą¨`-algebra) and
let

```text
  u : ūĚíü ‚Üí ūĚíě
```

be a `g`-morphism `(0, 4.4.1)`. Then, if `ūĚíě'` (resp. `‚Ą¨'`) is the integral
closure of `ūĚíú` (resp. `‚Ą¨`) in `ūĚíě` (resp. `ūĚíü`), the restriction of `u` to `‚Ą¨'`


Indeed, if `j` is the canonical injection `‚Ą¨' ‚Üí ūĚíü`, it suffices to show that


`g*(‚Ą¨')_x = ‚Ą¨'_{Ōą(x)} ‚äó_{‚Ą¨_{Ōą(x)}} ūĚíú_x` is integral over `ūĚíú_x` by the

**Proposition.**

`őď(U, ūĚí™_X)` in `őď(U, ūĚíú)`.

**Proof.** We may restrict to the case where `X = Spec(B)` is affine and
`ūĚíú = √É`, where `A` is a

`B`-algebra; let `B'` be the integral closure of `B` in `A`. Everything comes
down to seeing that for every `x ‚ąą X`, an element of `A_x` integral over `B_x`

commutative ring `C`, the operations of integral closure in a `C`-algebra

**(6.3.5)**

commute.

**(6.3.6)**

<!-- label: II.6.3.6 -->

Suppose that `X` has only a finite number of irreducible components `X_i`
(`1 ‚Č§ i ‚Č§ r`), with generic points `őĺ_i`, and consider in particular the

these hypotheses and with this notation:

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.6.3.7 -->

order to have `X' = X`, it is necessary and sufficient that `X` be normal.
When `X` is an integral prescheme, it follows from (6.3.8) that its

```text
  K ‚ĒÄ‚ĒÄ‚ĒÄ‚ĒÄ‚ĒÄ‚Üí L
```

(resp. `X`), hence as an `‚Ąõ(Y)`-algebra (resp. an `‚Ąõ(X)`-algebra), this means

integral closure of `X` (resp. `Y`) relative to `L‚āĀ` (resp. `K‚āĀ`), then `X'`

`K'`)

<!-- original page 120 -->

of `L` (resp. `K`) in `L‚āĀ` (resp. `K‚āĀ`), and there exists a canonical
(necessarily dominant) morphism `f' : X' ‚Üí Y'` making the diagram (6.3.5.1)

The most important case is that in which one takes `L‚āĀ = L`, so `K‚āĀ` is then
an extension of `K` contained in `L`, and one supposes `X` integral and
normal, hence `X' = X`. What precedes then shows that when `X` is normal, and
`Y'` is the integral closure of `Y` relative to a field `K‚āĀ ‚äā L = R(X)`, every
dominant morphism `f : X ‚Üí Y` factors as

```text
  f : X ‚Üí^{f'} Y' ‚Üí Y
```

where `f'` is dominant; moreover, when the monomorphism `K‚āĀ ‚Üí L` is given, `f'`
is necessarily unique, as one sees by reducing to the case where `X` and `Y`
are affine. We thus see that, for the data of `Y`, `L`, and a

is the solution of a universal problem.

**Remark.**

<!-- label: II.6.3.10 -->

Let us take up the hypotheses of (6.3.6), supposing further that each of the
algebras `A_i` is of finite rank over `őļ(őĺ_i)` (which implies that `A_i` is a
direct sum of a finite number of fields); we can assert in certain cases that
the structure morphism `X' ‚Üí X` is not only integral, but even finite. Let
us restrict to the case where `X` is reduced; since the question is local on
`X`, we may further assume that `X` is affine of ring `C`, and that `C` has
only a finite number of minimal ideals `ūĚĒ≠_i` (`1 ‚Č§ i ‚Č§ r`) such that the
`C_i = C/ūĚĒ≠_i` are integral; then `X'` will be finite over `X` if the integral
closure of each `C_i` in every extension of finite degree of its field of
fractions is a `C`-module of finite type (6.3.4). We know that this condition
is always satisfied when `C` is an algebra of finite type over a field
([13], t. I, p. 267, th. 9), or over `‚Ą§` ([9, I, p. 93, th. 3]), or over a
complete local Noetherian ring ([25], p. 298). We conclude that `X' ‚Üí X` will
be a finite morphism whenever `X` is a scheme of finite type over a field,
over `‚Ą§`, or over a complete local Noetherian ring.

**(6.4.1)**

<!-- label: II.6.4.1 -->

```text
  P(u, T) = T^n ‚ąí ŌÉ‚āĀ(u) T^{n‚ąí1} + ‚Ä¶ + (‚ąí1)^n ŌÉ_n(u)                         (6.4.1.2)
```

where `ŌÉ_i(u)` is an element of `A`, equal to a homogeneous polynomial of
degree `i` (with integer coefficients) in the entries of the matrix of `u`
relative to an arbitrary basis of `E`; we say that the `ŌÉ_i(u)` are the
_elementary symmetric functions_ of `u`, and in particular we have
`ŌÉ‚āĀ(u) = Tr u` and `ŌÉ_n(u) = det u`. Recall that, by the Hamilton‚ÄďCayley
theorem, we have

```text
  P(u, u) = u^n ‚ąí ŌÉ‚āĀ(u) u^{n‚ąí1} + ‚Ä¶ + (‚ąí1)^n ŌÉ_n(u) = 0                     (6.4.1.3)
```

<!-- original page 121 -->

which can also be written as

```text
  (det u) ¬∑ I_E = u ¬∑ Q(u)                                                  (6.4.1.4)
```

(`I_E` the identity automorphism of `E`), with

```text
  Q(u) = (‚ąí1)^{n+1} (u^{n‚ąí1} ‚ąí ŌÉ‚āĀ(u) u^{n‚ąí2} + ‚Ä¶ + (‚ąí1)^{n‚ąí1} ŌÉ_{n‚ąí1}(u)).  (6.4.1.5)
```

Let `ŌÜ : A ‚Üí B` be a ring homomorphism, making `B` an `A`-algebra; consider
the `B`-module `E_{(B)} = E ‚äó_A B`, which is free of rank `n`, and the
extension `u ‚äó 1` of `u` to an endomorphism of `E_{(B)}`; it is immediate
that one has `ŌÉ_i(u ‚äó 1) = ŌÜ(ŌÉ_i(u))` for every index `i`.

**(6.4.2)**

<!-- label: II.6.4.2 -->

Now suppose that `A` is an integral ring, with field of fractions `K`, and
let `E` be an `A`-module of finite type (but not necessarily free). Let `n`
be the _rank_ of `E`, i.e. the rank of the free `K`-module `E ‚äó_A K`; to
every endomorphism `u` of `E` canonically corresponds the endomorphism
`u ‚äó 1` of `E ‚äó_A K`; by abuse of language, we still call _characteristic
polynomial_ of `u`, and denote by `P(u, T)`, the polynomial `P(u ‚äó 1, T)`,
whose coefficients `ŌÉ_i(u ‚äó 1)` (which belong to `K`) we also denote by
`ŌÉ_i(u)` and call the _elementary symmetric functions_ of `u`; in particular
`det u = det(u ‚äó 1)` by definition. With this notation, formulas (6.4.1.3)
to (6.4.1.5) make sense and remain valid, provided one interprets `u^j` as
the homomorphism `E ‚Üí E ‚äó_A K` obtained by composing the endomorphism
`u^j ‚äó 1 = (u ‚äó 1)^j` of `E ‚äó_A K` with the canonical homomorphism
`x ‚Ü¶ x ‚äó 1`.

If `F` is the torsion submodule of `E` and if `E‚āÄ = E/F`, then `u(F) ‚äā F`,
hence, by passage to quotients, `u` gives an endomorphism `u‚āÄ` of `E‚āÄ`;
moreover, `E ‚äó_A K` is identified with `E‚āÄ ‚äó_A K` and `u ‚äó 1` with `u‚āÄ ‚äó 1`,
hence `ŌÉ_i(u) = ŌÉ_i(u‚āÄ)` for `1 ‚Č§ i ‚Č§ n`.

When `E` is torsion-free, `E` is canonically identified with a sub-`A`-module
of `E ‚äó_A K`, and the relation `u ‚äó 1 = 0` is equivalent to `u = 0`. When `E`
is a free `A`-module, the two definitions of `ŌÉ_i(u)` given in (6.4.1) and
(6.4.2) coincide by the above, which justifies the notation adopted.

Note also that when `E` is a torsion module ‚ÄĒ in other words, `E‚āÄ = {0}` ‚ÄĒ
the exterior algebra of `E‚āÄ` reduces to `K` and the determinant of the unique
endomorphism `u‚āÄ` of `E‚āÄ` is _equal to_ `1`.

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.6.4.3 -->

Let `A` be an integral ring, `E` an `A`-module of finite type, `u` an
endomorphism of `E`; then the elementary symmetric functions `ŌÉ_i(u)` of `u`
(and in particular `det u`) are elements of `K` integral over `A`.

**Proof.** Let `A'` be the integral closure of `A`; since `A'[T]` is an
integrally closed ring ([24], p. 99), it is the integral closure of `A[T]`
in its field of fractions `K(T)`. Replacing `u` by `T ¬∑ I ‚ąí u ‚äó 1` and `A` by
`A[T]`, we see that we are reduced to proving that `det u` is integral over
`A`. If `n` is the rank of `E`, we have `det u = det(‚čÄ^n u)` and
`(‚čÄ^n u) ‚äó 1 = ‚čÄ^n(u ‚äó 1)`, so we may assume `n = 1`. But then the map
`u ‚Ü¶ det u` is a homomorphism from the `A`-module `Hom_A(E, E)` to `K`; since
`E` is of finite type, `Hom_A(E, E)` is isomorphic to a sub-`A`-module of
the `A`-module of finite type `E^n` (if `E` admits a system of `n`
generators), so the elements `det u` belong to a sub-`A`-module of `K` of
finite type, and are consequently integral over `A`.

<!-- original page 122 -->

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.6.4.4 -->

Under the hypotheses of (6.4.3), if we further suppose `A` normal, then the
`ŌÉ_i(u)` (in particular `Tr u` and `det u`) belong to `A`.

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.6.4.5 -->

Let `A` be an integral ring, `E` an `A`-module of finite type of rank `n`,
`u` an endomorphism of `E` such that the `ŌÉ_i(u)` belong to `A`. For `u` to
be an automorphism of `E`, it is necessary that `det u` be invertible in `A`;
this condition is sufficient when `E` is torsion-free.

**Proof.** The condition is sufficient, since the hypothesis and the formulas
(6.4.1.4) and (6.4.1.5) (valid in `E`, and not only in `E ‚äó_A K`, since `E`
is torsion-free) prove that `(det u)‚ĀĽ¬Ļ Q(u)` is the inverse of `u`.

The condition is necessary, since if `u` is invertible, it follows from
(6.4.3) that `det(u‚ĀĽ¬Ļ)` belongs to the integral closure `A'` of `A` in its
field of fractions `K`, and is evidently the inverse of `det u` _in_ `A'`.
Our assertion then follows from:

**Lemma.**

<!-- label: II.6.4.5.1 -->

Let `A` be a subring of a ring `A'` such that `A'` is integral over `A`. If
an element `x ‚ąą A` is invertible in `A'`, it is also invertible in `A`.

**Proof.** In the contrary case, `x` would belong to a maximal ideal `ūĚĒ™` of
`A`, and it follows from the first theorem of Cohen‚ÄďSeidenberg
([13], t. I, p. 257, th. 3) that there would be a maximal ideal `ūĚĒ™'` of `A'`
such that `ūĚĒ™ = ūĚĒ™' ‚ą© A`; we would then have `x ‚ąą ūĚĒ™'`, which is absurd.

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.6.4.6 -->

Let `A` be an integral and integrally closed ring, `E` a torsion-free
`A`-module of finite type, `u` an endomorphism of `E`. For `u` to be an
automorphism of `E`, it is necessary and sufficient that `det u` be
invertible in `A`.

**Proof.** This follows from (6.4.4) and (6.4.5).

**Remark.**

<!-- label: II.6.4.7 -->

We will need below a generalisation of the preceding results. Consider a
reduced Noetherian ring `A`; let `ūĚĒ≠_őĪ` (`1 ‚Č§ őĪ ‚Č§ r`) be its minimal ideals,

`F_i(T)` (`1 ‚Č§ i ‚Č§ m`) form a system of generators of this `A[T]`-module,
then the coefficients of the `F_i(T)` are integral over `A`, hence generate

replace `A` by `A[T]` (which is Noetherian) and `E` by `E ‚äó_A A[T] = E'`,

    `ūĚíú`-module such that `‚Ą¨ ‚äó_ūĚíú ‚Ąõ(X)` is a locally free `‚Ąõ(X)`-module, and,
    for every section_ `f ‚ąą őď(U, ‚Ą¨)` _over an open `U ‚äā X`, `N_{‚Ą¨/ūĚíú}(f)` is
    a section of `ūĚíú` over `U`._

<!-- original page 126 -->

Note that this last condition is automatically satisfied when the locally
Noetherian prescheme `X` is normal (6.4.9).

On the other hand, the hypothesis that `‚Ą¨ ‚äó_ūĚíú ‚Ąõ(X)` is locally free can also
be expressed as follows: denote by `X_őĪ` the closed reduced subpreschemes of
`X` having as their underlying spaces the irreducible components of `X`
`(I, 5.2.1)`, which are therefore locally Noetherian integral preschemes.
Every `x ‚ąą X` belongs to a finite number of the subspaces `X_őĪ`; on the other
hand, `‚Ą¨ ‚äó_ūĚíú ‚Ąõ(X_őĪ)` is a locally free `‚Ąõ(X_őĪ)`-module of constant rank
`k_őĪ` `(I, 7.3.6)`; to say that `‚Ą¨ ‚äó_ūĚíú ‚Ąõ(X)` is a locally free
`‚Ąõ(X)`-module means that, for every `x ‚ąą X`, the ranks `k_őĪ` corresponding
to the indices such that `x ‚ąą X_őĪ` are equal. The question is in fact local,
and one reduces to the case `X = Spec(C)`, where `C` is a reduced Noetherian
ring, and `‚Ą¨ = DŐÉ`, where `D` is a `C`-algebra that is a `C`-module of finite
type; if `ūĚĒ≠_i` (`1 ‚Č§ i ‚Č§ m`) are the minimal prime ideals of `C`, the total
ring of fractions `L` of `C` is the direct sum of the fields of fractions
`K_i` of the integral rings `C/ūĚĒ≠_i`, and `D ‚äó_C L` is the direct sum of the
`D ‚äó_C K_i`, whence the conclusion.

It is clear that under hypotheses (I) or (II), one thus defines a
_homomorphism of sheaves of multiplicative monoids_ `N_{‚Ą¨/ūĚíú} : ‚Ą¨ ‚Üí ūĚíú`, also
denoted by `N` if no confusion may arise, and called the _norm_ homomorphism.

```text

```

for the corresponding sections of `ūĚíú` over `U`;

```text
  N_{‚Ą¨/ūĚíú}(1_‚Ą¨) = 1_ūĚíú;                                                      (6.5.1.2)
```

finally, for every section `s` of `ūĚíú` over `U`,


if the rank of `‚Ą¨` is constant and equal to `n` (for `s = 0_ūĚíú`, this formula
gives `N(0_‚Ą¨) = 0_ūĚíú` if `n ‚Č• 1`, and `N(0_‚Ą¨) = N(1_‚Ą¨) = 1_ūĚíú` if `n = 0`).

Under hypothesis (I), for `f ‚ąą őď(U, ‚Ą¨)` to be invertible it is necessary and

hypothesis is satisfied:

- (II bis) `(X, ūĚíú)` _is a locally Noetherian reduced prescheme, `‚Ą¨` is a
    coherent `ūĚíú`-module such that `‚Ą¨ ‚äó_ūĚíú ‚Ąõ(X)` is a locally free
    `‚Ąõ(X)`-module, and, for every section `f ‚ąą őď(U, ‚Ą¨)` over an open `U`
    such that `‚Ą¨ ‚äó_ūĚíú ‚Ąõ(X)|U` is of constant rank `n` over `‚Ąõ(X)|U`, the
    `ŌÉ_j(f)` (`1 ‚Č§ j ‚Č§ n`) are sections of `ūĚíú` over `U`._

(Again note that this condition is satisfied when `X` is normal.)

`X''`, the ranks defined like the `k_i` for `X'` are all equal to `k`;
taking `(I, 7.3.3)` into account, one immediately concludes that for every
affine open `T` of `X`, `(g'_*(ūĚí™_{X''}) ‚äó_{ūĚí™_X} ‚Ąõ(X))|T` is an
`(‚Ąõ(X)|T)`-module isomorphic to `(‚Ąõ(X)|T)^k`.

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.6.6.12 -->

If, in the statement of (6.6.11), we have `W = X`, then for an invertible
`ūĚí™_X`-module `‚Ąí` to be ample relative to `f`, it is necessary and sufficient
that `g*(‚Ąí)` be ample relative to `f ‚ąė g`.

**Remark.**

<!-- label: II.6.6.13 -->

In Chapter III, we will see that, if `Y` is Noetherian, `f` of finite type,
and if the restriction of `f` to the closed reduced subprescheme of `X`
having `X ‚ąí W` as its underlying space is proper, then the conclusion of
(6.6.12) is still valid. But we shall give in Chapter V examples of algebraic
schemes `X` over a field `K` (the structure morphism `X ‚Üí Spec(K)` not being
proper) whose normalisation `X'` is quasi-affine, but which is not
quasi-affine (so that `ūĚí™_X` is not ample, even though `ūĚí™_{X'}` is (5.1.12)
and the morphism `X' ‚Üí X` is finite and surjective (6.3.10)). We shall see
in the next section that this circumstance cannot occur when we replace
"quasi-affine" by "affine".

## 6.7. Chevalley's theorem

We shall establish (with the help of Serre's criterion (5.2.1)) the
following theorem, which was proven by C. Chevalley by other methods, in the
case of algebraic schemes.

<!-- original page 136 -->

**Theorem.**

<!-- label: II.6.7.1 -->

Let `X` be an affine scheme, `Y` a Noetherian prescheme, and `f : X ‚Üí Y` a
finite surjective morphism. Then `Y` is an affine scheme.

**Proof.** It is clear that `f_red : X_red ‚Üí Y_red` is finite (6.1.5, (vi));
since `X_red` is an affine scheme, and saying that `Y` is affine is equivalent
to saying that `Y_red` is affine (since `Y` is Noetherian `(I, 6.1.7)`), we
see that we may suppose `Y` reduced. For every closed part `Y'` of `Y`, there
is then a single reduced subprescheme of `Y` having `Y'` as its underlying
space `(I, 5.1.2)`; its inverse image `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(Y')`, canonically isomorphic to
`X √ó_Y Y'` `(I, 4.4.1)`, is affine as a closed subprescheme of `X`, and the
restriction of `f` to `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(Y')`, which is identified with `f √ó_Y 1_{Y'}`, is
a finite surjective morphism (6.1.5, (iii)). By virtue of the principle of
Noetherian induction `(0, 2.2.2)`, we may therefore (taking `(I, 6.1.7)`
into account) reduce to proving the theorem under the hypothesis that for
every closed part `Y' ‚Č† Y`, every closed subprescheme of `Y` having `Y'` as
its underlying space is affine. We thus conclude that, _for every coherent
`ūĚí™_Y`-module `‚ĄĪ` whose (closed) support `Z` is distinct from `Y`, one has_
`H¬Ļ(Y, ‚ĄĪ) = 0`. Indeed, there exists a closed subprescheme `Y'` of `Y` having
`Z` as its underlying space and such that, if `j : Y' ‚Üí Y` is the canonical
injection, one has `‚ĄĪ = j_*(j*(‚ĄĪ))` `(I, 9.3.5)`; consequently (5.2.3)
`H¬Ļ(Y, ‚ĄĪ) = H¬Ļ(Y', j*(‚ĄĪ)) = 0` by `(I, 5.1.9.2)`.

Suppose first that `Y` is not irreducible, and let `Y'` be an irreducible
component of `Y` and `Y'' = Y ‚ąí Y'`; we again denote by `Y'` the closed
reduced subprescheme of `Y` having `Y'` as its underlying space, and by `j`
the canonical injection `Y' ‚Üí Y`. Let `‚ĄĪ` be a coherent `ūĚí™_Y`-module, and
consider the canonical homomorphism

```text
  ŌĀ : ‚ĄĪ ‚Üí ‚ĄĪ' = j_*(j*(‚ĄĪ))
```

`(0, 4.4.3)`; `‚ĄĪ'` is a coherent `ūĚí™_Y`-module by virtue of `(0, 5.3.10)` and
`(0, 5.3.12)`, since `j_*(ūĚí™_{Y'}) = ūĚí™_Y/ūĚí•`, denoting by `ūĚí•` the sheaf of
ideals of `ūĚí™_Y` defining the subprescheme `Y'`. Consequently `ūĚíĘ = Ker ŌĀ` and
`ūĚí¶ = Im ŌĀ` are also coherent `ūĚí™_Y`-modules `(0, 5.3.4)`; now by definition
the fibre `‚ĄĪ'_y` of `‚ĄĪ'` at the generic point `y` of `Y'` is equal to `‚ĄĪ_y`,
since `y` is interior to `Y'` and therefore `ūĚí•_y = 0`, since `Y` is reduced.
We conclude that `y` is not contained in the (closed) support of `ūĚíĘ`;
moreover, the support of `‚ĄĪ'` (and _a fortiori_ that of `ūĚí¶`) is contained
in `Y'`; in other words, the supports of `ūĚíĘ` and `ūĚí¶` are distinct from `Y`.
We deduce that `H¬Ļ(Y, ūĚíĘ) = H¬Ļ(Y, ūĚí¶) = 0`, and the exact sequence of
cohomology applied to the exact sequence `0 ‚Üí ūĚíĘ ‚Üí ‚ĄĪ ‚Üí ūĚí¶ ‚Üí 0` shows that
`H¬Ļ(Y, ‚ĄĪ) = 0`. We conclude by Serre's criterion (5.2.1).

So suppose `Y` irreducible, and hence integral. We may also suppose `X`
integral: indeed, if we denote by `X_i` the closed reduced subpreschemes of
`X` having as their underlying spaces the irreducible components of `X`
`(I, 5.2.1)` and by `g_i` the restriction of `g` to `X_i`, then at least one
of the `g_i` is dominant, and since it is a finite morphism (6.1.5), it is
surjective (6.1.10); since `X_i` is also an affine scheme, we see that we
may replace `X` by `X_i` in the statement.

**Lemma.**

<!-- label: II.6.7.1.1 -->

Let `X`, `Y` be two integral Noetherian preschemes, `x` (resp. `y`) the
generic point of `X` (resp. `Y`), `f : X ‚Üí Y` a finite surjective morphism.
Let `‚Ąí` be an invertible `ūĚí™_X`-module such that there exists an affine open
neighbourhood `U` of `y` and a section `g ‚ąą őď(X, ‚Ąí)` for which

<!-- original page 137 -->

`x ‚ąą X_g ‚äā f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(U)`. Then there exist two integers `m > 0`, `n > 0`, a
homomorphism `u : ūĚí™_Y^m ‚Üí f_*(‚Ąí^{‚äón})`, and an open neighbourhood `V` of `y`
such that the restriction `u|V` is an isomorphism of `ūĚí™_Y^m|V` onto
`f_*(‚Ąí^{‚äón})|V`.

**Proof.** Let `C` be the (integral) ring of `U`, `k = ūĚí™_y` its field of
fractions: since `f` is finite, `U' = f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(U)` is affine (1.3.2); let `D` be
its (integral) ring of fractions `K = ūĚí™_x`; by hypothesis `D` is a `C`-module
of finite type (6.1.4), so `K` is an extension of finite rank of `k`. The
fibre `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(y) = X √ó_Y Spec(őļ(y)) = X √ó_Y Spec(ūĚí™_y)` is identified with
`Spec(K)` `(I, 3.6.5)`; let `s_i` (`1 ‚Č§ i ‚Č§ m`) be elements of `D` forming a
basis of `K` over `k`. There exists `n > 0` such that the sections
`(s_i|X_g) g^{‚äón}` of `‚Ąí^{‚äón}` over `X_g` extend to sections `b_i`
(`1 ‚Č§ i ‚Č§ m`) of `‚Ąí^{‚äón}` over `X` `(I, 9.3.1)`. The `b_i` are also, by
definition, sections of `f_*(‚Ąí^{‚äón})` over `Y` and therefore define a
homomorphism `u : ūĚí™_Y^m ‚Üí f_*(‚Ąí^{‚äón})` `(0, 5.1.1)`; we shall see that `u`
answers the question. One has `‚Ąí^{‚äón}|U' = MŐÉ`, where `M` is a `D`-module of
finite type, so if `ŌÜ` is the injection `C ‚Üí D` corresponding to the
morphism `f‚ĀĽ¬Ļ(U) ‚Üí U` given by the restriction of `f`, then `M_{[ŌÜ]}` is a
`C`-module of finite type; consequently

```text
  f_*(‚Ąí^{‚äón})|U = (M_{[ŌÜ]})ŐÉ
```

`(I, 1.6.3)` is coherent, and since `U` is an arbitrary affine open of `Y`,
`f_*(‚Ąí^{‚äón})` is coherent; moreover, `u|U = őłŐÉ`, where `őł` is a
`C`-homomorphism `C^m ‚Üí M_{[ŌÜ]}`, and `u_y = őł_y` is the homomorphism
`őł ‚äó 1 : k^m = C^m ‚äó K ‚Üí M_{[ŌÜ]} ‚äó K`; but the latter is by definition an
_isomorphism_, since the `(b_i)_x` form a basis of `M_{[ŌÜ]} ‚äó K` over `k`,
`g_x` being by hypothesis a generator of `(‚Ąí^{‚äón})_x`. We conclude that the
supports of the `ūĚí™_Y`-modules `Ker u` and `Coker u` do not contain `y`;
since these `ūĚí™_Y`-modules are coherent `(0, 5.3.3)`, their supports are
closed `(0, 5.2.2)`, whence the lemma.

This being so, the hypotheses of the lemma (6.7.1.1) are fulfilled in the
case we are considering, by taking `‚Ąí = ūĚí™_X`, since `X` is affine
`(I, 1.1.10)`; we shall set `ūĚíú = ūĚí™_Y` and `‚Ą¨ = f_*(ūĚí™_X)`. By virtue of
Serre's criterion (5.2.1.1), it suffices to prove that for every coherent
`ūĚí™_Y`-module `‚ĄĪ`, one has `H¬Ļ(Y, ‚ĄĪ) = 0`; it even suffices to prove this
when `‚ĄĪ ‚äā ūĚí™_Y`, which entails that `‚ĄĪ` is torsion-free since `Y` is
integral; in fact we shall show that `H¬Ļ(Y, ‚ĄĪ) = 0` for every coherent
torsion-free `ūĚí™_Y`-module `‚ĄĪ`. Now the homomorphism `u : ūĚíú^m ‚Üí ‚Ą¨` defines
a homomorphism

```text
  v : ūĚíĘ = ūĚďóūĚďłūĚď∂_ūĚíú(‚Ą¨, ‚ĄĪ) ‚Üí ūĚďóūĚďłūĚď∂_ūĚíú(ūĚíú^m, ‚ĄĪ) = ‚ĄĪ^m.
```

We first show that `v` is injective: by hypothesis `ūĚíĮ = Coker u` has a
support not meeting `V`, hence is a torsion `ūĚí™_Y`-module `(I, 7.4.6)`; the
exact sequence

```text
  ūĚíú^m ‚Üí ‚Ą¨ ‚Üí ūĚíĮ ‚Üí 0
```

gives, by left exactness of the functor `ūĚďóūĚďłūĚď∂_ūĚíú`, the exact sequence

```text
  0 ‚Üí ūĚďóūĚďłūĚď∂_ūĚíú(ūĚíĮ, ‚ĄĪ) ‚Üí ūĚíĘ ‚Üí^v ‚ĄĪ^m.
```

But since `‚ĄĪ` is torsion-free, one has `ūĚďóūĚďłūĚď∂_ūĚíú(ūĚíĮ, ‚ĄĪ) = 0` `(0, 5.2.6)`,
whence our assertion. We therefore have the exact sequence

```text
  0 ‚Üí ūĚíĘ ‚Üí ‚ĄĪ^m ‚Üí Coker v ‚Üí 0
```

<!-- original page 138 -->

where `ūĚíĘ` and `Coker v` are coherent `ūĚí™_Y`-modules (`(0, 5.3.4)` and
`(0, 5.3.5)`); by virtue of the exact sequence of cohomology, it will
suffice to show that `H¬Ļ(Y, ūĚíĘ) = H¬Ļ(Y, Coker v) = 0` in order to deduce
`H¬Ļ(Y, ‚ĄĪ^m) = (H¬Ļ(Y, ‚ĄĪ))^m = 0`, and therefore `H¬Ļ(Y, ‚ĄĪ) = 0`. Now the
restriction `v|V` is an isomorphism, hence the support of `Coker v` is
distinct from `Y`, whence `H¬Ļ(Y, Coker v) = 0` by the hypothesis at the
start. On the other hand, `ūĚíĘ` is a coherent `‚Ą¨`-module `(I, 9.6.4)`; since
`X` is affine over `Y`, there exists a quasi-coherent `ūĚí™_X`-module `ūĚí¶` such
that `ūĚíĘ` is isomorphic to `f_*(ūĚí¶)` (1.4.3); since `X` is affine, one has
`H¬Ļ(X, ūĚí¶) = 0` `(I, 5.1.9.2)`, hence also `H¬Ļ(Y, ūĚíĘ) = 0` by (5.2.3), which
completes the proof of theorem (6.7.1).

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.6.7.2 -->

Let `X` be a Noetherian prescheme, `(X_i)_{1 ‚Č§ i ‚Č§ n}` a finite cover of the
space `X` consisting of closed sets. For `X` to be affine, it is necessary
and sufficient that, for each `i`, there exist a closed subprescheme of `X`
having `X_i` as its underlying space and which is affine.

**Proof.** Indeed, if this is so, let `X'` be the scheme sum of the `X_i`;
it is clear that `X'` is affine, and one defines a surjective morphism
`f : X' ‚Üí X` by taking as the restriction of `f` to `X_i` the canonical
injection. Everything comes down to verifying that `f` is finite, by virtue
of (6.7.1), and this has been seen in (6.1.5).

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.6.7.3 -->

For a Noetherian prescheme `X` to be affine, it is necessary and sufficient
that the closed reduced subpreschemes having as their underlying spaces the
irreducible components of `X` be affine.
