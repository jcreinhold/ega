# §5. Quasi-affine morphisms; quasi-projective morphisms; proper morphisms; projective morphisms

## 5.1. Quasi-affine morphisms

**Definition.**

<!-- label: II.5.1.1 -->

A _quasi-affine scheme_ is a scheme isomorphic to the subscheme induced on a
quasi-compact open subset of an affine scheme. We say that a morphism
`f : X → Y` is _quasi-affine_, or also that `X` (considered as a `Y`-prescheme
via `f`) is a _quasi-affine `Y`-scheme_, if there exists a covering `(U_α)` of
`Y` by affine opens such that the `f⁻¹(U_α)` are quasi-affine schemes.

It is clear that a quasi-affine morphism is _separated_ (`I, 5.5.5` and
`I, 5.5.8`) and _quasi-compact_ `(I, 6.6.1)`; every affine morphism is
obviously quasi-affine.

Recall that, for any prescheme `X`, setting `A = Γ(X, 𝒪_X)`, the identity
homomorphism `A → A = Γ(X, 𝒪_X)` defines a morphism `X → Spec(A)`, called
_canonical_ `(I, 2.2.4)`; this is moreover none other than the canonical
morphism defined in (4.5.1) in the special case `ℒ = 𝒪_X`, once we recall that
`Proj(A[T])` is canonically identified with `Spec(A)` (3.1.7).

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.5.1.2 -->

Let `X` be a quasi-compact scheme or a prescheme whose underlying space is
Noetherian, and `A` the ring `Γ(X, 𝒪_X)`. The following conditions are
equivalent:

1. (a) `X` is a quasi-affine scheme.
1. (b) The canonical morphism `u : X → Spec(A)` is an open immersion.
1. (b') The canonical morphism `u : X → Spec(A)` is a homeomorphism from `X`
    onto a subspace of the underlying space of `Spec(A)`.
1. (c) The `𝒪_X`-module `𝒪_X` is very ample relative to `u` (4.4.2).
1. (c') The `𝒪_X`-module `𝒪_X` is ample (4.5.1).
1. (d) As `f` ranges over `A`, the `X_f` form a basis of the topology of `X`.

<!-- original page 95 -->

1. (d') As `f` ranges over `A`, those of the `X_f` that are affine form a
    covering of `X`.
1. (e) Every quasi-coherent `𝒪_X`-module is generated by its sections over `X`.
1. (e') Every quasi-coherent sheaf of ideals of finite type of `𝒪_X` is
    generated by its sections over `X`.

**Proof.** It is clear that (b) implies (a), and (a) implies (c) by virtue of
criterion (b) of (4.4.4) applied to the identity morphism (taking into account
the remark preceding the statement); on the other hand, (c) implies (c')
(4.5.10, (i)), and (c'), (b), and (b') are equivalent by (4.5.2, criteria (b)
and (b')). Finally, (c') is equivalent to each of the criteria (d), (d'),
(e), (e') by virtue of (4.5.2, criteria (a), (a'), (c)) and (4.5.5, criterion
(d'')).

We also observe that, with the preceding notation, those of the `X_f` that are
affine form a _basis_ of the topology of `X`, and that the canonical morphism
`u` is _dominant_ (4.5.2).

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.5.1.3 -->

Let `X` be a quasi-compact prescheme. If there exists a morphism `v : X → Y`
from `X` into an affine scheme `Y` that is a homeomorphism of `X` onto an open
subspace of `Y`, then `X` is quasi-affine.

**Proof.** Indeed, there exists a family `(g_α)` of sections of `𝒪_Y` over
`Y` such that the `D(g_α)` form a basis of the topology of `v(X)`; if
`v = (ψ, θ)` and we set `f_α = θ(g_α)`, then `X_{f_α} = ψ⁻¹(D(g_α))`
`(I, 2.2.4.1)`, so the `X_{f_α}` form a basis of the topology of `X`, and
criterion (d) of (5.1.2) is satisfied.

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.5.1.4 -->

If `X` is a quasi-affine scheme, then _every_ invertible `𝒪_X`-module is very
ample (relative to the canonical morphism), and _a fortiori_ ample.

**Proof.** Indeed, such a module `ℒ` is generated by its sections over `X`
(5.1.2, (e)), so `ℒ ⊗ 𝒪_X = ℒ` is very ample (4.4.8).

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.5.1.5 -->

Let `X` be a quasi-compact prescheme. If there exists an invertible
`𝒪_X`-module `ℒ` such that `ℒ` and `ℒ⁻¹` are ample, then `X` is a quasi-affine
scheme.

**Proof.** Indeed, `𝒪_X = ℒ ⊗ ℒ⁻¹` is then ample (4.5.7).

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.5.1.6 -->

Let `f : X → Y` be a quasi-compact morphism. The following conditions are
equivalent:

1. (a) `f` is quasi-affine.
1. (b) The `𝒪_Y`-algebra `f_*(𝒪_X) = 𝒜(X)` is quasi-coherent, and the
    canonical morphism `X → Spec(𝒜(X))` corresponding to the identity
    homomorphism `𝒜(X) → 𝒜(X)` (1.2.7) is an open immersion.
1. (b') The `𝒪_Y`-algebra `𝒜(X)` is quasi-coherent, and the canonical morphism
    `X → Spec(𝒜(X))` is a homeomorphism from `X` onto a subspace of
    `Spec(𝒜(X))`.
1. (c) The `𝒪_X`-module `𝒪_X` is very ample for `f`.
1. (c') The `𝒪_X`-module `𝒪_X` is ample for `f`.
1. (d) The morphism `f` is separated, and for every quasi-coherent
    `𝒪_X`-module `ℱ`, the canonical homomorphism
    `σ : f*(f_*(ℱ)) → ℱ` `(0, 4.4.3)` is surjective.

Furthermore, when `f` is quasi-affine, every invertible `𝒪_X`-module `ℒ` is
very ample relative to `f`.

**Proof.** The equivalence of (a) and (c') follows from the local character on
`Y` of `f`-ampleness (4.6.4), Definition (5.1.1), and the criterion (5.1.2,
(c')). The other properties are local on `Y`

<!-- original page 96 -->

and so follow immediately from (5.1.2) and (5.1.4), taking into account the
fact that `f_*(ℱ)` is quasi-coherent when `f` is separated `(I, 9.2.2, a)`.

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.5.1.7 -->

Let `f : X → Y` be a quasi-affine morphism. For every open `U` of `Y`, the
restriction `f⁻¹(U) → U` of `f` is quasi-affine.

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.5.1.8 -->

Let `Y` be an affine scheme and `f : X → Y` a quasi-compact morphism. For
`f` to be quasi-affine, it is necessary and sufficient that `X` be a
quasi-affine scheme.

**Proof.** This is an immediate consequence of (5.1.6) and (4.6.6).

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.5.1.9 -->

Let `Y` be a quasi-compact scheme or a prescheme whose underlying space is
Noetherian, and `f : X → Y` a morphism _of finite type_. If `f` is
quasi-affine, then there exists a quasi-coherent `𝒪_Y`-subalgebra `ℬ` of
`𝒜(X) = f_*(𝒪_X)`, of finite type `(I, 9.6.2)`, such that the morphism
`X → Spec(ℬ)` corresponding to the canonical injection `ℬ → 𝒜(X)` (1.2.7)
is an immersion. Furthermore, every quasi-coherent `𝒪_Y`-subalgebra `ℬ'` of
`𝒜(X)` of finite type that contains `ℬ` has the same property.

**Proof.** Indeed, `𝒜(X)` is the inductive limit of its quasi-coherent
`𝒪_Y`-subalgebras of finite type `(I, 9.6.5)`; the result is then a particular
case of (3.8.4), taking into account the identification of `Spec(𝒜(X))` with
`Proj(𝒜(X)[T])` (3.1.7).

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.5.1.10 -->

1. (i) A quasi-compact morphism `X → Y` that is a homeomorphism from the
    underlying space of `X` onto a subspace of the underlying space of `Y`
    (in particular, a closed immersion) is quasi-affine.
1. (ii) The composition of two quasi-affine morphisms is quasi-affine.
1. (iii) If `f : X → Y` is a quasi-affine `S`-morphism, then
    `f_{(S')} : X_{(S')} → Y_{(S')}` is a quasi-affine morphism for every base
    change `S' → S`.
1. (iv) If `f : X → Y` and `g : X' → Y'` are two quasi-affine `S`-morphisms,
    then `f ×_S g` is quasi-affine.
1. (v) If `f : X → Y` and `g : Y → Z` are two morphisms such that `g ∘ f` is
    quasi-affine, and if `g` is separated or the underlying space of `X` is
    locally Noetherian, then `f` is quasi-affine.
1. (vi) If `f` is a quasi-affine morphism, then so is `f_red`.

**Proof.** Taking into account criterion (5.1.6, (c')), (i), (iii), (iv), (v)
and (vi) follow immediately from (4.6.13, (i bis), (iii), (iv), (v) and (vi))
respectively. To prove (ii), we may restrict to the case where `Z` is affine,
and then the assertion follows directly from (4.6.13, (ii)) applied to
`ℒ = 𝒪_X` and `𝒦 = 𝒪_Y`.

**Remark.**

<!-- label: II.5.1.11 -->

Let `f : X → Y`, `g : Y → Z` be two morphisms such that `X ×_Z Y` is locally
Noetherian. Then the graph immersion `Γ_f : X → X ×_Z Y` is quasi-affine,
being quasi-compact `(I, 6.3.5)`, and `(I, 5.5.12)` shows that in (v) the
conclusion still holds if we drop the hypothesis that `g` is separated.

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.5.1.12 -->

Let `f : X → Y` be a quasi-compact morphism, and `g : X' → X` a quasi-affine
morphism. If `ℒ` is an `𝒪_X`-module ample for `f`, then `g*(ℒ)` is an
`𝒪_{X'}`-module ample for `f ∘ g`.

**Proof.** Indeed, since `𝒪_{X'}` is very ample for `g`, and the question is
local on `Y` (4.6.4), it follows from (4.6.13, (ii)) that there exists (for
`Y` affine) an integer `n` such that

```text
  g*(ℒ⊗ⁿ) = (g*(ℒ))⊗ⁿ
```

is ample for `f ∘ g`, so `g*(ℒ)` is ample for `f ∘ g` (4.6.9).

## 5.2. Serre's criterion

**Theorem (Serre's criterion).**

<!-- label: II.5.2.1 -->

Let `X` be a quasi-compact scheme or a prescheme whose underlying space is
Noetherian. The following conditions are equivalent:

1. (a) `X` is an affine scheme.
1. (b) There exists a family of elements `f_α ∈ A = Γ(X, 𝒪_X)` such that the
    `X_{f_α}` are affine and the ideal generated by the `f_α` in `A` is equal
    to `A`.
1. (c) The functor `Γ(X, ℱ)` is exact in `ℱ` on the category of quasi-coherent
    `𝒪_X`-modules; in other words, if

    ```text
      0 → ℱ' → ℱ → ℱ'' → 0                                                  (*)
    ```

    is an exact sequence of quasi-coherent `𝒪_X`-modules, then the sequence

    ```text
      0 → Γ(X, ℱ') → Γ(X, ℱ) → Γ(X, ℱ'') → 0
    ```

    is also exact.

1. (c') Property (c) holds for every exact sequence (\*) of quasi-coherent
    `𝒪_X`-modules such that `ℱ` is isomorphic to an `𝒪_X`-submodule of a
    finite power `𝒪_X^n`.
1. (d) `H¹(X, ℱ) = 0` for every quasi-coherent `𝒪_X`-module `ℱ`.
1. (d') `H¹(X, 𝒥) = 0` for every quasi-coherent sheaf of ideals `𝒥` of
    `𝒪_X`.

**Proof.** It is evident that (a) implies (b); furthermore (b) implies that
the `X_{f_α}` cover `X`, since by hypothesis the section `1` is a linear
combination of the `f_α` and the `D(f_α)` then cover `Spec(A)`. The last
assertion of (4.5.2) then implies that `X → Spec(A)` is an isomorphism.

We know that (a) implies (c) `(I, 1.3.11)`, and (c) trivially implies (c').
Let us prove that (c') implies (b). First, (c') implies that, for every
_closed_ point `x ∈ X` and every open neighbourhood `U` of `x`, there exists
`f ∈ A` such that `x ∈ X_f ⊂ X − U`. Indeed, let `𝒥` (resp. `𝒥'`) be the
quasi-coherent sheaf of ideals of `𝒪_X` defining the closed reduced
subprescheme of `X` whose underlying space is `X − U` (resp.
`(X − U) ∪ {x}`) `(I, 5.2.1)`; it is clear that `𝒥' ⊂ 𝒥` and that
`𝒥'' = 𝒥/𝒥'` is a quasi-coherent `𝒪_X`-module with support `{x}` and such
that `𝒥''_x = κ(x)`. Hypothesis (c') applied to the exact sequence
`0 → 𝒥' → 𝒥 → 𝒥'' → 0` shows that `Γ(X, 𝒥) → Γ(X, 𝒥'')` is surjective. The
section of `𝒥''` whose germ at `x` is `1_x` is therefore the image of a
section `f ∈ Γ(X, 𝒥) ⊂ Γ(X, 𝒪_X)`, and by definition `f(x) = 1_x` and
`f(y) = 0` on `X − U`, which establishes our assertion. Moreover, if `U` is
affine, then so is `X_f` `(I, 1.3.6)`, so the union of those `X_f` that are
affine (`f ∈ A`) is an open set `Z` containing every closed point of `X`;
since `X` is a quasi-compact Kolmogorov space, we necessarily have `Z = X`
`(0, 2.1.3)`. Because `X` is quasi-compact, there are finitely many
elements `f_i ∈ A` (`1 ≤ i ≤ n`) such that the `X_{f_i}` are affine and cover
`X`. Consider then the homomorphism `𝒪_X^n → 𝒪_X` defined by the sections
`f_i` `(0, 5.1.1)`; since for every `x ∈ X` at least one of the `(f_i)_x` is
invertible, this homomorphism is surjective, and we therefore have an exact
sequence `0 → ℛ → 𝒪_X^n → 𝒪_X → 0`, where `ℛ` is a quasi-coherent
`𝒪_X`-submodule of `𝒪_X^n`. It then follows

<!-- original page 98 -->

from (c') that the corresponding homomorphism `Γ(X, 𝒪_X^n) → Γ(X, 𝒪_X)` is
surjective, which proves (b).

Finally, (a) implies (d) `(I, 5.1.9.2)`, and (d) trivially implies (d'). It
remains to show that (d') implies (c'). If `ℱ` is a quasi-coherent
`𝒪_X`-submodule of `𝒪_X^n`, the filtration
`0 ⊂ 𝒪_X ⊂ 𝒪_X^2 ⊂ ⋯ ⊂ 𝒪_X^n` defines on `ℱ` a filtration formed by the
`ℱ_k = ℱ ∩ 𝒪_X^k` (`0 ≤ k ≤ n`), which are quasi-coherent `𝒪_X`-modules
`(I, 4.1.1)`, and `ℱ_{k+1}/ℱ_k` is isomorphic to a quasi-coherent
`𝒪_X`-submodule of `𝒪_X^{k+1}/𝒪_X^k = 𝒪_X`, that is, to a quasi-coherent
sheaf of ideals of `𝒪_X`. Hypothesis (d') therefore implies
`H¹(X, ℱ_{k+1}/ℱ_k) = 0`; the exact cohomology sequence
`H¹(X, ℱ_k) → H¹(X, ℱ_{k+1}) → H¹(X, ℱ_{k+1}/ℱ_k) = 0` then allows us to
prove by induction on `k` that `H¹(X, ℱ_k) = 0` for every `k`. Q.E.D.

**Remark.**

<!-- label: II.5.2.1.1 -->

When `X` is a Noetherian prescheme, in the statements of (c') and (d') one
may replace "quasi-coherent" by "coherent". Indeed, in the proof that (c')
implies (b), `𝒥` and `𝒥'` are then coherent sheaves of ideals, and moreover
every quasi-coherent submodule of a coherent module is coherent `(I, 6.1.1)`;
whence the conclusion.

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.5.2.2 -->

Let `f : X → Y` be a separated quasi-compact morphism. The following
conditions are equivalent:

1. (a) `f` is an affine morphism.
1. (b) The functor `f_*` is exact on the category of quasi-coherent
    `𝒪_X`-modules.
1. (c) For every quasi-coherent `𝒪_X`-module `ℱ`, `R¹f_*(ℱ) = 0`.
1. (c') For every quasi-coherent sheaf of ideals `𝒥` of `𝒪_X`,
    `R¹f_*(𝒥) = 0`.

**Proof.** All these conditions being local on `Y`, by definition of the
functor `R¹f_*` `(T, 3.7.3)`, we may suppose that `Y` is affine. If `f` is
affine, then `X` is affine and property (b) is none other than `(I, 1.6.4)`.
Conversely, let us show that (b) implies (a): for every quasi-coherent
`𝒪_X`-module `ℱ`, `f_*(ℱ)` is a quasi-coherent `𝒪_Y`-module
`(I, 9.2.2, a)`. By hypothesis, the functor `f_*(ℱ)` is exact in `ℱ`, and the
functor `Γ(Y, 𝒢)` is exact in `𝒢` (on the category of quasi-coherent
`𝒪_Y`-modules) since `Y` is affine `(I, 1.3.11)`; hence
`Γ(Y, f_*(ℱ)) = Γ(X, ℱ)` is exact in `ℱ`, which proves our assertion by
virtue of (5.2.1, (c)).

If `f` is affine, `f⁻¹(U)` is affine for every affine open `U` of `Y`
(1.3.2), hence `H¹(f⁻¹(U), ℱ) = 0` (5.2.1, (d)), which by definition implies
`R¹f_*(ℱ) = 0`. Finally, suppose condition (c') is satisfied; the exact
sequence of low-degree terms in the Leray spectral sequence
`(G, II, 4.17.1` and `I, 4.5.1)` yields in particular the exact sequence

```text
  0 → H¹(Y, f_*(𝒥)) → H¹(X, 𝒥) → H⁰(Y, R¹f_*(𝒥)).
```

Since `Y` is affine and `f_*(𝒥)` is quasi-coherent `(I, 9.2.2, a)`,
`H¹(Y, f_*(𝒥)) = 0` (5.2.1); hypothesis (c') therefore implies
`H¹(X, 𝒥) = 0`, and we conclude by (5.2.1) that `X` is an affine scheme.

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.5.2.3 -->

If `f : X → Y` is an affine morphism, then for every quasi-coherent
`𝒪_X`-module `ℱ`, the canonical homomorphism
`H¹(Y, f_*(ℱ)) → H¹(X, ℱ)` is bijective.

<!-- original page 99 -->

**Proof.** Indeed, we have the exact sequence

```text
  0 → H¹(Y, f_*(ℱ)) → H¹(X, ℱ) → H⁰(Y, R¹f_*(ℱ))
```

of low-degree terms of the Leray spectral sequence, and the conclusion
follows from (5.2.2).

**Remark.**

<!-- label: II.5.2.4 -->

In Chapter III, §1, we will prove that if `X` is affine, then
`H^i(X, ℱ) = 0` for every `i > 0` and every quasi-coherent `𝒪_X`-module `ℱ`.

## 5.3. Quasi-projective morphisms

**Definition.**

<!-- label: II.5.3.1 -->

We say that a morphism `f : X → Y` is _quasi-projective_, or that `X`
(considered as a `Y`-prescheme via `f`) is _quasi-projective over `Y`_, or
that `X` is a _quasi-projective `Y`-scheme_, if `f` is of finite type and
there exists an invertible `𝒪_X`-module that is `f`-ample.

Note that this notion _is not local on `Y`_: the counterexamples of Nagata
[26] and Hironaka show that, even when `X` and `Y` are non-singular algebraic
schemes over an algebraically closed field, every point of `Y` may have an
affine neighbourhood `U` such that `f⁻¹(U)` is quasi-projective over `U`,
without `f` being quasi-projective.

Note that a quasi-projective morphism is necessarily _separated_ (4.6.1).
When `Y` is quasi-compact, it amounts to the same thing to say that `f` is
quasi-projective or that `f` is of finite type and there exists an
`𝒪_X`-module very ample relative to `f` (4.6.2 and 4.6.11). Furthermore:

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.5.3.2 -->

Let `Y` be a quasi-compact scheme or a prescheme whose underlying space is
Noetherian, and let `X` be a `Y`-prescheme. The following conditions are
equivalent:

1. (a) `X` is a quasi-projective `Y`-scheme.
1. (b) `X` is of finite type over `Y`, and there exists a quasi-coherent
    `𝒪_Y`-module `𝓔` of finite type such that `X` is `Y`-isomorphic to a
    subprescheme of `ℙ(𝓔)`.
1. (c) `X` is of finite type over `Y`, and there exists a quasi-coherent
    graded `𝒪_Y`-algebra `𝒮` such that `𝒮₁` is of finite type and generates
    `𝒮`, and such that `X` is `Y`-isomorphic to a subprescheme induced on an
    everywhere-dense open subset of `Proj(𝒮)`.

**Proof.** This follows immediately from the preceding remark and from
(4.4.3), (4.4.6), and (4.4.7).

Note that when `Y` is a Noetherian prescheme, in conditions (b) and (c) of
(5.3.2) we may drop the hypothesis that `X` is of finite type over `Y`, which
is then automatically satisfied `(I, 6.3.5)`.

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.5.3.3 -->

Let `Y` be a quasi-compact scheme such that there exists an ample
`𝒪_Y`-module `ℒ` (4.5.3). For a `Y`-scheme `X` to be quasi-projective, it is
necessary and sufficient that it be of finite type over `Y` and isomorphic to
a `Y`-subscheme of a projective bundle of the form `ℙ_Y^r`.

**Proof.** Indeed, if `𝓔` is a quasi-coherent `𝒪_Y`-module of finite type,
then `𝓔` is isomorphic to a quotient of an `𝒪_Y`-module
`ℒ^{⊗(−n)} ⊗_{𝒪_Y} 𝒪_Y^k` (4.5.5), hence `ℙ(𝓔)` is isomorphic to a closed
subscheme of `ℙ_Y^{k−1}` (4.1.2 and 4.1.4).

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.5.3.4 -->

1. (i) A quasi-affine morphism of finite type (and in particular a
    quasi-compact immersion, or an affine morphism of finite type) is
    quasi-projective.
1. (ii) If `f : X → Y` and `g : Y → Z` are quasi-projective and `Z` is
    quasi-compact, then `g ∘ f` is quasi-projective.

<!-- original page 100 -->

1. (iii) If `f : X → Y` is a quasi-projective `S`-morphism, then
    `f_{(S')} : X_{(S')} → Y_{(S')}` is quasi-projective for every base
    change `S' → S`.
1. (iv) If `f : X → Y` and `g : X' → Y'` are two quasi-projective
    `S`-morphisms, then `f ×_S g` is quasi-projective.
1. (v) If `f : X → Y`, `g : Y → Z` are two morphisms such that `g ∘ f` is
    quasi-projective, and if `g` is separated or `X` locally Noetherian, then
    `f` is quasi-projective.
1. (vi) If `f` is a quasi-projective morphism, then so is `f_red`.

**Proof.** (i) follows from (5.1.6) and (5.1.10, (i)). The other assertions
are immediate consequences of Definition (5.3.1), of the properties of
morphisms of finite type `(I, 6.3.4)`, and of (4.6.13).

**Remark.**

<!-- label: II.5.3.5 -->

Note that `f_red` can be quasi-projective without `f` being so, even when
`Y` is the spectrum of an algebra of finite rank over `ℂ` and `f` is proper.

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.5.3.6 -->

If `X` and `X'` are two quasi-projective `Y`-schemes, then `X ⊔ X'` is a
quasi-projective `Y`-scheme.

**Proof.** This follows from (4.6.18).

## 5.4. Proper morphisms and universally closed morphisms

**Definition.**

<!-- label: II.5.4.1 -->

We say that a morphism of preschemes `f : X → Y` is _proper_ if it satisfies
the following two conditions:

1. (a) `f` is separated and of finite type.
1. (b) For every prescheme `Y'` and every morphism `Y' → Y`, the projection
    `f_{(Y')} : X ×_Y Y' → Y'` is a closed morphism `(I, 2.2.6)`.

When this is so, we also say that `X` (considered as a `Y`-prescheme with
structure morphism `f`) is _proper over `Y`_.

It is immediate that conditions (a) and (b) are local on `Y`. To verify that
the image of a closed subset `Z` of `X ×_Y Y'` under the projection
`q : X ×_Y Y' → Y'` is closed in `Y'`, it suffices to see that
`q(Z) ∩ U'` is closed in `U'` for every affine open `U'` of `Y'`; since
`q(Z) ∩ U' = q(Z ∩ q⁻¹(U'))` and `q⁻¹(U')` is identified with `X ×_Y U'`
`(I, 4.4.1)`, we see that to verify condition (b) of Def. (5.4.1) we may
restrict to the case where `Y'` is an affine scheme. We will see later
(5.6.3) that if `Y` is locally Noetherian, we may even restrict to verifying
(b) when `Y'` is of finite type over `Y`.

It is clear that every proper morphism is closed.

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.5.4.2 -->

1. (i) A closed immersion is a proper morphism.
1. (ii) The composition of two proper morphisms is proper.
1. (iii) If `X`, `Y` are `S`-preschemes and `f : X → Y` a proper
    `S`-morphism, then

    ```text
      f_{(S')} : X_{(S')} → Y_{(S')}
    ```

    is proper for every base change `S' → S`.

1. (iv) If `f : X → Y` and `g : X' → Y'` are two proper `S`-morphisms, then
    the `S`-morphism `f ×_S g : X ×_S X' → Y ×_S Y'` is proper.

<!-- original page 101 -->

**Proof.** It suffices to prove (i), (ii), and (iii) `(I, 3.5.1)`. In each
of these three cases, the verification of condition (a) of (5.4.1) follows
from earlier results (`I, 5.5.1` and `I, 6.3.4`); it remains to verify
condition (b). It is immediate in case (i), because if `X → Y` is a closed
immersion, then so is `X ×_Y Y' → Y ×_Y Y' = Y'` (`I, 4.3.2` and `3.3.3`).
To prove (ii), consider two proper morphisms `X → Y`, `Y → Z`, and a
morphism `Z' → Z`. We may write `X ×_Z Z' = X ×_Y (Y ×_Z Z')` `(I, 3.3.9.1)`,
and so the projection `X ×_Z Z' → Z'` factors as
`X ×_Y (Y ×_Z Z') → Y ×_Z Z' → Z'`. Taking the initial remark into account,
(ii) thus follows from the fact that the composition of two closed morphisms
is closed. Finally, for every morphism `S' → S`, `X_{(S')}` is identified
with `X ×_Y Y_{(S')}` `(I, 3.3.11)`; for every morphism `Z → Y_{(S')}`, we
may write

```text
  X_{(S')} ×_{Y_{(S')}} Z = (X ×_Y Y_{(S')}) ×_{Y_{(S')}} Z = X ×_Y Z;
```

`X ×_Y Z → Z` is closed by hypothesis, which proves (iii).

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.5.4.3 -->

Let `f : X → Y`, `g : Y → Z` be two morphisms such that `g ∘ f` is proper.

1. (i) If `g` is separated, then `f` is proper.
1. (ii) If `g` is separated and of finite type and if `f` is surjective, then
    `g` is proper.

**Proof.** (i) follows from (5.4.2) by the general procedure
`(I, 5.5.12)`. To prove (ii), only condition (b) of Def. (5.4.1) needs to
be verified. For every morphism `Z' → Z`, the diagram

```text
                    f × 1_{Z'}
   X ×_Z Z' ─────────────────→ Y ×_Z Z'
         ╲                       │
        p ╲                      │ p'
           ↘                     ↓
                                 Z'
```

(where `p` and `p'` are the projections) commutes `(I, 3.2.1)`; furthermore,
`f × 1_{Z'}` is surjective since `f` is `(I, 3.5.2)`, and `p` is a closed
morphism by hypothesis. Every closed subset `F` of `Y ×_Z Z'` is then the
image under `f × 1_{Z'}` of a closed subset `E` of `X ×_Z Z'`, hence
`p'(F) = p(E)` is closed in `Z'` by hypothesis, whence the corollary.

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.5.4.4 -->

If `X` is a prescheme proper over `Y`, and `𝒮` a quasi-coherent
`𝒪_Y`-algebra, then every `Y`-morphism `f : X → Proj(𝒮)` is proper (and _a
fortiori_ closed).

**Proof.** Indeed, the structure morphism `p : Proj(𝒮) → Y` is separated,
and `p ∘ f` is proper by hypothesis.

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.5.4.5 -->

Let `f : X → Y` be a separated morphism of finite type. Let `(X_i)_{1 ≤ i ≤ n}`
(resp. `(Y_i)_{1 ≤ i ≤ n}`) be a finite family of closed subpreschemes of `X`
(resp. `Y`), and `j_i` (resp. `h_i`) the canonical injection `X_i → X`
(resp. `Y_i → Y`). Suppose that the underlying space of `X` is the union of
the `X_i`, and that for every `i` there is a morphism `f_i : X_i → Y_i` such
that the diagram

```text
              f_i
   X_i ───────────→ Y_i
    │                │
 j_i│                │ h_i
    ↓                ↓
    X ──────────────→ Y
              f
```

commutes. Then for `f` to be proper, it is necessary and sufficient that
each `f_i` be proper.

<!-- original page 102 -->

**Proof.** If `f` is proper, so is `f ∘ j_i`, since `j_i` is a closed
immersion (5.4.2); since `h_i` is a closed immersion, hence a separated
morphism, `f_i` is proper by (5.4.3). Conversely, suppose that each `f_i` is
proper, and consider the prescheme `Z` sum of the `X_i`; let `u` be the
morphism `Z → X` which reduces to `j_i` on each `X_i`. The restriction of
`f ∘ u` to each `X_i` equals `f ∘ j_i = h_i ∘ f_i`, hence is proper since both
`h_i` and `f_i` are (5.4.2); it then follows immediately from Def. (5.4.1)
that `u` is proper. But since `u` is surjective by hypothesis, we conclude
that `f` is proper by (5.4.3).

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.5.4.6 -->

Let `f : X → Y` be a separated morphism of finite type; for `f` to be
proper, it is necessary and sufficient that `f_red : X_red → Y_red` be.

**Proof.** This is a particular case of (5.4.5) with `n = 1`, `X_1 = X_red`,
and `Y_1 = Y_red` `(I, 5.1.5)`.

**(5.4.7)**

<!-- label: II.5.4.7 -->

If `X` and `Y` are Noetherian preschemes and `f : X → Y` a separated morphism
of finite type, then to verify that `f` is proper we may reduce to the case
of dominant morphisms of integral preschemes. Indeed, let `X_i`
(`1 ≤ i ≤ n`) be the finitely many irreducible components of `X` and
consider, for each `i`, the unique closed reduced subprescheme of `X` having
`X_i` as underlying space, which we again denote by `X_i` `(I, 5.2.1)`. Let
`Y_i` be the unique closed reduced subprescheme of `Y` having `f(X_i)` as
underlying space. If `g_i` (resp. `h_i`) is the injection morphism `X_i → X`
(resp. `Y_i → Y`), one concludes that `f ∘ g_i = h_i ∘ f_i`, where `f_i` is
a dominant morphism `X_i → Y_i` `(I, 5.2.2)`; we are then in the conditions
to apply (5.4.5), and for `f` to be proper, it is necessary and sufficient
that the `f_i` be.

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.5.4.8 -->

Let `X`, `Y` be `S`-preschemes separated and of finite type over `S`, and
let `f : X → Y` be an `S`-morphism. For `f` to be proper, it is necessary
and sufficient that, for every `S`-prescheme `S'`, the morphism
`f ×_S 1_{S'} : X ×_S S' → Y ×_S S'` be closed.

**Proof.** First note that if `g : X → S` and `h : Y → S` are the structure
morphisms, then by definition `g = h ∘ f`, hence `f` is separated and of
finite type (`I, 5.5.1` and `I, 6.3.4`). If `f` is proper, then so is
`f ×_S 1_{S'}` (5.4.2); _a fortiori_, `f ×_S 1_{S'}` is closed. Conversely,
suppose the condition of the statement is satisfied and let `Y'` be a
`Y`-prescheme; `Y'` may also be regarded as an `S`-prescheme, and since
`Y → S` is separated, `X ×_Y Y'` is identified with a closed subprescheme of
`X ×_S Y'` `(I, 5.4.2)`. In the commutative diagram

```text
                  f × 1_{Y'}
   X ×_Y Y' ─────────────────→ Y ×_Y Y' = Y'
       │                            │
       ↓                            ↓
   X ×_S Y' ─────────────────→ Y ×_S Y'
                  f × 1_{S'}
```

the vertical arrows are closed immersions; it follows at once that if
`f × 1_{S'}` is a closed morphism, then so is `f × 1_{Y'}`.

**Remark.**

<!-- label: II.5.4.9 -->

We will say that a morphism `f : X → Y` is _universally closed_ if it
satisfies condition (b) of Definition (5.4.1). The reader will observe that

<!-- original page 103 -->

in Propositions (5.4.2) to (5.4.8), one may throughout replace "proper" by
"universally closed" without altering the validity of the results (and in
the hypotheses of (5.4.3), (5.4.5), (5.4.6) and (5.4.8), one may omit the
finiteness conditions).

**(5.4.10)**

<!-- label: II.5.4.10 -->

Let `f : X → Y` be a morphism of finite type. We will say that a closed
subset `Z` of `X` is _proper over `Y`_ (or _`Y`-proper_, or _proper for
`f`_) if the restriction of `f` to a closed subprescheme of `X` with
underlying space `Z` `(I, 5.2.1)` is proper. Since this restriction is then
separated, it follows from (5.4.6) and `(I, 5.5.1, (vi))` that the foregoing
property does not depend on the closed subprescheme of `X` having `Z` as
underlying space. If `g : X' → X` is a proper morphism, then `g⁻¹(Z)` is a
proper subset of `X'`: if `T` is a subprescheme of `X` having `Z` as
underlying space, it suffices to note that the restriction of `g` to the
closed subprescheme `g⁻¹(T)` of `X'` is a proper morphism `g⁻¹(T) → T` by
(5.4.2, (iii)), and then to apply (5.4.2, (ii)). On the other hand, if `X''`
is a `Y`-scheme of finite type and `u : X → X''` a `Y`-morphism, then
`u(Z)` is a proper subset of `X''`; indeed, let us take `T` to be the
closed reduced subprescheme of `X` having `Z` as underlying space; the
restriction of `f` to `T` being proper, so is the restriction of `u` to `T`
(5.4.3, (i)), hence `u(Z)` is closed in `X''`; let `T''` be a closed
subprescheme of `X''` having `u(Z)` as underlying space `(I, 5.2.1)`, so
that `u|T` factors as

```text
  T ─v→ T'' ─j→ X''
```

where `j` is the canonical injection `(I, 5.2.2)`, and `v` is therefore
proper and surjective (5.4.5); if `g` is the restriction to `T''` of the
structure morphism `X'' → Y`, then `g` is separated and of finite type, and
we have `f|T = g ∘ v`; it then follows from (5.4.3, (ii)) that `g` is
proper, whence our assertion.

It follows in particular from these remarks that if `Z` is a `Y`-proper
subset of `X`, then:

1° For every closed subprescheme `X'` of `X`, `Z ∩ X'` is a `Y`-proper
subset of `X'`.

2° If `X` is a subprescheme of a `Y`-scheme of finite type `X''`, then `Z` is
also a `Y`-proper subset of `X''` (and in particular is closed in `X''`).

## 5.5. Projective morphisms

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.5.5.1 -->

Let `X` be a `Y`-prescheme. The following conditions are equivalent:

1. (a) `X` is `Y`-isomorphic to a closed subprescheme of a projective bundle
    `ℙ(𝓔)`, where `𝓔` is a quasi-coherent `𝒪_Y`-module of finite type.
1. (b) There exists a quasi-coherent graded `𝒪_Y`-algebra `𝒮` such that `𝒮₁`
    is of finite type and generates `𝒮`, and such that `X` is `Y`-isomorphic
    to `Proj(𝒮)`.

**Proof.** Condition (a) implies (b) by virtue of (3.6.2, (ii)): if `𝒥` is
a quasi-coherent graded sheaf of ideals of `𝕊(𝓔)`, the quasi-coherent
graded `𝒪_Y`-algebra `𝒮 = 𝕊(𝓔)/𝒥` is generated by `𝒮₁`, and the latter,
the canonical image of `𝓔`, is an `𝒪_Y`-module of finite type. Condition
(b) implies (a) by virtue of (3.6.2) applied to the case where `ℳ → 𝒮₁` is
the identity map.

<!-- original page 104 -->

**Definition.**

<!-- label: II.5.5.2 -->

We say that a `Y`-prescheme `X` is _projective over `Y`_, or is a
_projective `Y`-scheme_, if it satisfies the equivalent conditions (a) and
(b) of (5.5.1). We say that a morphism `f : X → Y` is _projective_ if it
makes `X` a projective `Y`-scheme.

It is clear that if `f : X → Y` is projective, then there exists an
`𝒪_X`-module very ample relative to `f` (4.4.2).

**Theorem.**

<!-- label: II.5.5.3 -->

1. (i) Every projective morphism is quasi-projective and proper.
1. (ii) Conversely, let `Y` be a quasi-compact scheme or a prescheme whose
    underlying space is Noetherian; then every morphism `f : X → Y` that is
    quasi-projective and proper is projective.

**Proof.** (i) It is clear that if `f : X → Y` is projective, it is of
finite type and quasi-projective (hence in particular separated); on the
other hand, it follows at once from (5.5.1, (b)) and (3.5.3) that if `f` is
projective, so is `f ×_Y 1_{Y'} : X ×_Y Y' → Y'` for every morphism
`Y' → Y`. To show that `f` is universally closed, everything therefore
comes down to proving that a projective morphism `f` is closed. The question
being local on `Y`, we may suppose that `Y = Spec(A)`, hence (5.5.1)
`X = Proj(S)`, where `S` is a graded `A`-algebra generated by a finite
number of elements of `S₁`. For every `y ∈ Y`, the fibre `f⁻¹(y)` is
identified with `Proj(S) ×_Y Spec(κ(y))` `(I, 3.6.1)`, hence with
`Proj(S ⊗_A κ(y))` (2.8.10); consequently `f⁻¹(y)` is empty if and only if
`S ⊗_A κ(y)` satisfies condition (TN) (2.7.4), in other words
`S_n ⊗_A κ(y) = 0` for `n` sufficiently large. But since `(S_n)_y` is an
`𝒪_y`-module of finite type, the preceding condition means that
`(S_n)_y = 0` for `n` sufficiently large, by Nakayama's lemma. If `𝔞_n` is
the annihilator in `A` of the `A`-module `S_n`, the preceding condition
also means that `𝔞_n ⊄ 𝔧_y` for `n` sufficiently large `(0, 1.7.4)`. Now,
since `S_n S₁ = S_{n+1}` by hypothesis, we have `𝔞_n ⊂ 𝔞_{n+1}`, and if `𝔞`
is the union of the `𝔞_n`, we see that `f(X) = V(𝔞)`, which proves that
`f(X)` is closed in `Y`. If now `X'` is an arbitrary closed subset of `X`,
there exists a closed subprescheme of `X` having `X'` as underlying space
`(I, 5.2.1)`, and it is clear (5.5.1, (a)) that the morphism
`X' → X → Y` is projective, so `f(X')` is closed in `Y`.

(ii) The hypothesis on `Y` and the fact that `f` is quasi-projective imply
the existence of a quasi-coherent `𝒪_Y`-module `𝓔` of finite type and of a
`Y`-immersion `j : X → ℙ(𝓔)` (5.3.2). But since `f` is proper, `j` is
closed by (5.4.4), hence `f` is projective.

**Remarks.**

<!-- label: II.5.5.4 -->

(i) Let `f : X → Y` be a morphism such that: 1° `f` is proper; 2° there
exists an `𝒪_X`-module `ℒ` very ample relative to `f`; 3° the quasi-coherent
`𝒪_Y`-module `𝓔 = f_*(ℒ)` is of finite type. Then `f` is a projective
morphism: indeed (4.4.4), there is then a `Y`-immersion `r : X → ℙ(𝓔)`, and
since `f` is proper, `r` is a closed immersion (5.4.4). We will see in
Chapter III, §3, that when `Y` is locally Noetherian, condition 3° above is
a consequence of the other two, hence conditions 1° and 2° characterize, in
this case, the projective morphisms, and if `Y` is quasi-compact, one may
replace condition 2° by the hypothesis of the existence of an `𝒪_X`-module
ample for `f` (4.6.11).

(ii) Let `Y` be a quasi-compact scheme such that there exists an ample
`𝒪_Y`-module. For a `Y`-scheme `X` to be projective, it is necessary and
sufficient that it be `Y`-isomorphic to a closed `Y`-subscheme of a
projective bundle of the form `ℙ_Y^r`. The condition is obviously sufficient.

<!-- original page 105 -->

Conversely, if `X` is projective over `Y`, then it is quasi-projective, so
there exists a `Y`-immersion `j` of `X` into some `ℙ_Y^r` (5.3.3) which is
closed by (5.4.4) and (5.5.3).

(iii) The argument of (5.5.3) shows that for every prescheme `Y` and every
integer `r ≥ 0`, the structure morphism `ℙ_Y^r → Y` is _surjective_, for if
we set `𝒮 = 𝕊_{𝒪_Y}(𝒪_Y^{r+1})`, we evidently have
`𝒮_y = 𝕊_{κ(y)}(κ(y)^{r+1})` (1.7.3), hence `(𝒮_n)_y ≠ 0` for every
`y ∈ Y` and every `n ≥ 0`.

(iv) It follows from the examples of Nagata [26] that there exist proper
morphisms that are not quasi-projective.

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.5.5.5 -->

1. (i) A closed immersion is a projective morphism.
1. (ii) If `f : X → Y` and `g : Y → Z` are projective morphisms, and if `Z`
    is a quasi-compact scheme or a prescheme whose underlying space is
    Noetherian, then `g ∘ f` is projective.
1. (iii) If `f : X → Y` is a projective `S`-morphism, then
    `f_{(S')} : X_{(S')} → Y_{(S')}` is projective for every base change
    `S' → S`.
1. (iv) If `f : X → Y` and `g : X' → Y'` are projective `S`-morphisms, then
    so is `f ×_S g`.
1. (v) If `g ∘ f` is a projective morphism and if `g` is separated, then `f`
    is projective.
1. (vi) If `f` is projective, so is `f_red`.

**Proof.** (i) follows at once from (3.1.7). Here we must prove (iii) and
(iv) separately, because of the restriction introduced on `Z` in (ii)
(cf. `(I, 3.5.1)`). To prove (iii), we reduce to the case `S = Y`
`(I, 3.3.11)` and the assertion then follows at once from (5.5.1, (b)) and
(3.5.3). To prove (iv), we are at once reduced to the case `X = ℙ(𝓔)`,
`X' = ℙ(𝓔')`, with `𝓔` (resp. `𝓔'`) a quasi-coherent `𝒪_Y`-module (resp.
`𝒪_{Y'}`-module) of finite type. Let `p`, `p'` be the canonical projections
of `T = Y ×_S Y'` to `Y` and `Y'` respectively; by (4.1.3.1) we have
`ℙ(p*(𝓔)) = ℙ(𝓔) ×_Y T`, `ℙ(p'*(𝓔')) = ℙ(𝓔') ×_{Y'} T`; whence

```text
  ℙ(p*(𝓔)) ×_T ℙ(p'*(𝓔')) = (ℙ(𝓔) ×_Y T) ×_T (T ×_{Y'} ℙ(𝓔'))
                          = ℙ(𝓔) ×_Y (T ×_{Y'} ℙ(𝓔')) = ℙ(𝓔) ×_S ℙ(𝓔')
```

upon replacing `T` by `Y ×_S Y'` and using `(I, 3.3.9.1)`. Now `p*(𝓔)` and
`p'*(𝓔')` are of finite type over `T` `(0, 5.2.4)`, hence so is
`p*(𝓔) ⊗_{𝒪_T} p'*(𝓔')`; since `ℙ(p*(𝓔)) ×_T ℙ(p'*(𝓔'))` is identified
with a closed subprescheme of `ℙ(p*(𝓔) ⊗_{𝒪_T} p'*(𝓔'))` (4.3.3), this
completes the proof of (iv). To obtain (v) and (vi), we may apply
`(I, 5.5.13)`, since every closed subprescheme of a projective `Y`-scheme is
a projective `Y`-scheme by (5.5.1, (a)).

It remains to prove (ii); by virtue of the hypothesis on `Z`, this follows
from (5.5.3), (5.3.4, (ii)) and (5.4.2, (ii)).

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.5.5.6 -->

If `X` and `X'` are two projective `Y`-schemes, then `X ⊔ X'` is a
projective `Y`-scheme.

**Proof.** This is an evident consequence of (5.5.2) and (4.3.6).

**Proposition.**

<!-- label: II.5.5.7 -->

Let `X` be a projective `Y`-scheme, and `ℒ` a `Y`-ample `𝒪_X`-module; for
every section `f` of `ℒ` over `X`, `X_f` is affine over `Y`.

<!-- original page 106 -->

**Proof.** The question being local on `Y`, we may suppose `Y = Spec(A)`;
furthermore, `X_{f^{⊗n}} = X_f`, so by replacing `ℒ` with a suitable
`ℒ^{⊗n}`, we may suppose that `ℒ` is very ample for the structure morphism
`q : X → Y` (4.6.11). The canonical homomorphism
`σ : q*(q_*(ℒ)) → ℒ` is then surjective, and the corresponding morphism

```text
  r = r_{ℒ, σ} : X → P = ℙ(q_*(ℒ))
```

is an immersion such that `ℒ = r*(𝒪_P(1))` (4.4.4); furthermore, since `X`
is proper over `Y`, the immersion `r` is closed (5.4.4). Moreover, by
definition `f ∈ Γ(Y, q_*(ℒ))` and `σ♭` is the identity of `q_*(ℒ)`; it then
follows from formula (3.7.3.1) that `X_f = r⁻¹(D_+(f))`. Therefore `X_f` is
a closed subprescheme of the affine scheme `D_+(f)` and is thus an affine
scheme.

Taking in particular `Y = X`, one obtains (taking (4.6.13, (i)) into
account) the following corollary, whose direct proof is moreover immediate:

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.5.5.8 -->

Let `X` be a prescheme and `ℒ` an invertible `𝒪_X`-module. For every section
`f` of `ℒ` over `X`, `X_f` is affine over `X` (and consequently an affine
scheme when `X` is an affine scheme).

## 5.6. Chow's lemma

**Theorem (Chow's lemma).**

<!-- label: II.5.6.1 -->

Let `S` be a prescheme, `X` an `S`-scheme of finite type. Suppose that one
of the following hypotheses is satisfied:

1. (a) `S` is Noetherian.
1. (b) `S` is a quasi-compact scheme, and `X` has a finite number of
    irreducible components.

Under these conditions:

1. (i) There exists an `S`-scheme `X'` quasi-projective over `S` and an
    `S`-morphism `f : X' → X` that is projective and surjective.
1. (ii) One can take `X'` and `f` such that there exists an open `U ⊂ X` for
    which `U' = f⁻¹(U)` is dense in `X'` and the restriction of `f` to `U'`
    is an isomorphism `U' ⥲ U`.
1. (iii) If `X` is reduced (resp. irreducible, integral), one may suppose
    `X'` reduced (resp. irreducible, integral).

**Proof.** The proof proceeds in several steps.

A) One can first reduce to the case where `X` is irreducible. Indeed,
under hypothesis (a), `X` is Noetherian, so, in either hypothesis, the
irreducible components `X_i` of `X` are finite in number. If the theorem is
proved for each of the closed reduced subpreschemes of `X` having the
`X_i` as their underlying spaces, and if `X'_i` and `f_i : X'_i → X_i` are
the corresponding prescheme and morphism, then the prescheme `X'` given by
the sum of the `X'_i`, and the morphism `f : X' → X` whose restriction to
each `X'_i` is `j_i ∘ f_i` (with `j_i` the canonical injection `X_i → X`)
will answer the question. It is immediate that `X'` is reduced if the
`X'_i` are; on the other hand, (ii) is satisfied by taking for `U` the
union of the sets `U_i ∩ ∁(⋃_{j ≠ i} X_j)`. Finally, since the `X'_i` are
quasi-projective over `S`, so is `X'`

<!-- original page 107 -->

(5.3.6); likewise, the morphisms `X'_i → X` are projective by (5.5.5, (i)
and (ii)), hence `f` is projective (5.5.6), and is obviously surjective by
definition.

B) Now suppose that `X` is irreducible. Since the structure morphism
`r : X → S` is of finite type, there is a finite covering `(S_i)` of `S`
by affine opens, and for each `i` a finite covering `(T_{ij})` of
`r⁻¹(S_i)` by affine opens, the morphism `T_{ij} → S_i` being affine and
of finite type, hence quasi-projective (5.3.4, (i)); since in each of the
hypotheses (a), (b) the immersion `S_i → S` is quasi-compact, it is a
quasi-projective morphism (5.3.4, (i)), hence the restriction of `r` to
`T_{ij}` is a quasi-projective morphism (5.3.4, (ii)). Denote the
`T_{ij}` by `U_k` (`1 ≤ k ≤ n`). For each index `k` there exists an open
immersion `φ_k : U_k → P_k`, where `P_k` is projective over `S` (5.3.2 and
5.5.2). Let `U = ⋂_k U_k`; since `X` is irreducible and the `U_k`
non-empty, `U` is non-empty, hence everywhere dense in `X`; the
restrictions to `U` of the `φ_k` define a morphism

```text
  φ : U → P = P_1 ×_S P_2 ×_S ⋯ ×_S P_n
```

such that the diagrams

```text
              φ
   U ─────────────→ P
   │                │
 j_k│                │ p_k                                                  (5.6.1.1)
   ↓                ↓
   U_k ───────────→ P_k
              φ_k
```

commute, `j_k` being the canonical injection `U → U_k` and `p_k` the
canonical projection `P → P_k`. If `j` is the canonical injection
`U → X`, the morphism `ψ = (j, φ)_S : U → X ×_S P` is an immersion
`(I, 5.3.14)`. Under hypothesis (a), `X ×_S P` is locally Noetherian
((3.4.1) and `(I, 6.3.7` and `6.3.8)`); under hypothesis (b), `X ×_S P` is a
quasi-compact scheme `(I, 5.5.1` and `I, 6.6.4)`; in both cases the closure
`X'` in `X ×_S P` of the subprescheme `Z` associated to `ψ` (and having
`ψ(U)` as underlying space) exists, and `ψ` factors as

```text
  ψ : U ─ψ'→ X' ─h→ X ×_S P                                                 (5.6.1.2)
```

where `ψ'` is an open immersion and `h` a closed immersion `(I, 9.5.10)`.
Let `q_1 : X ×_S P → X` and `q_2 : X ×_S P → P` be the canonical
projections; we set

```text
  f : X' ─h→ X ×_S P ─q_1→ X,                                               (5.6.1.3)
```

```text
  g : X' ─h→ X ×_S P ─q_2→ P.                                               (5.6.1.4)
```

We will see that `X'` and `f` answer the question.

C) Let us first show that `f` is projective and surjective, and that the
restriction of `f` to `U' = f⁻¹(U)` is an isomorphism of `U'` onto `U`.
Since the `P_k` are projective over `S`, so is `P` (5.5.5, (iv)), hence
`X ×_S P` is projective over `X` (5.5.5, (iii)), and so is `X'`, which is
a closed subprescheme of `X ×_S P`. Furthermore,
`f ∘ ψ' = q_1 ∘ (h ∘ ψ') = q_1 ∘ ψ = j`, hence `f(X')` contains the open
everywhere-dense subset `U` of `X`; but `f` is a closed morphism (5.5.3),
hence `f(X') = X`. Note now that `q_1⁻¹(U) = U ×_S P` is induced on an open
subset of `X ×_S P`, and by definition the prescheme
`U' = h⁻¹(U ×_S P)` is induced by `X'` on the open subset `U'`; it is
therefore the closure with respect

<!-- original page 108 -->

to `U ×_S P` of the prescheme `Z` `(I, 9.5.8)`. But the immersion `ψ`
factors as
`U ─Γ_φ→ U ×_S P ─j × 1→ X ×_S P`, and since `P` is separated over `S`,
the graph morphism `Γ_φ` is a closed immersion `(I, 5.4.3)`, so `Z` is a
closed subprescheme of `U ×_S P`, whence `U' = Z`. Since `ψ` is an
immersion, the restriction of `f` to `U'` is an isomorphism onto `U`, the
inverse of `ψ'`; finally, by the definition of `X'`, `U'` is dense in `X'`.

D) Let us now prove that `g` is an immersion, which will establish that
`X'` is quasi-projective over `S`, since `P` is projective over `S`. Set

```text
  V_k  = φ_k(U_k)     (open subset of P_k)
  W_k  = p_k⁻¹(V_k)   (open subset of P)
  U'_k = f⁻¹(U_k)     (open subset of X')
  U''_k = g⁻¹(W_k)    (open subset of X').
```

It is clear that the `U'_k` form an open covering of `X'`; we will first
see that the `U''_k` likewise do, by proving that `U'_k ⊂ U''_k`. For this
it suffices to show that the diagram

```text
              g|U'_k
   U'_k ─────────────→ P
    │                  │
 f|U'_k                │ p_k                                                (5.6.1.5)
    ↓                  ↓
   U_k ─────────────→ P_k
              φ_k
```

commutes. Now the prescheme `U'_k = h⁻¹(U_k ×_S P)` is induced by `X'` on
the open subset `U'_k`, hence is the closure of `Z = U' ⊂ U'_k` with
respect to `U'_k` `(I, 9.5.8)`. To show the commutativity of (5.6.1.5), it
therefore suffices (since `P_k` is an `S`-scheme) to show that composing
this diagram with the canonical injection `U' → U'_k` (or equivalently, in
view of the isomorphism between `U'` and `U`, with `ψ`), we obtain a
commutative diagram `(I, 9.5.6)`. But by definition the diagram one then
obtains is precisely (5.6.1.1), whence our assertion.

The `W_k` therefore form an open covering of `g(X')`; to prove that `g`
is an immersion, it suffices to show that each restriction `g|U''_k` is
an immersion into `W_k` `(I, 4.2.4)`. For this, consider the morphism

```text
  u_k : W_k ─p_k→ V_k ─φ_k⁻¹→ U_k → X;
```

since `X` is separated over `S`, the graph morphism
`Γ_{u_k} : W_k → X ×_S W_k` is a closed immersion `(I, 5.4.3)`, hence the
graph `T_k = Γ_{u_k}(W_k)` is a closed subprescheme of `X ×_S W_k`; if we
show that this subprescheme majorizes `U'`, it will also majorize the
subprescheme induced by `X'` on the open subset `U''_k` of `X'`, by
`(I, 9.5.8)`. Since the restriction of `q_2` to `T_k` is an isomorphism
onto `W_k`, the restriction of `g` to `U''_k` will be an immersion into
`W_k`, and our assertion will be proved. Let `v_k` be the canonical
injection `U' → X ×_S W_k`; we must show that there exists a morphism
`w_k : U' → W_k` such that `v_k = Γ_{u_k} ∘ w_k`. By the definition of the
product, it suffices to prove that `q_1 ∘ v_k = u_k ∘ q_2 ∘ v_k`
`(I, 3.2.1)`, or, composing on the right

<!-- original page 109 -->

with the isomorphism `ψ' : U → U'`, that
`q_1 ∘ ψ = u_k ∘ q_2 ∘ ψ`. But since `q_1 ∘ ψ = j` and `q_2 ∘ ψ = φ`, our
assertion follows from the commutativity of (5.6.1.1), taking into account
the definition of `u_k`.

E) It is clear that since `U`, and consequently `U'`, is irreducible, so
is `X'` in the preceding construction, and the morphism `f` is therefore
_birational_ `(I, 2.2.9)`. If in addition `X` is reduced, so is `U'`,
hence `X'` is also reduced `(I, 9.5.9)`. This completes the proof.

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.5.6.2 -->

Suppose one of the hypotheses (a), (b) of (5.6.1) is satisfied. For `X` to
be proper over `S`, it is necessary and sufficient that there exist a
scheme `X'` projective over `S` and an `S`-morphism surjective
`f : X' → X` (which is then projective by (5.5.5, (v))). When this is so,
one may furthermore choose `f` so that there exists a dense open `U` of
`X` such that the restriction of `f` to `f⁻¹(U)` is an isomorphism
`f⁻¹(U) ⥲ U` and `f⁻¹(U)` is dense in `X'`. If furthermore `X` is
irreducible (resp. reduced), one may suppose that `X'` is too; when `X`
and `X'` are irreducible, `f` is a birational morphism.

**Proof.** The condition is sufficient by virtue of (5.5.3) and (5.4.3,
(ii)). It is necessary, because with the notation of (5.6.1), if `X` is
proper over `S`, then `X'` is proper over `S`, being projective over `X`
hence proper over `X` (5.5.3), and our assertion follows from (5.4.2,
(ii)); furthermore, since `X'` is quasi-projective over `S`, it is
projective over `S` by virtue of (5.5.3).

**Corollary.**

<!-- label: II.5.6.3 -->

Let `S` be a locally Noetherian prescheme, `X` an `S`-scheme of finite type
over `S`, `f_0 : X → S` its structure morphism. For `X` to be proper over
`S`, it is necessary and sufficient that for every morphism of finite type
`S' → S`, `(f_0)_{(S')} : X_{(S')} → S'` be a closed morphism. It suffices
even for this condition to be satisfied for every `S`-prescheme of the
form `S' = S ⊗_ℤ ℤ[T_1, …, T_n]` (`T_i` indeterminates).

**Proof.** The condition being obviously necessary, let us show that it is
sufficient. The question being local on `S` and `S'` (5.4.1), we may
suppose `S` and `S'` affine and Noetherian. By Chow's lemma, there exist
an `S`-scheme `P` projective, an immersion `j : X' → P`, and a projective
surjective morphism `f : X' → X`, such that the diagram

```text
    X ←─f── X'
    │       │
 f_0│       │ j
    ↓       ↓
    S ←─r── P
```

commutes. Since `P` is of finite type over `S`, the first hypothesis
implies that the projection `q_2 : X ×_S P → P` is a closed morphism. But
the immersion `j` is the composition of `q_2` and the morphism `f × 1`
from `X' ×_S P` to `X ×_S P`; now `f`, being projective, is proper
(5.5.3), hence `f × 1` is closed. We conclude that `j` is a closed
immersion, hence proper (5.4.2, (i)). Furthermore, the structure morphism
`r : P → S` is projective, hence proper (5.5.3), so
`f_0 ∘ f = r ∘ j` is proper (5.4.2, (ii)); finally, since `f` is
surjective, `f_0` is proper by virtue of (5.4.3).

To prove the proposition using only the second hypothesis, it suffices to
show that it implies the first. Now, if `S'` is affine and of finite type
over `S = Spec(A)`,

<!-- original page 110 -->

then `S' = Spec(A[c_1, …, c_n])` `(I, 6.3.3)`, and so `S'` is isomorphic to
a closed subprescheme of `S'' = Spec(A[T_1, …, T_n])` (`T_i`
indeterminates). In the commutative diagram

```text
                  1_X × j
   X ×_S S' ─────────────→ X ×_S S''
       │                         │
(f_0)_{(S')}              (f_0)_{(S'')}
       ↓                         ↓
       S' ─────────────────→ S''
                    j
```

`j` and `1_X × j` are closed immersions `(I, 4.3.1)` and `(f_0)_{(S')}` is
closed by hypothesis; so it is also for `(f_0)_{(S'')}`.
